The first time a muskellunge follows your lure to the boat — a metre of muscle gliding a hand's width behind your bucktail, dorsal fin cutting the surface — you understand why anglers call it the fish of ten thousand casts. Then it sinks away, and you learn the second lesson: in muskie fishing, the cast does not end at the boat. The figure-eight, a wide sweeping pattern drawn with the rod tip in the water at boatside, converts followers into eaters, and serious muskie anglers finish every single retrieve with one. Northern pike, the muskie's circumpolar cousin, are less haughty but no less thrilling — and together these two esocids define predator fishing across the northern hemisphere.

## Two Predators, One Family

Northern pike (Esox lucius) range across the entire northern hemisphere — Canada, Alaska, Scandinavia, the Baltic, Britain, Ireland, Russia — and grow huge in the right water: the rod-caught records hover around 25 pounds in North America and over 55 pounds in Europe, with Germany's Grefeern lake and Sweden's Baltic archipelagos producing giants. Muskellunge (Esox masquinongy) are North American only, native to the Great Lakes basin, the St. Lawrence, the upper Mississippi and Wisconsin and Minnesota lake country; the long-standing world record stands at 67 pounds 8 ounces from Lake Court Oreilles, Wisconsin (1949). Pike have light bean-shaped spots on a dark body; muskies reverse it with dark bars or spots on a light body, and they grow longer — true giants pass 50 inches. Where they overlap, the hybrid tiger muskie adds a third, strikingly barred target.

## Why You Always Need a Wire or Heavy Leader

Both species carry hundreds of backward-slanting teeth plus razor gill rakers, and they bite through monofilament and ordinary fluorocarbon effortlessly. A leader is not optional. For pike, 30 to 45 centimetres of 40- to 60-pound knottable titanium or seven-strand wire is standard; for muskies, many guides run 130- to 150-pound fluorocarbon or heavy wire of 90 pounds plus. The leader debate matters less than the principle: no esocid lure should ever swim without bite protection, and that includes the perch jig you were casting when a pike appeared. Carry long-nosed pliers, side cutters for hooks, a wide rubberised net or cradle, and jaw spreaders — unhooking is where both fish and fingers get hurt.

## Seasonal Location: Follow the Temperature

Pike spawn immediately after ice-out in flooded vegetation, often in water below 10 degrees Celsius, so early spring finds them shallow in bays, marshes and river mouths. As water warms past about 18 degrees, the biggest pike slide deeper to weed edges, rock reefs and open-water baitfish schools — in stratified lakes the trophy fish often suspend near the thermocline chasing ciscoes or whitefish. Muskies spawn later, around 9 to 15 degrees, and summer muskies relate to weed flats, rock bars and saddles between islands. Autumn is the giant season for both: cooling water pushes big females onto steep breaks and baitfish concentrations, and trolling large crankbaits or working oversized rubber baits slowly produces the heaviest fish of the year. In winter, pike remain active under the ice and tip-up fishing with dead baits is a northern tradition from Minnesota to Finland.

## Lures That Move Big Fish

The esocid lure box is gloriously oversized. Bucktail inline spinners — from the classic Mepps Musky Killer to double-bladed models with size 10 blades — create thump and flash that muskies track from far off. Large soft rubber baits like the Bull Dawg or Swedish-style shads in 20 to 30 centimetres can be cast or trolled and dominate cold water. Jerkbaits — gliders and divers such as the Suick or the Buster Jerk — dart erratically with rod snaps and excel along weed edges. Topwater prop baits and walk-the-dog lures convert ferociously in low light and calm summer evenings. For pike specifically, the weedless spoon fished over cabbage beds and the classic red-and-white spoon still account for thousands of fish every season. Match lure size to the calendar: moderate in spring, big in summer, enormous in autumn.

## The Figure-Eight and Boatside Discipline

Muskies and big pike routinely follow without striking, and the figure-eight is the answer. Before the lure reaches the boat, plunge the rod tip half a metre into the water and pull the lure into wide, smooth oval turns — wide is critical, because a 120-centimetre fish cannot turn inside a tight circle. Keep the lure speeding up through the turns, never slowing. Follows happen most at dawn, dusk, and the so-called moonrise and moonset windows that many guides plan around. Stay disciplined: eight to ten hours of casting heavy lures demands a balanced 8-foot 6-inch to 9-foot extra-heavy rod, a 400-size low-profile reel and 80- to 100-pound braid, which together turn the work from punishing to merely demanding.

## Handling, Release and Regulations

Both species are slow-growing apex predators; a 50-inch muskie may be 17 to 20 years old, and removing one alters a whole bay's ecology. Modern muskie culture is almost entirely catch-and-release, with many jurisdictions enforcing high minimum sizes — 54 inches on Minnesota muskie waters, for instance — and Wisconsin and Ontario protect spawning with closed seasons that typically open in late May or early June. Keep fish in the water in a cradle or net, support the belly horizontally for a fast photo, and revive thoroughly in warm-water months when mortality risk climbs. For pike in Europe, check national rules: Ireland encourages release of trophy pike, while Baltic Sea pike in Sweden are protected by bag limits of three fish and a 40-to-75-centimetre harvest window in many areas.

## Where in the World to Hunt Them

For muskies, the shortlist is the St. Lawrence River and Lake of the Woods for true giants, Minnesota's Mille Lacs and Vermilion, Wisconsin's Hayward lakes, and Ontario's Georgian Bay. For pike, Canada's fly-in lakes in Saskatchewan and Manitoba offer hundred-fish days, while Europe counters with the Baltic archipelagos of Sweden and Finland, Ireland's loughs like Corrib and Derg, the Netherlands' polder canals, and the vast Irish-style trolling water of Lough Mask. Each fishery has a character: numbers in the north, size where forage is rich and pressure is low.
