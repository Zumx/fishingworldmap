The first run is the conversion experience. A bonefish in ankle-deep water takes a shrimp pattern almost gently, then realises its mistake and leaves — a hundred metres of backing evaporating off the reel in seconds, the line hissing through water so shallow the fish's wake stands up like a speedboat's. The Bahamas is where this addiction is most reliably fed: more than 700 islands and cays scattered over a quarter-million square kilometres of turquoise bank, with arguably the largest concentration of bonefish habitat on the planet. This deep dive compares the famous islands — Andros, Abaco, Grand Bahama, the Exumas and the far-out islands — and weighs the two great approaches: do-it-yourself wading versus guided skiff fishing from a lodge.

## The quarry: ghosts, permit and the odd tarpon

Bonefish (Albula vulpes) are the headline act — averaging 2 to 4 pounds across the archipelago, with Andros and the southern islands giving honest shots at fish over 8. They feed on shrimp, crabs and worms across sand and turtle-grass flats on a tidal clock, and they are spooked by a heavy footfall at twenty metres. Permit are the heartbreaker: present in numbers around the Joulter Cays, Crooked and Acklins, and the southern Exumas, and famously indifferent to perfect casts. Add resident barracuda that hit poppers like missiles, sharks to keep releases honest, and small migratory tarpon in the creeks of Andros' west side. Since 2017, Bahamian law requires a personal flats-fishing licence for visiting anglers — inexpensive, bought online — and bonefish on the flats are catch-and-release as standard practice.

## Andros: the big one

Andros is the largest and least developed Bahamian island, and for bonefish it is the reference point. Its three great bights — North, Middle and South — cut the island into a labyrinth of creeks and flats that no angler could learn in a lifetime. The east side around Fresh Creek and Cargill Creek hosts the classic lodges; the remote west side is a wilderness of mangrove shoreline reachable only by boat, where double-digit fish cruise unbothered. To the north, the Joulter Cays — a protected national park area — offer vast, firm, wadeable sand flats and a real permit population. Red Bays, the island's old sponger settlement, supplies some of the country's most storied guides. If you want one trip that shows what bonefishing can be, Andros is the answer.

## Abaco and the Marls

Abaco, in the north-east, balances fishing with comfort: good flights into Marsh Harbour, rental cars, restaurants and beaches for non-fishing partners. Its trump card is the Marls — roughly 400 square miles of mangrove flats on the western shore, soft-bottomed and skiff-only, holding huge numbers of eager 2-4 pound fish. The eastern side offers harder, wadeable flats at Cherokee Sound and the Casuarina to Sandy Point shoreline, where bigger singles tail on the tide. Abaco rebuilt determinedly after Hurricane Dorian in 2019, and fishing tourism has been central to that recovery — your visit counts here. For DIY-minded anglers, Abaco is probably the best blend of access and quality in the country.

## Grand Bahama and the East End

Grand Bahama is the convenience play: 55 minutes from Florida, with Freeport's infrastructure and flats fishing concentrated toward the island's East End around McLean's Town and Deep Water Cay. The North Riding Point Club made its name running skiffs to remote ocean-side flats where the average fish runs notably larger than the archipelago norm — 4 to 6 pounds, with genuine trophies each season. The south shore holds creek systems and wadeable stretches reachable by car, making Grand Bahama a strong choice for a long weekend: fly in Friday, fish three tides, home Monday.

## The out islands: Exuma, Long Island, Crooked and Acklins

The further down the chain you go, the emptier the flats. The Exumas pair Georgetown's easy logistics with wadeable flats and a serious permit fishery in the southern cays. Long Island offers the great DIY compromise — affordable guesthouses, a car-accessible string of bays and creeks around Deadman's Cay, and locals who will point you to the tide. Crooked and Acklins, enclosing a huge shallow bight, are the connoisseur's frontier: tiny populations, one or two small lodges, and flats that may not see another angler all week. Inagua, far south, is for the genuinely adventurous. Out-island travel runs on small planes and island time; build slack into connections and carry backup tackle.

## Lodge versus DIY: the honest comparison

A good lodge buys you three things: a guide's eyes (which will spot ten fish for your one), a skiff that opens unwadeable country like the Marls or Andros' west side, and the removal of all logistics. Expect roughly 600 to 900 US dollars per day all-in at quality operations — painful, but the fastest learning curve in flats fishing. DIY costs a fraction: a guesthouse, a rental car, free maps and tide tables, and flats you reach on foot. The price is fewer and spookier fish, hours lost to dead water, and the discipline of self-guiding — polarised glasses, careful tide planning, and the humility to wade slowly. The smart first trip blends both: three guided days to learn the water's language, three DIY days to speak it yourself. Wherever you fish, respect private land, avoid pressuring the same flat daily, and tip guides properly — 10 to 15 percent is customary.

## Seasons, weather and the wind window

Bonefish are present year-round, and the real variables are wind, light and fronts. October to early December and March to June are the sweet spots: warm water, long light, manageable breezes. Mid-winter (January-February) brings cold fronts that push fish off the flats for a day or two at a time — fine on a week's trip, frustrating on a weekend. Summer is hot, glassy at dawn, stormy by afternoon, and hurricane season formally runs June to November; September is the statistical peak and the month most lodges close. Whatever the month, the fishing day is built around tide: the last of the falling and the first of the rising water push fish onto the flats to feed.

## Tackle for the salt

One rod can do it: a 9-foot 8-weight with a sealed-drag reel, 200 m of backing, a floating tropical line and 12-16 lb fluorocarbon leaders of 9 to 12 feet. Flies are simple — Gotchas, Crazy Charlies and small crab patterns in tan, pink and white, sizes 2 to 8, weighted variously for depth. Add a 10-weight if permit or big barracuda tempt you. Flats boots are non-negotiable for DIY wading (coral and urchins are real), as are two pairs of polarised glasses, sun gloves and a buff: the Bahamian sun is the trip's only genuine hazard. Practise casting in wind at home — a quick, accurate 15-metre shot beats a pretty 25-metre one every day of the week.
