The steelhead is a rainbow trout that went to sea. It returned enormous, chrome-bright, and extraordinarily powerful — a fish that has inspired more lyrical writing, more obsessive devotion, and more monomaniacal winter suffering than perhaps any other species in North American fishing. Oncorhynchus mykiss irideus is genetically identical to the rainbow, but ocean life transforms it: a 3-year-old smolt that left a Pacific Northwest river at 20 cm may return four years later as a 90 cm, 9 kg fish, fresh from the North Pacific, scales like hammered platinum, fight measured in 50-metre runs and vertical leaps. The steelheader goes to the river in February in driving rain, wades in chest-high flow at 4°C, and swings a Spey fly through green water at dawn — and considers it a privilege. The fish of 10,000 casts earns that name honestly.

## Summer-Run and Winter-Run Fish

Steelhead populations are divided into summer-run and winter-run fish based on when they enter freshwater. Summer-run fish enter rivers from June through September, when water is lower and clearer and temperatures are moderate. They hold in rivers for months before spawning in late winter, which makes them famously good fly targets — they are aggressive, visible, and spread throughout the watershed. The Deschutes River in Oregon is the classic summer-run fishery; the North Umpqua, also in Oregon, is legendary; the Thompson River in British Columbia holds some of the largest summer-run fish in the world, averaging 5–8 kg with monsters exceeding 12 kg. Winter-run fish enter rivers from November through February, often in high, cold, coloured water that makes them difficult to find and harder to move to a fly. The Skagit and Sauk rivers in Washington State, the Hoh and Bogachiel on the Olympic Peninsula, and the North Fork of the Stillaguamish are premier winter destinations.

## Pacific Northwest Rivers

The Pacific Northwest is steelhead country by birthright. In Oregon, the Deschutes between Maupin and the Columbia is arguably the most famous summer steelhead river in the world, with classic rifle-and-pool character, accessible to wading, and a June–October run of fish that respond well to surface flies and wets. The North Umpqua, running through the Umpqua National Forest near Roseburg, has a hallowed fly-only section — the famous Camp Water — that Zane Grey fished in the 1930s. In Washington, the Sauk and Skagit watersheds drain the North Cascades and receive runs of wild winter steelhead that are among the largest in the lower 48 states. In British Columbia, the Skeena River system — including the Bulkley, Babine, and Morice tributaries — is the most productive wild steelhead system remaining, with fish averaging 5–7 kg and exceptional fish exceeding 15 kg.

## Great Lakes Steelhead

The Great Lakes support massive steelhead populations, introduced as stocked fish from Pacific Northwest stock beginning in the 1870s and now reproducing wild in many tributaries. Fish enter streams from Lake Michigan, Lake Erie, Lake Ontario, and Lake Superior from October through April. The Pere Marquette River in Michigan is a legendary Great Lakes steelhead river — a small, intimate stream with wild and hatchery fish, best in March and April when chrome winter fish are actively moving. Sandy Creek in New York, Sandy River tributaries in Ohio, and the Salmon River near Pulaski, New York, are also major destinations. Great Lakes fish often run smaller than Pacific fish — 2–5 kg — but are present in extraordinary numbers.

## Swung Flies on Two-Handed Rods

The traditional method for steelhead is the swung fly — a large wet fly or Spey fly quartered downstream and allowed to swing across the current with the rod held at the downstream angle. Two-handed Spey rods of 13–15 feet make this possible in wide, deep rivers where single-handed casting is impractical. The swing covers water methodically, and a steelhead that takes a swung fly typically pulls the rod down abruptly and then erupts into the air. Classic patterns include the Purple Peril, Freight Train, General Practitioner, and modern intruder-style flies with ostrich and marabou that breathe in slow current. The swung fly is the preferred method among traditionalists, requiring patience to fish — covering each run methodically before moving down a step.

## Nymphing for Winter Steelhead

In high, cold, off-colour winter water, a swung fly often produces few touches. Nymphing — presenting weighted flies and egg patterns under an indicator on a dead drift at depth — is more reliable when fish are holding tight to the bottom. Beadhead nymphs, stonefly imitations, and egg clusters fished on 8- to 10-weight rods with 10–12 foot fluorocarbon leaders down to 10 lb tippets are standard. On the Skagit in February, a guide might have clients nymphing an Egg Sucking Leech or a glo-bug under a Thingamabobber 20 cm off the gravel — unglamorous, but effective.

## Tackle for Steelhead

For swinging, 13–14 foot Spey rods in 7 or 8 weight, matching Skagit or Scandi heads, and two-handed casts (Snap T, Double Spey, Perry Poke) are the tools. For nymphing on a single-hand rod, 9 feet in 8 weight suffices. Reels need strong, smooth drags and capacity for 150 m of backing. Waders must be breathable and warm; neoprene remains preferable for December–February in Pacific Northwest rivers. Studded boots or felt soles on wet, algae-covered Pacific Northwest cobble are essential for safety.

## Seasons and Regulations

Steelhead regulations vary enormously by state, province, and river. Washington State has closed many rivers entirely for wild steelhead retention; Oregon allows limited wild retention on some rivers. In BC, many Skeena tributaries operate under strict wild steelhead no-kill guidelines enforced by fishing lodges and provincial regulation. Great Lakes rivers generally allow retention within catch limits. All serious steelheaders practice selective catch-and-release of wild fish regardless of legal obligation.
