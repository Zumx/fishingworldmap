Every angler has lived both versions of the same day. One afternoon the water boils with feeding fish and everything you throw gets eaten; a week later, same spot, same bait, and the lake might as well be concrete. The difference is rarely luck. Fish are cold-blooded animals suspended in a medium that transmits every change in pressure, light, temperature and current, and they respond to weather and tides with a consistency that borders on the mechanical. Learn to read a forecast and a tide table the way fish experience them, and you can stop fishing on hope and start fishing on probability.

## Barometric pressure: the invisible hand

Atmospheric pressure, normally hovering around 1013 hectopascals at sea level, presses down on the water and, researchers believe, on the gas-filled swim bladders that most fish use for buoyancy. The cliché is broadly true: stable or slowly falling pressure means confident, feeding fish, while a sharp rise after a front often shuts things down for a day or two. The golden window is the fall before a storm. As a low-pressure system approaches and the barometer slides — say from 1018 down toward 1005 — fish frequently feed with abandon, as if stocking up before conditions turn. Shallow-water species like bass, carp and perch show the effect most strongly; deep-water fish, with hundreds of millibars of water pressure already above them, barely notice. The practical rule: a falling barometer is a reason to cancel other plans, and the first bright, high-pressure day after a cold front is the day to mow the lawn instead.

## Fronts: feast before, famine after

A weather front is the pressure story made visible. Ahead of a warm front or an approaching cold front, you typically get falling pressure, thickening cloud, rising wind and that heavy, expectant feel — prime feeding time, often the best fishing of the week. The hours immediately ahead of a summer thunderstorm can be spectacular (get off the water before lightning arrives; carbon rods are excellent conductors). Then the cold front passes, the wind veers, the sky scrubs itself into brilliant post-frontal blue, and the pressure climbs steeply. These bluebird days are notorious. Fish slide deeper, hug cover and develop lockjaw, with predators like pike and largemouth bass particularly affected. If you must fish a post-frontal day, downsize, slow down, fish tight to structure and aim for the warmest part of the afternoon.

## Wind: your friend more often than not

Wind is uncomfortable for anglers and wonderful for fishing. A steady breeze breaks up the surface, which cuts light penetration and makes fish less wary, and it pushes the warm upper layer — along with plankton, insects and baitfish — toward one bank. The old saying about fishing the windward shore is sound: the food chain stacks up where the waves land. On big trout lakes from Ireland's Lough Corrib to Sweden's Vänern, a wave called by drifting boats is the classic search tactic. Direction has folklore value too — wind from the west, fish bite best; wind from the east, fish bite least — and the kernel of truth is that easterlies in the northern hemisphere often accompany high pressure and post-frontal conditions. Flat calm under bright sun is usually the genuinely hard scenario, especially for predators that use waves as ambush cover.

## Light, cloud and temperature

Most freshwater fish lack eyelids and treat bright midday sun the way we treat a torch in the face: they retreat to shade, depth and cover. Overcast days extend feeding windows across the whole day, which is why a grey, drizzly Tuesday so often out-fishes a glorious Saturday. Dawn and dusk concentrate activity in nearly all conditions, as low-angle light gives predators a hunting edge over prey. Temperature matters because it sets metabolism: carp and tench feed hardest in warm water, trout become stressed above roughly 19 to 20 °C (stop fishing for them in heatwaves — release mortality climbs sharply), and a winter sun warming a shallow bay by two degrees can switch on an entire swim. After heavy rain, look for incoming streams and ditches: the dirty, food-laden inflow is a buffet line, though a violent flush of cold runoff can equally kill a bite stone dead.

## Moon phases and the solunar question

In 1926, the American outdoorsman John Alden Knight published the solunar theory: fish and game feed hardest when the moon is overhead or underfoot, with stronger activity around new and full moons. A century later the evidence is mixed — some studies of catch records find modest correlations, others none — but two things are uncontroversial. First, in saltwater the moon acts through the tides, and that mechanism is ironclad: spring tides around full and new moons move more water and more food. Second, big-fish lore clusters around moon events too consistently to dismiss entirely; many tropical guides plan around the days either side of the new moon. A sensible position: treat solunar tables as a tiebreaker between two otherwise equal sessions, never as a reason to stay home when the barometer says go.

## Tides: the saltwater metronome

For coastal fishing, tide beats everything else on this page. Most coasts see two highs and two lows daily, on a cycle of roughly 12 hours 25 minutes, with the range swelling to spring tides just after full and new moons and shrinking to neaps at the quarters. Moving water is the key: current sweeps crabs, shrimp, worms and baitfish out of cover, and predators position themselves down-tide to intercept. Slack water — the dead pause at the top and bottom of the tide — is usually the quietest period. The classic prime windows are the last two hours of the flood and the first hour of the ebb, when water is high, food is dislodged and fish hunt close in. But every mark has its own signature: an estuary sandbar might fish best on the early flood, a deep harbour wall at low-water springs. Local knowledge is really just tide knowledge with mileage.

## Building a go/no-go decision

Pull it together into a checklist you can run in two minutes. Green lights: barometer steady or falling, overcast or broken cloud, a warm breeze onto a fishable shore, mid-tide current movement (salt), dawn or dusk in the window, stable overnight temperatures. Yellow: flat calm and bright, neap tides, a moderate post-frontal rise more than 48 hours old. Red: pressure spiking after a cold front, the first icy north-easterly of autumn, slack water at midday under full sun, flood-stage rivers running brown and violent. None of this is a guarantee — fish read no forecasts — but stack three green lights and your odds multiply. The anglers who seem lucky are mostly just the ones who looked at the pressure graph on Tuesday and booked Thursday off work.
