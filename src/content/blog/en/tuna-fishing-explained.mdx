Off Port Hood, Nova Scotia, in October, a deckhand ladles herring chunks over the gunwale for two hours straight, building an unbroken scent lane in the tide. Nothing happens — until the sonar lights up and a bluefin tuna the size of a motorcycle inhales a hookbait drifted back in the slick, and 130-pound-class gear bends to the cork. Three thousand kilometres south, an angler on a Panama panga throws a popper the size of a beer can at a boiling school of yellowfin riding on the backs of spinner dolphins. Both are tuna fishing, yet they share almost nothing in method. Understanding the four core techniques — chunking, trolling, jigging and popping — and where each dominates is the key to planning any tuna trip on Earth.

## Meet the Family: Bluefin, Yellowfin and Longtail

Atlantic bluefin (Thunnus thynnus) are the giants, warm-blooded missiles that exceed 450 kilograms; the rod-and-reel record, 678.6 kilograms, was caught off Aulds Cove, Nova Scotia, in 1979. They range from the Gulf of Mexico to Norway and spawn in the Mediterranean and the Gulf of Mexico. Yellowfin (Thunnus albacares) are the tropical workhorses — fish of 30 to 100 kilograms with sickle-like yellow finlets — abundant from Panama and Mexico's Baja to the Indian Ocean and the Coral Sea. The longtail tuna (Thunnus tonggol) is the underrated inshore specialist of the Indo-Pacific: Australians chase them as "northern bluefin" in Queensland bays and along the Pilbara, and they take small lures in surprisingly shallow water. Pacific bluefin off California and Japan, bigeye on deep seamounts, and hard-fighting albacore round out the clan.

## Chunking: The Patience Game

Chunking — known as chumming in some ports — builds a drifting lane of cut baitfish (herring, menhaden, sardine, mackerel) that pulls tuna to the boat and switches them on to feed. The hookbait, a whole or cut bait with a circle hook hidden inside, drifts back at the same speed as the free chunks, often set at staggered depths with balloons or weights. It dominates where big bluefin gather over bait grounds: Nova Scotia and Prince Edward Island in autumn, the mid-Atlantic canyons off New Jersey, and Massachusetts' Stellwagen Bank. The technique demands current discipline — a chunk line that sinks unnaturally or a leader that spins will be refused by fish that see thousands of baits. Fluorocarbon leaders of 60 to 130 pounds and well-concealed circle hooks are the norm, and modern fleets watch the slick on sonar rather than waiting blind.

## Trolling: Covering Blue Water

Trolling is the search engine of tuna fishing: spreads of skirted lures, cedar plugs, spreader bars and daisy chains pulled at 5 to 8 knots across temperature breaks, current edges and canyon walls. It rules wherever fish are scattered over big water — the Gulf of Mexico's lump fields, the Canary Islands' spring bluefin run, Cape Town's yellowfin grounds off the continental shelf, and Australia's southern bluefin circuit out of Portland, Victoria. Green-stick fishing, a Japanese refinement that dances squid imitations from a tall fibreglass pole, takes yellowfin in the Gulf with remarkable efficiency. Trolling also finds fish for other methods: many crews troll until they mark life, then stop and switch to jigs or poppers.

## Jigging and Popping: The Modern Sport Revolution

Vertical jigging — dropping knife jigs of 150 to 500 grams to marked fish and ripping them up with long rod sweeps — and popping, the surface assault with oversized poppers and stickbaits, transformed tuna into a sport-casting target over the past two decades. The spiritual home of popping is the Indo-Pacific: yellowfin boiling on baitballs off Panama's Tuna Coast and Costa Rica, dogtooth tuna (a cousin) on reef drop-offs in Fiji and the Maldives, and longtail tuna smashing small stickbaits in Australian bays from Hervey Bay to Exmouth Gulf. Pacific bluefin to 150 kilograms are now taken on poppers off San Diego, and Mediterranean anglers cast to bluefin schools feeding on anchovies. Tackle is specialised: 8-foot popping rods rated PE8 to PE10, large spinning reels holding 400 metres of 80- to 130-pound braid, and terminal hardware tested far beyond the line class, because a tuna will find any weak split ring.

## Reading Water: Temperature, Birds and Bait

Tuna are creatures of edges. Sea-surface temperature charts reveal the breaks — a half-degree line between green shelf water and cobalt blue often holds the fleet — and chlorophyll maps show where bait concentrates. On the water, the signs are older than electronics: shearwaters and terns wheeling and diving, dolphins pushing bait (yellowfin ride with spinner dolphins across the eastern Pacific), whales feeding, and the unmistakable white water of a surface-feeding school. Most tuna bites concentrate around dawn, tide changes and moon phases; full-moon periods often push the bite deeper as fish feed at night on squid.

## The Fight and the Endgame

Hooking a tuna is the easy part. These fish never stop swimming — they cannot, or they suffocate — and even a 50-kilo yellowfin will take 200 metres of line in a first run, then settle into stubborn deep circles under the boat. Fight big fish with harness and gimbal, low rod angles and maximum sustainable drag; prolonged fights kill fish you intend to release and exhaust anglers in tropical heat. At the boat, decisions are final: a fish for the table is taken cleanly with gaff and ike jime for sashimi quality, while released fish — mandatory for many bluefin sizes — should swim off strongly, revived alongside if needed.

## Rules, Quotas and Doing It Right

Bluefin are among the most regulated fish on Earth. ICCAT quotas govern the Atlantic; the United States requires an HMS permit and strict size categories, Canada runs a tightly capped charter fishery, and EU anglers face national catch-and-release or tag systems in countries like Croatia, Spain and Italy. Yellowfin rules vary from bag limits in the Gulf of Mexico to local closures around fish-aggregating devices. Check current regulations before every trip — quota windows open and close mid-season — and choose operators who fight fish hard and handle releases properly. The stock recovery of Atlantic bluefin since the 2010s is proof that rules work.
