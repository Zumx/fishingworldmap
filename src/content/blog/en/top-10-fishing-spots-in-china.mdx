Every morning on Qiandao Lake, crews haul nets that sometimes hold bighead carp heavier than a grown man — the lake's famous fish heads sell for hundreds of yuan in Hangzhou restaurants. Recreational fishing in China is enormous, organised and almost invisible to outsiders: tens of millions of anglers fish urban canals, commercial ponds and vast reservoirs, while a new lure-fishing generation chases snakehead and culter with Japanese tackle. For a travelling angler, China offers water on a scale Europe cannot match, wrapped in regulations worth understanding before you cast.

## Qiandao Lake, Zhejiang: a thousand islands of giant carp

Created in 1959 by the Xin'an River dam, Qiandao (Thousand Island) Lake spreads 573 km² around 1,078 forested islands. It is famous nationally for organic bighead and silver carp management — fish over 50 kg exist — and licensed angling platforms and boat trips target them alongside predatory culter, mandarin fish and catfish. The lakeside town of Chun'an has grown a genuine sport-fishing scene with guided lure trips for topmouth culter at dawn. An hour from Hangzhou by high-speed rail, it is the easiest world-class freshwater venue in eastern China.

## Hainan Island: reefs, wrecks and tropical boat fishing

China's tropical island province is its blue-water playground. Charter boats out of Sanya, Lingshui and Wanning fish reef edges and FADs for grouper, trevally, Spanish mackerel and dorado, while popping trips around the offshore banks raise GTs in the warm months. Shore anglers work jetties at Houhai and the rocks near Wuzhizhou Island for snapper and queenfish. Winter, November to March, brings the calmest seas and the bulk of visiting anglers escaping the mainland cold.

## The Amur and Ussuri frontier, Heilongjiang: taimen and northern pike

Along the Russian border in China's far north-east, the Amur (Heilongjiang) and Ussuri rivers and tributaries like the Huma He hold taimen, lenok, Amur pike and grayling. Autumn is prime, when cooling water pushes big predators onto gravel runs. The river town of Mohe — China's coldest, beyond 53° N — serves as a staging post. This is genuine expedition fishing with local guides, long drives and the reward of fish few westerners have ever targeted in China.

## Kanas Lake and the Altay rivers, Xinjiang

Glacial Kanas Lake, 188 m deep in the Altay Mountains, is wrapped in legend about giant lake monsters — almost certainly outsized Hucho taimen, the species the lake is known for. Fishing in the protected core is restricted, but the Burqin and Irtysh river systems below offer lenok, grayling and pike in spectacular larch-forest scenery. China's only Arctic-draining river, the Irtysh, gives this region a Siberian character found nowhere else in the country.

## Beijing's reservoirs and the heikeng culture

Around the capital, anglers fish managed sections near Miyun and Huairou reservoirs and the Chaobai River for carp, crucians and culter. Just as interesting is urban heikeng (commercial pond) culture: stocked pay lakes where regulars fish competition-style for carp with poles, paste baits and astonishing skill. Venues with names like Huatian fishing ground or the friendly neighbourhood diaoyuchang appear in every district — places such as the Qinghua angling platform typify the genre. An evening session among Beijing regulars teaches more about Chinese angling than any guidebook.

## The Pearl River Delta: urban predators of the south

Guangzhou, Foshan and Shenzhen sit on a maze of tidal channels, sluice gates and embankments where lure anglers catch snakehead, culter and tilapia within sight of skyscrapers. Classic local spots cluster around bridge pilings and sluice mouths — the kind of marked corner swims and floodgate points local apps list by the dozen. Reservoirs in the surrounding hills, like Liuxihe, add largemouth bass and mandarin fish. The subtropical climate means fishing twelve months a year, with summer typhoons the only real interruption.

## Lotus ponds and snakehead: the blackfish obsession

China's signature lure quarry is the northern snakehead — heiyu, the blackfish. From June to September, anglers cast hollow-body frogs across lotus and water-chestnut ponds throughout Jiangsu, Anhui and Hubei, waiting for the violent hole-in-the-weed strike. Heavy rods and 80 lb braid are standard, the fishing is visual and brutal, and a 4 kg snakehead from a farm-pond margin fights like nothing else in freshwater. Many villages rent pond access cheaply; ask at the local tackle shop.

## Erhai and the Yunnan plateau lakes

At 1,972 m beside the old town of Dali, Erhai Lake mirrors the 4,000 m Cangshan range. Fishing was traditionally done with trained cormorants, a spectacle still demonstrated today; modern angling is regulated through closed seasons protecting the lake's recovering native fish. Nearby plateau waters such as Fuxian Lake — 155 m deep and famously clear — and the Lugu Lake area on the Sichuan border offer carp and introduced species against some of China's most beautiful mountain backdrops. Always check current local rules: Yunnan's lakes are conservation priorities.

## The Yangtze ban and where it pushes anglers

Since January 2021, the entire Yangtze main stem and key tributaries sit under a ten-year total fishing ban to rescue collapsed stocks — the reason you will see no rods on the river in Wuhan or Chongqing. The ban has redirected enormous angling energy into reservoirs, tributside pay waters and lakes like Poyang's permitted zones. For visitors the lesson is simple: in China, where you may fish matters as much as how, and enforcement is real, with drones and river police.

## Practicalities: rules, tackle and getting access

China has no national recreational licence; regulation is local, built on closed seasons (typically spring), protected areas and gear limits — one rod, two hooks is a common standard. Commercial ponds and resort waters are the simplest legal access, while guided trips smooth everything on big water like Qiandao or Hainan charters. Tackle shops are excellent and cheap, high-speed rail puts most venues within hours, and translation apps bridge the rest. Fish with a local whenever you can; doors open fast.
