The Northeast is fishing country with deep roots. It was on the limestone spring creeks of Pennsylvania and the freestone rivers of the Catskills that American fly fishing found its philosophical footing in the late 19th century; it was in the harbors of Gloucester and New Bedford that commercial fishing traditions were established that still define the region's culture. Today the Northeast offers a remarkable range for the sport angler: wild brook trout in the Adirondacks, striped bass crashing over rips in Vineyard Sound, giant bluefin tuna circling baitfish off Cape Ann, and landlocked Atlantic salmon in the rivers of Maine. All within a day's drive of some of the most densely populated cities in the Western Hemisphere.

## The Catskills — Birthplace of American Dry-Fly Fishing

The Catskill Mountains of New York State have given the world more than folklore. The Beaverkill River, the Willowemoc, the Esopus, and the Neversink are the streams where Theodore Gordon, Edward Hewitt, and George LaBranche developed the light-hackle dry-fly style that defined American fly tying for a century. Today the Beaverkill flows through Roscoe — self-declared Trout Town USA — where the junction pool at the confluence of the Willowemoc is considered one of the most historically significant trout-fishing sites in the Western Hemisphere. Wild and stocked brown trout are the primary quarry; Catskill-pattern dry flies in sizes 14–18 remain the local currency. The season runs April through November; the Green Drake hatch in late May is the year's most anticipated event.

## The Delaware River System

The Upper Delaware River, forming the New York-Pennsylvania border, is one of the finest wild trout rivers in the eastern United States. Predominantly brown trout, with some rainbow populations in the colder tributaries, the river holds exceptional fish in the 40–60 cm range. Guided wade and drift trips on the West Branch and Main Stem Delaware are popular from April through October. The Upper Delaware Scenic and Recreational River designation protects the character of the water and riparian corridor. Below Port Jervis, the Delaware transitions to a warmer river with bass, walleye, and shad — another distinct fishery worth exploring.

## Cape Cod — Striped Bass and Bluefish

Cape Cod and the island waters of Martha's Vineyard and Nantucket represent the heart of the Northeast's inshore saltwater fishery. Striped bass migrate up the coast from the Chesapeake Bay in spring, reaching Cape Cod by May and remaining through October. The rips off Chatham, the Race at the tip of the Cape, and the Vineyard Sound channel between the Cape and Martha's Vineyard produce exceptional striper fishing. Fish average 4–8 kg in most years, with cows (large females) over 15 kg taken each season. Bluefish run in summer in aggressive surface-feeding frenzies that can be seen from shore. Fly fishing from beaches and wading the tidal flats of Cape Cod National Seashore is increasingly popular.

## Gloucester and the Atlantic Bluefin Tuna

The waters off Cape Ann — Gloucester, Rockport, and the Stellwagen Bank National Marine Sanctuary — host one of the most spectacular inshore bluefin tuna fisheries on the planet. Atlantic bluefin tuna congregate on Stellwagen Bank from July through October to feed on mackerel, herring, and sandlance on the edge of the productive mixing zone. Fish average 100–180 kg, with individuals exceeding 300 kg taken in exceptionally productive years. The sight of a school of giant bluefin churning the surface off the Cape Ann headlands — with Gloucester's iconic harbor in the background — is one of the most dramatic fishing scenes in the Northeast. Charter trips depart from Gloucester and Rockport daily during the season.

## Penobscot River and Maine Landlocked Salmon

The rivers of Maine, particularly the Penobscot system and the Kennebec River, are the last stronghold of wild Atlantic salmon in the United States — though the anadromous population is critically endangered and largely protected. The fishing story in Maine centers on landlocked Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar, the same species but a non-migratory form) in lakes like Sebago Lake, Grand Lake Mahoosuc, and the West Branch Penobscot. Sebago Lake salmon average 1.5–2.5 kg with occasional fish over 4 kg. Spring (April–June) and fall (September–October) produce the best action; smelt fly patterns and streamers are most effective. The Penobscot River below Medway holds both landlocked salmon and wild brook trout in the pockets and pools.

## Block Island and Rhode Island Sound

Block Island, located 19 km off the Rhode Island coast, sits at the intersection of major migratory fish highways. Striped bass, bluefish, false albacore, and bonito make seasonal appearances. The North and South Rips off Block Island — productive rip currents caused by shallow water over rocky shoals — concentrate baitfish and the predators that follow them. False albacore arrive from late August through October and represent some of the finest light-tackle saltwater sport in the Northeast: fast, nearly uncatchable fish that require 12-weight fly rods or light spinning gear with fluorocarbon leaders. The Southwest Ledge is a classic striped bass spot in early season, before the fish move offshore.

## The Housatonic River, Connecticut

The Housatonic River below Falls Village in northwestern Connecticut is one of the finest limestone spring-creek fisheries in the Northeast. Stable year-round temperatures from the spring-fed tributaries support an exceptional wild brown trout population; fish in the 40–55 cm range are common in the productive catch-and-release sections. The Housatonic's limestone chemistry supports dense hatches of Sulphurs, BWOs, and caddis from April through October. This is proper technical dry-fly water — long, clear pools, selective fish, fine tippets. The falls at Housatonic Falls and the stretch between West Cornwall and the Route 7 bridge are classic sections.

## The Salmon River, New York

The Salmon River near Pulaski in Oswego County, New York, receives one of the largest fall steelhead and Chinook salmon runs in the Great Lakes system. Fish enter the river from Lake Ontario from September through November, with chinook (king salmon) averaging 8–12 kg and steelhead following through winter and spring. The Douglaston Salmon Run, a private section managed as a trophy fishery, produces exceptional fish in controlled conditions. The public water below the Trestle Pool holds good numbers of fish when water levels are adequate. It is a crowded fishery by late September, but the quality of the fish — Great Lakes chinook are among the largest inland salmon in North America — justifies the early alarm calls.
