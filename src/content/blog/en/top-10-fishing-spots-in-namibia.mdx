Namibia offers one of fishing's strangest and most thrilling contrasts. On the Atlantic side, the cold Benguela current pushes a conveyor of nutrients against the Skeleton Coast, where anglers standing on empty desert beaches hook bronze whaler sharks heavier than the angler — then release them in the wash. Sixteen hundred kilometres to the northeast, the Zambezi Strip is pure Africa: hippos, fish eagles and tigerfish that hit a spinner like a slammed door. Between the two lie desert dams with surprising stocks. Here are the ten places that define fishing in this empty, spectacular country.

## Henties Bay — capital of the surf

The holiday town of Henties Bay, 70 km north of Swakopmund, exists substantially because of fishing. Its tackle shops, fish-cleaning stations and beach-buggy culture serve the C34 salt road running north past named marks every local knows — Mile 8, Mile 14, the wreck of the Zeila at Die Walle, and dozens of gullies in between. Kob (kabeljou), West Coast steenbras and blacktail come from the same water, and the town's December competitions draw hundreds of rods to the beach.

## The bronze whaler fishery — sharks from the sand

From roughly November to March, big copper sharks — bronzies — patrol the surf line chasing mullet and kob. Guided operators out of Henties Bay and Swakopmund run clients up the coast in 4x4s, set heavy 50-80 lb stand-up outfits with fish baits beyond the breakers, and the fight that follows lasts up to an hour, angler harnessed and walking the beach. Fish between 100 and 180 kg are routine, everything is released, and no boat is ever involved. It is arguably the world's best land-based shark fishery.

## Mile 108 and the Omaruru mouth — the long, lonely north

Past Cape Cross — pause for the 100,000-strong fur seal colony — the salt road runs to the campsites at Mile 72 and Mile 108, bases for the loneliest fishable beaches in southern Africa. The Omaruru river mouth area and marks like Black Rock produce kob in the gullies on a making tide, with steenbras in the shallow inshore tables. Fog, cold wind and absolute emptiness are part of the deal; carry everything, including water.

## Terrace Bay and Torra Bay — inside the Skeleton Coast Park

North again, inside the Skeleton Coast National Park, the seasonal camp at Torra Bay (open December-January) and the small resort at Terrace Bay give legal access to beaches that fish as close to untouched as the Atlantic offers. Galjoen — Namibia's hardest-fighting inshore panfish — kob and steenbras feed along ledges that may not see an angler for weeks. Permits and park rules are strict; book through Namibia Wildlife Resorts and respect the closed areas.

## Walvis Bay lagoon and Donkey Bay — light tackle in the harbour lee

South of Swakopmund, the sheltered water around Walvis Bay offers a different register: spotted gully sharks and smoothhounds on light tackle near Donkey Bay and the famous Pelican Point spit, plus kob around the harbour structures. Kayak and ski-boat anglers work the bay for snoek runs in season, and the lagoon — a Ramsar site stained pink with flamingos — makes the rest day unforgettable. Sandwich Harbour, 40 km south among giant dunes, is a 4x4 trophy trip in itself.

## The Zambezi at Impalila — tigerfish at the four-corners

At Namibia's far northeastern tip, where Namibia, Botswana, Zambia and Zimbabwe almost meet, Impalila Island lodges fish the confluence of the Chobe and Zambezi. This is premier tigerfish water: drifting fillets or casting copper spoons along current seams produces fish of 2-4 kg constantly, with genuine 8 kg+ trophies every season. August to December, as the floodplains drain, is prime. Elephants swimming the channel between casts are not a rumour.

## Katima Mulilo and the upper Zambezi floodplain

Upstream, the Zambezi around Katima Mulilo braids through papyrus and sandbanks. Tigerfish dominate, but the floodplain lagoons hold nembwe (a thick-set bream that smashes spinnerbaits), thinface and threespot tilapia — superb light-tackle sport. Lodges along the river arrange barbel-run trips in late winter, when migrating catfish herd baitfish and the tigers go berserk behind them.

## The Kwando and the quiet Caprivi backwaters

The Kwando river, along the Bwabwata National Park, is the Zambezi Strip's intimate option: narrow, papyrus-lined channels where you cast small spoons for tigerfish and African pike, with sitatunga and elephant on the banks. Nearby, the Okavango's Namibian stretch at Divundu, above Popa Falls, gives tigerfish and nembwe fishing inside a riverine forest that feels closer to the Delta than to the desert coast.

## Hardap Dam — desert carp and barbel

Namibia's interior surprises: Hardap Dam near Mariental, a 25 km² reservoir on the Fish River, holds carp, sharptooth catfish (barbel), smallmouth yellowfish and mudfish. Local anglers fish from the resort bank or boats beneath red desert hills, and the dam's barbel grow well past 10 kg. Von Bach Dam near Okahandja adds bass and carp within an hour of Windhoek — the capital's after-work water.

## The galjoen season — winter's specialist prize

When summer's bronzies depart, the Namibian shore angler turns to galjoen, the southern African endemic that fights like a fish twice its size in the foamy white water it loves. June to September, baits of red bait and white mussel cast into churning gullies south of Swakopmund and around Wlotzkasbaken produce fish toward the magic 3 kg mark. Strict bag and size limits apply — the species is protected in neighbouring South Africa, and Namibian rules are tightening too.

## Practical notes — permits, vehicles and seasons

Shore angling requires an inexpensive monthly permit from the Ministry of Fisheries (sold in Swakopmund, Henties Bay and Walvis Bay shops); the Skeleton Coast park needs separate entry permits, and freshwater fishing in the Zambezi region runs through lodges. A 4x4 is essential off the salt road — beach driving has its own etiquette and legal limits. Summer (November-March) is shark and kob time on the coast and builds to tigerfish prime in the northeast; winter brings galjoen and steenbras. Distances are enormous and fuel stops scarce: Windhoek to Katima Mulilo is nearly 1,300 km.
