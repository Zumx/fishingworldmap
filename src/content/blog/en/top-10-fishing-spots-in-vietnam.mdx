Vietnam stretches more than 1,600 km from the limestone bays of the north to the mangrove estuaries of the Mekong Delta, and almost every kilometre of it touches water. Fishing here is woven into daily life — every city has its hồ câu paylakes, every coastal town its squid fleet — yet visiting anglers remain rare enough to draw a curious crowd. Between brackish delta channels alive with barramundi, coral reefs off Phu Quoc and highland reservoirs where giant snakehead hunt, Vietnam offers far more rod-bending variety than its modest angling reputation suggests.

## The Mekong Delta estuaries

The delta's nine river mouths spread across provinces like Ben Tre, Tra Vinh and Soc Trang, forming a vast brackish nursery where barramundi, mangrove jack and estuary grouper ambush bait along the mangrove edges. Local boatmen fish handlines with live shrimp, but lure casting around fallen timber and confluences works beautifully on the moving tide. Can Tho makes a comfortable base, with floating markets to explore between sessions. The dry season from December to April brings clearer water and the most predictable fishing; book a local boat for the dawn tide and watch the delta wake around you.

## Can Gio and the Saigon River mouth

An hour from Ho Chi Minh City, the Can Gio mangrove biosphere reserve guards the mouth of the Saigon River. Around the fishing port of Cần Thạnh, the channels hold barramundi, threadfin and big estuarine catfish, and night sessions produce broadnose rays and the occasional small shark. It is the easiest genuine saltwater fishing to reach from the city — local guides run simple wooden boats on the tides — and the seafood shacks along the harbour will cook your legally kept table fish while you watch the boats come in.

## Phu Quoc and the An Thoi islands

Vietnam's largest island sits in the Gulf of Thailand, ringed by reefs that stay fishable nearly year-round. Day boats out of An Thoi harbour drift the southern archipelago for grouper, snapper, queenfish and cobia, while evening trips chase the famous squid under lights. Serious anglers can arrange popping sessions around the more distant pinnacles, where giant trevally still patrol. The dry season from November to March offers calm seas and 30-metre visibility; combine morning fishing with afternoon snorkelling and a sundowner on Long Beach for the full island routine.

## Nha Trang and the south-central coast

Nha Trang's bay shelters one of Vietnam's busiest charter scenes. Boats work the islands around Hon Tre and the marine reserve fringes for grouper, trevally and Spanish mackerel, with deeper water beyond producing dogtooth tuna for jigging crews. The fishing harbour at Hon Ro is the place to negotiate a traditional boat for a budget day on the reef. February to August brings the calmest seas. Further south, Cam Ranh Bay's sand flats hide flathead and queenfish for light-tackle enthusiasts who prefer casting to bottom fishing.

## Da Nang and the Cham Islands

Central Vietnam's coast funnels bait along the Son Tra peninsula, where shore anglers cast from the rocks below the Linh Ung pagoda for trevally and barracuda. Offshore, the Cham Islands — a UNESCO biosphere reserve reached from Hoi An — offer reef fishing over coral gardens for snapper and grouper, with strict no-take zones to respect around the conservation core. The window matters here: the northeast monsoon makes seas rough from October to January, so plan for March through August, and pair fishing days with Hoi An's old town in the evenings.

## Halong Bay and the northern gulf

Fishing among Halong Bay's 1,600 limestone karsts is as scenic as angling gets. Local boats jig and bottom-fish the channels between the towers for grouper, snapper and croaker, and squid fishing under lights is a beloved night activity on overnight cruises — many junks hand guests a rod as standard. The bay's sheltered waters fish year-round, though winter mist from December to February turns the karsts ghostly and cools the bite. For quieter water, base yourself in neighbouring Lan Ha Bay off Cat Ba Island, where the same fishing comes with fewer boats.

## Tri An Reservoir and the snakehead lakes

Two hours northeast of Ho Chi Minh City, Tri An Reservoir is the south's premier lure-fishing destination. Giant snakehead patrol the flooded timber and explode on frogs and poppers, with hampala barb and jungle perch adding variety. Local lure anglers run long-tail boats out of the Ma Da area at first light; the dry months from December to April concentrate fish as water levels fall. Further north, Dau Tieng Reservoir offers similar fishing with even less pressure. Hooking a 5-kg snakehead in standing timber on 30-lb braid is not a fight you forget.

## Lak Lake and the central highlands

On the Dak Lak plateau near Buon Ma Thuot, Lak Lake spreads shallow and weedy beneath M'nong longhouse villages. Snakehead, walking catfish and climbing perch thrive in its margins, and fishing from a dugout canoe at dawn — mist on the water, gongs sounding from the shore — is travel theatre of the highest order. The highlands also hide crater lakes and hydropower reservoirs around Pleiku and Kon Tum that see almost no sport fishing at all. Bring a travel rod, hire a motorbike and explore; locals will point you to water no website lists.

## Paylakes, rules and practical matters

Vietnam has no recreational fishing licence; aside from marine protected zones and military areas, you can generally fish where locals do. The paylake culture is its own world worth sampling: Hanoi Fishing Park in the capital and dozens of hồ câu around Ho Chi Minh City — Thế Giới Câu Cá among the best known — stock big catfish and carp, rent gear cheaply and serve cold beer at your peg. Respect the monsoons when planning: the south fishes best December to April, the centre February to August, the north March to November. A folding travel rod opens up every roadside pond on the journey.
