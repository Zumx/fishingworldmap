Stand on the estuary mouth of the Casamance River at dawn and the water is amber with suspended clay, the surface broken only by the bow-wave of a rolling tarpon. West Africa's fishing scene rarely gets the international attention it deserves, yet Senegal packs an astonishing diversity into its coastline — 531 kilometres of Atlantic shore, a web of mangrove creeks, two navigable rivers, and the vast central plateau drained by the Gambia River tributaries. Permit stalk the sandy flats near the Sine-Saloum Delta with the same quiet authority they show in the Florida Keys. Cubera snapper of 20 kg lurk under oyster bars that local oysterwomen tend at low tide. The country's artisanal fishing culture, rooted in Lebou and Diola communities, means traditional pirogues still set out before first light every morning — a reminder that these waters have always been generous.

## The Casamance River and Its Tidal Flats

The Casamance is the spine of Senegal's freshwater and estuarine fishery in the south. The river runs 320 km through dense forest before widening into a mangrove-fringed estuary near Ziguinchor. On the incoming tide, tarpon — locally called "poisson-lune" — push deep into the mangroves, and fly anglers working 10-weight rods with large baitfish streamers intercept them as they move out. Cubera snapper stack under the root systems in numbers that would astonish anyone used to Caribbean fishing. The Casamance's black-water tributaries also hold the African barramundi relative, the capitaine or Nile perch (Lates niloticus), which averages 3 to 8 kg here but can reach 25 kg in the deeper holes below Kolda.

## Sine-Saloum Delta: Permit and Ladyfish Country

The Sine-Saloum Delta, a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve two hours south of Dakar, is a labyrinth of tidal channels, sandbanks, and open bolongs stretching 180,000 hectares. Permit — the same Atlantic permit (Trachinotus falcatus) prized on Belizean atolls — cruise the sandy flats between the islands of Ngor and Joal-Fadiouth in surprisingly large numbers. They are spooky and demanding, refusing anything that lands too heavily; an 8-weight rod with a size 4 crab imitation is the standard setup. Ladyfish, small jack species, and the occasional bonefish round out the flats fishing. The area is best fished from a shallow-draught pirogue between November and April, when northeast trade winds flatten the sea surface.

## Dakar Peninsula and Offshore Banks

Dakar sits on Cap-Vert, the westernmost point of Africa, and its position funnels deep Atlantic currents close to shore. The underwater canyons beginning just 12 nautical miles offshore drop to over 1,000 m, producing reliable pelagic action. Yellowfin tuna of 40 to 80 kg are common from January through April; wahoo peak between October and December. Local charter boats depart from the Port de Pêche Artisanale, near the central Quai de débarquement de pêche on the northern waterfront, before sunrise. The seamounts at "La Roche de Coco" and the 24-metre reef system known locally as "Le Plateau" hold dogtooth tuna and amberjack year-round.

## Joal-Fadiouth and the Petite Côte

Joal-Fadiouth, about 110 km south of Dakar along the Petite Côte, is both a pilgrimage site and one of the best shore-fishing positions in the country. The Quai de pêche here receives hundreds of pirogues daily, and local guides know the submerged rock formations offshore where barracuda, GT, and various grouper species congregate. Bottom fishing around these limestone pinnacles at 15 to 25 m depth produces large red scorpionfish (Scorpaena scrofa) and dentex, the sea bream family's heavyweight. Night fishing with live bait around the lit jetty yields excellent cubera snapper.

## The Gambia River Mouth and Barra Point

The Gambia River, though mostly within The Gambia, spreads a vast plume of freshwater across the southern Senegalese shelf before narrowing around Barra Point. This mixing zone — where river silt meets Atlantic salt — is a magnet for snook-like Nile perch, oversized African threadfin (Polydactylus quadrifilis), and schools of barracuda hunting mullet. The threadfin, locally called Capitaine blanc, is perhaps Senegal's most prized table fish; hook-and-line fishing from dugout pirogues is the traditional method, though sport anglers have begun popper fishing with success.

## Djirnda Island and the Oyster Fishery

Djirnda, a small island in the Sine-Saloum, hosts the Site de production Djirnda and the Embarcadère de djirnda — community landing points built around the local oyster-culture tradition. The submerged mangrove root matrices that hold the oysters also hold enormous concentrations of snapper and jack. Fishing a live prawn under a float along the oyster lines at the Quai de pêche de Djirnda regularly produces cubera snapper above 12 kg. Between March and June, schools of crevalle jack push into the creek system chasing anchovy, creating surface feeding frenzies visible from shore.

## Saint-Louis and the Senegal River Mouth

Saint-Louis, the old colonial capital in the north, straddles the final kilometres of the Senegal River before it meets the Atlantic through a constantly shifting sandbar mouth called the Langue de Barbarie. This barrier beach creates a brackish lagoon system rich in mullet, sole, and thin-lipped grey mullet up to 3 kg — superb fly-fishing targets. Beyond the bar, Atlantic waters hold African pompano (Alectis alexandrinus), a hard-fighting trevally relative, and occasional cobia. The Débarcadère on the river's southern bank serves as the main departure point for boat fishing into the lagoon.

## Ziguinchor Creek System

Ziguinchor, the main city of the Casamance region, is encircled by tidal creeks no wider than a city street in places. Kayak fishing through these mangrove tunnels — known locally as marigots — at high tide is surreal: branches brush the rod tip, ibis flush overhead, and a cast between the mangrove legs can yield a snapper of 6 kg that has never seen a lure before. Tilapia also congregate in the fresher upper reaches from July to October during the rainy season high-water period.

## Offshore Bluewater: The Cape Verde Rise

From October to February, Senegal's offshore waters — particularly around the 200-metre contour 30 to 40 nautical miles west of Dakar — produce some of the Atlantic's best blue marlin fishing outside of Madeira. Grander fish exceeding 450 kg have been recorded. The upwelling driven by the cold Canary Current pushing south along the African coast concentrates baitfish, which in turn draws sailfish, striped marlin, and dorado in extraordinary numbers. A 400 km² zone around 14°N latitude is recognized by local skippers as the "marlin highway" between November and January.

## Station d'Aquaponique and Freshwater Alternatives

For anglers who prefer inland waters, the Station d'Aquaponique near Ziguinchor and similar aquaculture research points along the Casamance have introduced tilapia and catfish management models that have enriched natural populations in the river system. The upper Casamance above Kolda holds indigenous African catfish (Clarias gariepinus) reaching 15 kg, along with several tigerfish species that require medium-heavy spinning gear to handle. The dry season from December to April concentrates fish in the deeper permanent pools of the Casamance's tributaries, making this the best time for freshwater prospecting in the interior.
