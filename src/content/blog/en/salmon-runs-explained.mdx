Every June, somewhere around forty to sixty million sockeye salmon turn toward Bristol Bay, Alaska, and begin the last act of a journey that started in the same gravel four or five years earlier. A fish that smelled its natal stream as a five-gram smolt navigates thousands of kilometres of open Pacific and returns, in many documented cases, to within metres of where it hatched. For anglers, this homing miracle has a practical consequence: salmon fishing is not a place, it is a date. The river that boils with chrome fish in the second week of July can be empty water in August. Timing the run is the entire game, from the Aberdeenshire Dee to the rivers of Kamchatka.

## The Biology of the Run

Salmon are anadromous: born in freshwater, fattened at sea, and compelled home to spawn by a combination of olfactory imprinting, geomagnetic navigation and day-length cues. Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) can survive spawning and return as kelts to spawn again; the six Pacific species — chinook (king), sockeye (red), coho (silver), pink (humpy), chum (dog) and the Asian masu — die after spawning, one immense pulse of marine nutrients delivered to forest watersheds. Entry timing is tuned to each river's hydrology: long, cold systems like the Yukon receive kings in June so they can travel up to 3,000 kilometres before autumn, while short coastal streams take coho almost ripe in October. Within one river there may be distinct early and late races, which is why the angler's question is never just where, but which run, which week.

## Atlantic Salmon: Scotland, Norway and Iceland

The Atlantic fish is the aristocrat of fly water. Scotland's big four — Tweed, Tay, Spey and Dee — offer spring fishing for rare, prized "springers" from February, summer grilse runs in June and July, and a famous autumn run on the Tweed into November. Norway is the heavyweight arena: the Alta produces fly-caught salmon over 20 kilograms under the midnight sun, and the Gaula and Orkla near Trondheim peak from mid-June to mid-July. Iceland runs on a different rhythm — compact, crystal-clear rivers like the Laxá í Aðaldal and the Rangá fish best from late June to September, managed with strict rod limits and logbooks. Russia's Kola Peninsula, with the Ponoi and Varzuga, delivers the highest catch rates in the Atlantic world during the June smelt-fed frenzy.

## Pacific Abundance: Alaska and British Columbia

Alaska compresses five species into a hundred-day season. Kings enter the Kenai and Nushagak from late May through July; the Kenai gave up the 44.1-kilogram world record in 1985. Sockeye flood Bristol Bay's Kvichak, Naknek and Egegik systems in late June and early July in runs counted by the tens of millions. Coho arrive August into September, the best fly-rod salmon of the lot, and pinks stack every other (even-numbered) year in southeast Alaska. British Columbia counters with the Skeena system — the Babine and Kispiox steelhead and the Skeena's own giant chinook — and the Fraser's enormous, carefully managed sockeye cycles, which peak dramatically every fourth year, as in the famous Adams River dominant years.

## The Wild East: Kamchatka and Beyond

The Kamchatka Peninsula holds arguably the most intact salmon ecosystems left on Earth: rivers like the Zhupanova, Ozernaya and Bolshaya receive all six Pacific species plus the resident rainbow trout that gorge on their eggs and flesh. The fishing is helicopter-access, float-trip style, with king runs in June and July and silvers from August. Japan's Hokkaido has chum and pink runs with a unique sea-run char fishery alongside, and the masu salmon — the cherry salmon — runs rivers of northern Honshu and Hokkaido in early summer, a cult target for Japanese fly anglers.

## Reading a Run: Water, Tides and Temperature

Within the season window, day-to-day success rides on conditions. Fresh fish move on rising tides and enter rivers on rain spates; a falling, clearing river after a flood is the classic taking water for Atlantic salmon. Temperature gates behaviour — Atlantics take a swung fly best between roughly 8 and 14 degrees Celsius, and in cold spring water the fly must swim slow and deep, while warm low-water summers call for small hitched flies and dawn sessions. Pacific fish bite best when chrome-bright and recently in from salt; the farther upriver and the closer to spawning, the less they chase. Counting facilities help modern anglers enormously: fish ladders and sonar counters on rivers like the Tyne or Columbia publish daily numbers worth checking before you book anything.

## Methods Across the World

Tradition and regulation shape technique. On classic Atlantic beats, the double-handed spey rod of 13 to 15 feet swinging size 8 to 2/0 flies remains the standard, with sink-tips in spring and riffle-hitched micro-tubes in summer. Scandinavian rivers blend spey lines with modern shooting heads; many Norwegian beats also allow spinning with spoons and Rapala-style lures. In Alaska, single-handed 8- to 10-weights with egg patterns, flesh flies and big streamers do the work, while back-trolled plugs and drift-fished roe dominate guided king fishing. Always check the rulebook: many Scottish rivers are fly-only on certain beats and seasons, Iceland is essentially all fly, and barbless single hooks plus mandatory release of large hens are increasingly the norm everywhere.

## Conservation: The Other Half of the Story

Atlantic salmon returns have fallen steeply since the 1970s — barriers, warming seas, aquaculture impacts — and most fisheries now run on catch-and-release, with Scotland reporting release rates above 95 percent. Pacific runs are healthier but volatile: Bristol Bay set all-time sockeye records in the early 2020s while Yukon and Fraser chinook struggled badly. The practical angler's code is simple: keep fish wet, use single barbless hooks where required, respect closures, and put money into fisheries — Iceland's and the Kola's pricing models are, in effect, conservation funding. The run you time correctly this decade depends on the habitat defended in the last one.
