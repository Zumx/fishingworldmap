Taiwan sits at the junction of the Kuroshio Current and the continental shelf, a collision of warm tropical water and cold upwellings that concentrates baitfish and, by extension, almost every pelagic species imaginable. Offshore, the water drops from 30 m to over 3,000 m within a few nautical miles of the east coast, giving anglers access to true deep-water billfish without spending a day at sea. Add gin-clear mountain streams in the north, volcanic lake basins in the centre, and a southern reef system teeming with amberjack and barracuda, and you have an island that rewards every style of angling.

## Hualien — Blue Marlin Capital of Asia

Hualien on the east coast is the epicentre of Taiwan's offshore scene. The Kuroshio flows past almost within sight of the harbour, and the submarine canyon just 5 km out provides the thermal gradient that Pacific blue marlin prefer. Taiwan's IGFA-certified records include fish exceeding 500 kg taken from these waters, and the summer window from May through September produces consistent action. Charter boats operate from Hualien Fishing Port, running 30–50 nautical miles into the blue before trolling large skirted lures and rigged ballyhoo. Giant trevally, mahi-mahi, and wahoo fill out the offshore card when marlin are absent.

## Yilan (Ilan) County — Masu Salmon in the Mountains

Few anglers outside Asia know that Taiwan harbours a native population of masu salmon (Oncorhynchus masou formosanus), a subspecies found only in the high-gradient streams of northern Taiwan. The Lanyang River system in Yilan County above 1,500 m elevation holds these fish, though access is tightly restricted under conservation law — a government permit is required and only a limited quota of catch-and-release fishing days is granted annually. The season runs from late March to mid-June, water temperatures stay below 15 °C, and successful anglers report fish of 30–45 cm responding well to tiny dry flies and nymphs. Surrounding the masu zone, rainbow trout stocked in lower reaches offer more accessible sport year-round.

## Taitung — Dorado and GT on the Wild Coast

South of Hualien, the Taitung coastline delivers inshore fishing that rivals anything in the western Pacific. Giant trevally patrol the rocky headlands and reefs, ambushing surface prey with spectacular strikes on poppers and stickbaits worked over shallow ledges. During the dorado migration — typically April through July — the thermocline sits just a few miles off Taitung's shore, and mahi-mahi schools can be found beneath floating debris lines. Local skippers know to look for frigate birds working baitballs at first light; under those birds you will often find wahoo and yellowfin tuna as well.

## Sun Moon Lake — Freshwater Largemouth and Peacock Bass

Nantou County's Sun Moon Lake at 748 m elevation is Taiwan's largest natural body of water and the spiritual heart of the island's freshwater scene. Peacock bass introduced in the 1990s have thrived in the warm, clear water and now reach 3 kg regularly. Largemouth bass and snakehead also inhabit the weed edges and submerged timber around the western shore. Fishing is permitted from the bank at designated points, and small kayaks can be rented to reach the quieter inlets where bass stack up under lily pads. Dawn and dusk produce the best action; midday summer temperatures push surface temperatures above 28 °C and fish become lethargic.

## Penghu Archipelago — Reef Fishing and Grouper

The Penghu Islands, scattered 50 km off Taiwan's west coast in the Taiwan Strait, hold one of the island's most productive reef fisheries. Local anglers target grouper, parrotfish, and Napoleon wrasse over the basalt reef flats using whole squid and live bait on heavy bottom rigs. The current between the islands is formidable — tides run at 3–4 knots through the channels — so lead weights of 200–400 g are standard. Visiting anglers should hire experienced local boatmen who know the safe passages. October through January is prime grouper season, with fish regularly exceeding 5 kg.

## Keelung — Squid Jigging and Night Fishing

Keelung Harbour north of Taipei has evolved into one of Asia's great squid jigging destinations. Between September and December, Japanese flying squid migrate through the Taiwan Strait and accumulate in the lit waters off Keelung's commercial piers. Night fishing is culturally embedded here — families and groups of friends arrive after dark with multi-hook jig rigs, and the atmosphere on a busy night resembles a street festival more than a fishing expedition. Squid of 15–30 cm are the norm, but occasionally larger arrow squid mix in. The catch is typically eaten that same night, grilled on the pier-side stalls.

## Tamsui Estuary — Urban Snook and Bass Fishing

The Tamsui River mouth in New Taipei City offers surprisingly productive fishing within 30 minutes of central Taipei. Japanese snook (barramundi-related suzuki) move into the estuary on tidal flows from October to March, averaging 40–60 cm with occasional fish pushing 80 cm. Soft-plastic lures on light jig heads worked along the rocky bank under the old Tamsui Fort produce reliable sport. The estuary also holds mullet and tilapia that can be taken on small hook-and-bobber rigs during daylight hours. The waterfront is fully lit at night, making this perhaps the most atmospheric urban fishing location in northern Taiwan.

## Xitou and Alishan — Alpine Trout Fishing

The mountain forests of central Taiwan host several high-altitude streams where rainbow trout and brook trout have been stocked into cold, fast-flowing water. The catchment above Xitou Nature Education Area in Nantou County offers designated trout fishing zones where a daily rod permit is purchased at the ranger station. Streams here run between 1,500 and 2,000 m elevation, water temperatures remain below 12 °C even in July, and the bamboo forest canopy creates a perpetually damp, misty atmosphere. Small spinners and natural bait — earthworms, insect larvae — are the most productive presentations. The Alishan area offers similar experiences with the added drama of cedar forest giants reaching 50 m in height.
