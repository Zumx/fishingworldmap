Austria runs its fishing the way it runs its railways: precisely, locally, and to a standard that rewards anyone willing to learn the system. Every river is divided into beats (Reviere) managed by clubs, hotels or private owners, every angler needs both a state licence and a beat permit, and the payoff is water of astonishing quality — gin-clear alpine rivers where grayling sip duns over pale gravel, deep Salzkammergut lakes hiding lake trout and char, and lowland rivers where the huchen, Europe's giant salmonid, still hunts. These ten destinations show the country at its best.

## The Gmunden Traun — the historic grayling water

Flowing out of Traunsee at Gmunden, the Traun was the most famous grayling river in the world a century ago, and its revival is one of European fly fishing's good-news stories. Brown trout, rainbows and grayling to 50 cm feed in long glides over light limestone gravel, and hatches — March browns in spring, sulphurs in June — can carpet the water. Day cards are limited, so book ahead through the local beat offices and fishing hotels in Gmunden.

## The Salza — Styria's turquoise canyon river

The Styrian Salza, rolling beneath the Hochschwab massif past Wildalpen, may be the most beautiful trout river in the Alps: water the colour of glacier glass, limestone walls, and wild rainbows, browns and grayling that demand long leaders. The beat structure is run largely through the Wildalpen area, with kayak traffic in high summer — fish early and late. Wading studs are essential on the polished bedrock.

## Hallstätter See and the Salzkammergut lakes — lake trout and char

The deep, cold lakes of the Salzkammergut — Hallstätter See, Traunsee, Attersee, Grundlsee — hold Seeforelle (lake trout) that grow beyond 10 kg, plus Arctic char and whitefish (Reinanke). Locals troll spoons deep along the thermocline from May onward, or jig for char over 40-80 m of water. Fishing beneath Hallstatt's UNESCO village, with the Dachstein glacier overhead, is as scenic as European angling gets. Nearby, the Fischereiverein Gosau manages the Gosau valley waters, including the postcard-famous Gosausee.

## The Pielach and Lower Austria's huchen rivers

The huchen, or Danube salmon, is Europe's largest resident salmonid, and Lower Austria's Pielach, Erlauf and Ybbs — plus the Danube itself around Melk — remain strongholds. The season is short and cold (typically February-March and autumn, varying by beat), the method is big streamers or swimbaits, and a metre-long fish is a realistic life goal rather than a fantasy. Strict quotas and mandatory release on many beats protect a fragile population.

## The Tiroler Lech — the last wild alpine braided river

The Lech in Tyrol's Außerfern is one of the Alps' last braided, free-moving rivers, its gravel banks rearranged by every flood. Browns and grayling live lean and strong in the shifting channels between Steeg and Reutte. The Weidachsee at Leutasch, a spring-fed stocked lake nearby at over 1,100 m, offers a contrast: rainbow trout and char in transparent water with the Wetterstein range behind — ideal for a family day between river sessions.

## The Mur through Graz — urban huchen and street-corner grayling

Graz is one of the few cities in the world where you can hook a huchen downtown. The Mur runs fast and green through the city, holding grayling, browns and the big predator itself below the weirs. Ten minutes west, the small Thalersee offers relaxed pike, carp and trout fishing around a café-ringed lake. Styria's licensing is straightforward via the provincial fishing card plus the beat owner's permit.

## The Gail and Drau in Carinthia — southern light, big grayling

Carinthia's Gail valley, pressed against the Italian border, fishes like a southern river: warmer light, prolific caddis, and grayling that average noticeably larger than further north. The Drau's tailwater stretches near Lienz and through the Rosental hold strong rainbows and huchen. Many beats here are managed by fishing-friendly Gasthöfe that bundle rooms, cards and advice — the classic Austrian package.

## Stausee Kops and the high-mountain reservoirs

At 1,809 m in the Montafon, Stausee Kops is one of several alpine reservoirs — along with Silvretta-Stausee on the Bielerhöhe — where brook trout, browns and char feed through a short, intense season (roughly June to September). The setting is pure high-mountain theatre: marmots whistling, glaciers across the valley. Cards are sold locally and the fish, fattened on alpine invertebrates, fight far above their weight.

## The Steyr and Enns valleys — Upper Austria's workhorses

The Steyr river, milky-green from its karst springs, and the broader Enns offer reliable trout and grayling fishing within an hour of Linz. The Steyr's beats around Grünburg and Steyr town mix pocket water with deep pools holding surprise lake-run browns. This is unpretentious, excellent water — the kind local anglers fish every week of the season from 1 May to 30 September.

## The stocked lakes and family waters

Austria's valleys are dotted with managed fishing ponds where a day card buys guaranteed action: Hubertusteich in the Waldviertel's pond country, the trout ponds of the Lammertal near Salzburg, and dozens of village Fischteiche from Vorarlberg to Burgenland. They matter because Austrian river fishing demands planning — and these waters let you fish spontaneously, teach children, or salvage a day when the rivers run high with snowmelt.

## Practical notes — licences, beats and seasons

Austria has nine provincial systems: you generally need an official fishing licence from the Bundesland (often available as a guest licence for tourists) plus the day or week card for a specific beat. Brown trout season typically runs spring to September or early October, grayling often to December at lower elevations, and huchen rules vary tightly by water. Barbless hooks and catch limits are common; carry your documents — checks happen. Booking beats through fishing hotels in Gmunden, Wildalpen or Lienz solves the paperwork in one email.
