No country on earth holds more fresh water than Brazil, and few use it so generously on anglers. The Amazon basin alone drains an area the size of Australia, its black-water tributaries hiding peacock bass that hit surface lures harder than any freshwater fish alive. South of the forest, the Pantanal's rivers run thick with golden dorado, and along 7,000 km of Atlantic coastline blue marlin, dorado and roosterfish-fast surf species patrol from the equator to the pampas. Picking ten spots from this embarrassment of riches is brutal — but these waters belong on any serious angler's list.

## The Rio Negro and the Barcelos lagoons

The middle Rio Negro around Barcelos, some 400 km upriver from Manaus, is the spiritual home of trophy peacock bass. In the low-water months from September to February, guides pole skiffs into a labyrinth of black-water lagoons — local names like Lago do Veado and Lago da Galinha pass between boatmen like currency — where giant tucunare acu over 10 kg ambush prop baits and big jerkbaits. Liveaboard yachts and jungle lodges run weekly packages, and the fly fishing, with 9-weights and massive streamers, is world class. The explosion of a big peacock on topwater is freshwater fishing's most violent moment.

## The Pantanal: golden dorado country

The world's largest tropical wetland, spread across Mato Grosso and Mato Grosso do Sul, is Brazil's golden dorado heartland. The Rio Paraguai and tributaries like the Miranda and Aquidauana hold dorado to double figures alongside pacu, pintado catfish and ferocious piraputanga. Lodges around Corumbá and Miranda fish the season from roughly March to October, between the floods and the piracema spawning closure. Drifting a live bait or swinging a big streamer through a current seam while hyacinth macaws cross overhead is the Pantanal's signature scene — half safari, half fishing trip.

## The Rio Araguaia

Forming the border between Goias, Mato Grosso and Tocantins, the Araguaia is Brazil's great beach river. In the dry season from June to September the water drops, exposing vast white sandbars where camps spring up, and the fishing turns on: peacock bass in the lagoons, payara and bicuda in the runs, and immense redtail catfish — pirarara — in the deep holes. The stretch around Aruana and the 350-km-long Bananal Island, the largest river island on earth, is the classic water. This is also Brazil's most family-friendly wilderness fishery, with comfortable boat camps and easy access from Goiania.

## Teles Pires and Juruena: the trophy jungle rivers

Where Mato Grosso's plateau breaks into the Amazon basin, the clear-running Teles Pires and Juruena rivers offer the Amazon's most varied trophy fishing. Payara — the sabre-toothed vampire fish — hold below the rapids, bicuda smash lures alongside several peacock species, and the catfish run to genuinely frightening sizes: piraiba over 100 kg are hooked every season. Remote lodges, reached by air taxi, fish big water with modern bass boats. September and October, at the bottom of the dry season, concentrate fish in the channels and produce the heaviest multi-species weeks.

## The Tapajos and Alter do Chao

Near Santarem, where the clear, green Tapajos meets the muddy Amazon, the village of Alter do Chao fronts white-sand beaches that National Geographic once ranked among the world's most beautiful. The river's clarity makes it the Amazon's best sight-fishing water: tucunare, jacunda and aggressive piranha species over sand flats, fished with light spinning gear or a 7-weight fly rod. Day trips run straight from the village, no expedition logistics required, which makes Alter do Chao the easiest genuine Amazon fishing to bolt onto a wider Brazil itinerary.

## Vitoria: the blue marlin capital

The deep ledges off Vitoria, capital of Espirito Santo, produce more grander-class blue marlin per boat than almost anywhere on the planet. The season runs from roughly November to April, when warm currents push bait over the seamounts, and fish between 300 and 800 lb are regular, with genuine 1,000-pounders hooked every year. A serious sportfishing fleet operates from Vitoria's marinas, and white marlin, sailfish, yellowfin and dorado fill the days between blue-marlin bites. For a big-game angler, this stretch of coast is Brazil's crown jewel.

## Cabo Frio, Buzios and Ilhabela

The southeast coast bends through a series of legendary saltwater marks. The cold upwelling off Cabo Frio and Arraial do Cabo concentrates bait year-round, feeding anchova (bluefish), big snook in the surf and dorado offshore. Buzios offers light-tackle reef and rock fishing between its 20-plus beaches, while the island of Ilhabela, off the Sao Paulo coast, is a sailfish and wahoo station with a strong charter scene and the Sao Sebastiao channel's strange, fishy currents. All three combine first-rate fishing with the kind of food and scenery that keeps non-fishing companions happy.

## Licences, seasons and planning the trip

Brazil requires an inexpensive recreational fishing licence, easily arranged online or by lodges as part of the package. The piracema closed season — roughly November to the end of February in the Pantanal and many southern basins — protects spawning runs, so plan Pantanal trips for March to October. Amazon peacock-bass season follows low water: September to March on the Rio Negro system, June to September on the Araguaia. Saltwater fishing is open year-round. Book established operations, take the recommended vaccinations for jungle trips, and remember the distances — internal flights are part of nearly every serious itinerary.
