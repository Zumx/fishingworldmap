Alaska is the pinnacle of North American fishing. Its rivers run with all five Pacific salmon species simultaneously in peak season; its bays shelter halibut that push 180 kg; its remote lakes hold rainbow trout and arctic char that have never seen a hook. The sheer scale of the fishing opportunity here — roughly 54,000 km of coastline, three million lakes, and 12,000 rivers — means that even after a lifetime of visiting, anglers return for new discoveries. From the Kenai Peninsula's roadside salmon combat fishing to the fly-out wilderness lodges of Bristol Bay, Alaska offers fishing experiences at every level of adventure and investment.

## The Kenai River: King Salmon Capital of the World

The Kenai River drains Kenai Lake in the Chugach Mountains and flows 145 km to Cook Inlet, passing through the towns of Cooper Landing, Sterling, and Kenai along the way. The river is famous globally for its chinook (king) salmon, which enter from Cook Inlet in two distinct runs — a first run peaking in mid-May through June, and a larger second run peaking in late July. The world record king salmon of 44 kg was caught in the Kenai in 1985. Today the river is closely managed, with sections closed to protect spawning fish, and daily bag limits for the second run king salmon. Sockeye (red) salmon provide action through July and August in enormous numbers, sometimes exceeding 400,000 fish per day entering the river. Drift-boat fishing with guides is the standard approach.

## Nushagak River: King Salmon on a Wilderness River

The Nushagak drains from the Wood-Tikchik State Park wilderness east of Dillingham in southwest Alaska and enters Nushagak Bay at Dillingham. It is one of the most productive king salmon rivers in the world and sees far less pressure than the Kenai despite its extraordinary fishing. King salmon arrive from late May through July, with fish regularly exceeding 20 kg. The river is accessible only by small aircraft from Dillingham, which keeps numbers manageable and the experience wilderness-quality. Float trips of 7–10 days allow anglers to cover the best runs. Sockeye, chum, and pink salmon also use the Nushagak system in huge numbers.

## Bristol Bay: Sockeye Salmon and Rainbow Trout

Bristol Bay, fed by the Nushagak, Wood, Kvichak, Egegik, Ugashik, and Naknek rivers, supports the world's largest sockeye salmon run. In peak years over 50 million sockeye return to Bristol Bay, providing fishing of an intensity that is simply inconceivable in most of the world. The Naknek River and its connection to Naknek Lake and Brooks Camp is perhaps the most famous individual fishery in the complex, where massive rainbow trout — fish to 70 cm — feed year-round on salmon eggs and smolt. The Brooks Falls bear-watching platform at Katmai National Park sits at the top of the falls where sockeye salmon leap, making Brooks Camp one of the most iconic wildlife destinations on earth.

## Homer: Halibut Capital of the World

Homer, at the tip of the Kenai Peninsula on Kachemak Bay, markets itself as the Halibut Capital of the World, and the claim is well-founded. Pacific halibut grow to extraordinary sizes in the cold, rich waters of lower Cook Inlet and the outer Gulf of Alaska. Fish of 50–100 kg are caught regularly from charter boats working the deep holes and rock piles off Homer Spit, and the record for the area exceeds 185 kg. The charter boat fleet at Homer Spit is well-organised, with trips ranging from half-day to multi-day offshore adventures targeting halibut, lingcod, and Pacific cod. The season runs from spring thaw to October.

## Kodiak Island: Brown Bear Country and Salmon Rivers

Kodiak Island, 400 km south-west of Anchorage in the Gulf of Alaska, is famous for its enormous Kodiak brown bears, but the island's rivers offer fishing to match. The Karluk River on the island's western coast is a legendary salmon stream with chinook, sockeye, coho, pink, and chum salmon all using the system. Pink salmon arrive in July in overwhelming numbers — the Karluk has produced single-day catches of over 200 fish by skilled anglers. Steelhead trout run the Karluk in late September through November. Access is by floatplane or boat from Kodiak City.

## Prince William Sound: Silver Salmon and Rockfish

Prince William Sound, east of Anchorage, is a complex of fjords, islands, and passages surrounded by the Chugach and Kenai mountains. Coho (silver) salmon fishing in the Sound during August and September is exceptional — the fish are bright, powerful, and willing to take flies and spinners in the clear tidal channels. Rockfish, lingcod, and halibut are available throughout the Sound, and the scenery — glaciers calving into salt water, humpback whales in the passages — makes it among the most spectacular fishing environments anywhere.

## The Copper River: Prized Salmon and Steelhead

The Copper River drains the largest watershed in Alaska — 63,000 km² — from the Wrangell Mountains to the Gulf of Alaska at Cordova. Copper River chinook and sockeye salmon are among the most highly prized food fish in the world, due to their exceptional fat content from the long river migration. The delta near Cordova is the primary access point for fishing, and the Copper River Highway provides road access to several fishing spots above the delta. The river also supports steelhead trout runs in spring and autumn.

## The Kenai Peninsula: Rainbow Trout and Dolly Varden

The upper Kenai River system, above Kenai Lake, contains the Russian River, Quartz Creek, and numerous small tributary streams that hold resident rainbow trout and Dolly Varden char year-round. The Russian River produces excellent fly-fishing from June through September, with fish to 55 cm common. These streams are accessible by road from Anchorage along the Sterling Highway, making them among the most accessible quality trout fishing in Alaska. Evening hatches of caddis and stoneflies in July and August produce memorable dry-fly action.

## Sitka and Southeast Alaska: Saltwater Variety

Southeast Alaska, the panhandle stretching south from the main body of the state, offers a different style of fishing from the mainland — sea-run cutthroat trout in the estuary zones, feeder king salmon in the nearshore kelp beds, and coho fishing of exceptional quality. Sitka is a base for saltwater fishing in the outer waters of Sitka Sound, where chinook salmon of 15–25 kg are caught year-round by trolling. King salmon fishing here is productive even in winter when the fish hold near the coast.

## The Denali Area: Arctic Grayling and Lake Trout

The rivers and lakes draining the Alaska Range near Denali National Park — including the Nenana River and the lakes of the Parks Highway corridor — hold arctic grayling and lake trout in their remote headwaters. Arctic grayling of 45 cm rise freely to dry flies in the clear, cold rivers above the tree line, offering the kind of solitary, scenically overwhelming fishing that defines the Alaska experience for fly-fishers. Lake trout to 10 kg lurk in the deeper lakes, accessible by float-plane or snowmobile in winter.
