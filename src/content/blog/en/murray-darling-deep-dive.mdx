The Murray-Darling system is Australia's equivalent of the Mississippi — a vast drainage basin covering one million square kilometres across New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, and South Australia. The Murray River, at 2,508 kilometres, is Australia's longest river; the Darling at 1,472 kilometres is its longest tributary. Together they drain a catchment of semi-arid plains, woodland, and the western slopes of the Great Dividing Range, flowing ultimately into the Southern Ocean at Encounter Bay near Goolwa. For freshwater anglers, this system is not merely a fishery — it is a continent's worth of waterways, home to ancient fish in astonishing sizes.

## Murray Cod: The King of Australian Freshwater

No fish in Australian freshwater carries the mystique of the Murray cod. These prehistoric predators — closely related to no other fish on earth, diverged from their nearest relatives over 65 million years ago — inhabit the snags, undercut banks, and deep bends of the Murray-Darling system from its Queensland headwaters to the lower lake near Goolwa. Murray cod grow slowly and live long: a fish of sixty centimetres is at least five years old; a fish over a metre may have been in that snag for forty years. Fish over a metre in length are still present in the river, particularly in sections with good snag habitat such as the Murrumbidgee River above Carrathool. Fishing with large paddle-tail lures worked along submerged timber at dawn produces the most consistent results. A slot limit between fifty-five and seventy-five centimetres applies on most waters; fish outside this range must be released.

## Golden Perch: The Acrobat of the Murray

Golden perch — locally called yellowbelly in New South Wales and Queensland, and goldie in South Australia — are the Murray-Darling system's most popular sportfish for good reason. They respond well to a wide variety of lures, strike hard, and fight above their weight with a habit of running under the boat at the most inconvenient moment. Golden perch favour warmer water than cod and move upstream to spawn following the first big floods of spring; the Murrumbidgee, Lachlan, and Darling River systems all host significant self-sustaining populations. Fig Tree Hole, a celebrated section of the Darling near Bourke, is known among locals for producing golden perch on surface lures in the evening. Government Wharf areas near Marlow Lagoon are popular access points for casual anglers targeting yellowbelly along the lower Murray.

## The Murrumbidgee River: A Study in Contrasts

The Murrumbidgee — "big water" in the Wiradjuri language — rises in the Snowy Mountains at around 1,500 metres elevation and drops through spectacular gorge country before spreading across the Riverina plain and meeting the Murray near Balranald. The upper Murrumbidgee above Yass supports a thriving trout fishery stocked by New South Wales Fisheries; the middle reaches hold redfin perch and catfish in the irrigation impoundments; and the lower river from Gundagai downstream is prime Murray cod and golden perch country. The Murrumbidgee River Bridge at Carrathool is a known access point for the lower reaches, where deep holes below the bridge structure consistently hold cod.

## The Darling River: The Unpredictable Giant

The Darling runs north to south through western New South Wales, arriving at the Murray near Wentworth after a journey of over 1,400 kilometres. It is simultaneously the most exciting and most unreliable river in the system — capable of being reduced to a string of waterholes in drought and a raging torrent after tropical rain events in Queensland. When it flows well, the Darling produces extraordinary golden perch and Murray cod fishing; fish that have been concentrated in the surviving waterholes during drought emerge fat and aggressive when floodwaters reconnect the system. Jewel spots like Menindee Lakes are the Darling's famous rest stops — large natural lakes that fill from the river and become outstanding fisheries for bream, golden perch, and redfin during and after floods.

## Lake Wallace and Accessible Fishing Infrastructure

Not all Murray-Darling fishing requires a boat or remote access. New South Wales and Victoria have invested in accessible fishing infrastructure at multiple locations. Lake Wallace near Wallerawang — created by a nineteenth-century dam — hosts a purpose-built accessible fishing pontoon designed for anglers with limited mobility, stocked with rainbow trout and redfin. The Disabled Fishing Platform at Emerald Lake near Melbourne provides a similar service for urban anglers. These facilities, maintained by the respective state fisheries agencies, are worth noting for anyone planning a trip who cannot wade or manage a boat ramp independently. The Windermere Pontoon and Jetty on the upper Murray also provides jetty access to otherwise bank-less sections of the river.

## Redfin Perch: The Introduced Sportfish

European perch — called redfin by Australians — were introduced to Victoria in 1861 and have since spread through virtually every waterway in the Murray-Darling system. They are classified as a noxious fish in New South Wales and Queensland; their possession and release is illegal in many states, and anglers are encouraged to dispatch all redfin they catch. Yet there is no denying their sporting appeal: redfin fight well for their size, and a two-kilogram fish on light gear provides a satisfying tussle. Lake Nobelius near Emerald is one of Victoria's best redfin fisheries, consistently producing fish above the one-kilogram mark that respond enthusiastically to small hardbody lures and soft plastics fished in cold water from May through August.

## The Lower Lakes and Coorong: Where River Meets Sea

The Murray mouth near Goolwa and the connected lake systems of Lake Alexandrina and Lake Albert form the transition zone between the freshwater system and the Southern Ocean. Historically this area was one of Australia's richest fisheries, with yellow-eyed mullet, bream, and black bream moving between the salt and fresh depending on the season. The drought years of the early 2000s reduced freshwater flow to the point where the lakes turned saline, affecting the fishery severely. Since increased environmental flows began in 2010, the lower lakes have recovered significantly. The Gantry at Milang, a converted wharf structure, provides vehicle-accessible fishing from a jetty over deep water that holds bream and carp year-round. Pettmans Beach and the nearby Coastal 4 Track areas are noted access points for beach and shore fishing around the lake perimeter.

## Snags, Structure, and Murray Cod Tactics

Murray cod are fundamentally structure fish. They spend their lives associated with a particular snag — a submerged tree, a rock shelf, an undercut bank — rarely moving more than a few kilometres in their lifetime. Understanding this is the foundation of all Murray cod fishing. The most successful approach is to systematically cast to every likely piece of submerged timber with a large lure — a 100 mm to 200 mm paddle-tail rigged on a heavy jig head, or a large deep-diving hard body — worked slowly along the bottom. The Austin Gilchrist Park section of the Murray near Tocumwal has accessible bank fishing for exactly this style. Dawn and dusk are the most productive periods; deep mid-summer nights with a surface lure can also produce explosive strikes from large fish that would not consider moving during daylight. Patience and repetition across known structure is the consistent cod angler's creed.
