The Red Sea is one of the world's youngest oceans — a geological infant that opened along the East African Rift roughly 25 million years ago and is still widening today. Its enclosed geography, intense solar heating, and high salinity produce an extraordinary concentration of marine life, and its coral reef systems rank among the most diverse and intact in the world. For sport anglers, the Red Sea is most celebrated for the predatory species that patrol its isolated coral formations: giant trevally that ambush from the reef edge, dogtooth tuna that hunt open water, and the enormous Napoleon wrasse that shelter in deep coral caves.

## Giant Trevally: The King of the Flat

Giant trevally — Caranx ignobilis, known universally by the initials GT — is the fish that brings dedicated lure anglers to the Red Sea above all others. Fish in the 20 to 40-kilogram range are encountered regularly around the isolated coral bommies that rise from the Red Sea floor, and specimens above 50 kilograms are taken each season. The fishing method is popping: heavy rod-and-reel outfits rated for lures up to 250 grams are used to cast large surface plugs to the edges of coral structures. The retrieve is aggressive — fast, irregular, and designed to create maximum surface disturbance. GTs attack with extraordinary violence, often in front of witnesses on a liveaboard, and the fight is a pure power contest with a fish that actively seeks the reef.

## Dogtooth Tuna: Vertical Game

Where GTs dominate the surface, dogtooth tuna — Gymnosarda unicolor — are the lords of the mid-water column around Red Sea pinnacles. These large scombrid fish, not true tunas despite the name, hold at 30 to 80 metres over steep reef drop-offs and attack jigs with ferocious speed. Slow-pitch jigging with asymmetric metal jigs in the 120 to 300-gram range, fished with light pressure and an erratic flutter, is the refined modern technique. The dogtooth's teeth are as formidable as its name suggests, and solid wire or heavy crimped cable leaders are mandatory. Fish above 30 kilograms are taken regularly in the southern Red Sea, particularly around the Brothers Islands — two small remote outcrops 70 kilometres off the Egyptian coast — and at Daedalus Reef.

## The Brothers Islands and Remote Reef Fishing

The Brothers Islands — Al Akhawein in Arabic — are perhaps the most famous sport fishing and diving destination in the Egyptian Red Sea. The two islands, Big Brother and Little Brother, rise vertically from water nearly 800 metres deep. The current sweeps past the walls and pinnacles with force, concentrating baitfish and the large predators that feed on them. Liveaboard trips from Hurghada or Marsa Alam typically cover the Brothers along with Daedalus Reef and Elphinstone Reef over five to seven nights. The fishing calendar runs from October through May — the Red Sea summer between June and September brings extreme heat and often unfishable conditions in the far south.

## Napoleon Wrasse: A Species Under Pressure

The Napoleon wrasse — Cheilinus undulatus — is the largest living member of the wrasse family, with adults reaching 2 metres in length and over 190 kilograms. Its distinctive humped forehead and vibrant blue-green coloration make it unmistakable. Napoleon wrasse are not traditional sport fishing targets — their population has been devastated by the live fish trade and spearfishing across their range — but they are frequently encountered by liveaboard anglers in the Red Sea, particularly around deep coral structures in the Sudanese and Eritrean zones. The species is listed as Endangered by the IUCN, and all encounter fishing is now strictly catch-and-release at responsible operators.

## Egyptian Red Sea: Hurghada and Marsa Alam

Egypt is the dominant gateway for Red Sea liveaboard fishing, with Hurghada in the north and Marsa Alam further south providing the primary departure ports. Day trips from Hurghada reach productive reefs at Giftun Island and the offshore structures at Abu Ramada, where snapper, grouper, and smaller trevally are accessible without liveaboard commitment. Marsa Alam is closer to the premier GT and dogtooth fishing grounds and is the preferred base for serious big-game anglers. Tackle shops in both cities carry adequate supplies of surface poppers and jigs, though serious anglers travelling from Europe or Australia bring their own equipment to ensure quality.

## Saudi Arabia and the Northern Red Sea

The northern Red Sea along Saudi Arabia's Hejaz coastline contains some of the most pristine reef systems in the region, with coral structures largely undisturbed by heavy fishing pressure. The Saudi Vision 2030 tourism initiative has opened parts of this coast to international sport fishing, particularly around Neom, the Tabuk coast, and the Farasan Islands near Jizan in the south. The Farasan Islands are an archipelago of over 100 low coral islands with exceptional GT and barracuda fishing in their channels and on their reef edges. Access remains logistically complex but is improving with each successive season.

## Tackle, Technique, and Safety

Red Sea popping demands heavy-duty gear. Rods rated for lures to 200 grams matched with 8000 to 14000-class spinning reels spooled with 80 to 100-pound braid are the minimum for serious GT work. Treble hooks on poppers should be replaced with strong single hooks to reduce reef snagging and to allow cleaner releases. The Red Sea can produce strong currents and occasional rough conditions — liveaboard captains monitor wind forecasts carefully in the straits off Yemen, where the Bab-el-Mandeb narrows channel dangerous sea states.
