Belgium packs an astonishing amount of water into 30,689 square kilometres: 1,500 km of navigable canals, the Meuse cutting limestone gorges through the Ardennes, trout streams falling out of the High Fens, and a polder network in Flanders where pike hunt under windmills. Most travelling anglers drive straight through on their way to France or the Netherlands, which is precisely why Belgian zander, barbel and carp see so little tourist pressure. Two regional licences — Flanders and Wallonia — unlock all of it for a few dozen euros.

## The Albert Canal: Flanders' zander highway

Running 129.5 km from Antwerp to Liège, the Albert Canal is Belgium's most underrated predator water. Its sheet-piled banks, harbour basins and warm industrial outflows hold serious zander, with vertical jigging from boats around Genk, Hasselt and the Lanaken stretch producing fish over 80 cm, plus bonus asp along the rip-rap. Night fishing zones and a dense network of access points make it a favourite of street-fishing locals. Big container ships create current seams — fish them like a river.

## The Meuse at Dinant and Namur: barbel and big-river class

The Walloon Meuse is Belgium's grandest river, sliding beneath the citadel of Namur and the cliff-hugging church of Dinant. Weir pools and gravel runs hold barbel and chub that respond beautifully to feeder tactics, while the deep navigation reaches grow pike past the metre mark and zander in numbers. The tributary mouths — Lesse, Sambre, Ourthe — are classic ambush points. Public towpaths give kilometres of free bank, and a Wallonia permit covers nearly everything.

## Ardennes streams: the Ourthe, Amblève and Semois

Southern Belgium hides genuine trout country. The two arms of the Ourthe around La Roche-en-Ardenne, the Amblève below the Coo waterfalls and the Lesse near Han-sur-Lesse fish like miniature alpine rivers: wild brown trout, grayling in the deeper glides, and dry-fly evenings in May and June. The Semois, looping through tobacco-growing valleys past Bouillon's medieval castle, adds ferocious chub and some of Wallonia's best wild pike fishing in its slower meanders. Many beats are run by local societies selling day cards in village cafés — part of the charm.

## Lacs de l'Eau d'Heure: Belgium's inland sea

This five-lake complex on the Hainaut-Namur border is Belgium's largest body of water at around 600 hectares, and a managed trophy fishery. The Plate Taille, the biggest basin, is known for pike approaching 120 cm and powerful carp, with boat and float-tube fishing organised under a specific permit system. Lower lakes hold zander, perch and roach in quantity. With diving, sailing and rental cottages on-site, it doubles neatly as a family base.

## The High Fens lakes: Bütgenbach and Robertville

In the German-speaking east, the cool reservoirs of Bütgenbach and Robertville sit at around 550 m altitude amid the High Fens moorland. Both hold pike, perch and stocked trout in clear, deep water, and their forested banks feel more Scandinavian than Belgian. Nearby, the upper Warche and Our rivers offer intimate trout fishing, and the spa town of Spa lends its day-ticket waters: the Pêcherie de Polleur and the Carpodrome de Nivezé are friendly stops for trout and carp respectively.

## Flemish polder canals and the Damme Vaart

North of Bruges, the tree-lined Damme Canal and the wider polder network toward the Dutch border are postcard pike water: shallow, weedy, and stuffed with bait fish. Spinnerbaits and jerkbaits along the reed lines from September to December produce fish to a metre, and rudd fishing in summer is superb. The canals around Veurne and the Yser (IJzer) river add zander and big bream. Almost everything is covered by the inexpensive Flemish licence bought online or at post offices.

## Carpodromes and club lakes: easy sport everywhere

Belgium's club scene is dense and welcoming. Wallonia's commercial carp and trout fisheries — the Étangs Yernault in Hainaut, the Pêcherie du Moulin and the Pêcherie des Trois Ponts among them — offer day-ticket sport in well-kept surroundings. In Flanders, waters such as Visput Pareinpark in Beveren, the historic De Zoet Water lakes at Oud-Heverlee near Leuven, and the Domein Ter Heide fishing zone show how seriously municipalities take public angling. For pure numbers fishing, the carpodrome format — match-style carp to 5 kg on pole tackle — is a Belgian specialty.

## The Scheldt and Ghent's urban predators

The tidal Scheldt below Ghent and the city's ring of canals have become a street-fishing arena: perch and zander hold around moored barges, marina pontoons and lock gates, and a lunch-break session with dropshot tackle is entirely realistic. The river's tidal reaches toward Antwerp, refreshed by improving water quality, now produce surprising zander and even sea trout sightings. Kayak anglers do particularly well working structure the bank crowd cannot reach.

## The coast: Nieuwpoort, Ostend and Zeebrugge

Belgium's 67 km North Sea coast is short but busy. Beach casting at Nieuwpoort and De Panne produces bass, flounder and whiting depending on season; the harbour walls of Ostend and the giant breakwaters at Zeebrugge are reliable platforms for bass on lures from May to October. Charter boats out of Nieuwpoort and Blankenberge fish wrecks for cod and turbot. No licence is needed for sea fishing from public structures — check local harbour rules and you are away.

## Practicalities: two regions, two permits

Flanders sells its visverlof online for around 13 euros a year (night and extended rights slightly more); Wallonia's permis de pêche works similarly through the Maison wallonne de la pêche. Closed seasons differ — pike protection in spring is taken seriously in both regions — and trout streams in Wallonia generally open the third Saturday of March. Distances are tiny: from Brussels you can reach polder pike, Meuse barbel or Ardennes trout inside ninety minutes.
