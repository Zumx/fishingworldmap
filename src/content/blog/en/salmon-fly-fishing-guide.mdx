There is a particular quality of light on a November morning on the River Tweed — a pewter sky pressing low over frost-bleached banks, the river running cold and dark at around 6°C — that focuses the mind wonderfully. You are there for Atlantic salmon, Salmo salar, the king of freshwater fish, and every cast you make must count. The water is low and clear, the fish have been in since September, and they are lying in very specific lies that took generations of ghillies and visiting rods to locate. Understanding how to find those lies, present a fly convincingly, and cast efficiently with a double-handed rod is the entire art of salmon fly fishing.

## The Double-Handed Rod and Why It Matters

Modern salmon fly fishing is built around the double-handed Spey rod, typically between 13 and 15 feet in length and rated for lines in the AFTM 7 to 10 range. The length is not arbitrary. A long rod gives you extraordinary control over a long line, allows you to mend efficiently, and makes it possible to fish big pools from the bank without wading deep. On wide rivers like the Spey in Scotland, the Gaula in Norway, or the Laxa in Iceland, a 15-foot rod throwing a Scandi shooting head or a Skagit system can cover water that would be physically impossible with a single-handed trout rod. Shorter rods in the 12 to 13-foot range suit tighter, tree-lined beats on rivers like the Exe in Devon or the Erriff in western Ireland.

## Spey Casting: The Core Technique

Spey casting was developed on the River Spey in the Scottish Highlands specifically to fish confined banks where a traditional overhead cast is impossible. The Single Spey, Double Spey, and Snake Roll are the three casts every salmon angler needs. Each creates a D-loop of line behind the caster — essentially replacing the backcast — before driving the rod forward to shoot the line across the river. The key discipline is forming a tight, high anchor point close to the bank and a big, energised D-loop before the forward stroke. Common faults include a flat D-loop, an anchor that splashes down too far away from the rod tip, and a forward cast that begins before the D-loop has fully formed. On rivers running left to right, use an Upstream Single Spey; on right-to-left currents, the Double Spey is safer for right-handed casters.

## Reading a Salmon Pool

Salmon do not feed in fresh water. They hold in specific lies that offer a combination of dissolved oxygen, shade or cover, and an energy-efficient resting position relative to the current. Classic lies fall into three types. Running lies are found in the wide, even glides below rapids where fish pause to rest during upstream migration — typically where depth is between 1.5 and 3.5 metres and current speed is moderate. Holding lies are deeper, quieter pools, often below boulders or on the inside of bends, where fish settle for days or weeks. Lies at the crease — the exact boundary between fast main current and slower water on the pool edges — are often the most productive because the fish can sit in slow water with oxygenated current close by. On a beat of the River Dee in Aberdeenshire, a ghillie will point to a specific submerged ledge as the holding lie, a place that produces fish year after year because the hydraulics are simply right.

## Classic Wet Fly Patterns and When to Use Them

The traditional salmon wet fly arsenal is vast, stretching back to Victorian-era patterns tied with exotic feathers from jungle fowl, golden pheasant, and blue chatterer. Modern practice has simplified things considerably, but the classics still work. The Cascade, with its orange and red body and heavy flowing wing, is a workhorse on medium to high water. The Ally's Shrimp in its various colour combinations covers fish at low water in summer. The Stoat's Tail is one of the most consistently effective all-round flies, particularly in sizes 8 to 14 on low, clear water. For cold late-season fishing on the Tweed or the Tay, larger tube flies and Waddington shanks carrying flies like the Collie Dog or Temple Dog, fished on sinking or intermediate shooting heads, can provoke takes from fish that have been ignoring conventional patterns for weeks.

## Line Systems and the Modern Approach

Salmon fly fishing has been transformed by modern running line, shooting head, and sink tip technology. The Scandinavian or Scandi system uses a short, compact shooting head of 30 to 40 feet with a long tapered leader, ideal for presenting small flies gently on low clear water. The Skagit system, developed in the Pacific Northwest for steelhead, uses a heavier, shorter head designed to carry and turn over heavy sink tips and large tube flies — exactly what you need for cold, high, coloured water in autumn. Many experienced rods carry two complete line setups on the river and switch according to conditions. Water temperature is the most important variable: below 6°C, fish a sinking tip and larger fly deep and slow; above 10°C, switch to a floating line and a smaller pattern fished at or just under the surface.

## Beat Etiquette and Shared Rivers

Most Atlantic salmon rivers in the British Isles, Scandinavia, and Iceland are managed as beats — discrete sections of river that are either privately owned or rented to paying guests. On a shared beat with multiple rods, etiquette is everything. The convention is to start at the top of the pool and work down, covering the water with each cast and then taking two or three steps downstream before recasting, allowing the next rod to drop into the pool above you. Resting a pool after a fish is hooked and played is standard courtesy. On popular rivers like the Tweed during prime autumn weeks, beats change hands at ludicrously high prices and the social rules are enforced with quiet but firm disapproval by the ghillie.

## Handling Salmon and Conservation

Atlantic salmon are under severe pressure across their range. Populations on many rivers in the UK, France, and Scandinavia are a fraction of historical levels, and the science is unambiguous that catch-and-release significantly improves survival rates and contributes to long-term river health. On many Scottish rivers and all rivers in Iceland, catch-and-release is now either mandatory or strongly observed by social convention. When releasing a salmon, keep the fish in the water, remove the hook quickly with forceps, and support the fish facing upstream in moderate current until it kicks away strongly on its own.
