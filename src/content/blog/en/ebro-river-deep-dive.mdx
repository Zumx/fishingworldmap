The lower Ebro in Catalonia is unlike any other European river. Since the wels catfish (*Silurus glanis*) was first introduced in 1974 — released illegally into the Mequinenza reservoir by a German angler who brought a handful of specimens from Bavaria — the population has grown into something extraordinary. Four decades of mild winters, abundant bream and carp as forage, and almost no natural predation pressure combined to produce the densest population of large wels catfish in Europe. Today the river from the Mequinenza and Riba-roja reservoirs down through the natural stretch between Miravet and Amposta draws hundreds of specialist anglers each year from Britain, Germany, the Netherlands, and beyond.

## The Mequinenza Reservoir: Ground Zero

The Mequinenza reservoir — known locally as the Mar de Aragón — is where the Ebro catfish story truly began. Stretching 93 kilometres and holding a surface area of 7,540 hectares, the reservoir has consistent depths of 20–40 metres in its main body and carved-out channels reaching 60 metres in the old river bed sections. These deep, warm stretches provide ideal over-wintering habitat, and the catfish emerge from their cold-water torpor in late March to begin a feeding frenzy that lasts through October. Fish catalogued by the Ebro Fishing Association have confirmed specimens up to 264 cm in length, though weights above 130 kg remain extremely rare — most fish in the 200 cm range weigh between 60 and 80 kg due to the species' elongated body form.

## The Segre and Cinca Confluence

Below the Mequinenza dam, the Ebro is joined by the Segre and Cinca rivers within a short distance, creating a complex of currents and deep scours where catfish gather in large numbers, especially during the spring spawning period. The area around Mequinenza town, including the boat ramp at the old castle embankment, gives access to some of the best day-fishing within range of organised guiding operations. Most established guiding companies work out of Mequinenza, Caspe, and Flix, operating aluminium drift boats of 4–5 metres length that can safely navigate both reservoir and natural river sections.

## Tactics That Work on the Ebro

The dominant technique on the Ebro has evolved significantly from the early years of simple dead-bait bottom fishing. Buoy fishing — suspending a live or fresh dead bait under a large spherical marker float at 4–8 metres depth — accounts for the majority of fish caught by guided parties today. The large float keeps the bait off the riverbed where passive catfish rest during daylight, and the gentle current imparts movement that triggers investigative responses. A 3–4 kg bream or carp flank, fresh from the same river, is the preferred bait. Circle hooks in the 10/0–12/0 range prevent deep-hooking and allow almost all fish to be released without injury.

Drift fishing is the other primary method: the boat moves slowly downstream with a large shad, eel, or lamprey section dragged just above the bottom in known catfish lies. This technique requires precise knowledge of the riverbed topography — guides who fish the same 20 km beat daily develop mental maps of every boulder, gravel run, and deep pool where fish hold in different conditions.

## Riba-roja Reservoir and the Natural River

The Riba-roja reservoir, immediately downstream of Mequinenza, has shallower margins and more diverse habitats including extensive island complexes and flooded terrace structures. Its catfish population is younger and slightly less numerous than Mequinenza's, but it offers exceptional sport for anglers who prefer river-like conditions to deep-reservoir fishing. The natural river section below the Riba-roja dam, running through the spectacular limestone gorge country of the Terra Alta, has a lower density of very large fish but produces active feeding catfish almost year-round in the deep bends near Móra d'Ebre and Benissanet.

## Tackle, Regulations, and Seasons

Spanish fishing regulations on the Ebro require a Catalan government fishing licence, available online through the Agència Catalana de l'Aigua. There is no minimum size for catfish on most Ebro beats, but all responsible guides operate strict catch-and-release policies. The legal season runs from approximately March to October depending on the specific stretch; the Mequinenza reservoir typically opens in late March after the catfish spawning moratorium. Rods and lines need to handle sustained pressure from large fish in current: minimum 50-lb braid main line, 80-lb fluorocarbon shock leader, and rods rated for 150–300 g lure weights are typical among specialist catfish anglers.

## Accommodation and Access in the Lower Ebro

Mequinenza is a small town of around 2,400 people perched above the old confluence point, and it has developed a modest but functional infrastructure around the catfish fishing industry. Several guesthouses and small hotels cater specifically to fishing parties; some operators offer full packages including accommodation, boat hire, guiding, and bait. Flix, Ascó, Móra d'Ebre, and Tortosa further downstream all provide accommodation bases for fishing the natural river sections. The motorway network connecting Barcelona to Zaragoza passes close to Mequinenza, making it accessible within two hours of the airport.

## Beyond Catfish: Other Species on the Ebro

While catfish dominate the Ebro's reputation, the river supports a surprisingly diverse fish community. European eel (*Anguilla anguilla*), now critically endangered, survives in modest numbers in the natural river section. Carp exceeding 30 kg have been taken from deep sections of the Mequinenza reservoir. Mullet species ascend the tidal lower river as far as Tortosa, and black bass were introduced to several Ebro tributary reservoirs in the mid-twentieth century, providing excellent sport on light lure tackle.
