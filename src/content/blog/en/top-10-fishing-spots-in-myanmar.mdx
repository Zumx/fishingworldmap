Myanmar remains one of Southeast Asia's least-pressured fishing destinations, a consequence of decades of political isolation that limited commercial development along most of its major river systems. The Irrawaddy, Myanmar's great artery, drains from the high Kachin mountains south through the dry central zone and spreads into a vast delta where the river meets the Andaman Sea near Yangon. Snakehead, catfish, and featherback carp inhabit this lowland system in numbers that have declined sharply in accessible areas near towns but remain impressive in remote reaches. Further north, in the mountains bordering Tibet and Yunnan, streams draining from the Hkakabo Razi massif — the highest peak in Southeast Asia at 5,881 metres — run cold and clear, with reports of wild trout-like species in the highest altitude streams that have barely been investigated by sport fishers.

## Irrawaddy River Giant Catfish

The Irrawaddy giant catfish (Pangasianodon hypophthalmus) and several related giant catfish species inhabit the main Irrawaddy channel in the Sagaing region, where the river is broad and slow above the Chindwin confluence. Trophy specimens of 50 kilograms and above have been documented in the stretch between Mandalay and Shwebo. Local fishermen use large drift nets and handlines — visiting sport anglers can arrange sessions through fishing guides operating out of Mandalay. Water clarity in the lower Irrawaddy is limited, so bait fishing with large pieces of freshwater shrimp or fishmeal paste is the standard approach.

## Chindwin River

The Chindwin joins the Irrawaddy near Monywa and drains the Sagaing Division's western mountains toward the Indian border. Its middle reaches around Homalin and Thaungdut hold snakehead, giant gourami, and various catfish species in waters that remain largely unfished by visitors. The river runs between September and February at fishable levels; the monsoon from June to August brings flash floods that make it dangerous and turbid. Bamboo-raft excursions down the Chindwin from Homalin offer multi-day fishing access to the most remote sections.

## Inle Lake

Inle Lake in Shan State is Myanmar's second-largest lake, famous for its leg-rowing fishermen and floating gardens. The lake holds endemic species of snakehead and carp that exist nowhere else on earth. Sport fishing here has traditionally been subordinate to artisanal netting, but local fisheries organisations have in recent years piloted catch-and-release sessions for visiting anglers targeting the lake's endemic snakehead (Channa harcourtbutleri). The lake sits at 900 metres altitude and can be surprisingly cool in the dry season from November through February.

## Ayeyarwady Delta Snakehead

The Ayeyarwady (Irrawaddy) Delta south of Yangon is a labyrinth of tidal channels, mangrove-fringed creeks, and freshwater swamps extending over 25,000 square kilometres. Giant snakehead (Channa micropeltes) approaching 10 kilograms inhabit the freshwater zones above tidal influence, while bullseye snakehead and barramundi (known locally as nga htauk) occupy the brackish transition zones. Fishing the delta requires a boat and local knowledge; guided trips depart from Pathein, the delta's main town, and from boat operators along the Twante Canal connecting Yangon to the western delta.

## Hkakabo Razi Highland Streams

The Hkakabo Razi area in Kachin State, bordering Tibet to the north and Yunnan to the east, remains one of the most biologically unexplored regions of mainland Southeast Asia. Streams descending from altitudes above 3,000 metres through pine and rhododendron forest have been reported to contain wild salmonid-like species by researchers from the Wildlife Conservation Society who surveyed the region in the early 2000s. Formal sport fishing access is effectively unavailable; any visit requires special permits from the Myanmar government and is logistically challenging beyond the experience of most independent travellers. This is frontier fishing territory in the truest sense.

## Salween River

The Salween (Thanlwin) River enters Myanmar from the Yunnan province of China and runs south for over 1,000 kilometres through gorge-cut terrain in Kayah and Karen States before entering the Andaman Sea near Moulmein. It holds mahseer in its upper reaches, including the large golden mahseer (Tor putitora), as well as large catfish, snakehead, and barramundi in its lower reaches near the sea. Political sensitivity in the border regions has historically limited access; the most feasible sections for visiting anglers are below the Dawna Hills near Moulmein.

## Myitnge River

The Myitnge River drains from the Shan Plateau west into the Irrawaddy near Sagaing, opposite Mandalay. Its fast-flowing rocky reaches hold the Shan mahseer (Tor tambroides), an impressive game fish that runs to 5 kilograms in productive sections. Local Shan fishermen use traditional hand-woven traps and small drift nets; visiting sport anglers have found that fly fishing with large streamers or spinning with metal lures works in the pool-and-riffle sections downstream of the plateau edge. October to March is the most productive period.

## Meinmahla Kyun Wildlife Sanctuary Waters

The Meinmahla Kyun Wildlife Sanctuary, a Ramsar-listed wetland in the Irrawaddy Delta, covers approximately 4,000 square kilometres of tidal forest, mangrove, and freshwater marsh. Barramundi up to 15 kilograms are the target species in the tidal channels, where the fish push upstream on rising tides to hunt in the flooded mangrove roots. Sanctuary regulations require all fishing to be catch-and-release with approved guides; access is by boat from Bogale on the delta's eastern edge.

## Bago River System

The Bago River, which drains the Pegu Yoma hills east of Yangon and enters the Irrawaddy near Twante, holds a mixed population of freshwater species accessible for day fishing from Yangon. Snakehead, catfish, and the peacock bass (introduced decades ago) are the primary targets. The lower Bago near the Bago town itself produces reasonable catches of catfish on dough baits in the dry season.
