The productive spots on any fishery share a common denominator: structure. In freshwater, it is a drop-off where a gravel shoal falls into cold, dark water; a sunken tree root spreading into a pool; the seam between a fast mid-channel current and a slack eddy behind a boulder. In saltwater, it is a canyon edge at 40 m depth where warm and cold water meet, a reef pinnacle rising to within 8 m of the surface, or a mussel bed on a tidal flat that concentrates feeding flatfish at high water. Locating structure efficiently and returning to exactly the same position on successive visits — to the metre — is what GPS technology does for anglers, and it does it better than any landmark, compass bearing, or memory.

## GPS Device Versus Smartphone

Dedicated GPS units and smartphones both offer navigation, but they perform differently in fishing environments. A standalone GPS device — Garmin's Montana 750i, the Garmin GPSMAP 67, or the Garmin inReach Mini for offshore emergency communication — runs on AA batteries, operates at extreme temperatures, and is waterproof to IP68 standard. Its screen remains legible in direct sunlight. A smartphone with GPS (using apps such as Navionics, Garmin ActiveCaptain, or CalTopo) is more versatile in terms of software, but it consumes battery rapidly when the screen is active, and most are only splash-resistant. For freshwater bank fishing and river wading, a smartphone in a waterproof case is perfectly adequate. For boat fishing and offshore work, a dedicated chartplotter mounted at the helm with a redundant handheld GPS provides proper safety margins.

## Marking Waypoints: The Core Skill

A waypoint is a saved GPS coordinate — a digital pin in the water. The skill of marking waypoints well develops over time and is worth approaching systematically. When you land a fish from a specific spot, mark it immediately and annotate it with species, approximate size, depth, water temperature if available, and date. Many experienced anglers use a naming convention: "TROUT-DEC-2024-CHALK" or "BASS-FLOOD-2024-GROYNE3." Over time, these accumulate into a personal database of productive moments that reveals patterns invisible to casual observation. On a river, mark the entry and exit points of productive pools, the positions of visible large fish, and any hazards — submerged fences, shallow gravel bars, deep unexpected channels. In offshore fishing, mark bait schools, weed lines, temperature breaks, and any floating debris under which fish congregate.

## Reading Underwater Contour Maps

A bathymetric chart is to a water angler what an ordnance survey map is to a hillwalker — the fundamental document for understanding what lies beneath the surface. Navionics' Sonar Chart layers, freely available in the app with a subscription, compile crowdsourced sonar readings into detailed contour maps of thousands of lakes and reservoirs worldwide. On these charts, widely spaced contour lines indicate a gradual shelf; tightly packed lines indicate a steep drop-off where fish often patrol the edge. Fingers of shallow water extending into deeper basins are classic holding structures in lakes like Loch Lomond in Scotland, Lake Windermere in the English Lake District, and any of the Scandinavian glacial lakes. In the sea, published Admiralty charts (UK) and NOAA ENC charts (US) show depth contours, bottom type (rock, sand, gravel, mud), and shipping lanes — all useful for locating reef and wreck marks.

## Contour Map Interpretation for Different Species

Different fish relate to bathymetric features differently. For perch in a reservoir, look for a point extending from a bank that drops steeply to 6 m or deeper — perch pack along the drop-off and move up the slope to feed in low light. For brown trout in a stillwater, the shoulder of a submerged island, where depth transitions from 1.5 m to 4 m over 20 m, is often a consistent dawn location. For pike, the weed-bed edge at 2 to 3 m depth where it meets open water is a classic ambush position. In saltwater, sea bass use reef edges and tidal races over rocky ground; cod prefer mixed ground where mud and rubble create feeding habitat for sandeel and ragworm. Red snapper in the Gulf of Mexico and Caribbean concentrate along ledges and shelf breaks between 30 and 60 m depth.

## Planning Routes to Fish

Efficient route planning reduces time spent travelling and maximises time on productive water. Navionics' route-planning function allows anglers to draw a boat course that avoids shallow areas, marks intermediate waypoints for fuel stops, and calculates estimated time of arrival based on entered speed. For flats fishing — wading the bonefish or permit flats of the Bahamas' Andros Island or Mexico's Yucatán — printing or downloading tide-timed access maps showing which areas can be reached at what water level transforms what might be a muddy walk into a productive session. For wilderness river fishing in remote areas of Canada, Alaska, or Siberia, the CalTopo app (free, highly recommended) allows pre-trip route planning using USGS topographic layers, satellite imagery, and custom waypoint lists that can be exported to a GPS device.

## Sharing and Archiving Waypoints

Waypoints are valuable personal data that deserve backup and organisation. Garmin's BaseCamp software (free) allows desktop management, editing, and backup of waypoints and tracks from Garmin devices. Navionics Planner (web browser) allows cloud storage of fishing charts with saved markers. For advanced users, GPX files — the standard exchange format for GPS data — can be imported and exported between most apps and devices, enabling transfer from a Garmin handheld to a chartplotter, a phone app, or a browser-based planning tool. Consider storing a backup of your waypoint library in cloud storage; losing several years of productive marks to a device failure is a genuine loss.

## Topo Charts for River Fishing

Topographic maps — which show elevation contours, waterways, and land features — are as useful as bathymetric charts for the river angler, even though they show the land rather than the riverbed. A 1:25,000 OS Explorer map of a chalk stream valley reveals the course of side channels, millponds, carrier streams, and historical fish management features that are not visible from river level and are not always marked on digital mapping. In mountainous terrain, the shape of the valley tells you where the river pools and where it runs fast — information that translates directly into fishing strategy. For wild fishing on remote spate rivers in the Scottish Highlands or Norwegian fjordland, understanding how catchment area and gradient interact with rainfall events requires a topographic mindset alongside digital tools.
