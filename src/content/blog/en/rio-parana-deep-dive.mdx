The Rio Parana is South America's second-longest river, running over 4,880 kilometres from the highlands of the Brazilian Mato Grosso to its junction with the Rio Paraguay near Corrientes, Argentina, and ultimately to the Rio de la Plata estuary at Buenos Aires. Along its course it collects the tributaries of the Serra da Canastra, drains the Pantanal's southern margins, and carves through limestone and sandstone into the broad alluvial plain of northeastern Argentina. It is here, in the braided channels, flooded forests, and oxbow lakes below the Yacyretá Dam and around Corrientes city, that the river has developed an international reputation as one of the world's finest freshwater sport-fishing destinations.

## The Golden Dorado: Tiger of the River

Golden dorado — Salminus brasiliensis — is the undisputed star of Parana fishing. Its common name in Spanish, dorado del agua dulce, refers to its burnished golden flanks and crimson tail, though its Spanish nickname "el tigre del río" better captures its character. Adults reach 90 centimetres and occasionally exceed 15 kilograms, though the average catch in prime areas runs between 3 and 7 kilograms. The dorado's strike is one of the most violent in freshwater fishing: it attacks flies, lures, and live bait simultaneously from below, and the take is invariably explosive. After the strike it erupts from the surface in a sequence of acrobatic jumps that can reach 60 centimetres above the water, and the head-shaking runs can strip 30 metres of backing from a fly reel in seconds.

## Corrientes and the Dorado Culture

The city of Corrientes, capital of Corrientes Province, sits at the confluence of the Parana and the Rio Paraguay and is the operational hub of the Argentine dorado fishing industry. The city hosts an annual dorado fishing festival that draws anglers from across South America and Europe, and the surrounding wetlands — the Iberá Wetlands to the east and the Parana backwaters to the west — provide exceptional habitat. Lodge operations downriver from Corrientes near the town of Esquina and at estancias along the Parana have developed a fly-fishing for dorado culture that mirrors the traditions of Atlantic salmon fishing in its combination of expert guiding, refined technique, and deep respect for the resource. Most quality operations run strictly catch-and-release.

## The Parana Shovelnose Catfish

The other great sporting fish of the Parana system is the Parana shovelnose catfish — Sorubim lima — a dramatically shaped predator with an elongated, flattened snout and bold black-and-white spotted markings. Fish of 5 to 12 kilograms are common, and the closely related and much larger spotted sorubim (Pseudoplatystoma corruscans), locally called surubí, regularly exceeds 30 kilograms and has been recorded to over 90 kilograms in the upper system. The surubí is nocturnal and holds in deep channel bends and flooded forest edges. Spinning tackle with large swimbaits or trolling with deep-diving lures after dark produces the most consistent surubí results. The fight of a large surubí is not acrobatic — it runs deep and uses the current mercilessly, the weight of a 20-kilogram fish in strong current testing both angler and equipment.

## Fishing the Channels and Islands

The Parana below Corrientes divides into a labyrinthine network of channels, islands, and backwaters that changes character with the river's seasonal floods. The high-water period from November to March floods the adjacent gallery forests, and dorado and surubí move into shallow water to feed among submerged trees. This creates an extraordinary fly-fishing opportunity: casting large streamers — weighted deceivers, rabbit fur patterns, and spun deer hair poppers in yellow and orange — beneath overhanging vegetation produces savage strikes from dorado at close range. Guides who know the river run shallow-draft aluminium skiffs with jet outboards to navigate channels barely 30 centimetres deep during high water.

## Fly Fishing and the Dorado on the Fly

Argentina's fly-fishing community has embraced the dorado as the southern hemisphere's answer to the Atlantic salmon, and the techniques have become sophisticated. The preferred setup uses an 8- or 9-weight rod with a floating or intermediate line and a 1-metre section of 40-pound fluorocarbon as a bite tippet. Flies range from articulated streamers with epoxy heads and flash in natural baitfish colours to large surface flies — poppers and divers — fished in fast current over gravel bars. The dorado's response to a popper stripped aggressively in shallow, clear water is an iconic South American fishing experience. When a fish targets the fly, it often rises behind it for several metres before the explosive take.

## Regulations and Conservation

Argentina's freshwater fishing regulations vary by province. In Corrientes Province, dorado have a minimum size of 40 centimetres fork length, and bag limits are enforced. The Iberá Provincial Park, gazetted as a national park in its core zone, restricts fishing within its boundaries. The Yacyretá Dam between Argentina and Paraguay has significantly affected dorado migration patterns in the upper river, blocking the upstream spawning runs that historically produced the largest fish. Conservation organisations including Fundación Vida Silvestre Argentina advocate for improved fish passage and for the expansion of catch-and-release-only zones around prime nursery areas.

## When to Go

The Parana fishing calendar divides into two main windows. The high-water season from January through March concentrates fish in predictable areas and produces the best dorado fly fishing in the flooded forest. The low-water season from June through September drops the river to its structural bones — gravel bars, channel edges, and deep holes become visible — and the dorado fishing shifts to faster, clearer water where sight fishing with small lures becomes possible. Water temperatures in the Parana range from around 15°C in midwinter (July) to over 28°C in midsummer, and the dorado is most active above 20°C.
