Lake Ladoga lies 40 kilometres east of St Petersburg and covers 17,700 square kilometres — the largest lake in Europe by far. Its maximum depth reaches 230 metres in the northern basin, carved by glacial action from hard Precambrian bedrock; the southern shoreline, by contrast, is shallow and sandy, backed by forests of pine and birch that extend into the Karelian isthmus. Ladoga has been strategically important throughout Russian and Soviet history — it was the only supply route into besieged Leningrad during World War II, via the famous Road of Life across the frozen lake surface. Today it is a working recreational fishery whose most significant residents are brown trout, landlocked salmon, and the world's only freshwater-adapted subspecies of ringed seal.

## Ladoga Brown Trout: Deepwater Trophy Fishing

The Ladoga brown trout (*Salmo trutta morpha lacustris*) is a deep-water lake form that reaches considerably larger sizes than its river-dwelling relatives. Fish of 8–12 kg are taken from Ladoga's open waters in spring and autumn when the trout move towards the surface as water temperatures cool. The primary technique is trolling with large spinners, spoons, and deep-diving crankbaits at depths of 10–30 metres along the thermocline. The northern basin, with its deeper, clearer, colder water fed by Karelian rivers like the Vuoksi and Suvanto, holds the densest concentrations of large trout. Organised fishing trips operating from the town of Priozersk target Ladoga trout specifically, with local captains who possess detailed knowledge of productive trolling lanes built up over decades of fishing.

## Landlocked Salmon of the Tributaries

The rivers that feed Lake Ladoga — particularly the Vuoksi system, the Burnaya, and the Syas — receive runs of landlocked Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar m. sebago*) that have been isolated from the sea since the lake's separation from the Baltic over 3,000 years ago. These fish, locally called the Ladoga salmon or nevskiy losos, can reach 12 kg and represent one of the most significant landlocked salmon populations in Europe. The Vuoksi, which crosses from Finland through Karelia before entering Ladoga, offers some of the finest salmon fly fishing in the region during the summer and autumn runs. Access to the Finnish headwaters requires a Finnish fishing licence; the Karelian section requires a Russian permit issued at the regional level by the Karelian Ministry of Natural Resources.

## The Ladoga Ringed Seal: Conservation Intersection

The Ladoga ringed seal (*Pusa hispida ladogensis*) is an endemic subspecies that has been isolated in the lake since the last ice age. The population, estimated at 3,500–5,000 individuals, competes directly with anglers for salmon and trout. Seals congregate in the northern archipelago zone — a complex of hundreds of small islands and skerries where they pup on ice in late winter — and this same zone is prime fishing territory for trolling and lure fishing in spring. The seal's presence is a key reason why Ladoga salmon and trout populations remain under pressure despite reduced commercial fishing pressure since the Soviet era. NatureScot-equivalent bodies in Russia have proposed seasonal no-fishing zones around key seal pupping areas, a policy that intersects directly with the most productive spring fishing period.

## Pike and Perch in the Southern Shallows

The shallow southern margins of Ladoga, particularly around the bay of Volkhov and the extensive reed systems east of Novaya Ladoga, hold excellent pike and perch populations accessible to shore anglers and small-boat fishermen. Pike exceeding 12 kg are taken from the southern bay systems in spring and autumn, and the perch fishing — particularly ice fishing in winter — is among the best in north-west Russia. The perch populations around the Volkhov river mouth, where nutrient-rich river water creates feeding concentrations, are particularly productive in autumn and through the ice season.

## Ice Fishing: A Karelian Winter Tradition

Ladoga freezes partially every winter, with reliable ice forming in the northern archipelago by December and in the shallow southern bays by January. The ice fishing season, typically January through March, draws thousands of Russian anglers onto the lake surface to target perch, pike, bream, and burbot (*Lota lota*) — the latter a nocturnal cold-water species that feeds most actively under ice and provides outstanding sport on small jigs and dead-bait rigs fished in holes drilled through 40–60 cm of ice. Ice fishing camps establish themselves on the main lake ice around Priozersk, Schlusselburg, and Svirskaya Guba. Anglers travel by snowmobile and ice-rated vehicles to distant productive areas identified by sonar.

## Accessing Ladoga: St Petersburg as Base

St Petersburg sits at the junction of the Neva River and the Gulf of Finland; the Neva drains Ladoga and the lake's western shoreline begins just 40 kilometres to the east. The city is served by Pulkovo International Airport with connections throughout Europe. Fishing packages to Ladoga are bookable through St Petersburg-based operators who provide transport, accommodation in waterfront lodges, boats, guide services, and the necessary regional fishing permits. Several lodges operate from Priozersk — reached in 90 minutes from St Petersburg via the Sortavala highway — and from the town of Svirstroi in Leningrad Oblast on the lake's southern shore.

## Regulations and the State of the Fishery

Russian federal fishing regulations apply across Ladoga, with regional additions from both Leningrad Oblast and the Republic of Karelia depending on where the fishing occurs. Minimum size limits for salmon and trout are enforced, and a daily bag limit applies. Specific tributaries may be closed during spawning periods — typically September through November for salmon and trout entering the rivers. The Vuoksi on the Russian side is subject to Finnish-Russian bilateral fisheries agreements that add additional seasonal restrictions.
