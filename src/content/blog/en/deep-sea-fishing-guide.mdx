The boat leaves the marina in the dark, and for the first hour there is nothing to do but drink coffee and watch the shore lights sink behind the wake. Then the engines drop to trolling speed somewhere over a canyon edge or a current line, the mate sets a spread of lures, and the waiting begins — until a reel screams in a way no freshwater angler is ever quite prepared for. A first offshore charter is one of fishing's great threshold experiences: mahi-mahi lit up neon gold under the transom, a sailfish tail-walking a hundred metres out, or simply a cooler full of snapper and the deep ache of arms that fought fish all day. It is also a day with real money, real logistics and a few unwritten rules attached. This guide covers what to expect, what to ask, what to bring — and how to keep your breakfast where it belongs.

## What a charter actually is — and what it costs

A private charter means the whole boat is yours: typically a crew of captain plus mate, fishing for 4 to 8 hours, with all tackle, bait, licences and usually ice included. Depending on region, boat size and fuel prices, expect roughly 600 to 2,500 US dollars for the boat — split between your group. The budget alternative is a party boat (head boat), where you pay 50 to 150 dollars per person for a spot at the rail alongside strangers, usually bottom fishing rather than trolling for billfish. Half-day trips (4 hours) stay in nearshore waters and suit families and the seasick-prone; three-quarter and full days reach the deeper reefs, wrecks and bluewater where the marquee species live. A 30-minute steam reaches different water in Key West than it does in San Diego or Cabo San Lucas — which is why choosing the port matters as much as choosing the boat.

## Choosing the right boat and captain

Book the captain, not the boat. Read recent reviews looking for the same names recurring — a mate praised by name across dozens of trips is the best signal in the business. Be honest about your goals when you enquire: meat fishing for the freezer, a first marlin, kids who need action every ten minutes — these are different trips, sometimes different boats. Good questions to ask before paying a deposit: What will we realistically catch in that week of the season? Is the full crew included, and what does the deposit cover if weather cancels? Who keeps the fish, and will the crew clean and bag the catch? Is fighting gear stand-up or chair? A captain who answers patiently and specifically before booking will run the same kind of day on the water. One who promises marlin in any month is selling something.

## Seasickness: the strategy that actually works

Seasickness ruins more first charters than weather and slow fishing combined, and the cruel part is that it is largely preventable. The medication rule is absolute: take it before you feel anything. Dimenhydrinate or meclizine the night before and again an hour before departure; or a scopolamine patch behind the ear the previous evening. Once nausea starts, pills no longer help. Skip the greasy breakfast and the big night out — alcohol and diesel fumes are a vicious pairing. On board, stay on deck in fresh air, keep your eyes on the horizon, stay out of the cabin and the head, and steer clear of the stern exhaust. Ginger chews and pressure-point wristbands help some people as a supplement. And if it happens anyway: the lee rail, downwind, and no shame whatsoever — every mate has seen it a thousand times, and many anglers feel human again within the hour.

## What to bring (and what to leave at the dock)

The boat provides rods, reels, bait and terminal tackle, so your bag is about comfort. Bring: non-marking soled shoes (dark soles scuff decks and annoy crews), polarized sunglasses with a retainer strap, reef-safe sunscreen reapplied hourly, a hat, a light rain jacket for spray, more water than you think reasonable, and food in a soft cooler — many boats sell nothing on board. A dry bag protects phone and wallet. Cash for the tip. Leave behind: bananas (the superstition is ancient, universal and enforced with surprising seriousness on some boats), glass bottles, spray sunscreen near the deck, and your own tackle unless the captain has agreed to it in advance. Seasoned hands also bring a small towel — between baits, gaffs and fish boxes, your hands will be interesting by mid-morning.

## On board: how the day actually unfolds

Listen to the safety briefing, then to the mate — on a charter boat the mate is your coach, gaffer, knot-tier and fish-finder all in one. When trolling, lines stay in the water and patience is the technique; strikes come in flurries, and when one comes the crew will shout who grabs which rod. Fight fish the way they coach you: rod tip up, short pumps, reel on the way down, no slack ever. When bottom fishing, the mate will show the rhythm — drop, two cranks up, wait for the distinct thump. Ask questions; good crews love teaching. Stay seated or hold a rail when the boat runs, keep fingers away from running lines, and never sit on the gunwale underway. The cooler hierarchy is simple: the crew decides what is legal and what gets released, because they carry the licence and the fines.

## Tipping etiquette: the part nobody explains

The standard tip on a charter is 15 to 20 percent of the boat price, in cash, handed to the mate or captain at the dock — on many boats the mate works for tips alone, and that figure assumes competent, hard-working service. On a 1,200-dollar full day, that means 180 to 240 dollars from the group, not per person. On head boats, 15 to 20 dollars per angler is customary, more if the deckhand untangled your lines all day, netted your fish and filleted your catch. Tip on effort, not on the fish count: crews do not control the ocean, and a mate who worked relentlessly through a slow day has earned the full amount. Fish cleaning is sometimes a separate, posted fee — ask. If service was genuinely poor, say so to the captain rather than silently zeroing the tip; word travels both ways in every marina.

## After the catch: fish, photos and paperwork

Decide before the trip what you want done with the catch. Most charters clean and bag your legal fish at the dock; in many tourist ports — Cabo, Cancún, the Florida Keys among them — local restaurants will cook your catch the same evening, which is one of travel fishing's purest pleasures. Billfish, and in many fisheries large breeding-size groupers and other vulnerable species, are released as a matter of law or ethics: take the photo boat-side with the fish in the water, and let it swim. If you are travelling internationally, know that fresh fish rarely crosses borders legally — eat it where you caught it and bring home the photos. Keep your paper or digital fishing licence if one was issued personally to you; some jurisdictions require you to carry it even on a licensed charter boat.
