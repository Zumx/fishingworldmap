The east coast of Madagascar faces the open Indian Ocean with almost no continental shelf to slow the deep-water currents, which means pelagic species come unnervingly close to shore. Drop baits off the reef edge near Île Sainte-Marie and you are fishing in water that plunges past 1,000 metres within a kilometre of the beach. Meanwhile, on the high central plateau, rivers that tumble down from Antsirabe and Ambositra carry clean, cold water through rice terraces and ravines where few foreign anglers have ever cast a line. Madagascar's dual nature — tropical saltwater giant meets highland freshwater mystery — makes it one of the most compelling fishing destinations the Indian Ocean region has to offer.

## Giant Trevally at Nosy Be

The islands around Nosy Be, off Madagascar's northwest coast, produce giant trevally — locally called carangue géante — that regularly exceed 30 kilograms. Popping over the shallow coral bomies at first light, when the tide runs hard through the passes between Nosy Tanikely and Nosy Komba, generates explosive surface strikes. Most visiting anglers fish from small locally operated pirogues, which keep the carbon footprint low and allow access to reef structure unreachable by larger vessels. Water temperatures around Nosy Be peak at 30°C from November through March.

## Blue Marlin and Sailfish off Toliara

The channel between Madagascar's southwest coast and the Mozambique mainland funnels baitfish and the billfish that follow them. Charter operators out of Toliara run 40 to 60 kilometre trips to the edge of the Mozambique Channel where blue marlin between 150 and 400 kilograms are caught on trolled rigged mackerel and skirted lures. Sailfish appear in large numbers from April through September when the southeast trade winds push surface temperatures into the mid-20s°C. The sea can run rough in the channel during June and July, so experienced skippers time departures carefully.

## Île Sainte-Marie Wahoo Run

Île Sainte-Marie, an elongated island 50 kilometres off Madagascar's eastern coast, hosts a remarkable wahoo run from June through August. Cold upwellings generated by the Agulhas Current system push baitfish against the island's drop-offs and wahoo stack up in schools that local fishermen have long relied on for food. Sport anglers targeting wahoo troll high-speed lures at 12 to 14 knots along the island's western flank. Fish between 15 and 25 kilograms are common; occasional specimens push 40 kilograms. The island's reef also shelters dogtooth tuna up to 60 kilograms.

## Mangoky River Freshwater Fishing

The Mangoky River drains a vast watershed from the central highlands southwest to the Mozambique Channel near Morombe. Its middle reaches hold snakehead, tilapia species endemic to Madagascar, and the Madagascar rainbow fish. The river runs gin-clear during the dry season from April through October, making sight fishing viable for anglers prepared to walk the sandy banks. No formal guiding infrastructure exists here — visiting anglers need to arrange transport from Morombe or Miandrivazo and negotiate locally for river access. The isolation is both the challenge and the reward.

## Highland Rivers near Antsirabe

Antsirabe, sitting at 1,500 metres above sea level on the central plateau, is Madagascar's thermal resort town and the gateway to some genuinely cold-water river fishing. Streams descending from the Ambohimahasoa range toward Ambositra carry introduced brown trout — stocked by French colonial administrators in the early twentieth century — in stretches that remain cool year-round thanks to altitude and forest cover. The trout are not large by European standards, running 200 to 400 grams on average, but the setting — volcanic rock outcrops, eucalyptus forest, and occasional red laterite cliffs — is spectacular. Fly fishing with small nymphs and dry flies works well from May through September.

## Masoala Peninsula Reef Fishing

The Masoala Peninsula in northeast Madagascar, protected by Madagascar's largest national park, has reef fishing of a quality that reflects the near-total absence of commercial pressure within park boundaries. Grouper up to 25 kilograms, coral trout, and emperor fish inhabit the bommies off Tampolo and Cap Est. A research permit is required for all fishing activities within the park boundaries and quotas are strictly enforced by the park authority. The peninsula is accessible only by boat from Maroantsetra, a two-hour journey in calm conditions across the Bay of Antongil.

## Pangalanes Canal System

The Pangalanes Canal is a 665-kilometre inland waterway running parallel to Madagascar's east coast, connected to the sea at several points and fed by rivers descending from the Analamazoatra highlands. It holds an extraordinary variety of endemic freshwater species — Madagascar perch, tilapia, and multiple species of snakehead — in a mix of lagoon, river, and flood-plain habitat. Dugout canoe fishing with local guides out of Manakara or Vatomandry produces the most authentic experience. The canals also hold large freshwater eels that local fishermen target with night lines baited with shrimp.

## Cap d'Ambre Blue Water

Madagascar's northern tip at Cap d'Ambre sits at the junction of the Mozambique Channel and the Indian Ocean proper, where three distinct current systems collide. The upwelling created by this convergence supports enormous populations of skipjack and yellowfin tuna from September through November. Charter vessels from Diego Suarez (Antsiranana) make the 120-kilometre run north along a coast of red sandstone cliffs and semi-arid scrub to reach the cap. Yellowfin tuna to 80 kilograms are not uncommon, and the rare presence of Pacific bluefin tuna in these latitudes has been documented by researchers from the Wildlife Conservation Society.

## Ranomafana River

Below the Ranomafana National Park, in the river valleys descending toward the east coast lowlands, the Namorona River and its tributaries hold populations of the Madagascar rainbowfish and various endemic cichlid species that are of interest to specialist freshwater anglers and aquarists travelling with a fishing permit. The Namorona runs fast and clear after the rains ease in May, with water temperatures between 18°C and 22°C in its upper reaches near the park boundary. Fly fishing with small weighted nymphs is the most productive method in the faster water.
