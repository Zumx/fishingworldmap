Serbia is landlocked but far from short of water. The Danube enters from the northwest near Bačka Palanka and flows through the dramatic Iron Gates gorge on the Romanian border before leaving the country near Negotin. The Sava drains from the west, the Tisa from the north, and the Drina marks the entire western border with Bosnia. From these great rivers, tributaries radiate into every region: the Morava, the Mlava, the Pek, and the limestone-filtered chalk streams of the south that support populations of brown trout and Danube huchen. Fishing culture in Serbia is serious and deeply embedded — the country has over 200 registered angling clubs, and the right to fish is considered part of national life.

## Danube Wels Catfish at the Iron Gates

The Iron Gates gorge, where the Danube cuts through the Carpathian and Balkan mountain ranges on the Serbian-Romanian border, is one of the most remarkable environments for giant freshwater fishing in Europe. Wels catfish (Silurus glanis) grow to exceptional sizes in the slow, deep eddies behind the rocks in the gorge. Fish of 100 kg and above are caught here by dedicated night anglers using dead bait — large roach, carp, or pike heads — on 12 to 14 inch circle hooks fished on 80 to 100 lb braid from anchored boats. The Djerdap National Park encompasses the gorge, and access is through the towns of Donji Milanovac and Kladovo, both of which have fishing associations offering guide services.

## Danube Salmon (Huchen) on the Drina

The Drina River, forming the border between Serbia and Bosnia, is one of the last strongholds of the Danube salmon (Hucho hucho) in the Balkans. Known locally as mladica, this powerful salmonid reaches 1.2 metres and 30 kg in the Drina's clear, cold water. Fishing for huchen is highly regulated in Serbia — a special permit is required and the season runs from December to March, covering the post-spawning feeding period. Large spinning lures, 14 to 20 cm articulated minnows in silver-blue or natural roach colours, are fished on heavy rods with 30 to 50 lb braid. The section between Zvornik and Bajina Bašta is the most productive stretch. Most successful anglers engage local guides from clubs in Ljubovija or Bajina Bašta.

## Grayling on the Drina Tributaries

The tributaries feeding the Drina from the Serbian side — the Rzav, the Lim, and the Uvac — hold fine populations of grayling in their upper reaches. The Uvac canyon, designated as a special nature reserve, is particularly dramatic: a meandering river cuts a deep gorge through white limestone, home to griffon vultures overhead and grayling in the pools below. Fish average 300 to 500 grams, with larger specimens to 800 grams in the deeper pools below canyon bends. Upstream nymph fishing with size 16 to 20 beaded patterns and dry fly fishing with parachute comparaduns from June to September are the recommended approaches. The Uvac reserve charges an entry fee and issues fishing permits through the Sjenica tourist office.

## Crni Vir Trout Fishery

Crni vir, near the Stara Planina mountain range in eastern Serbia, is one of the country's most respected private trout fisheries. Fed by ice-cold springs, the chalk-clear ponds and river section hold brown trout and rainbow trout of 1 to 6 kg. Fly-only rules apply on the river section, and barbless hooks are mandatory throughout. The landscape — beech forest dropping to open meadows, with Stara Planina's ridgeline visible above — provides the backdrop for some of the most scenic trout fishing in Serbia. Day permits are issued at the fishery office and include access to a rest house with traditional Serbian mountain food.

## Ада Сафари Catfish and Carp

Ada Safari, on the Danube south of Belgrade, is a purpose-built fishing resort on a river island targeting the Danube's large-bodied carp and catfish. Carp up to 30 kg inhabit the deep Danube channel on the island's downstream face, and a structured baiting programme using boilies, corn, and pellets draws fish to specific swims. Method feeder and bolt rig approaches are standard. Wels catfish average 30 to 60 kg on the resort's dedicated night swims. The operation is one of the most professional big-fish fishing setups in the Balkans, with platforms, bivvies, and Baitboat hire all included. Access is from Surčin, 20 kilometres west of Belgrade.

## Arkanj Danube Fishing Camp

Arkanj, near Apatin in the northern Vojvodina plain, is a fishing camp on a side arm of the Danube with access to some of the finest catfish and carp water in the entire river system. The Danube at this point is wide and powerful, and the back channels hold enormous catfish throughout the summer. The camp is famous on the European catfishing circuit and has produced catches of catfish over 150 kg on regular guided trips. Bait boat placement of large dead roach at night over the deep channel, combined with heavy carp gear and 100 to 200 lb breaking strain mainline, is the standard method. Accommodation is in wooden cabins with a catering service.

## Reka Mlava Brown Trout

The Mlava rises in eastern Serbia's Homoljske Planine mountains and flows northwest to join the Danube near Požarevac. Its upper section, above the town of Žagubica, runs through a limestone valley and holds self-sustaining populations of brown trout in pools of extraordinary clarity. The Mlava is a controlled fishery managed by local associations, and beat permits are issued weekly. Trout average 250 to 600 grams but fish to 2 kg are present in the deepest pools. Upstream nymphing and dry fly fishing are both productive from April to September. The Resava cave complex nearby makes Žagubica a destination worth combining with a two- or three-day fishing trip.

## Jokino Vrelo Spring Creek

Јокино Врело (Jokino Vrelo) is a chalk spring creek in the Kopaonik mountain foothills of central Serbia, flowing from a karstic spring in a meadow to join a larger tributary of the Ibar. The spring produces a constant flow of 10 to 12 degrees Celsius year-round, allowing trout fishing even in high summer when other streams warm. Brown trout of 300 to 900 grams inhabit the clear, weedy channel. Sight fishing to visible rising fish is possible from June to August. The association managing the creek limits daily rods to preserve quality. This is regarded by local fly anglers as one of the most challenging and rewarding short-rod experiences in Serbia.

## Tisa River Bream and Pike

The Tisa (Tisza) enters Serbia from Hungary near Horgoš and flows south through the flat Vojvodina plain. The lower Tisa and its oxbow lakes around Bečej hold exceptional populations of bream, roach, and pike. Bream to 4 kg are common in the deep main channel bends where the current slackens. Method feeder with pellet and maize produces consistent results in summer. Pike inhabit the oxbow lakes in large numbers and respond well to large rubber perch-pattern lures cast along the reed margins in early morning. The Bečej angling association manages several productive oxbow lakes and issues day permits at the town angling house.
