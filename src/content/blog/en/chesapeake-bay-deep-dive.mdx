There is a moment on the Chesapeake Bay, usually in late April or early May, when a westerly breeze flattens the chop over the Susquehanna Flats and the surface suddenly turns dark and boiling. Striped bass — locally called rockfish — have arrived from their ocean wintering grounds in numbers that make the water look alive. Pods of fish stretch for acres, birds work the edges, and a well-placed bucktail or soft plastic dropped into the melee produces a jarring hit within seconds. This is the spring run, the event that defines fishing on the largest estuary in North America.

## The Spring Striper Run

The Chesapeake Bay produces more striped bass than any other water body on the Atlantic coast. The river mouths of the Susquehanna, Potomac, Rappahannock, and James are critical spawning grounds, and the spring migration concentrates fish in a predictable corridor running from the Bay Bridge at Annapolis northward to the Elk River and the Susquehanna Flats near Havre de Grace, Maryland. Water temperature is the trigger: once surface temps climb into the 55 to 65°F range, the big pre-spawn females stage in the shallows. Trophy fish — those exceeding 36 inches — are common during the peak, which typically runs from mid-April through late May. Fly fishers walking the shoreline at Elk Neck State Park and at Turkey Point cast Clouser Minnows into the tidal shallows and hook fish that run past 20 pounds on 8-weight rods.

## Bluefish Blitzes and Surface Mayhem

By June, the bluefish arrive and the character of Bay fishing shifts from patient stalking to explosive opportunism. Blues push baitfish — primarily Atlantic menhaden, known locally as bunker — to the surface in what anglers call a blitz. In the lower Bay, around the mouth of the York River and the shipping lanes off Hampton Roads, a blitz can develop without warning and last only minutes. A chrome spoon or a surface popper worked at speed produces violent strikes, and the razor-edged teeth of a 10-pound bluefish make wire traces or heavy fluorocarbon a necessity. The fish run in mixed sizes from the so-called "snapper blues" of 12 to 16 inches up to doormat-class adults pushing 18 to 20 pounds when the peak migration hits in late summer.

## Tidal Rivers: A Different Kind of Fishing

Beyond the main Bay channel, the tidal creeks and rivers offer a more intimate form of sport fishing. The Patuxent River between Lower Marlboro and Benedict has a strong population of resident rockfish that hold tight to dock pilings, bridge abutments, and submerged timber throughout the warmer months. Light tackle enthusiasts using 6- to 8-pound fluorocarbon and small paddle-tail shads pick fish up to 24 inches with regularity. The St. Mary's River near Leonardtown and the Potomac at Cobb Island provide similar structure-oriented fishing. Kayak anglers find these rivers particularly accessible — launch ramps at Mattawoman Creek and Nanjemoy Creek put paddlers on productive water within minutes.

## Grass Flats and Light Tackle

The shallow grass flats of the mid-Bay around Tilghman Island, the Choptank River estuary, and the Tangier Sound are prime territory for light tackle and fly fishing for multiple species simultaneously. Submerged aquatic vegetation — primarily widgeon grass and eelgrass — holds blue crabs, small menhaden, and silversides, which in turn attract rockfish, large-mouth bass in the fresher upper tributaries, and even speckled sea trout in the southern reaches around Smith Island and Tangier Island. Wading anglers work the sand-and-grass edges on the flooding tide, and the clarity of the water — often several feet in good conditions — allows sight fishing with fly rods rigged with 10-weight lines and Deceiver-style flies in chartreuse and white.

## Regulations and Licensing

Fishing regulations on the Chesapeake Bay are governed by Maryland and Virginia separately, and the rules differ significantly between the two states. In Maryland, rockfish regulations in recent years have included a slot limit of 19 to 24 inches for most of the tidal Chesapeake, with a separate trophy season in May for fish over 35 inches. Virginia maintains similar slot limits. A Maryland Sport Fishing license is required for anyone 16 and older fishing tidal waters; Virginia requires its own license. Catch-and-release is strongly encouraged for large female stripers during the spring spawn, and many guide operations run exclusively catch-and-release during the pre-spawn staging period.

## Guide Culture and Access

The Eastern Shore city of Rock Hall, Maryland, is something of a sport-fishing hub for the mid-Bay, with a concentration of charter captains and headboats that run both nearshore and offshore trips. Annapolis, Oxford, and Cambridge also have active charter fleets. For shore-bound anglers, the fishing piers at Sandy Point State Park near Annapolis, William Preston Lane Jr. Memorial Bridge (the Bay Bridge), and the long public pier at North Beach give access to the main channel where rockfish hold over hard bottom and shell hash.

## Autumn and the Fall Run

The fall run, which builds from September through November, rivals the spring migration in quality. Stripers that have spent the summer feeding on menhaden return from the ocean fat and aggressive. The fall run pushes further south, and the lower Bay around the mouth of the Rappahannock and the northern tip of the Eastern Shore at Cape Charles produces fish well into December in mild years. Trolling with parachute jigs and umbrella rigs produces multiple hook-ups and is particularly effective in the shipping lanes between the Bay Bridge-Tunnel and the Thimble Shoal Light.
