Mexico stretches more than 3,000 km from its northern deserts to its jungle-clad southern frontier, and almost every coastal kilometre harbours something worth fishing for. The Pacific side runs warm current from the tropics northward along Baja California, concentrating billfish, dorado, and tuna in numbers that rival anywhere on earth. The Caribbean side, sheltered by the Yucatan's barrier reef system, provides a completely different experience: gin-clear flats, tarpon in flooded jungle channels, and permit on the coral-rubble banks. Between these coasts, Mexico's vast freshwater interior hides enormous bass lakes and tournament-grade largemouth fisheries. Few countries match this breadth.

## Los Cabos, Baja California Sur: Striped and Blue Marlin Capital

The waters at the southern tip of the Baja Peninsula, where the Pacific and the Sea of Cortez converge off Cabo San Lucas, are among the most productive billfish habitats on the planet. The 1,000-fathom line runs within 3 km of shore at Cabo, allowing anglers to reach prime blue water within thirty minutes of leaving the marina. Striped marlin are present year-round; blue marlin arrive in force from June through November, with individual fish exceeding 350 kg not uncommon. The annual Los Cabos Billfish Tournament draws international fleets every summer. Local operations including Cabo Marlini run professional sport-fishing charters with fully equipped vessels and experienced mates.

## East Cape, Baja California Sur: Dorado and Roosterfish on the Corridor

Between Los Cabos and La Paz, the East Cape corridor along the Sea of Cortez holds perhaps the best roosterfish habitat in Mexico. Roosterfish (Nematistius pectoralis) patrol the beach breaks and rocky points from La Ribera north to Los Barriles; the best approach is walking the beach at dawn with an 8–10 weight fly rod or spinning gear with large poppers. Fish of 10–25 kg are regularly encountered within 50 metres of the surf zone. Dorado runs in the offshore channel from May through October are spectacular — trolling a rigged ballyhoo past a floating kelp paddy can produce multiple fish in a single drift. The spring fishery for yellowfin tuna in April and May at Cerro Prieto, about 30 km southeast of Los Barriles, is consistently productive.

## La Paz: Roosterfish Flats and Wahoo at the Espiritu Santo Archipelago

La Paz, the capital of Baja California Sur, anchors one of Mexico's most diverse inshore and offshore fisheries. The protected waters between La Paz Bay and Isla Espiritu Santo shelter resident populations of pargo (Pacific cubera snapper), cabrilla (sea bass), and yellowtail. Wahoo in the fast channel between Isla Espiritu Santo and the mainland from October through December reach 30–35 kg; trolling with high-speed lures at 12–14 knots is the standard technique. The island's rocky points are also prime amberjack territory, and the protected bays on the island's eastern side host roosterfish that can be sight-cast on calm mornings. Don Carmelo's shore-based guide service in La Paz is well regarded among visiting anglers.

## Sea of Cortez, Loreto: Sierra Mackerel and White Seabass

Loreto, a colonial town halfway up the Baja Peninsula, serves as the gateway to the Loreto Bay National Marine Park — a UNESCO-listed reserve that includes five uninhabited islands and some of the most intact reef and kelp ecosystems in the Gulf. Sierra mackerel, large Pacific jack crevalle, and white seabass congregate along the rocky margins of Isla Danzante and Isla Carmen. White seabass (Atractoscion nobilis) reach 15–20 kg in these waters and are intensely pursued from February through April; the fishing involves drifting live squid and sardines over rocky structure at 20–40 metres depth. Water clarity in the Marine Park often exceeds 20 metres.

## Puerto Vallarta: Offshore Sailfish and Pacific Dorado

Puerto Vallarta, on the northern shores of Banderas Bay, sits adjacent to deep underwater canyons that bring sailfish, dorado, and blue marlin close to the city. The Corbeteña reef complex, about 60 km southwest of town, is a submarine landmark where fish concentrate around the topography year-round. Sailfish in Banderas Bay peak from November through February; the bay itself is large enough that offshore fishing grounds lie just 20–30 km from the marina at Marina Vallarta. Half-day inshore charters target jack crevalle, snook, and roosterfish on the reef systems of the Marietas Islands, a protected reserve visible from the Nayarit coast.

## Mazatlan: Marlin and Dorado at the Golden Zone

Mazatlan, on Sinaloa's Pacific coast, was Mexico's original billfishing destination; its offshore canyons produce consistent blue and striped marlin from May through October, with dorado and wahoo as abundant secondary targets. The Three Caños Canyon, about 30 km west of the Mazatlan port, concentrates fish in the deep rip where warm Pacific water meets cooler California Current upwellings. The Mazatlan sport fishing fleet — one of the largest in Mexico — operates from the marina adjacent to the old port, and full-day charters to the canyons are well organized. Roosterfish can be found inshore along the coast north toward El Dedo point, where rocky outcrops create favourable habitat.

## Cancun and Cozumel: Deep Blue and Reef Fishing in the Caribbean

On the Caribbean side, Cancun and Cozumel offer a fundamentally different proposition: crystal-clear water over the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef, the second-largest barrier reef in the world after Australia's Great Barrier Reef. Offshore trolling from Cancun targets Atlantic blue marlin, white marlin, and wahoo in the deep Yucatan Channel between the peninsula and Cuba. Cozumel's drift fishing over the island's dramatic wall produces grouper, snapper, and jack at 20–50 metres depth, while the shallow eastern flats are prime territory for permit and bonefish on fly. Casa Jansen operates guided flats tours from Cozumel, targeting permit on the coral-rubble banks east of the island.

## Holbox and the Yucatan Flats: Tarpon, Permit, and Grand Slam

The shallow lagoon systems north of the Yucatan Peninsula, particularly around Isla Holbox and the adjacent Yalahau lagoon, represent some of the finest fly fishing in the Americas. Tarpon migrating from the Caribbean into these channels between May and September offer sight-fishing at its most intense; fish of 50–90 kg roll and cruise in water less than 2 metres deep, and a perfect fly presentation must land within 30 cm of the fish's head. Permit patrol the white sand flats between Holbox and Isla Contoy. An angler achieving tarpon, permit, and bonefish in a single day — a grand slam — is a realistic possibility here, and Pesca Maya Fishing Lodge on the mainland south of Holbox specializes exactly in these three species.

## Merida and the Yucatan Freshwater Cenotes: Bass in the Jungle

The Yucatan's limestone karst landscape is honeycombed with cenotes — sinkholes and cave systems connected to a vast freshwater aquifer. Many of these water bodies hold populations of peacock bass (introduced) and the native Mayan cichlid (Mayaheros urophthalmus), a robust, aggressive fish that reaches 1.5 kg and strikes both lures and flies with determination. Centro Recreativo Dunas Campestre near Merida is a resort that offers organized freshwater fishing alongside other activities. The native Mayan cichlid fishing, while not well known internationally, produces consistent action on small jigs and topwater lures, and the cenote settings — surrounded by jungle with howler monkeys overhead — are unlike anything found elsewhere in North America.

## Campeche: Snook and Tarpon in the Gulf Mangroves

The Gulf of Mexico coast of Campeche state is characterized by vast mangrove lagoon systems fed by rivers draining the southern Yucatan. Common snook (Centropomus undecimalis) reach trophy sizes here — fish of 8–12 kg are not unusual — and are best targeted at night and early morning on topwater plugs around bridge pilings, mangrove edges, and channel confluences. Baby tarpon (30–80 cm) inhabit the upper reaches of these lagoon systems year-round, providing exciting light-tackle sport in scenic surroundings. Cultivo de mojarras aldahir, a local fish operation near Campeche, supplements the wild fishery scene with managed freshwater sport fishing opportunities.
