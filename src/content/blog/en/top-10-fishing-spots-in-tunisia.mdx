Tunisia's coastline curves for over 1,300 km around the southern Mediterranean, from the coral-fringed rocky coast of the northwest near Tabarka to the vast shallow Gulf of Gabes in the south, where tidal ranges of nearly 2 metres are unusual for the Mediterranean and create a uniquely productive intertidal zone. In between lies the Cap Bon peninsula, the Gulf of Tunis, and the island of Djerba — each with distinct fishing character. Add the reservoirs of the northern highlands, which hold European perch, pike, and carp in agricultural landscapes that few visiting anglers ever explore, and Tunisia reveals itself as one of the Mediterranean's most underrated fishing destinations.

## Tabarka — Grouper and Coral Bottom Fishing

Tabarka in the extreme northwest of Tunisia, close to the Algerian border and just 170 km from Sardinia by sea, is the country's most celebrated sport fishing destination. The sea floor around Tabarka drops quickly from a rocky shoreline into deep water (80–150 m within 5 km offshore) over a substrate of coral, gorgonian sea fans, and rocky outcrops that provides ideal habitat for dusky grouper (Epinephelus marginatus). Dusky grouper here average 2–4 kg with fish of 10 kg and above taken regularly by local boatmen fishing whole octopus or large squid on heavy tackle in the 50–80 m zone. The Tabarka archipelago — a small chain of rocky islets offshore — concentrates fish around its rocky bases, and drift fishing between these islets accounts for the largest grouper captures.

## Djerba — Sea Bass, Flounder, and the Lagoon

The island of Djerba in southern Tunisia is connected to the mainland by a Roman-era causeway and surrounded by a vast network of shallow lagoons, tidal channels, and sheltered bays that hold exceptional populations of European sea bass (Dicentrarchus labrax). Sea bass of 1–3 kg patrol the tidal channels during the flooding tide, chasing small mullet and sand eels into the shallows — conditions where lure fishing with soft plastics or small metal jigs produces dramatic surface strikes. The lagoon itself holds flounder and sole that can be taken at night on ragworm bottom rigs from small boats or kayaks. The outer coast of Djerba facing the Gulf of Gabes produces larger fish including meagre and sea bream when fished from the rocky headlands.

## The Gulf of Gabes — Tidal Fishing and Cephalopods

The Gulf of Gabes in southern Tunisia has the most pronounced tidal range on the North African Mediterranean coast — up to 1.8 m at spring tides — creating an extraordinary intertidal zone of sand flats, seagrass beds, and rocky pools that extend several kilometres from the shoreline at low water. Local fishermen work these flats traditionally with hand lines and small nets for octopus, cuttlefish, and sea bream; visiting anglers have found that spinning for sea bass and meagre in the tidal channels as the tide fills is productive, particularly at dawn and dusk in spring and autumn. The squid fishing from the Sfax waterfront in autumn, when Atlantic flying squid enter the Gulf, is comparable to the famous squid nights at Keelung in Taiwan.

## Bizerte — Bass and Bream from North Africa's Northernmost Point

Bizerte, at the northern tip of Tunisia adjacent to the Mediterranean's narrowest channel between the North African and European coastlines, benefits from strong tidal currents and deep, clear water. The rocky coast north of the city between Cap Bizerte and Cap Blanc produces good rock fishing for large sea bream (Sparidae), sea bass, and dentex — a powerful, predatory sparid of 3–6 kg that is one of the Mediterranean's most prized sport fish. Dentex are taken on whole fish bait (small bogue or mackerel) fished on running ledger rigs in 30–60 m, or on large soft-plastic lures worked slowly over rocky ground. The October–December period is the best for dentex as they move inshore to feed on baitfish before winter.

## Cap Bon Peninsula — Bluefin Tuna and Amberjack

The Cap Bon peninsula east of Tunis has a long tradition of tuna fishing; the famous Sicilian-originated mattanza tunny traps, known in Tunisia as madragues, operated along this coast for centuries catching Atlantic bluefin tuna during the spring migration. The mattanza is now largely historic, but the bluefin tuna passage in May and June still brings fish through the waters off Cap Bon, and charter boats from the marina at Hammamet intercept them on trolled lures and live bait. Amberjack (Seriola dumerili) are resident around the offshore banks at 30–60 m throughout summer and are arguably the most sporting fish available to visiting anglers, combining the power of a large trevally with a willingness to strike surface poppers.

## Sidi Daoud and Kelibia — Rock Fishing for Bream

The eastern side of Cap Bon between Kelibia and Sidi Daoud offers some of Tunisia's most productive rock fishing from accessible cliff-top platforms. Large gilt-head sea bream (Sparus aurata), known in Tunisia as orade, reach 1.5–2.5 kg in the sea grass beds close inshore, and fishing with peeled prawn or ragworm on a light ledger is the standard technique. The sea is clear and warm throughout summer, visibility to 10 m in calm conditions, and the underwater landscape of rocky outcrops and seagrass meadows is the same habitat that produces prime bream throughout the Mediterranean. White sea bream (Diplodus sargus) are also prolific and provide constant action when the larger gilt-heads are not feeding.

## Ichkeul Lake — Northern Freshwater Fishing

Lake Ichkeul in northern Tunisia, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and Ramsar wetland 60 km west of Tunis, is a shallow, brackish lake that seasonally connects to the sea and supports an unusual mix of freshwater and estuarine species. European eel, mullet, and sea bass move in and out with seasonal salinity changes. Around the lake margins and in the freshwater streams that feed it from the Mogod mountains, small brown trout and barbel are present in the cooler water above 100 m elevation. Fishing is controlled and a permit is required; the park authority manages the resource carefully. The lake's flamingo populations and spectacular birdlife make any fishing visit here doubly rewarding.

## Ain Draham Reservoirs — Trout and Perch in the Northern Highlands

The Ain Draham area in the Jendouba Governorate of northwestern Tunisia, at elevations between 600 and 1,000 m in the Kroumirie mountains, holds the country's most productive freshwater angling in the form of small reservoirs stocked with rainbow trout and holding natural populations of European perch. The village of Ain Draham itself, surrounded by cork oak forest, sits at 800 m elevation in a landscape that looks nothing like the stereotypical image of Tunisia — cool, green, and damp even in midsummer. The perch in these reservoirs reach 400–600 g and provide accessible spin-fishing for visitors staying in the area. Trout are stocked each spring and the season runs through to early autumn.
