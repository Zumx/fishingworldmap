The golden age of fishing media is not behind us — it is happening right now. Between podcasts recorded beside Scottish chalk streams, YouTube channels filmed on remote Amazon tributaries, and Instagram accounts that document the obsessive detail of fly tying, the collective knowledge available to an angler today would have been unimaginable twenty years ago. The challenge is no longer finding information but knowing which voices to trust, which productions are genuinely instructional, and which communities will sharpen rather than distract you.

## Podcasts for Fly Fishing

The Orvis Fly Fishing Podcast, hosted by Tom Rosenbauer, has been running since 2006 and remains the gold standard for instructional fly fishing audio. Rosenbauer covers everything from casting faults to entomology, gear selection to destination fishing in Alaska, Patagonia, and Iceland. His guests include guides from the Madison River in Montana, biologists tracking Atlantic salmon populations on the River Tweed in Scotland, and commercial tiers of classic dry flies. The archive alone — now over 700 episodes — constitutes a fishing library of remarkable depth.

The Wet Fly Swing Podcast with Dave Jensen focuses more on streamer fishing, nymphing, and trout tactics, with heavy emphasis on technical detail and water-reading. Jensen interviews professional guides and competition fly fishers who bring a precision-oriented approach that differentiates the show from broader lifestyle content. For anglers targeting brown trout in rivers like the Provo in Utah or the Beaverhead in Montana, the tactical episodes are indispensable.

The Trout Bum Diaries, originally a film series and now expanded into podcast territory, captures the wandering ethos of serious fly fishing — a week on the Tongariro in New Zealand followed by a return to Patagonia's Limay River. It leans into the travel and exploration side rather than technique, making it essential listening for anyone planning a destination fishing trip.

## Sea Fishing and Saltwater Media

The Saltwater Experience on YouTube, hosted by guide Tom Rowland out of the Florida Keys, is one of the longest-running saltwater fishing channels on the platform. Rowland's approach is methodical: episodes dissect tarpon presentations on the flats, permit behavior around coral heads, and the subtleties of reading tailing bonefish. The production quality is consistently high and the instructional content translates to coastal fishing anywhere from the Seychelles to the Florida backcountry.

In the UK, the HookLink YouTube channel has built a large following among sea anglers targeting bass, cod, and wrasse from the shore. Hosted by experienced anglers fishing the West Country, Pembrokeshire, and the Outer Hebrides, the channel documents multi-day sessions in honest, unedited form — including the blank sessions — which makes it more realistic and more educational than highlight-reel content.

Fishtec TV covers European sea fishing broadly, with episodes from the Costa Blanca, the Portuguese Algarve, and the Gulf of Cadiz. For anglers planning surf or boat sessions in southern Europe, the location-specific content includes practical information on permits, tide patterns, and the best-producing rigs for local species.

## Coarse and Match Fishing Media

Angling Times, published in the UK since 1953, remains the most authoritative print voice in coarse fishing and is now a fully digital operation with a substantial video library. Their match fishing coverage — the Premier league of fishing in England — includes footage from national championships on venues like Barston Lake in Warwickshire and Tunnel Barn Farm in the Midlands, with commentary from top-ranked anglers who explain their peg selection and feeding strategies in real time.

The Rig Building channel on YouTube is exactly what it sounds like: meticulous, unspectacular, and genuinely useful. Episodes cover everything from a simple running leger for barbel on the River Trent to a complex Method feeder rig for F1 carps on commercial venues. There is no narrative or showmanship, just close-up footage and concise explanation, which makes it the kind of resource you return to again and again when setting up for a new venue.

Carpology Magazine, and its associated digital content, covers carp fishing with the rigour that the bass fishing community in North America devotes to tournament angling. It documents big fish captures from famous venues like Redmire Pool in Herefordshire, Lac du Der in France, and the Lower Pines in Surrey, while also covering emergent gear technology and bait science in a way that takes the sport seriously as a discipline.

## Podcasts for Bass and Predator Fishing

The Bass365 Podcast covers European sea bass fishing with guests who include professional shore guides, boat captains fishing the Channel Islands, and conservation biologists working on bass stock recovery in the Irish Sea. The balance between practical fishing advice and environmental context is handled better here than in almost any other fishing podcast.

Major League Fishing's podcast network is the largest in North American freshwater, covering tournament bass fishing from the Bassmaster Elite Series and Fishing League Worldwide circuits. Individual episodes with anglers like Kevin VanDam on cranking tactics or Aaron Martens on finesse techniques give access to elite-level thinking that has few equivalents in any sport. The production is polished and the content is deep.

## YouTube and Online Communities

The two most substantive online fishing communities in the English-speaking world are Reddit's r/flyfishing and the Fishing Forum UK's carp and sea fishing boards. Both have active populations of experienced anglers who provide genuine, detailed advice on everything from identifying river insects to troubleshooting casting problems to recommending local guides. The quality of advice varies, but the top-rated threads in both communities are consistently excellent.

In Europe, the German-language community around Angeln-Hessen and the French forum Carpe Fishing provide deep regional knowledge that English-language media rarely covers. Both maintain extensive databases of local fishing venues, regulations, and species guides, making them essential references for anyone fishing in central or western Europe.

## Magazines Worth Reading in Print

Sport Fishing Magazine in the US covers offshore, inshore, and freshwater topics with high-quality photography and a consistent editorial standard that has survived the shift to digital. Fly Fisherman Magazine, also American, remains the most technically rigorous print publication for trout and salmon anglers — its annual hatch guide issues, covering the insect emergences of rivers like the Henry's Fork in Idaho and the Battenkill in Vermont, are collector's items among dedicated fly fishers.

Sea Angler in the UK, now a monthly digital-first publication, covers shore and boat fishing across the British Isles and Channel Islands with particular strength in wreck fishing and uptiding. For saltwater anglers based in northern Europe, it is the most useful single resource outside of local club newsletters.
