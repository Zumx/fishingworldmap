At minus fifteen, a frozen lake is anything but silent. The ice booms and cracks as it expands, a sound that terrifies newcomers and reassures veterans — expanding ice is thickening ice. Across the northern world, from Minnesota's Lake Mille Lacs to Finland's Lake Saimaa and the reservoirs of Hokkaido, millions of anglers walk out each winter onto frozen water and drill holes through a lid that can be anywhere from a hand's width to a metre thick. What they find below is often the easiest fishing of the year: cold-slowed fish concentrated in predictable places, and no boat required. This guide covers what a first-season ice angler genuinely needs to know — starting with the ice itself, because nothing else matters until you trust what you are standing on.

## Ice thickness: the numbers that keep you alive

The standard North American guideline, used by Minnesota's Department of Natural Resources among others, runs like this: stay off ice under 4 inches (10 cm); 4 inches of new, clear ice supports a person on foot; 5 to 7 inches (13–18 cm) supports a snowmobile or ATV; 8 to 12 inches a small car; 12 to 15 inches a medium truck. These numbers apply to clear "black" ice only. White or "snow ice", formed from refrozen slush, has roughly half the strength, so double every figure. Ice is never uniform: springs, inlets, narrows between basins, flocks of waterfowl and submerged vegetation all thin it locally. Early and late season, check thickness every 50 metres or so with a spud bar or by drilling test holes. River ice is a different game entirely — current eats it from below — and many experienced lake anglers simply refuse to fish moving water through ice.

## Safety kit: cheap insurance you wear

Two items cost little and have saved countless lives. Ice picks — two spiked handles joined by a cord worn around the neck — let you claw yourself back onto slick ice if you go through; bare hands alone often cannot grip wet ice. A throw rope of 15–20 metres lets you rescue a companion without approaching the broken edge. Add ice cleats for your boots, because falls on bare ice cause more injuries each season than breakthroughs. Modern flotation bib-and-jacket suits from brands like Striker and Clam double as warm outerwear and life preservers. Fish with a partner when you can, tell someone your plan, and keep your phone in an inside pocket where the battery stays warm. If you do break through: stay calm, turn back toward the ice you walked in on (it held you once), kick horizontal, claw forward, and roll — do not stand — until you are well clear.

## The hole: augers and what to drill with

A hand auger in 6-inch (15 cm) diameter costs little, weighs around 3 kg and drills early-season ice in under a minute; classic models from Mora of Sweden have been doing this for decades. Once ice passes 40 cm, hand drilling becomes a workout, and most regulars move to an electric auger — lithium-powered units like the ION or a drill-powered auger bit handle 60 holes on a charge. An 8-inch (20 cm) hole is the all-round choice: big enough for pike and lake trout, small enough to drill fast. Add a skimmer scoop to clear slush, and remember the cardinal rule of mobility: the anglers who catch fish on hard water are the ones who drill ten holes and fish them all, not the ones who sit on one hole all day.

## Rods, line and the electronics revolution

Ice rods are short — 60 to 90 cm — because you fish straight below your feet. A light 70 cm rod with a sensitive tip handles perch, crappie and small trout; a medium-heavy rod manages pike, walleye and burbot. Spool with 2–4 lb monofilament or a thin braid with a fluorocarbon leader; cold water is clear water, and fish inspect baits for long seconds before committing. The single biggest upgrade in modern ice fishing is a sonar flasher or live-imaging unit: a Vexilar flasher or a forward-facing sonar shows your lure and any fish approaching it in real time. Watching a perch rise to your jig and tip its nose to the bait turns ice fishing from blind waiting into a hunting game — and teaches you more about fish behaviour in one weekend than a season of open-water casting.

## Jigging cadence: the rhythm that triggers bites

Most hard-water fish are caught on small jigs — tungsten jigs of 3–7 mm tipped with maggots or plastics for panfish, jigging spoons and rattle baits like the Jigging Rap for walleye and trout. Cadence matters more than lure choice. A reliable sequence: drop to the bottom, lift sharply 20–30 cm to raise a puff of silt and draw attention, then hold dead still. Follow with tiny quivers — millimetre-scale shakes of the rod tip — and pause again. The pause is when fish bite, almost without exception. If a fish appears on sonar and hesitates, slowly raising the lure away from it often provokes a strike; lowering it into the fish's face almost never does. Cold fish are deliberate. Downsize, slow down, and let the silence between movements do the work.

## Finding fish under a frozen lid

Fish concentrate in winter, and the calendar tells you where. At first ice, perch, walleye and pike roam shallow weed flats of 2–4 metres, feeding hard where the last green vegetation holds baitfish. By midwinter, as weeds die and oxygen levels drop in the shallows, fish slide to deeper structure: points, humps, and the soft-bottom basins of 8–15 metres where bloodworm larvae feed huge schools of crappie and perch. Late ice reverses the journey — fish stage near inflowing creeks and shallow bays as meltwater brings oxygen and the spawn approaches. Lake maps and contour apps matter as much in winter as in summer; drill your line of holes across a depth change, from 3 metres down to 10, and let the fish tell you which contour they are using that day.

## Comfort, shelters and making a day of it

Misery ends ice fishing careers faster than poor catches. A flip-over shelter — a sled with a fold-over tent canopy — sets up in thirty seconds and blocks the wind that does the actual freezing. Inside, a small portable propane heater brings the air to glove-free temperatures; always crack a vent for carbon monoxide. Dress in layers of wool and synthetics, never cotton, and bring twice the food and hot drink you think you need. In much of Scandinavia and Canada, the social side is the point: families spread out over a bay, sausages on a portable grill, kids skating between the holes. Plan a comfortable day rather than a heroic one, and you will still be fishing hard water in your seventies.

## Rules and licences on hard water

Ice fishing is regulated like any other fishing, and a few rules are winter-specific. Many jurisdictions limit the number of lines — Minnesota allows two per angler on ice, Sweden typically allows multiple angeldon (tip-up style sets) for pike, while some European waters restrict you to one rod. Shelters left overnight often need the owner's name displayed, and removal deadlines in late winter are strictly enforced before thaw. Check closed seasons too: some trout and char waters close in winter to protect spawners, while others — like many Norwegian and Swedish mountain lakes — are at their legal best through the ice. Buy the local licence, read the species rules, and carry a tape measure for slot limits.
