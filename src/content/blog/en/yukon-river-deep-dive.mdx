The Yukon River begins its life as a series of glacial streams draining the Coast Mountains of northern British Columbia, gathering strength through Tagish Lake and Lake Laberge before spilling north and west across Alaska to the Bering Sea. By the time it reaches the coast, it has covered roughly 3,190 kilometres — enough river to fill most continents. For the salmon that return each summer to spawn in the system's furthest tributaries, the journey is one of the most extraordinary feats of endurance in the natural world. For the Indigenous peoples who have depended on this run for ten thousand years, the fish are not merely food — they are the animating force of an entire civilisation.

## The Journey Begins: Bering Sea to the River Mouth

Chinook salmon that will ultimately spawn in the Yukon's headwaters enter the river at a biological peak. They have spent several years in the North Pacific building fat reserves that will fuel every beat of their tail across thirty-two hundred kilometres without a single meal. At the river's mouth near Emmonak, Alaska, counting weirs and sonar equipment maintained by the Alaska Department of Fish and Game record the passage of millions of fish each summer. The June chinook run — historically the largest and heaviest — produces fish of ten to fifteen kilograms on average, with occasional fish above twenty kilograms. By late July, the chum salmon arrive in numbers that can dwarf the chinook run, providing the bulk of the subsistence harvest for Alaska Native communities.

## Indigenous Fisheries: Ten Thousand Years at the River

The Yup'ik, Athabascan, and Gwich'in peoples have fished the Yukon and its tributaries for at least ten thousand years, as evidenced by the fish camp archaeology along the lower river. Traditional methods — fish wheels, dipnets, and gill nets suspended from wooden frames — are still legal and widely used in the subsistence fishery above the commercial zone. Fish wheels are particularly fascinating: built entirely from driftwood and positioned in the river's current, they turn continuously, scooping fish into a live box with no electrical power required. During the peak summer run, a single fish wheel positioned in productive water can harvest hundreds of salmon per day, which are then split and air-dried on wooden racks in the characteristic style of Yukon fish camps. The dried fish — called simply "dried salmon" in most communities — is the staple protein that carries villages through the nine months of sub-zero winters.

## The Fortymile and Stewart Rivers: Historic Klondike Fishing

The tributaries draining into the Yukon around the Yukon-Alaska border are among the river's most historically rich fishing grounds. The Fortymile River, a gold rush waterway explored in 1886, holds some of the largest chinook salmon in the system — fish that have swum six hundred kilometres from the Alaska border are well-rested and still heavy with roe. The Stewart River below Mayo was the site of one of the Yukon Territory's first commercial fisheries, supplying salmon to the gold rush camps. Both rivers today are within the Yukon Territory's catch-and-release sport fishery for chinook, which operates under strict daily bag limits designed to protect the depleted fall run.

## Yukon Flats: Chum and Coho in the Interior

Where the Yukon spreads across the vast Yukon Flats National Wildlife Refuge — a mosaic of marshes and oxbow lakes the size of South Carolina — the river loses its urgency and becomes a braided network of channels. This zone is critical chum salmon habitat; the warm, shallow channels warm quickly in the Arctic summer and accelerate the eggs' development in the gravel. The chum salmon of the Yukon — sometimes called dog salmon, a reference to their historical importance as sled dog food — reach weights of five to eight kilograms and provide the backbone of the subsistence economy for villages from Circle to Emmonak. Sport fishing for chum and coho in the Flats is uncrowded and genuinely wild; fly-out access from Fairbanks is the standard approach for visiting anglers.

## The Pelly and White Rivers: Trophy Chinook Country

Two of the Yukon's major right-bank tributaries — the Pelly, joining at Fort Selkirk, and the White, laden with glacial flour that turns the main Yukon grey at their confluence — produce some of the system's finest sport fishing. The Pelly River above Pelly Crossing is designated as a catch-and-release chinook fishery in the summer months; fish that have swum over eight hundred kilometres from the Bering Sea are still in good condition and fight with the raw power of their ocean muscle memory. The White River drainage, fed by Klutlan Glacier and marked by its opaque, white-grey water, carries smaller numbers of chinook but also holds grayling and lake trout in its clearer tributaries, providing multi-species fishing in one of the most remote watersheds in North America.

## Grayling and Whitefish: The Year-Round Fishery

Not every Yukon River angler is chasing salmon. Arctic grayling are present throughout the system in astounding numbers, from the clear creeks of the Tintina Trench near Dawson City to the sloughs of the lower river in Alaska. Grayling are arguably the most democratic fish in the north — willing to take a dry fly, a small spinner, or a bead on almost any day of the open water season. The Klondike River below Dawson City is a particularly celebrated grayling fishery accessible by road. Inconnu — also called sheefish — are the Yukon's most exotic quarry: a large, silvery whitefish that can exceed fifteen kilograms and attacks lures with the ferocity of a pike. The Porcupine River confluence near Fort Yukon is the classic inconnu ground, best fished in August when the fish congregate before their fall migration.

## The Salmon's Destination: The Headwaters

The chinook that survive the entire journey reach their natal gravels in tributaries of the Teslin River, the upper Yukon near Whitehorse, and the remote creeks of the lakes system above. A chinook that arrives in Tagish Lake has swum more than three thousand kilometres. By this point the fish has turned from silver to olive-bronze, its body beginning to break down as it approaches spawning. The eggs it carries — two thousand to ten thousand, depending on the female's size — will incubate through the Yukon winter under a metre of ice, emerging as fingerlings in May. The cycle of a Yukon River chinook, from egg to ocean to this final upstream push, spans four to seven years and encompasses a geography that most humans will never traverse.

## Conservation and the Future of the Run

The Yukon River chinook run has declined dramatically since the 1990s, a collapse that has forced progressively stricter limits on both subsistence and sport fisheries. The causes are multiple: warming ocean temperatures in the Bering Sea, changes in prey availability, bycatch in the Bering Sea pollock trawl fishery, and altered river conditions caused by permafrost thaw. The Yukon River Drainage Fisheries Association — a body representing Indigenous fishing communities across Alaska — has worked with the Alaska Department of Fish and Game and Canadian First Nations to implement emergency closures in years when the run falls below escapement goals. The story of the Yukon run is, ultimately, a story about what the world loses when a glacier calves and an ocean warms.
