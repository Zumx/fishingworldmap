Volta Lake stretches across the interior of Ghana like an inland sea, 8,502 square kilometres of brown water covering drowned river valleys and submerged forests. Created when the Akosombo Dam was completed in 1965, it is among the world's largest man-made lakes by surface area and holds fish populations of astonishing diversity and size. Nile perch (Lates niloticus) of 40 to 60 kg patrol the deeper channels where the old Volta River bed runs beneath the surface. Tilapia schools — predominantly Oreochromis niloticus — reach densities that blanket the echo sounder screen in the shallower bays. To the northwest, the Black Volta and White Volta rivers deliver African tigerfish (Hydrocynus forskahlii) in rocky gorge sections before they join the main lake. And out on the Gulf of Guinea, Ghana's 537 km of Atlantic coastline hides offshore seamounts where Atlantic sailfish and wahoo congregate between November and March.

## Lake Volta: Nile Perch Country

The main body of Volta Lake — especially the section between Kpando and Kete-Krachi — offers the country's best Nile perch fishing. Depth here ranges from 18 to 55 m along old river channels, and perch concentrate at the channel edges where prey fish stack. Large swimbaits, umbrella rigs, and deep-running crankbaits fished at 8 to 15 m depth on slow trolling passes produce the best results. Local pirogue guides based at Kpando and at the fishing town of Dzemeni know the exact channel contours. The best season runs April through October, when warming water temperatures accelerate the metabolism of large perch. Fish of 60 kg have been recorded by sport anglers near the Dente Fetish sanctuary islands off Kete-Krachi.

## Akosombo Dam Tailwater

The tailwater immediately below Akosombo Dam on the Volta River provides fast, oxygenated current unusual for lowland Ghana. This section — approximately 8 km of river between the dam face and Akosombo town — concentrates African tigerfish (Hydrocynus forskahlii), mudfish (Clarias gariepinus), and various labeo species in the aerated water. Tigerfish here average 1.5 to 3 kg with occasional fish to 5 kg. Spinning with small metal spoons retrieved upstream is the most effective method. The best fishing is during the dry season from December to March, when the river is lower and clearer.

## The Black Volta, Bui National Park

The Black Volta flows through the dramatic gorge of Bui National Park in the Brong-Ahafo Region before entering Bui Reservoir. The gorge section, accessible on foot from Bui, holds African tigerfish and the larger tigerfish relative Hydrocynus vittatus in its rocky plunge pools and riffles. Fishing inside the national park requires permission from the Bui National Park Authority, but the small entrance permit is worthwhile: the fish here have seen virtually no sport fishing pressure. The gorge section is best fished at the end of the dry season in March and April when water is low and clear.

## Bui Reservoir

Downstream of the gorge, Bui Reservoir — completed in 2013 — has created a secondary impoundment on the Black Volta. The new lake is still ecologically developing, but Nile perch populations have established themselves and are showing rapid growth. Local fishers around Bui town report Nile perch to 20 kg, and tilapia fishing in the shallow bays is prolific. The reservoir is less explored than Volta Lake and represents an emerging sport fishery.

## White Volta River, Northern Ghana

The White Volta drains the dry savannah zone of northern Ghana before turning south toward Lake Volta. In the dry season, particularly around Paga near the Burkina Faso border and along the stretch through Bolgatanga, the river concentrates African tigerfish, Nile tilapia, and the African carp Labeo senegalensis in its remaining deep pools. Fishing these desert-margin pools with lures in December and January, when heat and low water push fish to the deepest sections, is a uniquely intense experience.

## Ankobra River

The Ankobra River flows through the Western Region's gold-mining country to the sea near Axim. Despite its tannin-stained waters — running red-brown with laterite — it holds good populations of black-barred tigerfish in the mid-reach sections around Bogoso and Prestea. The Ankobra estuary near Axim, where it opens to the Atlantic through a mangrove system, offers snook-like African barramundi, snapper, and seasonal tarpon from March to May.

## Pra River and Coastal Lagoons

The Pra River, draining much of the Ashanti Region, passes through gold-country forest before emptying near Shama on the coast. The tidal reach below Shama holds large African snapper (Lutjanus species), juvenile tarpon, and the ever-present African threadfin. A series of coastal lagoons — including Benya Lagoon near Komenda and Esiama Lagoon — provide sheltered brackish water fishing for mullet, snapper, and occasional barracuda. These lagoons are traditional fishing grounds for Fante fishing communities.

## Elmina and the Cape Coast

Elmina, site of Ghana's oldest European fort, has a working fishing harbour and access to productive nearshore reef systems. The rocky headlands around Cape Coast hold grouper, barracuda, and large scorpionfish in 10 to 20 m of water. Shore casting from the rocks below Elmina Castle produces big barracuda on metal spoons. A few kilometres offshore, the water deepens to 40 m over hard-bottom reef that holds large dentex and red snapper, accessible from local fishing boats during the October to February calm season.

## Tema Offshore: Wahoo and Yellowfin Tuna

Tema, Ghana's main commercial port east of Accra, is the base for the country's most accessible offshore fishing. The continental shelf drops steeply 30 nautical miles south of Tema to the 200-metre contour, and this edge runs through productive upwelling water during the harmattan season. Wahoo averaging 15 to 25 kg run along this edge from October to February. Yellowfin tuna of 20 to 50 kg are present virtually year-round, with the major run peaking in January and February. Atlantic sailfish also appear along this offshore zone, particularly between November and February.

## Gulf of Guinea: Atlantic Sailfish

The outer continental shelf of Ghana — the area roughly between 50 and 120 nautical miles south of Accra — is little-fished by international sport anglers yet holds excellent populations of Atlantic sailfish (Istiophorus albicans). Local guides who know the seamount features at coordinates roughly 4°N, 2°W report regular encounters from November through January, when the cold Benguela-adjacent upwelling brings baitfish to the surface. Charter operations out of Tema have begun targeting these fish with dedicated sailfish tackle in recent years.
