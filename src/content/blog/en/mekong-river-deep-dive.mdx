At Si Phan Don in southern Laos, the Mekong splits into a maze of channels around four thousand islands, and at dawn the Khone Phapheng falls — the widest waterfall complex in the world — thunder loud enough to feel in your chest. Somewhere below, fishermen still set bamboo traps the way their grandfathers did. The Mekong runs roughly 4,300 km from the Tibetan Plateau through China, Myanmar, Laos, Thailand, Cambodia and Vietnam, and it is the most productive inland fishery on Earth: around two million tonnes of wild fish a year, feeding tens of millions of people. For the travelling angler it is a paradox — a river bursting with fish where actually wetting a line takes local knowledge, realistic expectations and respect for the people whose dinner swims past your lure.

## A river of superlatives — and giants

The Mekong basin holds over 1,100 fish species, second only to the Amazon. Its icons are outsized: the Mekong giant catfish (Pangasianodon gigas), which can exceed 3 m and 300 kilos and is now critically endangered; the giant freshwater stingray, confirmed in 2022 in Cambodia at 300 kilos — the largest freshwater fish ever reliably weighed; the giant barb, Cambodia's national fish; and the hard-fighting giant snakehead found throughout the system. Wild giant catfish are protected across the basin, and targeting them in the river is both illegal and pointless — your honest chance at one comes in Thailand's stocked lakes, which we cover below. The realistic river quarry is different: snakehead, catfish species like the redtail-like pla sawai, featherbacks, and the acrobatic jungle perch of the tributaries.

## Where you can actually fish: Thailand

Thailand offers the easiest legal entry point. Foreigners need no general freshwater licence for rod-and-line fishing in most public waters, and the country has built an entire industry of managed fishing lakes. Bungsamran Lake in Bangkok is the classic: Mekong giant catfish of 20 to 100 kilos hooked from bamboo platforms, with rods, guides and bait included. Further out, Gillhams Fishing Resorts in Krabi and the huge Jurassic Mountain Resort near Hua Hin stock arapaima, giant barb and Mekong catfish at record sizes. For the river itself, the stretch around Chiang Khong and Chiang Saen in the Golden Triangle — where Thailand, Laos and Myanmar meet — lets you hire long-tail boats through guesthouses and fish confluences for snakehead and smaller catfish with local handlines or your own lure gear.

## Laos: Si Phan Don and the slow river

Laos is the Mekong at its most atmospheric. In the Si Phan Don (Four Thousand Islands) region near the Cambodian border, guesthouses on Don Det and Don Khon arrange mornings with subsistence fishermen — you join the checking of traps and gill nets rather than casting yourself, and it is one of the great river-culture experiences in Asia. Around Luang Prabang, where the Nam Ou and Nam Khan meet the Mekong beneath gilded temples, operators run cast-and-cruise trips for snakehead and Asian redtail catfish. Rules are informal outside protected Fish Conservation Zones — village-managed no-take pools that have measurably rebuilt stocks since the 1990s. Always ask before fishing near a village; those zones are marked, sacred and enforced.

## Cambodia: the Tonlé Sap heartbeat

Cambodia's Tonlé Sap lake is the Mekong's flood-pulse engine: each wet season the Tonlé Sap river reverses direction and the lake swells from roughly 2,500 to over 12,000 square kilometres. The floating villages of Kampong Phluk and Chong Khneas near Siem Reap show industrial-scale subsistence fishing — the dai net fishery on the Tonlé Sap river historically took thousands of tonnes of trey riel, the small carp that becomes prahok fish paste. Sport fishing here is embryonic; a few Phnom Penh-based guides run trips on the Mekong and Bassac confluences for catfish and snakehead. Treat Cambodia as a culture-first destination with a rod in the bag, not a numbers trip.

## Vietnam: the delta's nine dragons

In Vietnam the river fans out into the Cuu Long — the Nine Dragons — of the Mekong Delta. Can Tho's floating market of Cai Rang, the orchard canals of Ben Tre and the catfish farms around Chau Doc show a river utterly domesticated: Vietnam's pangasius aquaculture industry exports over a million tonnes a year. Anglers can join night trips for catfish on delta canals, fish for giant gourami and snakehead in orchard ponds, or take a homestay in Vinh Long where cast nets come out at dusk. It is intimate, muddy, mangrove-warm fishing — closer to farming than hunting, and fascinating for exactly that reason.

## Tactics and tackle for the realistic Mekong

For the stocked Thai lakes, heavy gear is supplied, but bring your own braid in 80 lb class if you are particular. For travelling light on the river: a 4-piece spinning rod in the 20-40 g class, a 4000-size reel, 30 lb braid, and a box of soft plastics, spinnerbaits and shallow cranks covers snakehead and river predators. Snakehead fishing peaks around structure at dawn — weed edges, drowned timber, canal mouths — with noisy surface lures drawing explosive strikes. The monsoon calendar rules everything: November to May is the dry season with low, clear, fishable water; June to October the river rises by 10 m or more in places and most fishing stops. Plan dry-season, and check water releases from upstream dams, which increasingly scramble the natural rhythm.

## A fishery under pressure

Honesty matters here: the Mekong is in trouble. Eleven mainstream dams in China and two in Laos (Xayaburi and Don Sahong) have disrupted the sediment and fish migrations that built the delta; the giant catfish population has fallen by an estimated 90 percent in two decades. Travelling anglers can be part of the good story — fishing the pay lakes that fund conservation breeding, hiring village guides at fair rates, releasing river fish, and never buying wild giant-fish products in markets. The Mekong River Commission and WWF both publish basin reports worth reading before you go; an informed visitor asks better questions and spends money in better places.

## Building the trip

A workable three-week route: fly into Bangkok and warm up with a day at Bungsamran; head north to Chiang Saen and the Golden Triangle reaches; cross to Luang Prabang for the Nam Ou confluences; continue south through Vientiane to Si Phan Don; cross into Cambodia for the Tonlé Sap and Angkor; finish in the Vietnamese delta with a Can Tho homestay. Visas are straightforward for most nationalities (e-visas for Laos, Cambodia and Vietnam), the dry season from November to March is the comfortable window, and river travel itself — slow boats from Huay Xai to Luang Prabang especially — is half the point.
