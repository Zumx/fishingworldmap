Spearfishing is one of the oldest forms of fishing practised by humans, and also one of the most demanding. It requires the physical ability to hold your breath and dive to meaningful depths — typically 5 to 15 metres for coastal species, though experienced freedivers push to 20 metres and beyond — combined with the underwater navigation skills to approach fish without alarming them, the identification knowledge to target legal species, and the equipment competency to make a clean, humane shot. When all these elements come together, spearfishing delivers an intimacy with the marine environment that no other form of angling can match.

The sport is practised most extensively in the Mediterranean, particularly in France, Italy, Greece, Croatia, and Spain, where centuries of tradition have built a rich culture of underwater hunting. The Pacific Islands, South Africa, Australia, New Zealand, and Hawaii all have thriving spearfishing communities. In the Caribbean, targeting species such as invasive lionfish has given spearfishing an active conservation dimension; derbies in the Cayman Islands, Barbados, and across the Bahamas now pay bounties for lionfish taken by spearfishers.

## Essential Equipment

A spearfishing setup is simpler than most fishing tackle but demands higher quality at each component. The primary tool is either a pole spear — a simple rigid shaft propelled by a rubber sling — or a speargun, which stores energy in either rubber bands (band gun) or compressed air (pneumatic gun) and shoots a shaft called a spear or a bolt.

For beginners, a wooden or carbon-fibre rail gun of 75–90 cm with a single or double rubber band is ideal. It is accurate at short ranges (1.5 to 3 metres), forgiving to load, and creates enough resistance that beginners learn patience before shooting. Trigger mechanisms vary; a simple two-piece trigger is easier to maintain than a multi-component roller assembly, which becomes relevant when cleaning gear in salt water.

A wetsuit is essential for thermal protection and buoyancy control. A 3mm open-cell suit is standard in Mediterranean summer conditions (water temperatures above 22°C); a 5–7mm suit is appropriate for Atlantic coasts and winter diving. Fins designed for freediving are long (60–80 cm blade), soft, and efficient for the slow, relaxed kicks that conserve oxygen; avoid stiff SCUBA fins. A low-volume mask reduces the air needed to equalise and keeps the diver streamlined.

## Breath-Hold Training and Safety

Static apnea — holding your breath without movement — is the foundation of all freediving and spearfishing. A healthy adult with no training can typically hold their breath for 90 seconds without significant discomfort. With three months of consistent practice combining relaxation breathing and CO2 tolerance exercises, most people achieve three minutes or more.

Critically: never practise breath-holding in water alone. Shallow water blackout — caused by hyperventilation before a dive, which delays the CO2 response that triggers the urge to breathe — kills spearfishers every year in swimming pools and in the sea. A training partner who is watching you continuously is not optional; it is the single rule that separates safe spearfishing from a fatality statistic.

Training a Freediving 1 or Freediving 2 course through PADI, SSI, or AIDA with a certified instructor provides the physiological education, equalisation techniques, and rescue protocols that allow you to progress safely. Costs range from approximately 250 to 400 euros for a two-day course and the certification lasts a lifetime.

## Equalisation

The most common technical barrier for new spearfishers is ear equalisation. The pressure change from the surface to six metres — approximately 0.6 atmospheres — compresses the air in the middle ear, causing pain and risking eardrum injury if the diver descends without equalising. The Valsalva manoeuvre (pinching the nose and blowing gently against it) equalises most people efficiently to 10–15 metres. The Frenzel technique — isolating the throat and tongue to push air through the Eustachian tubes — is more efficient and recommended for deeper dives.

Equalise early and often: every metre below three metres on the descent. Descent speed should be comfortable and unhurried; forcing a descent against pain risks barotrauma, which can end a spearfishing career with a ruptured eardrum.

## Fish Approach Techniques

Fish are acutely sensitive to movement and contrast, but many species habituate to a motionless or slowly descending diver remarkably quickly. The technique known as passive hunting involves descending to depth, positioning on the seabed, and remaining completely still while fish investigate. Snapper, grouper, and bream routinely approach a stationary diver on rocky reefs within 45–60 seconds, presenting shots that would be impossible with any surface-based technique.

Active hunting involves a slow, diagonal approach from above and ahead of the fish, using the fin flutter from a slow kick to suggest a non-threatening presence. Approach on the fish's level and from the side rather than from above, which triggers a flight response in most reef species. On sandy bottoms, hovering at mid-water allows flounder and flathead to hold position while the diver descends to shooting range.

## Species Identification and Legal Framework

Spearfishing is heavily regulated in most countries. In France, it is prohibited within 150 metres of any swimming beach and in all marine reserves; minimum sizes apply to all target species and the spearfisher must be able to identify what they are shooting before firing. In Australia, size limits and bag limits apply, and many species — including all sharks, rays, and marine mammals — are protected regardless of circumstance.

Colour identification underwater differs significantly from surface identification because red light is absorbed first. A red fish at 10 metres appears grey-brown; a yellow-banded species retains its markings. Investing in a species identification card printed on waterproof film and worn on the wrist is a practical safeguard and demonstrates the standard of care expected by fisheries authorities.

## Shot Placement and Recovery

A clean shot through the brain stem or the lateral line directly above the pectoral fin ensures a humane, instant kill. A gut shot or a fin shot results in a struggling fish that must be killed by hand, is stressful for the animal, and often results in the fish tearing free from the spear. Always position yourself for a broadside shot at close range — under 2 metres for beginners — before pulling the trigger.

After the shot, maintain tension on the spear line while the fish runs, then hand-over-hand the fish toward you before securing it on a stringer. Do not retrieve the fish into a bag or tether it near your body in areas with significant shark activity; a struggling fish sends a vibration signal that can attract sharks quickly.
