Ireland's reputation among European anglers rests on a particular kind of abundance: free-rising wild brown trout on limestone loughs and spring-fed rivers, dense head counts of coarse fish in the midland waterways, and wild Atlantic salmon systems that have maintained runs despite enormous pressure elsewhere. The island's geology — vast sheets of Carboniferous limestone in the central lowlands — creates the alkaline water conditions that support extraordinary invertebrate life and in turn the fat, fast-growing trout and bream populations that make so many Irish waters exceptional. A day-ticket culture and generally accessible fisheries mean Ireland is also more affordable for visiting anglers than comparable British destinations.

## The River Moy — Ireland's Premier Salmon River

The Moy system in County Mayo and County Roscommon is Ireland's most productive salmon river and one of Europe's most accessible. The river flows north from the Ox Mountains through Foxford and Ballina to Killala Bay, and the main channel plus major tributaries — the Manulla, the Robe, and the Deel — together hold salmon from February through October. The Ridge Pool at Ballina, where the tidal influence meets the main river, is one of the most famous salmon pools in Europe; in a good run year, anglers can cast from the bank and watch dozens of salmon rolling in front of them. Beats on the Moy are managed by the Western Regional Fisheries Board and several are bookable on short-notice day-ticket basis, making this unusually democratic for a premium salmon fishery.

## Lough Conn and Lough Cullin — Wild Trout Heaven

Lough Conn in County Mayo covers nearly 1,600 hectares of limestone water, producing wild brown trout of exceptional quality. Trout averaging 0.7–1.2 kg with fish to 3 kg taken every season from drifting traditional wet flies — Mayfly, Green Peter, and Bibio — in the classic Irish style. The annual mayfly hatch from mid-May to early June draws anglers from across Europe. The connected Lough Cullin is smaller but equally productive on its day. Both loughs are managed as wild fisheries; there is no stocking, and the quality of the fish — deep-bodied, hard-fighting, wild Mayo trout — reflects years of careful management and the fertility of the limestone water.

## Lough Corrib — Europe's Largest Brown Trout Lake

Lough Corrib straddles Counties Galway and Mayo at almost 18,000 hectares, making it the Republic's largest lake and the second largest on the island. The great annual hatch of the green drake mayfly (*Ephemera danica*) in late May brings the lake's wild brown trout to the surface in spectacular fashion; this event — known locally as the mayfly — attracts anglers from across Europe for roughly two weeks of extraordinary dry-fly and sedge-fishing. The western limestone shores around Greenfields, Cornamona, and Doorus hold the most productive trout water; the eastern shoreline towards Galway city holds more coarse fish including pike, perch, and rudd in great numbers.

## The River Drowes — Free Fishing for Salmon

The Drowes in County Leitrim has the distinction of being Ireland's only major salmon river that is completely free to fish without a permit. The river flows from Lough Melvin — itself an internationally important site for the unique gillaroo trout subspecies — to the Atlantic at Tullaghan. Salmon run from January 1 and the early months can be productive if water levels allow. The short 5-kilometre length means it is heavily fished, but the free access makes it an iconic destination and a genuine opportunity for anglers on limited budgets to experience an Irish wild salmon river at no cost beyond the national rod licence.

## Lough Mask — Coarse Fish and Wild Trout

Lying just south of Lough Conn, Lough Mask in County Mayo sits on the limestone plateau and offers superb wild brown trout fishing in a lake of 9,300 hectares. The water is famously clear — visibility to 4 metres is normal — and during the mayfly it is possible to sight-fish to individual rising fish. Dapping with a natural daddy-longlegs or live mayfly is a traditional technique here; dry fly on a calm day in June can be extraordinary. The lake also holds large pike — fish over 10 kg are caught each winter — and a growing recreational coarse fishing scene around the southern end near Ballinrobe.

## Lough Ree — The Shannon Lake

Lough Ree, the second largest of the Shannon lakes, sits at the heart of the Irish Midlands between Counties Longford, Westmeath, and Roscommon. As a coarse fishing destination it is outstanding: bream to 3 kg, tench to 2.5 kg in the reedy southern bays, and a thriving roach and hybrids population. The northern end towards Lanesborough is the primary bream and hybrid zone; the more sheltered western shoreline yields good tench into July. Pike inhabit the lake in numbers and specimens over 12 kg are caught annually. The Shannon navigation channels offer productive bank fishing and several accessible pegs for wheelchair users, making Ree one of the most inclusive fisheries in Ireland.

## The Blackwater — Cork's Salmon River

The Munster Blackwater rises in County Kerry and flows east through Fermoy and Lismore before turning south to enter the sea at Youghal. It is sometimes called the Irish Rhine for its broad, stately character and for the limestone cliff banks through which it flows. Salmon runs occur from February (if water temperatures allow) through October, with the autumn run in September and October often bringing the most and largest fish. The Lismore beats, managed through the Lismore Estate, and the Fermoy Salmon Anglers Association beats downstream are among the most sought-after. Brown trout fishing on the Blackwater and its tributaries — the Allow, the Awbeg, and the Bride — is excellent on lighter gear from April through June.

## The River Bann — Coarse Fishing in the North

The Lower Bann in County Antrim and County Derry connects Lough Neagh — at 385 km² the largest lake in the British Isles — to the sea near Coleraine. It is one of the finest coarse fishing rivers in northern Ireland, with large catches of roach, bream, and perch taken in competitions and pleasure sessions. The tidal stretch below Coleraine holds sea trout and occasional salmon in summer. Lough Neagh itself holds pollan (a native whitefish), eel in the traditional eel fishery, and bream populations that produce some of Ireland's biggest competition catches.

## Lough Melvin — A Living Laboratory

Lough Melvin on the Leitrim–Fermanagh border is scientifically fascinating as the habitat of three distinct forms of wild brown trout found nowhere else in the world: the gillaroo, the sonaghen, and the ferox. These eco-types share the lake but occupy distinct niches — the gillaroo feeds on snails on the stony bottom, the sonaghen on insects at the surface, the ferox on smaller fish in the deeps. The lake is managed as a special scientific area and holds exceptional conservation value, but fishing is open under national rod licence and the quality of even a standard day's fly fishing for the smaller gillaroo and sonaghen is remarkable.

## The Erriff River — Wild Mayo Salmon

The Erriff in County Mayo flows from the Partry Mountains through the spectacular Aasleagh Falls gorge to Killary Harbour, Ireland's only true fjord. It is a classic spate river — it fishes best after rain when the water drops from brown and peaty to gin-clear — and it holds both salmon and sea trout from June onward. Aasleagh Lodge manages a number of beats and offers week-long packages. The pool below Aasleagh Falls itself can be spectacular after a September flood, with grilse stacking in the deep water waiting for the next rise to push them upstream.
