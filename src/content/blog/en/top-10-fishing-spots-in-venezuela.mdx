Venezuela does not shout about its fishing, but anglers who make the journey return with stories that follow them for life. The Orinoco basin alone drains roughly 880,000 square kilometres of tropical lowland, nurturing populations of peacock bass that have made the country a pilgrimage destination for freshwater enthusiasts since the 1980s. Then there is Los Roques Archipelago, rising from impossibly blue water 166 kilometres north of Caracas, where shallow flats hold bonefish, permit, and barracuda in numbers rarely matched in the Caribbean. Add to that the open Caribbean off the northern coast and the possibilities multiply into marlin, sailfish, and yellowfin tuna. Venezuela's fishing geography spans ecosystems that most anglers spend a career trying to sample separately.

## Orinoco River — Peacock Bass Country

The Orinoco mainstream and its tributaries, particularly the Ventuari and Cinaruco rivers in Amazonas and Apure states, produce peacock bass — locally called pavón — that regularly exceed 8 kg. The Cinaruco is widely considered the finest peacock bass river in the world, and it flows through Cinaruco-Capanaparo National Park, which gives it a degree of protection that neighbouring waters lack. Anglers typically fly into Puerto Ayacucho and transfer to floating camps anchored in mid-river. The technique is either topwater plugs at dawn, when the fish are herding baitfish in the shallows, or slow-rolled jigs along submerged rock ledges during midday heat. The season runs roughly November through April, when water levels drop and concentrate fish in defined channels and lagoons.

## Manirote and the Upper Tributaries

The village of Manirote on the Ventuari River serves as a staging point for expeditions deeper into the Amazonian interior. Here the rivers narrow, the forest canopy closes overhead, and the peacock bass become even more aggressive, competing fiercely for territory in clear blackwater pools. The Ventuari also holds payara — the saber-toothed vampire fish — and large temensis peacock bass, the largest of the three peacock species. Local guides navigate by memory across a labyrinth of channels and oxbow lakes that no map fully captures. Catches in the 3–7 kg range are routine; fish exceeding 10 kg are possible and occasionally landed.

## Los Roques Archipelago — Flats Fishing Paradise

Los Roques is Venezuela's premier saltwater destination and one of the Caribbean's finest flats fisheries. The archipelago consists of more than 350 islands and cays surrounding a vast protected lagoon of knee-deep water with a white sand and turtle-grass bottom — perfect habitat for bonefish. Average specimens run 1–2 kg, but fish over 3 kg are encountered regularly. Permit also cross these flats, though they are harder to coax. The deeper channels between cays hold barracuda, snapper, and jacks. Gran Roque is the only inhabited island and provides the logistical base, with a handful of posadas offering guided wading and poling skiff packages. The archipelago is a National Park, so catch-and-release is the norm.

## Bahía de Moreno and the Caribbean Offshore

Off the northern coast near the town of Morrocoy and further east toward Sucre state, the Caribbean drops quickly to blue water. This offshore zone produces blue marlin, white marlin, and sailfish from May through August when the currents push warm, current-rich water against the coast. Trolling with ballyhoo rigged on circle hooks behind a spread of teaser birds is the standard approach. Yellowfin tuna schools also run here, sometimes in mixed schools with skipjack. Charter boats operate out of Puerto La Cruz and Cumaná, though the fleet is smaller than in neighbouring Trinidad and requires advance booking.

## Truchicultura Santo Domingo — Mountain Trout

High in the Andes of Mérida state, the Río Santo Domingo and its cold tributaries drain through páramo grassland at elevations above 1,500 metres. Rainbow trout were introduced decades ago and have established self-sustaining populations in the upper reaches. The Truchicultura Santo Domingo facility sits in a valley near Barinas road and marks the most accessible point for trout angling in the country. The river is narrow and technical, demanding careful wading and upstream nymph or dry-fly presentation. The fish are not large by alpine standards — most run 25–40 cm — but the scenery, with cloud forest pressing in from both sides, makes the experience unique in South America.

## Caura River — Remote Catfish and Peacock

The Caura River in Bolívar state is one of the last large, essentially undamified tributaries of the Orinoco. Its clear, brownish water supports both peacock bass and very large red-tailed catfish, some exceeding 50 kg. The river is accessed via the town of Maripa and requires multi-day boat travel to reach productive stretches. The remoteness is the appeal: no other anglers, no traffic, enormous trees overhanging the current, and fish that have seen few lures. Capybara graze on the banks, river dolphins surface in the calm pools, and at night the sky is free of light pollution. This is as wild as river fishing gets.

## Lake Guri — Tucunaré in a Reservoir

Represa de Guri in Bolívar state is one of the world's largest reservoirs by surface area, covering nearly 4,250 square kilometres. Created by the Guri Dam in 1978, the reservoir has developed a strong population of tucunaré (speckled peacock bass) that take jerkbaits and swimbaits along the flooded timber edges. The fish average 1–2 kg here, smaller than Orinoco fish, but they are abundant and accessible from the town of Ciudad Guayana with much less logistical effort than a remote river camp. Boat launches at Represa village and the facility at Caruachi allow easy access.

## San Félix and the Caroní Confluence

Where the Caroní River enters the Orinoco near San Félix and Ciudad Guayana, the mixing of clear and turbid water creates a productive fishing zone. Large wels-like catfish called bagre liso run into double figures in weight and are targeted with heavy bottom rigs. Surface lure fishing also produces payara in the stronger current near the confluence. This spot is accessible by road, making it one of the easiest places in Venezuela to reach quality river fishing without flying into a remote camp.

## Margarita Island — Inshore Action

The island of Margarita in the Caribbean is Venezuela's tourist hub, but its surrounding waters hold a surprisingly good inshore fishery. Snappers, grouper, and jack crevalle gather around the rocky headlands and submerged reefs that ring the island. Dawn fishing on light spinning tackle from small pangas produces reliable sport. The waters south of the island, between Margarita and the Araya Peninsula, hold schools of Spanish mackerel and kingfish during cooler months. For anglers who want to combine a beach holiday with some fishing action, Margarita is the obvious base.

## Delta del Orinoco — Tarpon and Snook

The Orinoco Delta, spreading across roughly 25,000 square kilometres of channels, mangroves, and tidal flats, holds populations of tarpon up to 40 kg that move in and out with the tides. The Warao indigenous communities guide visiting anglers through this labyrinth of waterways. Snook also lurk beneath the mangrove roots, taking surface lures worked along the edges at first light. The delta receives relatively little fishing pressure, largely because of its remoteness — getting there requires a flight to Tucupita and then a long boat journey. Those willing to make the effort find fish that have rarely encountered an artificial lure.
