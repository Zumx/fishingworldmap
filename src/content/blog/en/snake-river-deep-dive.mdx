The Snake River drains nearly 109,000 square miles of the northern Rockies and Columbia Plateau, tumbling from its headwaters near Yellowstone down through potato country, then splitting Idaho's western border in Hells Canyon — the deepest gorge on the North American continent. Along its 1,078-mile course it offers a range of fishing experiences so varied that anglers can spend entire seasons moving from one stretch to the next without ever repeating themselves. Steelhead charge through the lower canyon in autumn, while crystalline spring creeks feed the famous Fall River reach and tailwater rainbows sip dry flies through the September evenings.

## Hells Canyon Steelhead

Hells Canyon is 8,000 feet deep in places, carved by the Snake through basalt that predates the dinosaurs, and it forms one of the last great anadromous corridors in the lower 48 states. Wild B-run steelhead — thick, deep-shouldered fish averaging 10 to 14 pounds — push through from August into November, running past the Hells Canyon Dam complex through fish ladders and reaching holding water near Pittsburgh Landing and the Kirkwood Ranch reach. Most anglers float the canyon on guided drift boats or rafts, fishing swung flies on sink-tip lines in the 65-degree tailouts where the current slows above boulders. The classic pattern is a Purple Peril or a Skunk on a size 4 hook, fished across and down in long, deliberate arcs. Because the canyon is roadless for much of its length, fishing pressure is lighter than on the Clearwater to the north, and encounters with six-fish mornings are still possible.

## Trophy Trout Waters Below Palisades Dam

The 25-mile stretch of Snake River immediately downstream from Palisades Dam in eastern Idaho carries the formal designation of Trophy Trout Water under Idaho Fish and Game regulations, meaning catch-and-release only for trout and a single-barbless-hook artificial-only rule. The dam's deep-water releases keep summer temperatures between 48°F and 56°F even when surrounding valleys push 95°F, creating year-round habitat that supports densities of wild Snake River fine-spotted cutthroat trout and rainbow trout exceeding 3,000 fish per mile in the best sections. The run from Irwin to Lorenzo is particularly celebrated: a braided channel of back-channels and gravel bars that demands careful wading and precise casting. August brings prolific pale morning dun hatches between 10 a.m. and noon, followed by afternoon caddis and sporadic evening spinner falls. The average fish is 14 to 18 inches, but browns pushing 24 inches cruise the deep slots below the Highway 26 bridge.

## Dry-Fly Fishing on the Fall River Sections

The Henry's Fork of the Snake River, joining the main river near Rexburg, is often discussed as a separate fishery, but the Snake's own Fall River tributaries — and the main-stem reaches from Lorenzo downstream through Idaho Falls — carry the same dry-fly culture. September is the pilgrimage month: spawning rainbows push into the cleaner, shallower riffles, elk hair caddis patterns work from first light, and the Trico spinners that fall on calm mornings between 7 and 9 a.m. reward anglers who can present a size 22 spinner on a 6X tippet with zero drag. The water is gin-clear, so polarized glasses are non-negotiable and stalking skills count as much as casting accuracy. Local outfitters in Swan Valley and Ririe stock the specific local patterns — the Parachute PMD, the CDC Caddis, and the RS2 nymph for subsurface work on slow days.

## The Milner to Twin Falls Reach

Between Milner Dam and Shoshone Falls, the Snake flows through a lava-rock canyon that most Idaho anglers ignore in favour of the more famous tailwaters. That neglect is the attraction: smallmouth bass averaging 12 to 15 inches concentrate in every deep seam and eddy from June through September, and the occasional large brown trout lurks in the cold tributary confluences. The canyon walls shelter the water from afternoon wind, making it a pleasant float on the hottest days, and the black-rock scenery is dramatic. A 5-weight fly rod with a small popper or a size 10 Clouser Minnow covers most situations, though in-line spinners and soft plastics take more fish when the smallmouth are holding deep in mid-summer.

## Licenses, Regulations, and Access

Idaho requires a fishing license for all anglers 14 and older. Annual resident licenses cost around $34 and non-resident licenses around $100, with steelhead-specific tags required in addition. Regulations differ markedly by section: the Trophy Trout Water below Palisades is catch-and-release artificial only; most of the rest of the river allows bait for non-salmonid species. Hells Canyon requires awareness of Oregon and Idaho boundary rules — the main channel boundary means some sections fall under Oregon Fish and Wildlife jurisdiction. Public access is excellent via the Hells Canyon National Recreation Area boat ramps at Hells Canyon Creek and Pittsburg Landing, and the Bureau of Land Management maintains launch sites along the Twin Falls reach. Swan Valley Outfitters and South Fork Lodge are the two most prominent guided operations on the Palisades reach.

## When to Go

Spring runoff on the upper Snake peaks in late May and early June, muddying the water and raising levels. The Trophy Trout Water fishes best from mid-July through September, with the dry-fly action peaking in August. Steelhead fishing in Hells Canyon runs August through November, with October traditionally offering the best combination of fish numbers and comfortable temperatures. Winter on the lower canyon can be mild and the bass fishing surprisingly active on warm afternoons.

## Gear and Preparation

For the Trophy Trout Water, a 9-foot 5-weight with a long, tapered leader of at least 12 feet is standard. Leaders tapering to 5X cover most PMD and caddis situations; step to 6X or 7X for Trico fishing. Steelhead anglers fishing Hells Canyon typically use a 13-foot Spey rod rated for a 7- or 8-weight Skagit head and a variety of sink tips from T-8 to T-14 depending on flow. Wading staffs are essential in the canyon's slippery basalt, and felt-soled wading boots are still popular, though cleated rubber soles have improved and avoid the biosecurity concerns that led several states to ban felt. The canyon is remote enough that a personal flotation device, first-aid kit, and satellite communicator are sensible additions to any kit.
