Germany rarely tops lists of European fishing destinations, and that is precisely why it should. Between the Baltic lagoons of the north and the alpine lakes of Bavaria lie some of the continent's best zander rivers, a pike fishery that draws Scandinavian guides south, and a carp scene with waters that have produced fish over 30 kg. The catch — in every sense — is the famously thorough German permit system. This guide covers the ten waters most worth your time, then explains exactly how to fish them legally as a visitor.

## 1. The Bodden lagoons of Rügen

The shallow, brackish lagoons between the island of Rügen and the Mecklenburg mainland are arguably Europe's premier big-pike fishery. Herring spawn here in spring, and pike well over a metre follow them into water often less than two metres deep. The towns of Stralsund and Schaprode are the main guiding bases; March to April and October to December are prime. Boat traffic, seasonal protected zones and a strong catch-and-release ethic among guides keep the fishery remarkably healthy.

## 2. Fehmarn and the Baltic sea-trout coast

Schleswig-Holstein's Baltic shoreline is wading country. Around the island of Fehmarn and along the steep coast near Weissenhäuser Strand, sea trout cruise the leopard-spotted mixed bottoms within casting range from February to May and again in autumn. A pair of chest waders, a 9-foot spinning rod and a box of slim coastal lures are all the equipment required. The same marks produce garfish in May, when the water hits about 12 °C.

## 3. The Elbe at Hamburg

Few European cities offer urban fishing like Hamburg. The tidal Elbe through the port is dense with zander that hold around harbour basins, pilings and rip-rap; autumn nights produce fish over 80 cm to anglers fishing rubber shads close to structure. Upstream, the river turns rural — around the village of Drage, the campsite at Stover Strand is a popular base for boat anglers chasing zander, asp and the Elbe's increasingly large wels catfish.

## 4. The Rhine in North Rhine-Westphalia

The Rhine between Cologne, Düsseldorf and Duisburg is a hard, current-swept river that rewards anglers who learn its groyne fields. Each stone groyne creates a back-eddy holding zander, perch and barbel, while asp smash baitfish on the current seams in summer. Wels catfish over two metres are caught every season now. Day permits for long Rhine stretches are inexpensive and widely available online — one of the easiest big-river entries in the country.

## 5. The Müritz and the Mecklenburg Lake District

The Müritz is Germany's largest lake lying entirely within the country — about 117 square kilometres of clear, weedy water connected to a thousand smaller lakes. It is classic pike and perch country, with big shoals of bream and tench for coarse anglers and quiet bays full of carp. Mecklenburg-Vorpommern sweetens the deal with a tourist fishing licence available without any exam, which makes the region the simplest legal entry point for foreign anglers in all of Germany.

## 6. The Edersee, Hesse

This 27-kilometre reservoir behind the Eder dam — famously breached in 1943 — is one of central Germany's best predator waters. Deep, clear and surrounded by the wooded hills of Kellerwald-Edersee National Park, it holds large pike, zander, big perch and a respected stock of lake trout and whitefish. Summer drawdowns expose old village foundations and make autumn bank fishing unusually productive as fish concentrate in the remaining depths.

## 7. Lake Constance (Bodensee)

Shared with Austria and Switzerland, the Bodensee is an alpine inland sea: 63 km long and 251 m deep. Its whitefish (Felchen) are the classic local quarry, fished with delicate nymph rigs at depth, while char, lake trout, pike and outsized perch round out the menu. The Obersee requires its own international permit, a pleasingly simple document compared with most German paperwork. Spring perch fishing around the harbours of Lindau and Konstanz is superb.

## 8. The Chiemsee and Bavaria's alpine rivers

The Chiemsee — the Bavarian Sea — delivers whitefish, pike and big carp against a backdrop of the Chiemgau Alps. Within an hour you can also fish genuine alpine rivers: the upper Isar's turquoise gravel runs, the Loisach, and the Ammer gorge hold brown trout, grayling and char. Bavarian river beats are mostly club-run with limited day tickets, so reserve ahead; the trout season generally runs from March or April to the end of September.

## 9. The Moselle valley

The Moselle winds 240 German kilometres between steep slate vineyards from Perl on the French border to Koblenz, impounded into a chain of slow, deep pools. It is an underrated zander and catfish river: the lock approaches and harbour mouths hold predators year-round, and the canalised stretches are full of bream and roach for feeder anglers. Local clubs along the river — many villages have their own — sell guest tickets through tourist offices and tackle shops.

## 10. The Forellensee culture

No honest German top ten can ignore the country's hundreds of commercial trout lakes and club ponds — the Forellenhof and Angelpark waters found near almost every town. Purists tease them, but they serve a real purpose: many sell day tickets to anglers who hold no German licence at all (rules vary by state), they stock large rainbow trout, sturgeon and carp, and they are the easiest way to put a bent rod in a child's hands on a German holiday weekend.

## How the German permit system works

Germany regulates fishing at state level, and the standard requirement is a Fischereischein — a lifetime state fishing certificate earned by passing an exam — plus a water-specific permit (Erlaubnisschein) for each lake or river. Visitors have three realistic routes: states like Mecklenburg-Vorpommern, Schleswig-Holstein and Thuringia sell time-limited tourist licences with no exam; some states recognise foreign national licences; and commercial lakes often need only their own day ticket. Always carry photo ID, buy the water permit before fishing, and note that closed seasons and minimum sizes differ from state to state.
