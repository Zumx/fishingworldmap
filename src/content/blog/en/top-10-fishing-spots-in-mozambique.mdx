Mozambique's coastline stretches 2,700 kilometres along the Indian Ocean from the Tanzanian border to the Mozambique Channel meeting point with South Africa. This immense coastline encompasses everything from coral atolls and offshore blue-water canyons to mangrove delta systems and freshwater river backwaters. The country is one of the least-fished marine environments in the world, not because of scarcity but because of its remoteness and, historically, the limited development of tourist infrastructure. That is changing. The Bazaruto Archipelago is now firmly established as one of Southern Africa's premier gamefish destinations, Pemba in the north draws sailfish chasers, and the Zambezi River delta offers a genuinely unique combination of tropical freshwater and coastal game fishing. Mozambique rewards the adventurous angler with exceptional fishing and minimal crowds.

## The Bazaruto Archipelago — Blue Marlin and Giant Trevally

The Bazaruto Archipelago, a marine national park comprising five islands including Bazaruto, Benguerra, and Magaruque, sits 25 kilometres off the coast of Vilanculos in Inhambane Province. The park protects the last significant population of dugong in the Western Indian Ocean, and its marine waters are extraordinarily productive. The offshore grounds beyond the protected inner reef host blue marlin (regularly over 300 kg), sailfish, yellowfin tuna, and giant trevally (GT). The GT fishing on the outer reef drop-offs, where 40 kg fish slam surface poppers with explosive force, is rated among the finest in Africa. Several lodge operators on Bazaruto and Benguerra Islands run dedicated sportfishing programs with modern centre-console or sportfisher boats.

## Pemba Bay — Sailfish Capital of Northern Mozambique

Pemba, the capital of Cabo Delgado Province in northern Mozambique, sits on one of the world's largest natural bays. The offshore grounds north of Pemba Bay, where the continental shelf drops steeply into the Mozambique Channel, produce sailfish from October to April at rates that rival any destination in the Indian Ocean. The clarity and temperature of the Mozambique Channel current — typically 25 to 28°C during peak season — concentrate baitfish along the shelf edge, and sailfish numbers can be extraordinary. Charter operations from Pemba waterfront clubs cater to the growing number of visiting anglers. Marlin, wahoo, and dorado are common by-catch.

## The Zambezi Delta — Tigerfish and Back-Country Fishing

The Zambezi River builds one of Africa's great deltas as it approaches the Indian Ocean south of Quelimane. The delta's network of channels, lagoons, and backwater systems — extending over 12,000 square kilometres — holds tigerfish (Hydrocynus vittatus), goliath tigerfish (Hydrocynus goliath), vundu catfish, and large Nile perch-type predators. This is wild, remote, tidal fishing in a landscape of matted papyrus and palm-fringed channels, reached from Chinde or by boat from Quelimane. Tides complicate navigation but also activate feeding; the best fishing occurs on incoming tides when predators follow baitfish from the estuary into the channels. Cais Manarte is a riverside landing and staging point in the delta region.

## Vilanculos — Inshore and Nearshore Fishing

Vilanculos, the gateway town to the Bazaruto Archipelago on the mainland coast, is an excellent base for both offshore expeditions and productive inshore fishing. The shallow flats between the mainland and the archipelago hold bonefish, permit, and good-sized barracuda. The bonefish fishery on the Mozambique flats is largely undeveloped by international standards — fish of 2 to 4 kg are common, and double-figure bonefish (over 4.5 kg) are caught each season by the few fly fishers targeting them. Tanque Psicola is a managed freshwater fishing facility in the broader Vilanculos hinterland area, offering an alternative to marine fishing.

## Quirimbas Archipelago — Remote Northern Reef Fishing

The Quirimbas Archipelago, comprising 32 coral islands and countless reef structures stretching 200 kilometres along the Cabo Delgado coast, is one of the Indian Ocean's most biodiverse coral reef environments. Sport fishing here targets GT on the outer reef faces, coral-reef-associated species like red snapper and emperor fish, and offshore species where the reef gives way to open water. Access to most of the archipelago is by dhow or small boat from Pemba or from the handful of high-end lodges on Matemo and Ibo islands. The density of pristine reef structure means that fish live exactly where structure-targeting anglers cast, making success rates unusually high.

## The Pungwe River Estuary — Near Beira

The Pungwe River flows through central Mozambique and meets the Indian Ocean at Beira, the country's second-largest city and its principal port. The estuarine zone and the tidal flats around Beira hold barramundi-type giant perch, mangrove red snapper, giant queenfish, and occasional tarpon (Megalops cyprinoides). The port's proximity means boat access is straightforward. Fishing from the Beira foreshore on the incoming tide, particularly at night, produces large barramundi-type fish and snapper. The city itself has basic but improving tourist accommodation as Mozambique's infrastructure develops.

## Lake Cahora Bassa — Tigerfish of the Zambezi

Lake Cahora Bassa, created by the Cahora Bassa Dam on the Zambezi River in Tete Province, is one of Africa's largest reservoirs at 2,660 square kilometres. It is primarily famous for its tigerfish, which grow large and aggressive in the warm, food-rich impoundment. The annual Cahora Bassa Tigerfishing Tournament draws competitors from across southern and eastern Africa. Fish from 3 to 7 kg are typical; fish over 10 kg are caught regularly, and the reservoir holds some of the largest tigerfish available to visiting anglers anywhere in Africa. Songo, the town built to service the dam, has accommodation and boat-hire facilities.

## Tofo Beach — Dive and Fish

Tofo, on the Inhambane coast south of Vilanculos, is known primarily as a manta ray and whale shark diving destination, but its offshore waters are equally rich for game fishing. Sailfish, wahoo, and large dorado are present from September to April. The reef directly off Tofo Point drops steeply to over 40 metres, creating a productive blue-water channel within 5 kilometres of the beach. A small number of fishing operators at Tofo supplement the dominant dive tourism with offshore game-fishing days.

## Xai-Xai — Southern Mozambique Reef Fishing

Xai-Xai (pronounced "Shy-Shy") in Gaza Province sits 200 kilometres north of Maputo and offers excellent reef and nearshore fishing from a town that has been a domestic holiday destination for South Africans since the colonial era. The reef structures off Xai-Xai hold yellowfin tuna, kob (cobia), king mackerel, barracuda, and large queenfish. Rock and surf fishing from Chongoene and the beaches north of town produces kob and bream. Xai-Xai is the most accessible Mozambican fishing destination for visitors from South Africa and can be reached in approximately 3 hours from Maputo.

## Maputo Bay — Urban Fishing and Sailfish

Maputo Bay, fronting the capital city, is large enough — at 1,770 square kilometres — to hold significant gamefish populations including sailfish, kingfish, and large barracuda just 10 to 20 kilometres from the city centre. Dhow fishing from the Maputo waterfront is a centuries-old tradition. Modern sportfishing charters operate from Maputo Yacht Club and target sailfish, Spanish mackerel, and giant trevally on the bay's outer edge and beyond into the Mozambique Channel. The bay also holds enormous prawns — Mozambican prawns are famous throughout southern Africa — and prawn fishing at night is a popular local pursuit.
