Between the Tigris and the Euphrates lies a land with one of the oldest fishing traditions on earth. Mesopotamian anglers were harvesting the rivers' extraordinary bounty long before recorded history, and the fish are still here: the mighty shabbout (Tor grypus), the Mesopotamian barbel, massive catfish, and great shoals of common carp. In the reed-choked marshes of southern Iraq, where the two rivers merge before reaching the Arabian Gulf, a way of life unchanged for millennia persists among the Ma'dan people, who still fish from hand-built reed boats called mashuf. For the adventurous angler, Iraq offers experiences found nowhere else.

## The Tigris: Shabbout and Mesopotamian Barbel

The Tigris enters Iraq from Turkey through the mountains of Kurdistan and flows south for over 1,700 km before joining the Euphrates at Qurna. Its upper reaches, particularly the stretch near Mosul and south to Tikrit, hold the largest concentrations of shabbout. This endemic cyprinid, related to the mahseer of South Asia, is built for powerful river currents — a deep-flanked fish with a small, downturned mouth that feeds on molluscs and plant material. Fish exceeding 15 kg are not uncommon, and specimens to 25 kg have been recorded. The Mesopotamian barbel (Luciobarbus esocinus) shares these same stretches and can grow to 80 cm, offering savage runs on medium-weight spinning tackle.

## Baghdad's City Reaches: Carp and Catfish

In and around Baghdad, the Tigris broadens and slows, and the fishing changes character entirely. Common carp are abundant and grow to exceptional sizes in the warm, nutrient-rich water. Large wels-type catfish are also present, lurking beneath the stone-flagged bridge foundations and in the deeper bends below the city. Traditional Iraqi anglers use long bamboo poles with heavy line and large dough-based baits for carp, a technique refined over centuries. The fish markets along the river, including the famed night market near the old city, reflect just how central the Tigris remains to Baghdad's food culture.

## The Euphrates: Carp in the Central Desert

The Euphrates enters Iraq from Syria and flows through the central-western provinces before swinging south-east through Nasiriyah. Its Iraqi stretch is slower and shallower than the Tigris, and carp dominate the catch throughout. The irrigated wetlands created by the river's many branches and channels support enormous populations of fish. Near Ramadi and Fallujah, local anglers fish from wooden platforms over the river's edge. The Euphrates is particularly productive in the cooler months from October to March, when fish activity increases in the shallower, clearer water.

## The Southern Marshes: Traditional Reed-Boat Carp Fishing

The Iraqi marshes — the Ahwar — near Basra and Nasiriyah were designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2016, and their ecology is as extraordinary as their history. The Central Marsh, the Hammar Marsh, and the Hawizeh Marsh together form one of the world's largest inland delta systems. Among the papyrus and giant reed beds, the Ma'dan community has fished from mashufs for thousands of years. Carp, including very large individuals, are the primary target. The fish are often caught in hand-woven reed traps, by cast net, or on long-lines baited with bread and dates. Visiting anglers who arrange local guide services can experience this tradition firsthand.

## Darbandikhan Lake: Kurdish Highland Reservoir

In the Kurdistan Region of northern Iraq, Darbandikhan Lake sits in the Zagros foothills at around 500 m elevation, created by the Darbandikhan Dam on the Sirwan River. The reservoir is one of Iraq's best sportfishing destinations, holding rainbow trout stocked by the KRG fisheries authority, as well as large common carp and pike-perch. The surrounding mountains provide a dramatic backdrop, and the cooler climate of Iraqi Kurdistan makes fishing here comfortable even in summer.

## Dokan Lake: Trout in the Zagros Mountains

Dokan Lake, formed by the Dokan Dam on the Little Zab River, is another major Kurdish highland reservoir at roughly 700 m above sea level. The cooler, deeper water sustains a trout fishery unusual for Iraq, and the lake is popular with anglers from Sulaymaniyah, 65 km to the south-east. Wild brown trout have colonised some of the inflowing streams. The scenery around Dokan is spectacular — forested limestone ridges and narrow valleys characteristic of the inner Zagros range.

## The Little Zab and Great Zab Rivers

The two Zab tributaries of the Tigris in Iraqi Kurdistan are the most productive river fisheries in the north. The Great Zab descends from Turkey through Duhok province, while the Little Zab enters from Iran east of Sulaymaniyah. Both rivers hold shabbout, Mesopotamian barbel, and several endemic species of Capoeta and Carasobarbus. Spinning and bait fishing from the boulder-strewn banks is the standard approach; the rivers run clearest and most fishable from May through October after the spring snowmelt subsides.

## The Shatt al-Arab: Tidal Estuary Fishing

Near Basra, the Shatt al-Arab is formed by the confluence of the Tigris and Euphrates and flows for approximately 200 km to the Arabian Gulf. The tidal influence brings brackish-water species into the lower reaches, including mullet, sea bass, and occasional sea bream alongside the freshwater catfish and carp of the upper river. Local fishermen at Basra's Abu al-Khasib district fish the estuary from traditional wooden boats, targeting whatever the tide brings in. The fishing is at its most productive during the cooler winter months when the salinity balance shifts.

## Razaza Lake: Western Desert Fishery

Lake Razaza (Habbaniyah Lake's western neighbour) in Anbar province is a large, shallow depression that fluctuates considerably with irrigation demands and rainfall. In good water years it supports large carp populations and attracts significant numbers of migratory waterfowl, which in turn indicate healthy fish stocks. Access from the main Ramadi–Karbala road makes it one of the more accessible remote fisheries in central Iraq.

## Practical Considerations for Visiting Anglers

Security conditions across Iraq vary significantly by region. The Kurdistan Region of Iraq (Erbil, Sulaymaniyah, Duhok) is the most accessible for foreign visitors and offers the highest quality fishing infrastructure. Elsewhere in the country, visiting anglers should consult current travel advisories and arrange trips through established local contacts. Formal fishing licences are issued through Iraq's Ministry of Agriculture, but enforcement varies. The best fishing overall is between October and April, when temperatures are moderate and river levels manageable.
