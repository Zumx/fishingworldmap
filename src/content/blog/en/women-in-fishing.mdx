Walk the docks at any major bass tournament today and you will notice something that would have seemed remarkable twenty years ago: women in tournament bibs, rigging rods alongside the men, discussing drop-shot weights and current breaks with the same casual authority. Participation surveys from the Recreational Boating and Fishing Foundation show that women now account for roughly 36 percent of all anglers in the United States, and similar trends are visible across the United Kingdom, Australia, and Scandinavia. The growth is not a blip — it has been accelerating for over a decade, and the fishing industry has been scrambling, sometimes successfully and sometimes less so, to keep up.

## The Numbers Behind the Surge

Between 2010 and 2024 the number of female fishing licence holders in the US grew by more than four million. In England and Wales, the Angling Trust reported a 45 percent increase in women holding rod licences between 2018 and 2023. Norway's Norges Jeger- og Fiskerforbund logged similar gains, particularly among women aged 25 to 44. These are not marginal shifts — they represent a structural change in who the sport belongs to.

Social media has played a decisive role. Instagram and YouTube gave women anglers a platform before tackle shops or fishing magazines took their presence seriously. Accounts run by anglers like Hannah Barron, Brooke Thomas, and the cast of the Bass Queen show on YouTube collectively reach millions of subscribers. These creators normalised the image of a woman handling a largemouth bass or wading a trout stream without irony or novelty framing, simply showing the experience as what it is: excellent outdoor recreation.

## Competitive Bass Fishing Opens Its Doors

Bass fishing has historically been one of the most male-dominated formats in competitive angling, but the dam has broken. Major League Fishing and Bassmaster have both seen steady increases in female entries at open-level events. In 2022, Krista Meyer became the first woman to win an MLF Bass Pro Tour Stage in outright competition against the male field. Her win at Lake Chickamauga was not celebrated as a curiosity but as a result — she out-fished a field of professional men on a deep-ledge bite using a drop-shot rig and systematic electronics work.

The Women's Bassmaster Tour, running since 1995, has produced some of the sport's most technically complete anglers. Tami Baumgardner, Penny Berryman, and Kim Bain-Moore dominated particular eras of the tour and their tournament records stand up against any professional benchmark. The WBT operates with the same Bassmaster rules as the men's circuit, including live release, and the level of competition at the top is genuinely elite.

## Fly Fishing: Where Women Have Led Longest

If bass fishing is catching up, fly fishing is the discipline where women have been visible and celebrated the longest. Joan Wulff, widely regarded as the finest fly caster of the twentieth century regardless of gender, was setting distance casting records in the 1940s and teaching casting clinics well into her eighties. Her instructional books, particularly Fly Casting Techniques, remain standard reading for guides and serious anglers.

Today, organisations like Casting for Recovery bring together breast cancer survivors for guided fly fishing retreats in waters ranging from Montana's Gallatin River to the chalk streams of Hampshire, England. The therapeutic dimension of fly fishing — the rhythm, the focus, the immersion in moving water — has particular resonance, and the program has served over 12,000 women since its founding in 1996. Guides who work these programs frequently report that participants who had never fished before become some of the most dedicated fly anglers they have encountered.

## Gear Designed for Women, Not Shrunken for Women

For years the tackle industry's approach to female customers amounted to: take the existing product, shrink it slightly, and make it pink. The results were predictably uninspiring — stiff rods with too-small grips, waders that didn't fit through the hips, and vests with chest pockets positioned for male torsos. That approach has largely been abandoned by serious manufacturers.

Simms Fishing Products now produces a full wader and wading boot line built on female-specific lasts developed from foot-shape data rather than scaled male templates. Sage, Orvis, and G. Loomis all offer fly rods calibrated for the lighter average hand size and grip pressure of female casters without compromising action or power. Shimano and Daiwa have introduced baitcasting reels with shallower thumb bars and lower grip diameter suited to smaller hands. These are not cosmetic changes — they improve casting efficiency and reduce fatigue over a long fishing day.

## Female-Led Guide Services and Schools

Some of the most reputable guide operations in North America are now run by women. Mother's Day Creek Angler in Montana, run by guide Sarah Scudder, is booked six weeks in advance for spring runoff season. On the Florida Keys flats, Captain Tammy Thompson has guided permit and bonefish for over fifteen years and counts multiple IGFA records among her clients' achievements. In the UK, guide and instructor Joanne Holbrook runs one of the few chalk stream guiding operations on the Test specifically marketed at new-to-fly anglers, with a course completion rate that competes with any school in the Itchen valley.

These businesses have done something important beyond simply existing: they have made fishing feel less like walking into an exclusive club. Women who might have felt self-conscious asking a male guide basic questions frequently report that female-led trips eliminated that barrier entirely.

## Key Communities and Resources

Several organisations deserve specific mention. Women's Fly Fishing is a nonprofit network with chapters across North America that runs clinics, mentorship programs, and an annual gathering. Orvis has tracked which of its fly fishing schools attract the highest female enrolment and actively tailored course formats in response. The Orvis-endorsed 50/50 On The Water program set an explicit goal of achieving gender parity in their instructional programs — something that has shifted how they advertise and structure course times.

In the sea fishing community, the Lady Angler Fishing Club in Ireland has been organising women-only competitions since 1975 and today has affiliates in eight counties. Internationally, the IGFA maintains separate women's world records in every species and weight category, with current holders spanning disciplines from blue marlin on the open Pacific to brown trout on a Hampshire stream.
