Below the Inga Rapids on the lower Congo River, one of the most powerful river systems on earth compresses itself through a series of canyons and cataracts that no fish can ascend. This hydrological isolation over millions of years has produced an extraordinary fauna — including the goliath tigerfish (*Hydrocynus goliath*), an apex freshwater predator with a body plan unchanged since the Cretaceous. Elsewhere across sub-Saharan Africa, the spotted tigerfish (*Hydrocynus vittatus*) haunts the floodplains of the Zambezi, Okavango, Limpopo, and Rufiji. Both species combine explosive power, sharp intelligence, and teeth that have become the stuff of fishing legend.

## Two Species, Two Worlds

The goliath tigerfish is confined to the Congo River basin. Specimens exceeding 50 kg and 1.5 metres have been recorded, though most rod-caught fish weigh 10–30 kg. Its teeth are proportionally the largest of any African freshwater fish, interlocking like a crocodile's jaw, and its musculature is built for short, explosive ambush bursts in fast water. The spotted tigerfish (also called the tigerfish in anglophone southern Africa) is smaller — most fish run 2–8 kg — but more widely distributed and arguably more aerobatic when hooked, with a habit of throwing its entire body out of the water repeatedly.

## The Zambezi: Africa's Premier Tigerfish River

The Zambezi from the Caprivi Strip in Namibia eastward through Zimbabwe and into Zambia offers the most accessible world-class tigerfish fishing. The stretch around Chobe National Park, where the Chobe River meets the Zambezi floodplain, and the Mana Pools section in Zimbabwe are the most productive. Mana Pools National Park — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — restricts powerboat use and requires all fishing to be on foot or by canoe, creating an entirely different atmosphere from motorised day-trips. October and November, just before the Zambezi floods, are the peak months: tigerfish congregate in the lower pools, feeding aggressively before water temperatures drop and spawning begins.

The Kariba Gorge below Kariba Dam on the Zimbabwe-Zambia border is also renowned. The cold, clear releases from the dam produce ideal conditions for large tigerfish, and the gorge walls create a dramatic backdrop for fishing from 5 to 7 in the morning before temperatures rise.

## Lake Kariba and Cabora Bassa

Lake Kariba (spanning 5,580 square kilometres) and Mozambique's Lake Cahora Bassa (another 2,620 square kilometres) both hold large populations of spotted tigerfish, particularly around submerged timber at depth of 5–15 metres. Tiger trolling — pulling large spoons or diving plugs at 3–5 knots around dead tree lines at dawn — is the most productive lake technique. Kasane in Botswana and the Zimbabwe shore camps near Binga and Tashinga are the standard bases. Both lakes allow powerboat access, and bream fishing can be combined with tigerfish sessions.

## The Okavango and Linyanti Systems

The Okavango Delta in Botswana and the Linyanti River to its north provide exceptional tigerfish fishing in a setting of extraordinary beauty. Papyrus channels, hippo pools, and open floodplains make the Okavango more technically challenging — long, accurate casts are essential to reach fish in papyrus channels without snagging. The Linyanti and Kwando rivers in the Caprivi Strip hold consistently large fish; camps like Nambwa and Savuti fly camp offer dedicated fishing programs from June through October.

## Congo River: The Goliath Frontier

Accessing goliath tigerfish on the Congo remains an expedition-grade undertaking. The area around Kinshasa and the Pool Malebo section hold fish, but the most productive water lies in the Inga Gorge — restricted, logistically complex, and rarely visited by sport fishers. Several specialist operators offer guided expeditions to the lower Congo via Matadi; these are typically 10–14 day trips requiring yellow fever vaccination, DRC visa, and a tolerance for genuine frontier conditions. The reward is the chance to hook a fish of 20–50 kg in some of the most powerful water on earth.

## Tackle for Tigerfish

For spotted tigerfish on the Zambezi, a 7–9 ft medium-heavy spinning rod rated 20–60 g with a 3000–4000 series reel and 20–30 lb braid handles most situations. The preferred lure is a large, brightly coloured spoon or metal jig of 20–40 g in gold, silver, or chartreuse. Multi-section lures are avoided because tigerfish teeth slice through split rings easily; use strong single or treble hooks with welded rings. Wire traces of 15–20 cm in 20–30 lb single-strand or 7×7 wire prevent bite-offs. For the goliath tigerfish, scale up to 10-weight fly gear or a heavy spinning rod rated to 80–100 g, with 50+ lb braid and 60 lb fluorocarbon leaders.

## Best Seasons

The Zambezi peaks from September to November before the rains. The Okavango is best June to September during the dry season. Lake Kariba produces year-round with a peak in cooler months (May–August). Congo expeditions are best attempted May to October (the dry season). Water temperatures above 26 °C push fish deep; below 18 °C they slow down significantly. The ideal window is 20–25 °C with dropping water levels.
