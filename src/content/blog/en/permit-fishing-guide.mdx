The permit has broken more hearts than any other fish in fly fishing. It swims in the same warm, gin-clear water as the bonefish and the tarpon, but where the bonefish is merely nervous and the tarpon is spectacular, the permit is something else entirely — intelligent, suspicious, and utterly unpredictable. A permit that has accepted your fly is so statistically improbable that guides and anglers have celebrated single-digit successes across entire careers. On the flats of Belize, the Florida Keys, and the Yucatán, the permit is not hunted so much as patiently encountered and, very occasionally, persuaded.

## Why the Permit Is So Difficult

The permit's wariness is physiological as much as behavioural. Its eyes are positioned to give broad peripheral vision, and its lateral line senses pressure waves from a fly line landing 6 metres away. It feeds primarily on crabs and shrimp on the bottom, and it will inspect an artificial fly from multiple angles before deciding. On a spring tide flat in Belize in March, a tailing permit may turn and face your crab pattern, hover over it, suck in and then blow it out in under a second — a rejection so fast and complete that it looks like the fish never touched the fly. Guides estimate that even the best permit anglers are refused eight or nine times out of ten on days when fish are actively feeding.

## Where to Find Permit

The most consistent permit fishing in the world is found in four broad areas. The Florida Keys — Islamorada, Marathon, and Key West — hold large permit year-round, with the best conditions from March to June. The atoll systems of Belize, particularly Turneffe Atoll, Lighthouse Reef, and Glover's Reef, provide classic wade and skiff fishing with high densities of tailing fish. The Yucatán coast of Mexico — from the Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve south through Xcalak — is perhaps the premier destination for permit numbers, with experienced guides who specialize exclusively in the species. The Jardines de la Reina archipelago in Cuba also holds outstanding permit, often on deeper edges where they stack in September and October.

## Reading the Flats

Permit occupy a variety of flat types but show preferences. Coral rubble and sand flats adjacent to deeper channels are productive, as permit slide up from depth as the tide rises to tail and root for crabs. Turtle-grass edges are particularly important — crabs shelter in the grass and move onto sandy patches at certain tide stages, and permit follow predictably. The most important skill in permit fishing is reading the fish's attitude from a distance. A tailing permit with its nose down and tail waving slowly is actively feeding — approachable. A cruising permit with dorsal fin at the surface but moving purposefully is moving between spots — it may stop to investigate a well-placed fly but requires a precise lead of 1.5–2.5 metres ahead of its nose. A nervous, erratic permit that keeps changing direction is spooked — leave it alone.

## Fly Patterns

The literature on permit fly patterns is enormous, and the debate is ongoing. The Merkin crab — developed by Del Brown and Craig Mathews — remains the most widely used pattern and has accounted for more permit than any other. Tied on a #4 or #2 hook with rubber legs, a chenille or yarn body, and lead eyes to control sink rate, the Merkin lands softly, kicks as it sinks, and imitates a swimming or fleeing crab convincingly. The Rag Head Crab, the McCrab, and more recent patterns like the Avalon and the Ultra Crab have advocates. Size matters enormously — when permit are tailing on small crabs, a #6 or even #8 pattern on a 1 cm diameter body outfishes a large pattern repeatedly. Colour is secondary: tan, olive, and brown cover most conditions. The fly must land quietly, sink quickly, and be fished with minimal movement after the initial kick.

## Tackle and Leaders

Permit fishing demands a 9- or 10-weight rod capable of delivering a fly accurately in 25 knot winds — conditions common on the exposed Belizean atolls in winter. The line should be a tropical floating or intermediate taper, and the leader is critical: 12 feet of straight 20 lb fluorocarbon is standard on calm days; some guides in Mexico prefer a 9-foot leader with a 16 lb tippet on nervous fish. The reel must have a smooth, reliable drag, as permit run fast and hard — a 5 kg fish will take 60 metres of line on its first run with apparent ease. Landing is almost always by hand, cradling the fish horizontally, and release should be immediate.

## Tides and Conditions

Incoming tides pushing onto shallow flats are prime permit time. The first two hours of a flooding tide bring fish up from channels into the flat proper, where they tail aggressively. Overcast skies with moderate winds (10–18 knots) are ideal — the fish are less spooked than in flat calm and glare, and the chop helps disguise a landing fly line. Midday on a clear, windless day is the most challenging time: flat, mirrored water means every shadow and leader glint is visible to the fish. In Belize, guides prefer 7:00–11:00 am fishing, then a break, then the afternoon flood tide.

## Guide Culture and Ethics

The permit guide culture in Belize, Mexico, and the Florida Keys is deeply invested in the fish. Most operations are 100% catch-and-release, and guides will turn away clients who cannot cast competently. Sessions typically involve hours of poling silently across flats in search of fish, with long periods of inactivity punctuated by rapid-fire casting windows of seconds. The relationship between angler and guide is intensely collaborative — the guide spots fish, reads their attitude, positions the skiff, and calls the cast, while the angler executes under pressure. Tipping a guide generously after a landed permit is the expected protocol, not just courtesy.
