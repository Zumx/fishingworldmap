The take is unlike anything else in freshwater. A tigerfish does not sip or slurp; it runs down your lure at full sprint, hits it with a bony, interlocking jaw full of dagger teeth, and then goes airborne — and more often than not, throws the hook on that first cartwheeling jump. Hydrocynus vittatus, the African tigerfish, is the reason anglers fly halfway around the world to the Zambezi. The river runs 2,574 km from a marshy source in north-western Zambia to the Indian Ocean in Mozambique, passing through or alongside six countries, plunging over Victoria Falls and pooling into the man-made sea of Lake Kariba on the way. Few fishing trips on Earth pair so naturally with elephants at the waterline and lions calling at night.

## Knowing the enemy: the tigerfish

Tigerfish are characins — relatives of the piranha — and everything about them is built for violence: a streamlined, striped body, an orange-and-black tail, and teeth so prominent they remain visible when the mouth is closed. The mouth is almost pure bone, which is why hookup-to-landing ratios hover around one in four even for experts. A good Zambezi fish is 4 to 6 kilos; an exceptional one passes 8; the river record class runs into the mid-teens. (The monstrous goliath tigerfish of 50 kilos belongs to the Congo system, not the Zambezi.) Wire traces are mandatory, hooks must be needle sharp, and the strike is a strip-set or a firm sweep — lift the rod trout-style and you will lose every fish.

## The Upper Zambezi and Barotseland

The Upper Zambezi above Victoria Falls is the connoisseur's water: a broad, sandy, papyrus-lined river winding across the Barotse floodplain of western Zambia. This is the homeland of the Lozi people, whose king, the Litunga, still leads the Kuomboka ceremony each year — a flotilla moving the royal household to high ground as the flood rises. Lodges around Mongu, Lukulu and the Ngonye Falls area fish drifting baits and spinners along channel edges, and fly anglers swing big Clouser and brush-fly patterns on sink tips. August to December, as the flood drains and baitfish concentrate, is prime. The same water produces African pike, yellowfish and huge vundu catfish.

## Victoria Falls and the Batoka Gorge

At Mosi-oa-Tunya — the Smoke that Thunders — the river falls 108 m over a basalt lip 1,708 m wide, and everything about the fishery changes. The towns of Livingstone (Zambia) and Victoria Falls (Zimbabwe) are the most accessible bases on the entire river, with international airports and dozens of operators running half-day and full-day tiger trips above the falls. Below, the Batoka Gorge rapids hold a wilder, harder game best left to specialist guides. This is the logical hub for a first Zambezi trip: fish at dawn, stand in the spray of the falls at noon, sunset cruise among hippos in the evening.

## Lake Kariba: the inland sea

Downstream, the Kariba Dam (completed 1959) backs the river up into a lake 223 km long and up to 40 km wide — at the time of filling, the largest man-made reservoir on Earth by volume. Drowned mopane forests left petrified trees standing in the shallows, and tigerfish hunt sardine-like kapenta among them at dawn. Kariba is houseboat country: groups charter multi-deck boats out of Kariba town or Binga, fishing tender boats among the tree lines of the Sanyati and Ume river mouths. Every October the Kariba Invitational Tiger Fish Tournament — one of Africa's biggest freshwater competitions — takes over the lake. Matusadona National Park lines the southern shore, so elephant, buffalo and lion sightings come free with the fishing.

## Mana Pools and the Lower Zambezi

Below Kariba the river enters its most beautiful reach: a broad valley flanked by Mana Pools National Park (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) in Zimbabwe and Lower Zambezi National Park in Zambia. Here the fishing is done from skiffs drifting past pods of hippo, with ancient winterthorn forests behind and the Zambezi Escarpment walling the horizon. Camps around Chirundu and the Chongwe confluence offer the classic combination: morning tiger sessions, afternoon game drives or canoe safaris. September and October are peak — hot, dry, with the river low and clear and wildlife stacked along the water. Vundu catfish here exceed 30 kilos, and conventional anglers soak chunk baits for them in the deep holes.

## Tactics, tackle and the strike problem

Three approaches dominate. Trolled or cast lures: Rapala-style deep divers, copper spoons and spinners, on 20 lb braid and 30-40 lb wire trace. Drifted baits: strips of fillet fished in the current seams — deadly but hard on fish, so many camps restrict it. And fly: an 8 or 9-weight with a 250-350 grain sink tip, short heavy leader, wire bite tippet, and flashy 1/0 to 3/0 baitfish patterns stripped fast. Whatever the method, the cardinal rule is sustained pressure — a tiger given slack for half a second is gone. Barbless trebles or single hooks improve release survival and, counterintuitively, often improve landing rates. Catch-and-release is standard practice at every serious operation on the river.

## Seasons, logistics and safety

The Zambezi fishes year-round, but the great window is the late dry season: August to early December, with September-October the sweet spot for both tigers and game viewing. The rains from late December to March lift and colour the water. Getting there is straightforward: international flights to Livingstone or Victoria Falls for the upper river, Lusaka for the Lower Zambezi, Harare or Kariba for the lake. The KAZA univisa, when available, covers both Zambia and Zimbabwe — useful since the best plan often straddles the border. Respect the non-fish hazards: hippos kill more people in Africa than any other large animal, crocodiles take the wading question off the table entirely, and this is malaria country — prophylaxis and long sleeves at dusk are part of the kit list.

## Pairing rod and safari

The Zambezi's great luxury is that the fishing and the safari are the same trip. A proven two-week shape: three days at Victoria Falls for the falls and the upper-river tigers; four days on a Kariba houseboat; five days at a Lower Zambezi or Mana Pools camp mixing skiff fishing with game drives and canoe trails; and a final buffer day in Lusaka or Harare. Travelling anglers who want the full Barotseland floodplain experience instead can fly Lusaka-Mongu and build the trip around the upper river. Either way, you come home with two stories braided together — the fish and the wild country they swim through.
