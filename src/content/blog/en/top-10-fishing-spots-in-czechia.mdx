Czech nymphing conquered the competition fly-fishing world in the late 1980s, when Czech teams began short-lining heavy nymphs through pocket water and out-fishing everyone in sight. The method was born on home rivers — the Vltava, the Otava, the Dyje — and those rivers remain the best place to understand it. But Czechia is more than tight-line trout tactics: South Bohemia holds a Renaissance-era network of carp ponds unmatched anywhere in Europe, and the lowland Labe and Morava produce wels catfish, asp and barbel. Permits run through the Czech Anglers Union's well-organised revír system, with day tickets available to visitors. Here are the ten waters to know.

## 1. The Vltava tailwater below Lipno

Below the Lipno dam, the Vltava emerges cold and stable, and the stretch through Vyšší Brod down towards Rožmberk nad Vltavou is the country's signature trophy trout and grayling tailwater. Consistent 8–10 °C water grows brown trout well over 60 cm, and the river's long, even riffles are a textbook classroom for Czech-nymph technique. Canoe traffic is heavy on summer weekends, so locals fish early mornings and the September–October window, when grayling fishing peaks under the turning beech forests of the Šumava foothills.

## 2. Lipno Reservoir — the South Bohemian Sea

Upstream of that tailwater sits Lipno itself, Czechia's largest reservoir at about 4,870 hectares, ringed by the Šumava National Park. Pike and zander are the headline acts, with perch schools that erupt on whitefish fry in late summer and carp cruising the shallow Černá bay. Frymburk and Horní Planá are the main bases, with boat rental everywhere. In hard winters Lipno freezes, and ice fishing for perch — uncommon elsewhere in the country — draws a dedicated crowd.

## 3. The Otava through the Šumava foothills

Born from the confluence of the Vydra and Křemelná, the gold-bearing Otava is many Czech anglers' favourite grayling river. The classic beats around Sušice — Annín, Čepice, Žichovice, Rejštejn and the water below Hartmanice — are managed as numbered revíry (Otava 6, 7 and 8 in local parlance), each with campsites and footbridge access along the river. Wild browns dominate the upper, boulder-strewn sections near Rejštejn, while grayling thicken downstream of Sušice. May caddis evenings and crisp October mornings are the times to be standing in it.

## 4. The South Bohemian carp ponds of Třeboň

The fishpond landscape around Třeboň is a UNESCO-listed cultural monument in all but name: roughly 460 ponds dug from the 16th century onward, crowned by Rožmberk — at 489 hectares the largest fishpond in the country, completed in 1584 under pond-builder Jakub Krčín. Many ponds remain commercial carp farms, but several, along with adjacent sand pits, are open to sport fishing for carp into the 20-kilo class, tench and pike. The autumn výlov, the ceremonial net harvest, is worth planning a trip around even without a rod.

## 5. The Ohře — grayling under volcanic hills

Flowing from Germany past Cheb, Sokolov and Karlovy Vary, the Ohře is western Bohemia's premier mixed water: grayling and brown trout in the swift sections, barbel and chub in the deeper runs beneath the Doupov hills. The tailwater character below the Skalka and Kadaň dams keeps temperatures trout-friendly through summer. The Karlovy Vary stretch lets you swing nymphs within sight of spa colonnades — and the city's anglers take asp off the weirs in town.

## 6–7. The Morava, the Dyje and the Nové Mlýny reservoirs

Moravia answers Bohemia with two fine rivers. The upper Morava around Litovel braids through a protected floodplain forest full of chub, ide and barbel, while the Dyje below the Vranov dam is a celebrated trout and grayling tailwater winding beneath the castles of Podyjí National Park. Where the Dyje flattens out, the three Nové Mlýny reservoirs hold southern Moravia's biggest predators: wels catfish over two metres, zander, and asp that smash bleak along the dam riprap. Vine-covered hills and Pálava's white cliffs make the backdrop absurdly photogenic.

## 8. The Labe and Prague's Vltava

Czechia's lowland rivers reward anglers who think beyond trout. The Labe (Elbe) from Pardubice down past Ústí nad Labem is serious water for wels catfish, carp and especially asp — the runs below the big navigation weirs, such as Střekov, are famous for surface-smashing asp at dawn. In the capital itself, the Vltava through Prague holds surprising numbers of zander, perch and barbel; locals drift lures along the embankment walls below Charles Bridge on winter mornings, with the castle lit up above them.
