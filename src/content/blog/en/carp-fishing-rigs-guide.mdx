Carp fishing rig development over the last forty years represents one of the most intensive applied engineering exercises in all of angling. The genesis was a simple insight, articulated by Kevin Maddocks and Len Middleton in the late 1970s: if the hook is separated from the bait by a short length of line, the carp cannot eject it as easily as a bait mounted directly on the hook. The hair rig, as this arrangement became known, revolutionized carp fishing overnight and set off an ongoing arms race between rigs and fish. Today, a serious carp angler carries dozens of different rig variants, understanding precisely when each is appropriate — whether fishing over silt, gravel, dense weed, or open sand at ranges from 20 to 100 metres.

## The Hair Rig: The Foundation

The original hair rig consists of a boilie or pellet threaded on a short loop of braid or monofilament — the hair — that extends from the bend or shank of the hook. The hook is tied to a hooklink material, typically a coated braid or stiff monofilament, with the hair attached by a knotless knot or a separate loop tie. The rig is usually mounted in a semi-fixed arrangement on an inline or clip lead of 60–120 g. When a carp sucks in the bait, the hook follows slightly behind; the fish senses resistance as the lead hits the lakebed, pulls back, and drives the hook point into the bottom lip. The self-hooking principle is robust and reliable over hard bottoms and smooth gravel. Hair rig hooklinks typically run 10–25 cm; shorter links suit harder, more aggressive feeding, longer links work better on spooky fish or awkward angles.

## The Chod Rig

The chod rig is designed specifically for fishing over soft silt, thick weed, or debris on the bottom — conditions where a conventional bottom rig would lie in a buried or obscured position. A short (2–4 cm), stiff fluorocarbon hooklink is tied between a small swivel and a large curved hook, with the boilie presented in a rotary manner off the back. The whole rig is mounted on a leader with a free-running lead setup: the lead sinks into the silt while the hook and bait pop up on a short length of monofilament to the surface of the debris layer, remaining visible and functional regardless of bottom conditions. The chod rig transformed fishing over difficult bottoms on venues like Salagou Reservoir in France and vast silt-bottomed English gravel pits. It is typically fished with large pop-up boilies in bright colours — white, orange, pink — that remain buoyant and visible.

## The Zig Rig

The zig rig presents the hookbait suspended at a specific depth in the water column rather than on the bottom, targeting carp that are feeding midwater or just below the surface. A long monofilament hooklink — anywhere from 30 cm to several metres depending on the depth — is tied to a standard lead and hook combination, with a buoyant or critically balanced hookbait (foam, rubber imitation, or soaked foam) set at the target depth. Zig rigs are most effective in warm summer conditions when carp are basking or feeding on emerging insects near the surface. Lengths must be adjusted precisely to match the depth; 30-minute intervals of observation and adjustment are normal when learning a new venue. The surface zig — a zig so long that the bait sits just below the surface film — is one of the most challenging and exciting variations.

## The Spinner Rig

The spinner rig, or Ronnie rig, has become the dominant bottom-bait presentation in modern UK carp fishing. A curved shank hook is connected to a small swivel by a short length of shrink tube, allowing the hook to spin freely, theoretically improving hooking mechanics and making the rig harder for a carp to feel during inspection. The hooklink is a stiff coated braid of 10–15 cm, often tipped with a short section of supple braid to improve movement. A small pop-up boilie on a 2 cm hair sits critically balanced above the hook. The spinner rig consistently outperforms standard bottom rigs when fish are pressured and cautious, as the rotational freedom makes it less easy for them to eject. It is the rig of choice on heavily fished English lake complexes like Linear Fisheries and Yateley.

## Solid PVA Bag Fishing

A solid PVA bag — a small, tightly filled bag of dry pellets, crushed boilies, or mix — is placed around the complete lead and hooklink assembly before casting, and dissolves in the water within 60–120 seconds of landing. As it dissolves, it deposits a small pile of attractive freebies directly around the hookbait, giving the carp immediate reward incentive on every cast. Solid PVA fishing is extremely accurate — bags of 60–100 ml are cast to within a metre of showing fish or specific features — and is often used in conjunction with short hooklinks (10–15 cm) and critically balanced wafters or heavy bottom baits. It is particularly effective in winter when long loose-feed sessions are less productive and precision trumps quantity.

## The Multi-Rig

The multi-rig is a modular hookbait system in which the hook can be quickly changed without re-tying the entire rig. A stiff boom section (15–20 cm of coated braid stripped at the tip) holds the swivel and a separate hook section tied with a knotless knot and tiny bait screw. The angler can switch hook sizes and patterns in seconds while fishing. It is particularly popular for fishing different hookbait sizes through a session or matching the hook to the specific bait being used.

## Lead Systems and Leads

The choice of lead system is as important as the hooklink. Fixed leads on inline or clip-swivel systems create bolt-rig mechanics — the weight is felt immediately upon the first run. Running leads on a tail rubber allow the fish to pick up the rig without sensing resistance, which suits very cautious fish. Drop-off inline leads, designed to detach from the leader upon a run, are standard on silt or weed venues where a tethered fish should not carry a heavy lead. Lead weights typically range from 60 to 120 g for most UK lake fishing; distances exceeding 80 metres may require 140–200 g aerodynamic distance leads.
