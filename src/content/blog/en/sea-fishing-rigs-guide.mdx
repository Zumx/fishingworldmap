Every sea fishing rig is an engineering solution to a specific problem. The running leger evolved to present a bait as naturally as possible on a featureless sandy bottom while remaining sensitive to a cautious bite. The pulley rig was designed to swing the lead weight safely over rock ledges when a large fish is being brought in close. The pennel rig exists because a large bait on a single hook often folds and masks the hook point, reducing strike rates dramatically. Understanding the problem each rig solves transforms rig tying from a confusing list of knots into a logical, satisfying framework that immediately improves results.

## The Running Leger: Foundation of Bottom Fishing

The running leger is the simplest and most fundamental sea fishing rig. A lead weight slides freely on the main line, above a bead and a swivel to which the hooklink is attached. The sliding weight allows a fish to pick up the bait and move without immediately feeling full resistance — critically important for cautious species like bass, smoothhound, and large flatfish that will drop a bait at the first hint of tension. The hooklink length on a running leger matters considerably: 30 to 60 centimetres of lighter fluorocarbon is typical for flatfish; 60 to 90 centimetres is better for bass, which prefer a bait that can drift and lift naturally in the tide. Hook choice varies by target species — a 1/0 to 3/0 Aberdeen for flounders and small flatfish; a 4/0 to 6/0 strong pattern for bass and rays.

## The Pulley Rig: The Rock Fishing Essential

The pulley rig is one of the most widely used shore fishing rigs in the British Isles, particularly on rough, rocky marks where a conventional clipped-down rig would snag on retrieval. The pulley mechanism works by having the lead weight attached to a loop that runs through a swivel or a pulley bead on the main rig body. When a fish is hooked and retrieved, the weight swings up and parks alongside the hook and bait, making the rig streamlined for lifting over rocks, kelp, and ledges. The pulley rig is particularly associated with South West England — the rough-ground marks of Devon and Cornwall, the Jurassic Coast of Dorset, and the rocky headlands of Pembrokeshire in Wales — where large cod, bass, conger, and rays are targeted on big baits cast from exposed promontories.

## The Pennel Rig: Presenting Large Baits Effectively

The pennel rig uses two hooks in tandem on the same hooklink — a larger lower hook and a smaller upper hook, typically tied about 3 to 5 centimetres apart. The purpose is to allow a large bait such as a whole squid, a large lugworm cocktail, or a fillet of mackerel to be mounted naturally without masking either hook point. The lower hook goes through the bait first; the upper hook secures the top of the bait, keeping it straight and compact on the cast. The pennel rig is the standard setup for targeting cod from the shore in the North Sea, the Celtic Sea, and Cardigan Bay, and for boat fishing over wrecks and reefs for conger, ling, and large cod with whole squid or mackerel baits.

## Paternoster Rigs: Multi-Hook Shore and Boat Setups

The paternoster rig uses multiple hooks on short snood droppers above a weight at the bottom of the rig. The standard two-hook paternoster has snoods of 15 to 20 centimetres on boom arms or twisted-loop droppers at intervals of 30 to 40 centimetres above the weight. This setup is versatile and productive for a wide range of species, allowing two different baits or two sizes of the same bait to be presented simultaneously. A three-hook paternoster is commonly used for mackerel, garfish, and pollack from piers and breakwaters, typically baited with strips of mackerel or rubber sandeels. The Killer Rig — a short, stiff boom paternoster with size 2 Aberdeen hooks baited with lugworm — is a classic South Coast flatfish rig.

## Rotten Bottom Rigs: Fishing Snaggy Ground

When fishing over rough ground where losing a lead is common, a rotten bottom attachment allows the lead to be sacrificed while the fish and hook are recovered. A light line — typically 6 to 8 lb — attaches the lead to a weak link in the rig system; if the lead snags on rock, a firm pull parts the weak line and the rest of the rig, with any fish attached, can be retrieved. Rock marks around the Lizard Peninsula in Cornwall, Portland Bill in Dorset, and the basalt reefs of Northern Ireland are classic rotten-bottom territories where catching large fish is only possible if you are willing to lose leads.

## Wishbone Rigs and Lure Rigs

The wishbone rig uses two full-length snoods from the same swivel point, spreading the baits left and right so they do not tangle. It is popular for boat fishing for bream, bass, and plaice where casting distance is less important than keeping multiple baits separate and well-presented. Drifting lure rigs — sabiki-style feathers, trolled plugs, or pirks on a paternoster — are boat fishing staples for mackerel, pollack, and coalfish over wrecks, reefs, and open water.

## Trace and Snood Materials

The correct material for hooklinks and snoods matters significantly. Fluorocarbon in 20 to 40 lb breaking strain is the standard choice for most sea fishing hooklinks because of its near-invisibility underwater and its abrasion resistance against rocks and rough ground. Monofilament is adequate for clear-water flatfish fishing but does not offer the same abrasion resistance. Heavy nylon monofilament in 60 to 80 lb is used for conger traces because of the eel's ability to abrade and chafe through lighter materials. Wire traces of 40 to 60 lb seven-strand coated wire are essential for shark and tope fishing.
