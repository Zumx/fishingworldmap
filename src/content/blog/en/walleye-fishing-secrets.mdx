On Lake Erie in early June, a trolling spread of five planer boards deploys twelve crankbaits across 50 m of water column at exactly 2.4 m per second, and a rod doubles over 800 m behind the boat. This is industrial-scale walleye fishing at its most refined — a system of GPS speed control, temperature probe, planer board angles, and lure colour science that produces fish with near-mechanical consistency. At the other extreme, a single angler standing knee-deep in the Detroit River at midnight with a 14 g jig and a live shiver minnow is doing something older and quieter but fundamentally the same: presenting a bait in the dark to an animal built to see in the dark.

## The Walleye's Biology: Why Low Light Is Everything

The walleye (Sander vitreus) possesses a tapetum lucidum — the same reflective layer behind the retina found in cats and owls — that amplifies available light six-fold. In bright daylight, walleye retreat to deeper, darker water or the shade of heavy weed cover to avoid the discomfort of photosensitive retinas. As light fades in the late afternoon and dawn window, they move shallow to feed with a decisive visual advantage over their prey.

This light sensitivity creates the walleye calendar: peak feeding windows at dawn, dusk, and full dark; secondary windows on overcast days; almost no shallow-water activity on bright summer afternoons. Successful walleye fishing is largely about aligning your time on the water with these biologically determined windows.

## Spring: Rivers and Spawning Runs

Walleye spawn in early to mid-spring when water temperatures climb through 4-7°C, typically in rivers, rocky shoals, or wind-exposed gravel points. The Maumee River in Ohio draws thousands of wading anglers every March for one of the most celebrated walleye runs in North America. Fish stack in the fast, shallow riffles below the dams near Perrysburg and Napoleon, and a simple jig-and-twister-tail bounced along the gravel bottom produces limits of heavy pre-spawn fish.

In reservoir settings, walleye stage on the main-lake points and riprap banks adjacent to creek mouths before moving into the tributaries to spawn. Post-spawn fish return to the main lake and are often the most aggressive of the year — depleted from the spawn and feeding ravenously on any bait they encounter.

## Summer: Trolling the Open Water

From June through August on the Great Lakes and on large, deep reservoirs like Lake of the Woods in Minnesota/Ontario and Rainy Lake, trolling is the dominant technique. The reason is simple: walleye suspend in the thermocline at 8-14 m depth during summer heat, and covering water efficiently requires moving presentation.

Stick baits and deep-diving crankbaits in the Rapala Husky Jerk and Diver Trolling Stick categories are the workhorses. Colour selection has been studied obsessively on Lake Erie, and the consensus is that natural shad patterns — silver, blue-black, perch — produce in clear water, while high-visibility chartreuse and orange become more effective as water clarity drops or on overcast days.

Trolling speed between 2.1 and 2.7 m per second is the effective range for most walleye cranks. Below that speed, the lures run straight and lose action; above it, they break out of their wobble. Water temperature at the target depth, measured by a probe lowered on the down-rigger cable, is the most reliable indicator of where fish will be: they track the 16-18°C isotherm in summer.

## Jigging: The All-Season Technique

Jigging is arguably the most versatile walleye technique, effective from ice-off through late autumn and on the ice in winter. A round-ball jig from 7 to 21 g — heavier in current and deep water, lighter in still, shallow conditions — tipped with a plastic paddle tail, live minnow, or nightcrawler accounts for more walleye over the course of a full year than any other presentation.

The most productive jigging stroke for walleye is subtler than most anglers imagine. A sharp 30-cm lift followed by an 8-second dead fall — rod tip dropping all the way back to the original position during the sink — allows the jig to freefall and the attractor bead to click softly against the hook eye. Most bites occur on the fall, and detecting them requires watching the line for movement rather than waiting for a rod-tip signal.

## Night Fishing: The Walleye's Prime Time

The shallow-water night bite is the most reliable pattern in walleye fishing. On the upper Mississippi River pool system, on Lake Winnipeg in Manitoba, and on reservoirs throughout the Ohio River drainage, walleye move onto rocky flats and sand bars in 1-3 m of water after dark. A jig or a live-bait slip rig fished slowly along these features in the 90 minutes after full dark and 90 minutes before sunrise produces limits when daytime fishing is nearly impossible.

Slip-bobber fishing with a live nightcrawler or minnow suspended 50-100 cm off the bottom is among the most effective night techniques on natural lakes. The bobber can be seen by reflected headlamp light, and the slow, deliberate pull-down of a walleye bite is unmistakable.

## Ice Fishing: Winter Jigging Through the Ice

Walleye remain active under ice throughout winter, making them one of the most popular ice-fishing targets in the northern United States and Canada. Lake Simcoe in Ontario, Devils Lake in North Dakota, and Mille Lacs Lake in Minnesota are among the premier ice walleye destinations.

The most effective ice jigging presentations are small, heavy spoons in the 10-21 g range that can be worked in a tight vertical plane — a sharp upward snap of 15 cm followed by a completely dead fall. Tungsten jigs, pioneered for crappie and perch, have expanded into the walleye ice arsenal because their density allows smaller, more compact presentations that sink quickly to depth. At 7 m or more under ice, a dropper loop with a small plastic below the spoon increases hooking rate noticeably.

## Regulations and Conservation

Walleye populations are intensely managed across North American jurisdictions. Lake Erie has strict size limits (minimum 38 cm in Ontario, slot limits of 38-46 cm in Michigan waters) and a daily bag limit of six fish per angler. Many states and provinces have adopted protective slot limits that allow harvest of fish below and above the slot while protecting the peak reproductive age classes within it.

Understanding local regulations before targeting walleye is essential — the fishery's health in the Great Lakes is a direct result of decades of active management, and that quality fishery exists for everyone because limits are respected.
