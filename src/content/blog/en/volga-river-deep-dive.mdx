The Volga is Europe's longest river at 3,530 kilometres, draining a catchment of 1.35 million square kilometres before emptying into the Caspian Sea through an extraordinary delta of over 500 channels. For Russian anglers, the Volga is more than geography — it is a defining element of national identity, a river whose fish have fed cities and sustained cultures since the medieval period. The delta region around Astrakhan, in the far south of the watershed, concentrates the greatest diversity of large predatory species on the continent and draws fishing tourists from all over Russia and increasingly from western Europe.

## The Beluga: A Ghost of the Volga

No account of Volga fishing can ignore the beluga sturgeon (*Huso huso*), even though the species is effectively extinct as a wild fishery in the river. Before the construction of the Volgograd Hydroelectric Station in 1958 and the Zhigulevskaya dam upstream, beluga in the Volga regularly reached three metres in length and documented specimens exceeded 1,500 kg — the largest freshwater fish recorded in history. The dams blocked the migration routes that beluga used to access spawning grounds over 3,000 km upriver. By the 1990s, wild stocks had collapsed; today, a small state-backed captive-breeding programme at Astrakhan keeps the species from total extinction. Anglers occasionally encounter juvenile beluga in the lower delta — accidental catches — but targeted fishing for the species has been prohibited since 2000.

## Asp: The River's Explosive Predator

The asp (*Leuciscus aspius*) is the Volga's most exciting sport fish for lure anglers. Unlike most cyprinids, the asp is a dedicated surface-ambush predator, herding shoals of bleak and other small fish into tight balls before slashing through them at speed. In the Volga, asp grow exceptionally large due to the river's enormous food base; fish of 6–10 kg are not uncommon in the lower delta channels, and specimens above 12 kg have been recorded. The best asp fishing occurs from May through July when the fish are at their most active in the main channel and in the channels near Astrakhan. Spinnerbaits, large Mepps-style spinners, and surface poppers all produce fish, but the most consistent results come from 80–120 mm jerkbaits worked with a sharp twitch-and-pause retrieve that mimics an injured baitfish.

## Zander and Pikeperch of the Delta

The Volga zander (*Sander volgensis*) — often called Volga pikeperch — is a distinct species from the more widespread common zander, generally smaller but no less formidable as a predator in the delta's labyrinthine channels. Common zander also occur and reach 8–10 kg in the delta's deepest pools. Both species respond to jigging with shad-style lures on 15–30 g heads worked vertically in channels of 5–12 metres depth. The best jigging grounds shift seasonally: in spring the fish concentrate near the main channels where currents carry food; from July onward they disperse into quieter backwaters and lotus-fringed side channels that characterise the upper delta around Damchik and Ganbursya reserves.

## The Astrakhan Fishing Scene

Astrakhan, a city of half a million people on the west bank of the Volga, is the operational hub for lower-river fishing. Dozens of fishing bases — рыболовные базы — operate from the city and from smaller outposts across the delta. Some of the most respected operations are accessible only by helicopter or by long boat transfers into the delta's most remote channels. A typical guided trip from Astrakhan involves a two-to-three hour speedboat run to a floating camp moored in a productive channel, with daily boat fishing over multiple beats covering asp, zander, bream, carp, and occasional catfish. The city's tackle market on Lenin Street stocks an impressive range of Russian and imported lures, and several operators offer full packages that include accommodation, meals, boat hire, and guide fees.

## Carp, Bream, and the Volga's Coarse Species

The Volga supports one of Europe's largest populations of common carp and bream, neither of which receives the sporting attention they deserve in the context of Russian fishing culture. In the delta's quiet arms and flooded meadows, Caspian bream grow to 4–5 kg — significantly larger than their cousins in Western European rivers — and respond eagerly to float fishing with maggot, bread, or sweetcorn. Carp in the delta attain 25–30 kg in the richer sections where aquatic vegetation provides abundant feed. Float fishing, feeder fishing, and increasingly carp rigs in the European style are all productive approaches in these still waters.

## Regulations and the Reality of Volga Fishing

Russian fishing regulations on the Volga are extensive but variable by region and season. A federal fishing licence is required for most sport fishing, and specific regulations apply to protected species, minimum sizes, and catch limits for commercial-value fish. The beluga and stellate sturgeon are completely protected. The autumn season — September and October — is when the largest zander and asp are traditionally caught as fish fatten before winter. Ice fishing from December through February is enormously popular among Russian anglers, with vast encampments establishing themselves on the frozen river surface to target perch, pike, and bream through holes.

## Access and Practicalities

Astrakhan is served by direct flights from Moscow (2 hours) and by the M6 highway. The delta's most productive areas are best accessed by organised fishing tours, many bookable through Russian agencies that have English-language websites. A basic Russian fishing permit costs under 1,000 roubles; full guided packages from established Astrakhan bases typically range from 15,000 to 40,000 roubles per person per day depending on accommodation level and target species. June and September are widely regarded as the optimal months for visitors who want the full range of species.
