Costa Rica has built its international reputation on biodiversity and eco-tourism, but its fishing credentials are equally strong. The Pacific coast runs some 1,016 kilometres from the Gulf of Nicoya north to the Nicaraguan border and south through the Osa Peninsula, delivering one of the most consistent sailfish fisheries in the world. The Caribbean coast is shorter but no less productive — the canals and lagoons around Tortuguero hold resident tarpon populations that have attracted fly fishers for decades. Between the coasts, volcanic rivers full of rainbow trout tumble through highland valleys, and the remote jungle rivers of the Osa Peninsula hold giant snook that test both tackle and nerve. The country's geography compresses four or five distinct fishing environments into a nation smaller than West Virginia.

## Los Sueños — Pacific Sailfish

The Herradura Bay near Jacó, where the Los Sueños Resort and Marina is located, is arguably the most productive single departure point for Pacific sailfish in Central America. Sailfish congregate in the warm, bait-rich waters of the Nicoya Gulf and the open Pacific from December through April, with the peak running from January to March. Multiple sail bites per day are normal during these months; 10-release days are documented. Captains typically troll with hookless teasers to raise fish, then switch to live bait or circle-hook ballyhoo for the actual bite. Los Sueños has over 50 sportfishing vessels operating from its marina, making it easy to find quality captains and crews with recent intelligence on fish locations.

## Quepos — Offshore Capital of the South Pacific

The port town of Quepos near Manuel Antonio National Park is another major sport fishing hub on the Central Pacific coast. Both offshore trolling — for sailfish, marlin, and mahimahi — and inshore fishing for roosterfish, snapper, and jack crevalle are strong here. The roosterfish of the Central Pacific, taken on poppers or live blue runners just a few kilometres offshore, offer some of the most visually spectacular encounters in inshore saltwater fishing: the fish's distinctive comb-like dorsal fin raises above the water before the strike. The best months for roosterfish are May through October, coinciding with the green season when the coast is less busy.

## Tortuguero — Caribbean Tarpon

The Tortuguero Canal, running along the Caribbean coast of Costa Rica parallel to the shore, is the primary tarpon fishery in Central America. Tarpon — locally called sábalo — enter the canal system from the Caribbean and are present year-round, with the heaviest concentrations from January through June. Fish average 30–50 kg, but specimens exceeding 100 kg are caught regularly. The standard approach is fly fishing or light spinning with live mullet or sardines in the canal entrances and river mouths. Access to Tortuguero is only by boat or small aircraft, which preserves both the wildness of the setting and the fish populations. The town of Tortuguero itself is a simple village with a handful of lodges.

## Osa Peninsula — Jungle Snook

The rivers of the Osa Peninsula — the Rincón, the Sierpe, and the Río Grande de Térraba — hold populations of common snook that average 3–5 kg but can exceed 15 kg in the tidal stretches near the mangrove estuaries. These fish are true ambush predators, holding in the shadows beneath overhanging roots and striking surface lures with jarring suddenness. Fishing is done from small boats or kayaks, working the mangrove margins at high tide and the river channel edges as the tide drops. The Osa is also home to the Corcovado National Park, and fishing the rivers here means sharing the river bank with tapirs, peccaries, and troops of spider monkeys.

## Pesca de Trucha Los Prado — Mountain Trout

In the volcanic highlands of the Central Valley and the Tilarán Mountains, rainbow trout have been successfully introduced into cold mountain streams and managed stocked lakes. Pesca de Trucha Los Prado is one of the established trout fishing operations in the highlands, offering stocked pond and stream fishing in a mountain setting. The trout run 250–600 grams in these operations, making them ideal for families and children while the setting — misty highland forests, cool temperatures, and the sound of water over volcanic rock — is quintessentially Costa Rican. The Savegre River in the mountains above Quepos also holds wild rainbow trout and is one of the few rivers where truly free-rising fish can be targeted on dry flies.

## Boca Barranca — Roosterfish and Snook

At the mouth of the Río Barranca near Puntarenas on the Gulf of Nicoya, a combination of brackish water, baitfish schools, and rocky structure creates a productive inshore fishery for roosterfish, snook, jack crevalle, and snapper. This spot is accessible by road from San José — about 100 kilometres west — making it one of the most convenient quality fishing destinations for capital-based anglers. The fishing is best on the incoming tide in the early morning when roosterfish push baitfish against the river mouth structure.

## Barra del Colorado — Northern Caribbean Fishing

On the Caribbean coast near the Nicaraguan border, the town of Barra del Colorado sits at the mouth of the Río Colorado and is a legendary tarpon and snook destination. The Parismina and Colorado rivers produce tarpon year-round in the calm canals and river channels, while during the rainy season from May to October, massive schools of snook congregate at the river mouths. The Silver King Lodge and several other dedicated sportfishing lodges operate here, with fly fishing guides who know the tidal patterns intimately.

## Gulf of Nicoya — Roosterfish Flats

The Gulf of Nicoya, enclosing a large body of water between the Nicoya Peninsula and the mainland, holds one of the most accessible roosterfish fisheries in Costa Rica. The eastern edge of the Nicoya Peninsula, particularly the beaches around Paquera and Tambor, produces roosterfish on poppers and jigs from kayaks and small pangas. Further north near Playa Naranjo, roosterfish up to 25 kg patrol the sandy points and rocky headlands. The Gulf is also productive for Pacific jack crevalle, ladyfish, and smaller snapper that provide reliable light-tackle action year-round.

## Río Savegre — Wild Trout Country

The Río Savegre begins near San Gerardo de Dota, a highland valley at 2,200 metres above sea level, and descends through cloud forest to the Pacific coast near Dominical. The upper Savegre holds genuinely wild rainbow trout — not stocked — that are extremely wary and require careful presentations with lightweight tackle. The valley is also one of the best places in the world to see the resplendent quetzal. Trout fishing combines naturally with birdwatching here; both pursuits reward patience and attention to detail in equal measure.

## Golfito — South Pacific Remote Waters

The Golfo Dulce near Golfito is a warm, deep tropical fjord enclosed by the Osa Peninsula and the mainland of the far south Pacific. The calm, clear water holds bull sharks in the deep sections, large cubera snapper in the rocky margins, and roosterfish along the sandy beaches. Several small fishing lodges operate from Golfito and the nearby town of Puerto Jiménez on the Osa Peninsula. The remote nature of the area means fishing pressure is minimal and the fish are comparatively trusting of boats and lures.
