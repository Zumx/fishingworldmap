France treats fishing the way it treats food: regionally, seriously and with strong opinions. The country that invented modern carp-fishing legend at Saint-Cassien also hides Atlantic salmon rivers in the Béarn, chalk streams in Normandy that shaped fly-fishing history, and a bass coastline in Brittany to rival Ireland's. Around 1.5 million anglers buy the carte de pêche each year, yet most foreign visitors drive straight past the best water. These ten stops — and a short guide to the French permit system — will fix that.

## 1. Lac de Saint-Cassien, Provence

In the pine hills behind Cannes, this 430-hectare reservoir rewrote carp-fishing history in the 1980s, when its deep, timbered bays produced fish that broke world records and launched a thousand pilgrimages. Cassien remains a serious big-carp water with specimens over 25 kg, plus a notable population of black bass along the rocky margins. Night fishing is allowed only on designated sectors, bivvies fill up fast in spring and autumn, and the north arm's nature reserve is strictly off-limits — check the buoyed boundaries.

## 2. Lac du Der-Chantecoq, Champagne

At 4,800 hectares, the Der is one of western Europe's largest artificial lakes, built to tame the Marne, and it has become France's great public carp arena. Long night-fishing sectors, free-roaming fish that average high teens of kilos, and famously violent autumn runs make it the place many European carpers cut their teeth. The lake also fishes well for pike and zander, and in October its drawdown coincides with tens of thousands of migrating cranes — bring binoculars for the slow hours.

## 3. The Gave d'Oloron, Béarn

France still has Atlantic salmon, and the Gave d'Oloron in the Pyrenean foothills is their finest remaining river. From Navarrenx — where bridge-side spectators watch spring fish roll — down through Sauveterre-de-Béarn, the gave's powerful green pools hold salmon from March to July, plus sea trout at night in summer. Day permits are sold locally on top of the standard carte de pêche, and hiring a guide for the first day will save a week of guessing.

## 4. Pyrenean trout streams and mountain lakes

The high Pyrenees hold thousands of kilometres of wild brown trout water. The Nive in the Basque country, the Neste d'Aure, and the upper Gave de Pau around Lourdes all fish beautifully from the March opening to mid-summer. Higher still, the Néouvielle massif scatters more than 70 lakes between 1,800 and 2,500 m, many holding trout and brook char against granite-and-pine scenery. A single departmental card with reciprocity covers a remarkable amount of this water.

## 5. The Brittany bass coast

From the Golfe du Morbihan around to the Pointe du Raz, Brittany offers Europe's most varied sea-bass fishing: estuary drifting, rock fishing into white water, and sight-casting over sand in summer. The Morbihan's tidal currents concentrate bait — and bass — around its 40-odd islands, while Quiberon's wild side is classic lure water. Saltwater fishing needs no licence in France; just respect the bass regulations, which currently include a 42 cm minimum size and seasonal limits.

## 6. The Loire and Allier

France's longest river is a shifting world of sandbanks and side channels. The middle Loire around Orléans and Blois holds huge wels catfish, fast-growing asp and excellent zander fishing from the gravel bars. Its great tributary, the Allier, carries the last long-migrating salmon run in western Europe up toward Brioude — those fish are strictly protected, but the same wild river offers superb trout and grayling water in the upper gorges.

## 7. The Saône and Rhône at Lyon

Lyon sits on France's best urban predator fishery. The slow, deep Saône is one of Europe's premier wels catfish rivers — fish over 2.5 m have been recorded — and the confluence with the Rhône concentrates zander, perch and asp around bridge pilings and harbour mouths. Street-fishing culture thrives here: light rods, soft plastics, and catfish encounters that occasionally end with broken tackle on the quays beneath the old town.

## 8. Lake Annecy and Lac Léman

The Alps answer with two deep, clear giants. Annecy, often called Europe's cleanest lake, holds Arctic char, lake trout and big pike that patrol weedlines beneath limestone peaks; locals troll plumb-lines at 40 m and more for the char. Léman (Lake Geneva) adds the féra — a whitefish that supports both commercial boats and a devoted rod fishery. French-side permits are separate from Swiss ones, so check which waters your card covers before launching.

## 9. The Normandy and Picardy chalk streams

The Risle, the Andelle and the little rivers of the Pays de Caux are France's chalk-stream heritage, spring-fed and weed-waving like their English cousins. Charles Ritz fished and championed the Risle near Pont-Audemer, and private syndicates still tend its banks. Access runs through AAPPMA club stretches and paying beats; mayfly time in late May is the high point, with grayling extending the season into autumn on some waters.

## 10. The étang culture of rural France

France's secret weapon is its tens of thousands of village ponds and small lakes. In the wooded Northern Vosges, the Étang de Baerenthal offers family-friendly fishing on a postcard-perfect forest pond; in the Auvergne, crater and plateau lakes like the Lac de la Landie hold carp and pike in volcanic surroundings; everywhere, communal étangs sell cheap day tickets at the village café. Many AAPPMA associations also run dedicated night-carp sectors — look for the parcours carpe de nuit signs.

## How the French permit system works

Freshwater fishing requires a carte de pêche from an AAPPMA angling association, bought online in minutes via the federal cartedepeche.fr system. A full annual card costs around 100 euros, but holiday cards covering seven days and one-day cards exist, and the interfederal card covers 91 departments through reciprocity schemes. Waters are classed as first category (salmonid rivers, roughly mid-March to mid-September) or second category (open most of the year for coarse and predator fishing). The sea needs no licence at all.
