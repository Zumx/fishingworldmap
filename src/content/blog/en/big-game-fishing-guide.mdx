There is nothing in freshwater fishing that prepares you for the first run of a large blue marlin. The fish — which can exceed 600 kg in the waters off the Azores or the Kona Coast of Hawaii — takes a surface lure running at 7–9 knots and translates its velocity and mass into a run so fast and powerful that even a 130 lb-class outfit, with its drag pre-set at 27 kg of resistance, sounds like a zipper tearing open. The captain cuts the throttle; the angler is transferred from the cockpit to the chair; the gimbal pin locks into the fighting belt; and the world contracts to the 50 m of leader between the fish and the rod tip. Big game fishing at this level requires specialized equipment, physical preparation, team coordination, and a code of practice that prioritizes the survival of every fish released.

## Tackle Classes and the IGFA System

The International Game Fish Association (IGFA) defines tackle classes by the rated breaking strength of the leader or main line — 6, 8, 12, 16, 20, 30, 50, 80 and 130 lb are the recognised classes for world record purposes. For genuine large marlin or bluefin tuna in the 200–400 kg class, 80 lb (36 kg) or 130 lb (60 kg) outfits are standard. A 130 lb conventional reel — the Penn International 130, Shimano Tiagra 130A, or Accurate ATD 130 — holds 600–900 m of 130 lb monofilament or braided line and weighs 3–5 kg on its own. Mounted on a 1.8–2.0 m roller-guide stand-up rod or a 2.4 m bent-butt game rod, the total weight in the fighting harness can exceed 8 kg.

The drag system on a 130 lb conventional reel must deliver precise, consistent pressure at 25–35 kg without thermal failure during a fight that can last two to four hours. Lever-drag reels are preferred over star-drag designs in the 50 lb class and above, because the lever allows the angler to shift instantly between free-spool, strike drag and full-drag positions without losing control of the handle.

## Trolling for Billfish: The Spread

Billfish are almost universally targeted by trolling — running lures or rigged baits behind a moving vessel at 7–10 knots. The spread typically consists of four to six lures or baits arranged in a pattern that mimics a small school of bait fish: two long rigger positions at 40–60 m behind the boat, two short rigger positions at 20–30 m, and two flat-line positions directly from the stern at 15–20 m. Outrigger lines and release clips hold the rigger baits at the periphery, which creates a visual spread that a billfish sees as a vulnerable school from below.

Skirted lures — heads of polished resin or metal in shapes called jet heads, bullet heads, or plunger heads — are the modern standard. Kopus lures, Black Bart lures, and Mold Craft lures are among the most recognized brands in the sport. Each head shape creates a different swimming action: a cup-face plunger digs into the water surface and pops back repeatedly, generating a bubble trail and smoke that is visible from 10 m below the surface. Colour selection is empirical; blue-white, pink-white and black-red are consistent performers across species and oceans.

## Giant Bluefin Tuna

Atlantic bluefin tuna (*Thunnus thynnus*) can exceed 680 kg — the world record stands at 679 kg caught off Nova Scotia in 1979 — and they remain one of the most powerful and technically demanding opponents in any ocean. Giant bluefin fishing is concentrated in a handful of locations: the waters south of Nova Scotia and Prince Edward Island in Canada, the Strait of Gibraltar, the Tyrrhenian Sea off Sardinia and Calabria, and the waters around the Azores.

The fight with a giant bluefin on 80 lb tackle can last four to six hours, during which the fish uses its massive body mass in a series of deep, dogged circles beneath the boat. Unlike marlin, which make long surface runs, giant tuna tend to sound — running straight down toward 200–300 m depth — and then circle. Pumping the rod repeatedly in short 30 cm strokes while the fish is at depth, without allowing any line to be taken, is the core of tuna-fighting technique. The angler typically burns 1,500–2,000 calories per hour during a hard tuna fight.

## Swordfish: The Deep Water Challenge

Broadbill swordfish (*Xiphias gladius*) are unique among big game species in being a predominantly nocturnal, deep-water predator. Traditional daylight drifting for sword off the Florida Keys or in the Mediterranean historically required drifting a rigged bait on a weighted line to 300–500 m at night. The modern deep-drop technique — pioneered off Miami in the early 2000s by captains like Nick Smith — uses electric reels such as the Shimano Beastmaster 9000 to lower a live bait or large squid to depths of 300–600 m during daylight hours, targeting swordfish that rest in the thermocline.

A swordfish takes the bait at depth and the fight begins with the fish already 300–400 m below the boat. The first indication is the electric reel's resistance indicator spiking; the captain then puts the boat in reverse to keep tension while the angler begins the pump-and-wind retrieve from a stand-up harness. Swords at depth fight differently from surface billfish — they are slower initially but use the water pressure at depth to their advantage, and a fish that decides to run after being raised 100 m will dump line at a rate that can stall even a 50 lb outfit.

## Global Destinations

The Kona Coast of Hawaii's Big Island is arguably the single greatest blue marlin fishery in the world, combining deep water close to shore with warm, clear oceanic conditions year-round. Grander marlin — fish over 1,000 lb (453 kg) — have been caught here more times than anywhere else in the Pacific. The waters off Madeira are renowned for blue marlin in the Atlantic, particularly from June through October. Cabo San Lucas at the tip of Mexico's Baja California peninsula offers striped marlin, blue marlin, Pacific sailfish and giant bluefin tuna within 30 minutes of the marina. Off the coast of Australia, Cairns and the Great Barrier Reef in Queensland form the most famous black marlin fishery on earth, with fish exceeding 500 kg regularly encountered from September to December.

## Marlin Release Protocols

The International Billfish Foundation and the IGFA both strongly advocate catch-and-release for all billfish, particularly blue and black marlin. The release protocol requires bringing the fish alongside the boat without gaffing, keeping it in the water, cutting the leader as close to the hook as possible with a bolt cutter or heavy scissors, and reviving the fish by moving it forward in the water to force oxygenated water through its gills. If a fish is exhausted, it should be towed forward at idle speed for up to five minutes before release. Under no circumstances should a large marlin be removed from the water — vertical lifting of a fish over 50 kg by the bill causes fatal spinal injury.
