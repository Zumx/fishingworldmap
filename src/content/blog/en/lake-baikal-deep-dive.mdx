In March, the ice on Lake Baikal is more than a metre thick and so transparent that walking on it feels like levitation: black depths below your boots, pressure cracks booming like artillery somewhere out toward the horizon. Locals drive vans onto the lake, drill through with metre-long augers, and jig for grayling and omul in water that, just offshore, plunges toward the deepest point of any lake on Earth — 1,642 m. Baikal is around 25 million years old, holds roughly 20 percent of the planet's unfrozen fresh water, and contains more endemic species than any other lake. Fishing here is less a sport than an initiation into a place that behaves more like an ocean than an inland water.

## The science that shapes the fishing

Baikal sits in a rift valley that is still widening by about 2 cm a year — the lake is slowly becoming an ocean, geologically speaking. Its water is famously pure: a tiny endemic copepod, Epischura baikalensis, filters the entire upper water column, giving visibility down to 40 m. That clarity dictates tactics — fine lines, small lures, long leaders. Of the lake's 50-plus fish species, more than half occur nowhere else, including the bizarre golomyanka, a translucent, scaleless oilfish that gives birth to live young and makes up the bulk of the lake's fish biomass. Above it all swims the nerpa, the world's only exclusively freshwater seal, whose presence 1,500 km from any ocean still puzzles biologists.

## The fish that matter to anglers

The icon is the omul (Coregonus migratorius), a silver whitefish that smells like cucumber when fresh and tastes like nothing else when hot-smoked. Alongside it: black and white Baikal grayling, strong, broad-finned and beautifully marked; lenok, a trout-like char of the inflowing rivers; pike and perch in the warm, shallow bays; burbot under the winter ice; and in the tributaries of the north and east, taimen — the giant salmonid of Siberian legend, strictly protected and catch-and-release only where fishing for it is permitted at all. Note the omul's status: after stocks collapsed, Russia banned commercial omul fishing in 2017, and recreational harvest has since been tightly limited — your guide will know the current quota rules, and following them matters.

## Listvyanka and the southern shore

Most travellers meet Baikal at Listvyanka, an hour's drive from Irkutsk along the Angara — the lake's only outflowing river. It is touristy by Siberian standards, but a fair base: ice fishing day trips in winter, boat charters in summer, and the Baikal Limnological Museum to put science under what you have seen. Westward, the old Circum-Baikal Railway hugs the cliffs toward Slyudyanka through dozens of tunnels — hiking it in summer with a travel rod, casting for grayling where mountain streams tumble in, is one of the lake's quiet pleasures. The Angara outflow itself stays unfrozen all winter and holds fish year-round.

## Olkhon Island and the Maloye More

Olkhon, 71 km long, is Baikal's largest island and its spiritual centre — Shaman Rock at Khuzhir is one of Asia's most sacred sites, and the ribbons tied to posts above it twist in the same wind that brushes your fishing line. The strait between Olkhon and the western shore, the Maloye More (Small Sea), is shallow, warmer, and the lake's most productive amateur fishery: pike and perch in the sors (lagoon bays) in summer, and in winter a small city of ice camps jigging for grayling and omul. Khuzhir's guesthouses arrange everything from UAZ-van ice transfers to evening banya sessions that thaw the day out of your bones.

## The wild north and east

The further from Irkutsk you go, the wilder it gets. Severobaikalsk, reachable by the BAM railway, opens the lake's north: hot springs at Khakusy, omul-rich waters off the Upper Angara delta, and almost no other anglers. On the eastern shore in Buryatia, the Barguzin valley and Chivyrkuy Bay in Zabaikalsky National Park offer Baikal's best summer pike fishing among warm, weedy inlets — fish over a metre long are realistic. The Selenga delta, the lake's largest inflow, is a maze of channels thick with perch, pike and roach. These areas require permits for the protected zones, arranged through tour operators in Ulan-Ude or Irkutsk.

## Winter: the ice season

Baikal freezes late — solid ice usually forms only in January — and the prime ice season runs February to early April, when days lengthen and the surface becomes a highway. Standard technique is jigging with short rods: small spoons or mormyshka jigs tipped with bormash (the local freshwater amphipod) for grayling and omul, often attracted by chumming crushed amphipods down the hole. Omul commonly hold 15 to 30 m down even under ice, so locals fish multiple-hook rigs deep. Dress for minus 20 °C with wind; rent or bring an ice tent. Never drive or walk the ice without local knowledge — pressure cracks and methane vents create open leads even in deep winter, and every year vehicles are lost to them.

## Summer: open water and grayling streams

From June to September a different lake appears. Boats troll the drop-offs for omul schools located by sonar; fly and spin anglers work the mouths of the 300-plus rivers that feed the lake — the Barguzin, the Turka, the Snezhnaya — for grayling and lenok with dry flies and small spinners. The Great Baikal Trail sections between Listvyanka and Bolshie Koty let you combine hiking with stream fishing. August is the warmest month, with bay water reaching 20 °C and the famous Baikal winds — the sarma can hit hurricane force with little warning, which is why local captains watch the sky like hawks and you should respect a cancelled trip.

## Practicalities for the travelling angler

Fly to Irkutsk (for the west and south) or Ulan-Ude (for the east); both connect through Moscow and several Asian hubs. A fishing licence as such is not required for amateur rod fishing in most open areas, but protected species rules, national park permits and omul limits very much apply — book through a local guide or operator who handles paperwork. The Trans-Siberian Railway makes Baikal an easy stop on a larger Russian journey; Irkutsk to Moscow is about 87 hours of train if you want to earn the distance. Carry cash outside cities, learn ten words of Russian, and accept every invitation to smoked omul and tea — refusing hospitality is the only real faux pas on the lake.
