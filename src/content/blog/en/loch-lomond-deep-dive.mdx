Loch Lomond lies 23 kilometres north-west of Glasgow and covers 71 square kilometres, making it the largest body of freshwater in Great Britain by surface area. Its southern basin is broad and shallow, scattered with around 30 wooded islands and fringed with reeds; the northern basin plunges to over 190 metres, carving a glacial trench that holds water colder and clearer than almost anywhere else in lowland Scotland. This dual personality — warm, productive shallows in the south; cold, deep, oligotrophic water in the north — supports an unusually rich and varied fish community. Yet Loch Lomond's fishing is heavily constrained by conservation rules that reflect both the lake's ecological importance and the pressures placed on it by its proximity to one of Scotland's largest cities.

## The Powan: Scotland's Most Exclusive Fish

The powan (*Coregonus lavaretus*) is a deep-water coregonid — a whitefish — that has been landlocked in Loch Lomond since the last Ice Age, around 10,000 years ago. The Loch Lomond powan, together with a separate subspecies in Loch Eck, represent Britain's last native freshwater whitefish that cannot be found anywhere else in the country. Growing to around 30 cm and feeding almost exclusively on zooplankton, the powan occupies the deep, cold water of the northern basin at depths of 30–100 metres. They are rarely caught on a rod, though winter netting surveys conducted by NatureScot suggest the population remains healthy. Their main ecological significance in angling terms is that they form a significant part of the diet of Loch Lomond's pike.

## Pike Fishing: World-Class in Shallow Water

Loch Lomond's pike fishing is among the finest in Europe. The shallow southern basin, with its extensive reed systems around the islands of Inchmurrin, Inchcailloch, and Clairinsh, provides ideal pike habitat: dense vegetation, shallow warm water, and abundant roach and perch prey. Pike in Loch Lomond regularly exceed 15 kg, fish of 18–20 kg are taken most seasons by specialist anglers, and the loch's unofficial record stands close to 24 kg. The most productive pike fishing occurs from October through March, when the fish move into the shallower reed margins to hunt. Trolling with large shallow-diving Rapala lures at 3–6 metres depth covers ground efficiently during winter, while live-baiting with perch or roach under a float fished at anchor in open water between the islands produces the largest specimens. All pike fishing on Loch Lomond is strictly catch-and-release only, enforced by the Loch Lomond Angling Improvement Association.

## Sea Trout Runs from the Clyde

Loch Lomond is connected to the Clyde estuary via the River Leven, a short but powerful spate river of just 11 kilometres. Sea trout (*Salmo trutta*) enter the Leven from spring onwards, with the main run arriving between June and September. The best sea trout fishing within the loch itself occurs in the shallow southern basin where fish rest and acclimatise before moving upriver into the northern tributaries. Fly fishing with intermediate or floating lines, using tube flies and Waddington shanks in sizes 10–14, is traditional in the autumn months when water temperatures cool and the fish become more responsive. The River Falloch at the head of the loch receives particularly strong sea trout runs in autumn and is regarded as one of the finest small spate river sea trout fisheries in the central belt.

## Salmon Conservation and Restricted Access

Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) run Loch Lomond and its tributaries including the Endrick Water, the Fruin, and the Falloch, but the species is under severe conservation pressure across Scotland and the loch is subject to some of the most restrictive salmon regulations in the country. Since 2021, mandatory catch-and-release for salmon has been in force on most beats; in practice, this means that while salmon are present and catchable, the spirit of visiting anglers should be oriented toward conservation rather than harvest. The Endrick Water, which flows into the southern loch from the east, is a designated salmon and sea trout stream with its own permit structure managed by the Endrick Water Fishing Association.

## Perch, Roach, and the Loch's Silver Fish

Loch Lomond holds excellent populations of perch and roach that go largely underexplored by visiting anglers focused on pike and salmon. Perch in the southern loch regularly reach 1.5 kg, with fish of 2 kg occasionally reported from the deeper drop-offs around the larger islands. They respond to drop-shot rigs, worm or prawn fished on a light jig head, and small inline spinners worked along the rocky margins. Roach are numerous in the reedy bays of the southern basin and provide accessible sport for all anglers on light float or feeder tackle. They also represent the primary bait species for pike anglers who prefer live-baiting.

## Accessing Loch Lomond

The majority of bank-fishing access on Loch Lomond is governed by the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003, which grants a right of responsible access to most freshwater in Scotland. However, fishing rights are separate from access rights — permits are required from the Loch Lomond Angling Improvement Association for most species. Day tickets are available from tackle shops in Balloch, Luss, and Balmaha. Boat hire is available at Balloch, Luss, Balmaha, and Rowardennan. The A82 runs along the western shore and provides easy road access to multiple launch sites.

## The West Highland Way and Lochside Character

The West Highland Way walking route follows the eastern shore of Loch Lomond from Balmaha to Rowardennan, passing through native oak woodland that is itself a Special Area of Conservation. The combination of mountain backdrop — Ben Lomond rises to 974 metres directly from the eastern shore — ancient woodland, and island-studded water creates a fishing environment of extraordinary visual character. Even on a cold November day when pike are the objective, the scenery of the southern loch is consistently spectacular.
