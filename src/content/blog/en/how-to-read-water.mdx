Stand on any river bank and watch the foam. Bubbles and debris collect along a narrow drifting lane where two currents of different speed shear against each other — and that lane, the current seam, is a conveyor belt of drowned insects and disoriented baitfish. Trout, smallmouth, grayling, barbel and a dozen other species stack along it because they can hold in the slow water and eat from the fast water without burning energy. The old guide's saying that ninety percent of the fish live in ten percent of the water is roughly true everywhere on Earth; reading water is simply the skill of recognising that ten percent on sight. It is learnable, it transfers between continents, and it starts with understanding why a fish holds where it holds.

## The Three Things Every Fish Needs

Strip away species differences and every gamefish positions itself by the same budget: food, security and energy. It wants maximum drifting or swimming food, minimum exposure to predators (herons, ospreys, otters, bigger fish), and minimum current or travel cost to get both. That is why the best lies are compromises written in water: the boulder pocket that breaks the current next to a food-rich riffle, the weed edge beside a deep escape hole, the shaded undercut bank where the main bubble line passes within half a metre. When you assess a spot, score it on all three. A featureless sandy shallow fails on security; a deep still pit fails on food delivery; the magic spots score on everything at once.

## Rivers: Seams, Riffles, Pools and Pockets

A river repeats one structural sentence endlessly: riffle, run, pool, tail-out. The riffle is the grocery store — shallow, oxygen-rich, crawling with mayfly, caddis and stonefly larvae. The run below it, where depth and pace balance, is the prime feeding lie, and the seam between the fast tongue and the slack edge is its centrepiece. Pools hold the biggest fish, especially at the head where the current digs a trench and delivers food, and in the tail-out at dusk when fish slide up to intercept the evening drift. Pocket water — the broken staircase of boulders in steeper gradients — fishes like a series of one-metre ponds, each cushion in front of and behind a rock holding a fish. Add the bank features: undercuts on outside bends where the thalweg, the deepest thread of current, slams against the bank; log jams; tributary mouths injecting cool water in summer.

## Lakes: Structure Is the Map

Still water hides its logic below the surface, but the logic is firm. Fish relate to the breaks: the drop-off where a shallow flat tips into deep water, the points and bars that extend that edge, the saddles between islands, the old creek channels in reservoirs. Weed lines are the lake's current seams — bluegill and perch live inside the salad, pike and bass patrol the outside wall, walleye slide along the deep edge at dusk. Inflows matter in every season: a stream mouth means oxygen, food and cooler summer water. On unfamiliar lakes, the fastest education is a contour map plus ten minutes of asking where the wind has been blowing — sustained wind stacks plankton, then baitfish, then predators against the downwind shore, which is why veteran walleye and pike anglers fish the waves, not the calm.

## Temperature: The Invisible Structure

Water temperature organises fish as firmly as any boulder. Each species has a comfort band — brown trout roughly 10 to 18 degrees Celsius, largemouth bass happiest above 20, lake trout demanding cold water below the thermocline all summer. In stratified lakes, the thermocline (often 5 to 10 metres down in mid-summer temperate lakes) concentrates baitfish and predators along one horizontal plane: find its depth on a fish finder or a thermometer and you have found the invisible floor everything relates to. In rivers, summer fish crowd spring seeps, shaded tributaries and aerated riffles; in early spring, the warmest bay of a lake — often a dark-bottomed northern bay that catches afternoon sun — can be ten times livelier than the main basin. Carry a thermometer; it is the cheapest fish finder ever made.

## Light, Wind and the Edges of the Day

Fish are edge creatures in time as well as space. Low light at dawn and dusk pulls predators shallow and switches on surface feeding; bright midday pushes fish deeper, tighter to shade and cover. Wind is the river of the lake: a ripple breaks up the light and emboldens big fish to hunt shallow flats, and the mudline a blow stirs up along a beach becomes a hunting edge for bass and walleye. Even on rivers, the windward bank collects terrestrial insects — hoppers, beetles, ants — and the trout know it. The practical rule: plan your best water for the worst light, and use harsh midday hours to scout, map and watch.

## A Repeatable System for New Water

Reading water becomes powerful when you turn it into a sequence. First, before fishing, spend ten minutes high — a bridge, a bluff, polarised glasses on — mapping seams, depth changes, weed edges and shade. Second, rank three spots by the food-security-energy test and fish only those, thoroughly, instead of casting at everything. Third, fish the nearest good water first; the biggest mistake on every river is wading through the fish to cast at the far bank. Fourth, note what the productive spots shared — depth, speed, bottom, shade — and run that pattern downstream or down the shoreline. Five fish from five spots that share one feature is not luck; it is a pattern you can repeat on the next river, the next lake, the next country.

## The Same Grammar Everywhere

The beauty of water reading is that it travels lighter than any rod tube. The seam logic that catches brown trout on Montana's Madison catches grayling on Sweden's Kaitumälven and barbel on Spain's Ebro. The drop-off pattern that produces walleye on Lake Erie produces zander on the Netherlands' Markermeer and golden perch in Australia's Murray-Darling impoundments. Species and flies change; the grammar of current, depth, cover and temperature does not. Learn it on home water until it is instinct, and every fishing trip for the rest of your life begins with an advantage.
