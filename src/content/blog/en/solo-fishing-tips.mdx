There is a particular freedom in fishing alone. No negotiation over which pool to try first, no waiting for someone else to work a section before moving on, no managing another person's impatience or expectations. The solo angler makes every decision independently — where to wade, how long to stay, when to move — and is accountable only to the fish and the water. It is meditative in a way that group fishing rarely is. But fishing alone also carries genuine risks, and managing those risks intelligently is what separates a solo trip that renews you from one that endangers you.

## The Check-In Protocol: Non-Negotiable

Before every solo fishing trip, leave a detailed trip plan with a reliable contact — a friend, family member, or lodge operator — who is not on the trip. The plan should include: the precise location of the water you are fishing (name of river, section, grid reference or GPS coordinates), the access route, your vehicle description and registration plate, the time you expect to return, and the exact action they should take if you have not made contact by a specified time — typically two hours past your expected return.

The check-in protocol does not end at the trailhead. If you have mobile coverage, send a brief message when you arrive at the water and another when you leave. If you are moving between sections, update the location. On longer expeditions into remote water — backcountry rivers in Norway, Alaska, or New Zealand where you might be in the field for multiple days — a satellite communicator like the Garmin inReach Mini or SPOT X provides a two-way text link even where no cell signal exists. At around 300 to 500 USD including a subscription, this is cheap insurance for serious remote fishing.

## Wading Alone: The Elevated Risk

Wading in moving water is the activity most likely to result in a serious incident when fishing solo. A misstep on a slippery rock, an unexpected deep channel, or a sudden change in footing can put an angler underwater in seconds. In cold water — temperatures below 15°C — swimming ability degrades rapidly due to cold shock and swim failure; at 5°C, most people cannot swim effectively within minutes.

The practical mitigations: always use a wading staff in any water with current. A telescoping staff — the Simms Freestone Wading Staff or similar — gives a third contact point with the bottom and provides seconds of warning before a fall. Wear a wading belt cinched tightly over your waders: it limits the water that enters if you fall, extending the time before waders become dangerously heavy. A personal flotation device (inflatable PFD belt or vest) worn while wading in fast or deep water significantly improves the odds of recovery.

Know your own limits. Wading solo demands more conservative threshold assessment than wading with a partner. Water that you might cross with a friend's help is water to avoid alone. The principle is simple: if you would hesitate, don't.

## Self-Rescue Skills for the Solo Angler

If you do go in, the ability to self-rescue determines the outcome. The most important skill is maintaining calm: panic accelerates heat loss and exhausts energy. If the current takes you, adopt a defensive floating position — on your back, feet downstream, arms spread to control rotation — and work toward the nearest bank at an angle rather than fighting the current directly.

Carry ice picks around your neck in cold conditions. If you break through ice — increasingly relevant as ice fishing grows in popularity — ice picks allow you to grip the ice surface and haul yourself out by kicking and pulling. Practice this technique at the edge of shallow ice before venturing onto deeper water.

A throw bag in the kayak or at the edge of any bank-side camp is another practical tool. If a fishing partner were to need assistance — even in a solo scenario where you might see another angler in trouble — a throw bag extends reach dramatically. They weigh under 500 grams and cost around 30 to 50 EUR.

## First Aid and Emergency Preparation

Solo anglers should carry a more comprehensive first aid kit than those fishing in groups, simply because self-treatment must cover more scenarios. The minimum: wound closure strips and bandages for lacerations from hooks or rocks, a tourniquet (the CAT or SOFTT-W are combat-proven and easy to apply one-handed), antiseptic wipes, blister treatment, and an emergency blanket. Hypothermia is a genuine risk any time you are wet in cold conditions; the emergency blanket is for wrap and shelter, not merely for show.

A course in Wilderness First Aid (WFA) or Wilderness First Responder (WFR) gives solo outdoor travellers the judgment to assess and manage medical situations in remote settings without immediate access to professional help. Organisations including NOLS and SOLO Schools of Wilderness Medicine run these courses internationally.

## Navigation and Communication

Getting lost en route to or from a remote fishing location while alone creates a secondary emergency that exhausts rescue resources. Carry a detailed topographic map of the area (not just the section you plan to fish), a compass, and the knowledge to use both. A GPS device or phone with offline maps (Maps.me, Gaia GPS) provides backup navigation. On multi-day trips, identify emergency exit routes before you need them.

Phone coverage is absent on most backcountry fishing rivers. Where it exists, it is often intermittent. Do not assume your phone will function as your primary communication tool. The satellite communicator is the reliable option; failing that, a whistle (the FOX 40 is audible at extraordinary distances) gives you a signalling tool without battery dependency.

## The Philosophical Case for Solo Fishing

Beyond the logistics of managing risk, fishing alone offers something genuinely distinct from social fishing: the complete immersion in observation. Without another voice or presence to mediate the environment, the angler notices more — the rise form of a feeding trout, the behaviour of the heron upstream, the shift in light that tells you the evening hatch is imminent. Many experienced anglers describe solo sessions as the most honest fishing they do: no performance, no comparison, just the water and the fish and the task.

Solitude on the water can be a genuine antidote to the pressures of connected modern life. A day without notifications, without social obligation, attending only to the immediate sensory present, has measurable psychological benefit. The solo angler, particularly on a wild river or a remote lake, is engaging in something ancient — hunting by attention and patience — that connects to a practice older than history.
