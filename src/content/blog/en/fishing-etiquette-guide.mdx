The rules of fishing are not only in the rulebook. They live in the glance exchanged between anglers on a pool, in the way you slow your boat's speed near a bank angler's float, in the distance you leave between yourself and the man who arrived before you. These unwritten codes developed over generations because fishing is fundamentally about sharing finite resource — water, fish, peace — and everyone on the bank has an equal right to their fair share of it.

Breaking these codes is not illegal. But it damages relationships, spoils sessions, and marks you out to experienced anglers as someone who does not belong. Learning the etiquette before you arrive is a mark of respect for a tradition that predates most modern sport.

## Salmon and Sea Trout Rivers: Pool Etiquette

On beat-managed salmon rivers — the Tweed, the Tay, the Spey in Scotland, the Dee in Wales, the Laerdal in Norway — pool etiquette is the most rigorous code in all of game fishing. The essential rule: do not enter the pool above an angler who is already working through it.

Salmon anglers traditionally work down a pool from the top: they cast, take two or three paces downstream after each cast, and systematically cover the water. When they reach the bottom, they return to the top and begin again, or give way to the next angler. Wading into the pool above them resets their water and takes what they were patiently approaching. It is, in the words of more than one river keeper on the Spey, unforgivable.

Wait at the bank until the angler working the pool invites you in, or until they have clearly completed their run and stepped out. If you are unsure, ask. Every experienced salmon angler knows the procedure and will tell you where to enter if you ask politely.

## Fly Fishing: Giving Other Anglers Space

On trout streams — whether the chalk streams of Hampshire, the limestone rivers of Derbyshire, or the freestone rivers of Wales — rising fish are limited and positions are precious. The unwritten rule is a minimum 100-metre separation between anglers on foot, more on smaller streams. If someone is working a rise upstream of you, you do not pass them to reach a fish beyond. You wait, or you fish elsewhere.

Approaching rising fish from downstream means walking past other anglers' water. Do it on the bank, behind the vegetation line, as quietly and invisibly as possible. Clumsy bankside movement sends ripples through the grass that spook fish at distances of 10 to 15 metres.

On fly-only stillwaters such as Rutland Water, Grafham Water, or the highland lochs of Scotland, boat discipline matters. Drift boats working downwind have right of way over anchored boats in their path; bank anglers fishing a point or bay should not be approached within 50 metres by a boat that has not already established its drift in that direction.

## Boat Etiquette: Speed, Wash, and Noise

On rivers and lakes shared between anglers and boat traffic, the speed of your boat can destroy another angler's fishing. A wash from a motorboat passing at 8 knots rolls a float off its spot, drags ledger weights, and stirs up the bottom. On a coarse river, passing within 30 metres of bank anglers at speed is considered seriously bad form.

The International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGs) apply on tidal waters, but informal courtesy rules apply everywhere. When passing bank anglers at slow speed, cut the engine if possible, take the widest practical line, and acknowledge the angler with a wave. In many UK fisheries, motorboats are required to reduce speed to less than 4 knots within 30 metres of the bank — but even where this is not required, doing so voluntarily is expected of any angler who also boats.

On small commercial fisheries that allow boats, do not anchor directly in front of another angler's swim, and do not let your boat drift into someone else's feeding area.

## Commercial Fisheries: The Rules of the Peg

Commercial coarse fisheries — the type that dominates the English Midlands, with well-stocked carp ponds and numbered pegs — have clear rules about peg distances. Each angler is assigned or chooses a peg and has exclusive rights to the water in front of it, typically a 10-metre arc. Casting beyond your own arc into another angler's peg, even accidentally, is a significant breach.

Keepnets should be positioned at the edge of your peg, not sprawled across the walkway. Bait boats, used on some carp fisheries, should not be driven into the arc of adjacent pegs. And noise — loud music, phone calls on speaker, persistent shouting — is universally unwelcome. Fishing demands a level of quiet that respects the concentration and peace of everyone present.

## Leaving the Bank: No Trace

Every angler carries out their own litter. This is non-negotiable. Discarded monofilament, which is nearly invisible and decomposes slowly over decades, kills birds and small mammals regularly. Wrap used line in loops and deposit it in a bin; never leave it at the water's edge.

Groundbait and unused bait should be disposed of responsibly. Large quantities of unused pellets poured into a lake can create harmful algal blooms by overloading the water with nitrogen. Take excess bait home or use it sparingly over subsequent visits.

The bank should look the same when you leave as when you arrived. Pushed-down reeds, trampled margins, disturbed gravel — all of these signal carelessness and damage the fishery. Experienced anglers rebuild their fishing positions after each session.

## Digital and Social Media Etiquette

The modern dimension of angling etiquette extends to social media. Sharing a location publicly without the permission of the fishery owner or the club that manages it can lead to overcrowding, poaching, and the kind of pressure that destroys good fishing in a season. Many private stretches of chalk stream, sea-trout estuary, and salmon beat are precious precisely because they are not publicised.

Similarly, if you photograph someone else's fish or capture them in a video, ask permission before posting. Their catch, their moment, their decision to share.
