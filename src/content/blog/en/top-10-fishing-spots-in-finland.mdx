Finland is a country defined by water. Nearly eleven percent of its total area is covered by lakes, rivers, and coastal archipelagos, and its 188,000 lakes range from small tarns hidden in coniferous forest to Saimaa — the fourth-largest lake in Europe at 4,400 km². Into these waters runs a fish community shaped by millennia of post-glacial ecology: brown trout, Arctic char, grayling, pike, perch, and a native salmon that still runs a few wild river systems reaching to the Gulf of Bothnia. The country has one of the highest fishing participation rates in Europe — roughly a third of the population holds a fishing licence — and its regulations are designed to balance serious sport with sustainable stock management.

## Tornionjoki and Muonionjoki: The Wild Atlantic Salmon Rivers

The Tornionjoki (Torne River) and its major tributary the Muonionjoki form the longest free-flowing salmon river system in the EU, running 520 km from the Swedish–Finnish border in Tornio northward to the Caledonian headwaters near Kilpisjärvi. Atlantic salmon here average 5–8 kg, with the biggest fish exceeding 15 kg. Unlike most European salmon rivers, the Tornionjoki remains open for legal fly fishing on certain beats, though quotas are strictly enforced and the season varies by beat. The river is shared between Finland and Sweden; anglers need a joint Finnish–Swedish fishing licence obtainable from local tackle shops in Tornio. The salmon run peaks from late June through August. The classic approach is wading the shin-deep gravel shoals with a 13–15 foot Spey rod and swinging a tube fly across the main flow.

## Oulu River (Oulujoki) and the Pyhäkoski Rapids

The Oulujoki, flowing westward from the vast Oulu Lake system to the Gulf of Bothnia, was historically one of Finland's great salmon rivers before dam construction. Today, the compensatory stocking program at Pyhäkoski — an iconic set of Class III–IV rapids about 70 km east of Oulu — produces excellent brown trout and rainbow trout fishing accessible to all anglers with a regional permit. The Pyhäkoski rapids drop 12 metres over 2.5 km, creating fast, oxygenated water ideal for trout. Evening dry-fly fishing in July and August on the main current draws fish of 1–3 kg consistently; the adjacent plunge pools hold larger fish of 4–6 kg accessible only by careful wading or from the bank with long leaders.

## Lake Saimaa and the Vuoksi River: Pike and Perch in Finland's Blue Heart

Saimaa, in southeast Finland, is the country's largest lake and the heart of a connected lake system covering 4,400 km². Its cold, clear water supports healthy stocks of pike up to 12 kg, perch averaging 500 g with specimens to 2 kg, and zander (pikeperch) to 7 kg in the deeper bays. The Vuoksi River, which drains Saimaa southeast into Lake Ladoga in Russia, is one of Finland's premier stream fishing venues; it passes through the city of Imatra, where the Imatrankoski gorge section is managed as a fishable stretch during the summer season. Perch fishing on Saimaa is best in the archipelago sections north of Savonlinna, where the labyrinth of islands and channels concentrates fish on the rock weed edges. Vuoksen kalastuspuisto (Vuoksi Fishing Park) near Imatra provides organized, stocked fishing for families and visitors.

## Kuusamo and the Oulankajoki: Grayling and Brown Trout in Lapland Wilderness

The Kuusamo region in northern Finland, just below the Arctic Circle, contains some of the most spectacular river scenery in the Nordic world. The Oulankajoki, running through Oulanka National Park between Kuusamo and the Russian border, is classified as a Heritage River; its gin-clear water over sand and gravel beds provides perfect habitat for grayling and brown trout. Grayling of 500 g to 1 kg are caught reliably on dry flies throughout the summer. The Kitkajoki tributary and the Kuusinkijoki are also highly productive. Access is along the Karhunkierros Trail (Bear's Ring path), one of Finland's most popular hiking routes, which crosses several fishing beats. Permits are available from the Kuusamo tourist office and the national park headquarters.

## Kemijoki and the Lapland Rivers: Summer Salmon and Winter Ice Fishing

The Kemijoki is Finland's longest river at 550 km, flowing south from Savukoski near the Russian border to the Gulf of Bothnia at Kemi. Although its lower reaches are dammed, the upper Kemijoki and its tributaries — particularly the Ounasjoki and the Kitinen — provide excellent grayling, brown trout, and harling fishing from June through August. In winter, the frozen side channels and backwater ponds support productive ice fishing for perch, burbot, and pike; the Pelkosenniemi and Salla municipalities are popular hubs for winter ice fishing with local guides who operate comfortable heated ice fishing tents (lavvu-style shelters). Kalastuspaikka Lohilaakso near Rovaniemi offers organized salmon and trout fishing on the Ounasjoki.

## Inari and Lake Inarijärvi: Arctic Char and Brown Trout North of the Arctic Circle

Lake Inari (Inarijärvi) in the Finnish far north covers 1,040 km² at 119 metres above sea level and is fed by rivers draining the fells of Saariselkä. It hosts the northernmost char population in Finland — a genetically distinct form of Arctic char that inhabits the cold, oxygen-rich depths and is caught by vertical jigging from boats in summer and from ice holes in winter. Brown trout of 3–6 kg are caught in the river mouths in spring and autumn as fish move between the lake and the spawning tributaries. Access to the northern shores is via the village of Inari; guided trips are available through the Inari fishing guide network. The lake's remote eastern bays, reachable only by boat, hold virtually unfished pike populations where 10 kg fish are considered routine.

## The Tampere Rapids: Urban Grayling Fishing at Tammerkoski

Tammerkoski, the short, fast river running through the centre of Tampere between Näsijärvi and Pyhäjärvi lakes, supports a remarkable urban grayling fishery. The stretch is managed by the Tampere Fishing Club and permits are sold at the local tourism office; anglers fish from the public walkways alongside the historic textile mill buildings. Grayling of 30–40 cm rise reliably to dry flies on summer evenings — the city lights reflected in the dark water creating one of Finland's most unusual angling settings. Joensuun koskikalastusalue (Joensuu Rapid Fishing Area) in North Karelia similarly provides managed rapid fishing in an urban environment, stocked with brown trout and grayling for day visitors.

## Kymijoki Delta: Sea Trout in the Gulf of Finland

The Kymijoki, flowing from Päijänne south through Kouvola to the Gulf of Finland east of Kotka, has one of Finland's strongest sea trout runs. Sea trout (Salmo trutta trutta) enter the river from August through October, averaging 2–4 kg with occasional fish to 8 kg. The delta area near Pyhtää and Kotka provides productive fishing from the shore and from small rowboats on the channels. The river passes through several designated fishing beats managed by the Kymi River Fishing Association; day permits are available online. The estuary area at the river mouth, where freshwater and Baltic salinity mix, is particularly productive in early autumn when sea trout stack up in the lower pools before running upstream.

## The Archipelago Sea: Perch, Pike, and Baltic Sea Trout

Finland's southwestern archipelago — the Saaristomeri National Park area between Turku and the Åland Islands — provides saltwater pike and perch fishing that is unique in northern Europe. Baltic pike (the same species as freshwater pike but sea-adapted) patrol the kelp and bladder-wrack beds at depths of 1–3 metres in June and July; they are sight-fished from kayaks or small motorboats with large surface poppers and soft-plastic lures. The Swedish-speaking community of Pargas (Parainen) has a strong sport fishing culture; Mosavatten fiskeplats and Stipik flugfiskeplats near Pargas are well-known local venues for organized fly fishing for sea-run brown trout and perch in the archipelago channels.

## Wilderness Rivers of Inari-Sápmi: Permit-Free Fishing in Lapland's Headwaters

Under Finnish law, fishing for some species — including small perch, small pike, small roach, and vendace — is free of permit requirements in most waters. The small headwater streams and ponds of Lapland, particularly in the Kaldoaivi Wilderness Area north of Inari and the Lemmenjoki valley, provide genuinely wild fishing for grayling, brown trout, and whitefish accessible to any visitor who reaches them on foot. These rivers receive so little fishing pressure that the fish are unsophisticated; a small elk-hair caddis pattern on a 3-weight rod can produce grayling rises on every pool. The Jeesiöjoen esteetön kalastuspaikka (Jeesiö River Accessible Fishing Site) near Sodankylä provides barrier-free access with floating docks for anglers with mobility limitations.
