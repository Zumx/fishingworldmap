Pakistan is one of Asia's great undiscovered angling countries. The same land that holds five peaks above 8,000 metres also holds the golden mahseer — a freshwater fighter once called the tiger of the river — plus brown and rainbow trout introduced to Himalayan streams a century ago, vast Indus reservoirs full of carp, and an Arabian Sea coastline where sailfish and giant trevally patrol within day-trip range of Karachi. Infrastructure is thin and information thinner, which is exactly why the fishing remains so good. Here are the ten places that justify the journey.

## 1. The Poonch River, Azad Kashmir

The Poonch, flowing out of the Pir Panjal range toward Mangla reservoir, is Pakistan's flagship mahseer water and has been managed as a dedicated mahseer national park since 2010. Golden mahseer here take spinners and chunky plugs fished across boulder runs, and a good fish will strip fifty metres of line in its first surge downstream. The best windows are the pre-monsoon weeks of April to early June and again from late September, when the water clears. Local permission arrangements apply, so go through a Pakistani outfitter.

## 2. The Indus at Attock

Where the blue-grey Kabul River collides with the green Indus below Attock Fort, two currents and two colours meet in one of the country's most dramatic fishing scenes. The confluence holds mahseer, the huge Indus catfish locally called malli, and silver mullah carp. Fishing is from rock ledges with heavy spinning gear; winter brings the clearest water. The site sits in a historically sensitive area, so carry identification and fish with local contacts.

## 3. The Kunhar River and Kaghan Valley

The Kunhar tumbles 166 km through the Kaghan Valley in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, and its upper reaches around Naran are stocked-and-wild trout water set beneath 4,000-metre ridgelines. Brown trout hold in pocket water from Naran up toward Batakundi, while the glacial Lake Saiful Muluk at 3,224 m adds high-altitude scenery to the trip. The official trout season runs roughly April to September; permits are sold by the KP fisheries department in Naran bazaar at modest cost.

## 4. The Swat Valley: Kalam, Ushu and Mahodand Lake

North of Kalam, the Ushu and Gabral forks of the Swat River drain pine forests beneath the Hindu Kush, and both are classic trout streams — cold, quick and gin-clear by autumn. The road ends near Mahodand Lake at about 2,860 m, where rainbow trout cruise the margins of a postcard alpine basin. Summer (June to August) is peak tourist season and the valley gets busy; September is quieter and the fishing often better.

## 5. Phander Lake and the Ghizer River, Gilgit-Baltistan

Many Pakistani anglers will tell you the country's best trout water is the Ghizer River system west of Gilgit. Phander Lake — locally nicknamed Little Kashmir — and the connected river hold brown trout to impressive sizes in water so clear the fish are visible from the bluffs above. The single road onward crosses the 3,719 m Shandur Pass, home of the famous mountain polo festival each July. Trout permits come from the Gilgit-Baltistan fisheries department.

## 6. The Skardu basin: Kachura lakes

In Baltistan, the two Kachura lakes near Skardu — Upper Kachura and the resort-fringed Lower Kachura, better known as Shangrila — sit at about 2,500 m among apricot orchards and granite walls. Both hold trout, as do nearby tributaries of the Indus and the Shigar River. Skardu's airport makes this the most accessible corner of the high mountains, and anglers can pair fishing with trips toward the Deosai Plateau, whose streams also carry trout in summer.

## 7. Tarbela and Mangla reservoirs

Pakistan's two giant dams are its biggest stillwaters: Tarbela on the Indus covers roughly 250 square kilometres, and Mangla on the Jhelum is not far behind. Both hold rohu, catla and mrigal carp, mahseer around the inflows, and big catfish in the old river channels. Local boatmen work from villages along the shores; winter mornings, when the water is low and clear, are the productive time. These are commercial fisheries first, so sport anglers should arrange access through local fishing clubs.

## 8. Keenjhar Lake, Sindh

About 120 km from Karachi, Keenjhar (Kalri) Lake is one of Pakistan's largest freshwater lakes, some 24 km long, and a Ramsar wetland alive with wintering birds. It supplies Karachi's drinking water and supports generations of Mohana fisher families. For visiting anglers it offers relaxed float fishing for rohu and other indigenous carp from hired boats, best in the cool months from November to February when temperatures in lower Sindh become pleasant.

## 9. The Indus delta creeks: Korangi and Kharo Chhan

Southeast of Karachi the Indus dissolves into a labyrinth of mangrove creeks, and this is Pakistan's most exciting light-tackle saltwater fishing. From Korangi Creek on Karachi's edge down to the remote delta channels around Kharo Chhan in Thatta district, the tidal network holds barramundi (locally bhekti), mangrove jack, grunter and mud crabs the size of dinner plates. Fish the last of the ebb around creek mouths with soft plastics. October to March offers the kindest weather; local boat operators run from Korangi and Ibrahim Hyderi.

## 10. Offshore Karachi and Charna Island

An hour west of the city, the waters around Charna Island and the drop-offs beyond Mubarak Village hold genuine blue-water sport: sailfish in the post-monsoon months, longtail tuna, queenfish, cobia and giant trevally around the rocky pinnacles. Karachi's charter scene has grown quickly, with day boats out of the Marina Club and Mubarak Village. November to March is prime, when seas calm down after the southwest monsoon and bait balls push close to the island.

## Seasons, permits and practicalities

Pakistan's fishing year splits cleanly: high-mountain trout from April to September, mahseer in the shoulder months around the monsoon, and everything in Sindh — lakes, creeks and offshore — from October to March. Provincial fisheries departments issue inexpensive trout and freshwater permits, while coastal fishing is informally managed. Mahseer are protected in several areas, so release them. Hire local guides everywhere; they handle permissions, security formalities in border regions, and the boats themselves.
