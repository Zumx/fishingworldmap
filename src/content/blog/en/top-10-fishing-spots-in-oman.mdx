Oman sits at the meeting point of the Arabian Sea, the Gulf of Oman, and the narrow straits of the Musandam Peninsula, and the result is a diversity of marine habitats that few countries can match. Upwellings off the Dhofar coast in southern Oman cool surface temperatures and push nutrients upward, supporting tuna, kingfish, and billfish in quantities that serious sport fishers dream about. In the north, the Musandam's limestone fjords — locally called khors — channel currents that concentrate fish and create some of the most dramatic scenery in the Arabian Peninsula. Oman's fishing culture is ancient: wooden dhows have worked these seas for over two thousand years, and the tradition is very much alive.

## Musandam Peninsula: Fjord Fishing for Giant Trevally

The Musandam Peninsula, an Omani exclave separated from the rest of the country by the UAE, thrusts into the Strait of Hormuz and creates a geography unique in the Arabian world. The khors — narrow, steep-sided inlets carved from pale limestone — reach depths of over 100 m and channel currents from the deep Gulf around the rocky headlands. Giant trevally (GT) are the prized quarry here, and fish to 40 kg have been landed on surface poppers and stickbaits worked over the submerged shelves at first light. The diving and erupting of GT in flat, clear water against a backdrop of bare white cliffs is an experience burned into the memory of everyone who encounters it.

## Muscat and the Al Musanah Fishing Jetty

Around Muscat, the capital, a number of established fishing jetties and boat launch sites serve both local anglers and visiting sport fishers. The Al Musanah Fishing Jetty, roughly 120 km south-west of Muscat, is one of the most productive shore-based spots on the Gulf of Oman coast. Kingfish (narrow-barred Spanish mackerel) are caught here year-round, with the best action from October to April when the fish move inshore. Large queenfish and cobia also feature regularly. The jetty infrastructure is good, and the wider Musanah area supports a small fleet of charter boats for offshore work.

## Azaiba Beach Fishing Spot

Azaiba Beach, on Muscat's western outskirts, is a classic shore-fishing location that has been productive for generations of anglers. The sandy beach gives way to rocky outcrops that hold grouper, snappers, and emperors. Cast-netting and handline fishing are traditional here, but spinning for queenfish and barracuda along the rocky sections is increasingly popular. The spot is accessible, free, and productive — a combination that keeps it busy through the cooler months.

## The Dhofar Coast: Tuna and Billfish off Salalah

The Dhofar region in southern Oman, centred on Salalah, is cooled by the Indian Ocean monsoon (khareef) from June to September, creating an upwelling of cold, nutrient-rich water off the coast. This phenomenon drives one of the most productive marine ecosystems in the Arabian Sea. Yellowfin tuna to 80 kg, wahoo, mahi-mahi, and blue marlin are all within range of charter boats operating out of Salalah Port. The best offshore fishing is from October to May, before and after the monsoon, when seas calm and the pelagic species concentrate near the continental shelf edge.

## Sur: Traditional Dhow Country

Sur, on Oman's eastern coast at the tip of Ras al-Hadd, is the historical heartland of Omani dhow-building. The lagoon at Sur, where the wadi Sarfait meets the sea, is a sheltered spot for hand-lining and cast-netting that local families have used for generations. Offshore from Sur, the waters near Masirah Island to the south are famous for shark, kingfish, and large GT. The traditional fishing methods here — long-lines set from wooden boats, fish traps placed on the reef — are still in active use alongside modern sport-fishing techniques.

## Masirah Island: Offshore Wilderness

Masirah Island, 65 km off Oman's eastern coast, lies within a unique marine zone where deep-water upwellings create exceptional fishing. The island itself has no major development, which keeps the fishing pressure low and the fish large. Kingfish, cobia, queenfish, grouper, and various trevally species are taken from small boats launched off the island's beaches. The waters around the northern tip of Masirah can be rough, particularly during the south-west monsoon, but calm conditions between November and April offer excellent sport.

## Ras al-Hadd and the Arabian Sea Drop-off

At Ras al-Hadd, the easternmost point of the Arabian Peninsula, the seabed drops steeply from the continental shelf into the Arabian Sea. This proximity to deep water creates the conditions for large-scale tuna and billfish aggregations close inshore. Local fishermen have exploited these grounds for centuries. Charter services based in Sur cover the Ras al-Hadd grounds and offer trips for wahoo, tuna, and marlin to visiting anglers willing to make the journey from Muscat or Salalah.

## Daymaniyat Islands: Reef Fishing in a Marine Reserve

The Daymaniyat Islands, about 18 km offshore from Barka (between Muscat and Musanah), are a protected marine reserve and one of Oman's most impressive reef systems. Fishing within the reserve's core zone is prohibited, but the reef edges and surrounding waters outside the protected area hold large grouper, snapper, and the occasional kingfish that patrols the reef drop-offs. Dive-boat operators based in Muscat offer combination snorkel/fishing trips that include fishing in the permitted zones.

## Khasab and the Khor al-Sham Inlet

The town of Khasab in Musandam is the access point for the spectacular Khor al-Sham — at 35 km the longest of the region's khors. Boat trips into the khor pass through dolphin-rich waters and access fishing spots where grouper and snapper congregate in the deeper pool sections. GT, barracuda, and kingfish patrol the open sections of the khor where current flow is strongest. Khasab has established boat charter services, and the combination of scenery and fish makes it a top destination for visiting anglers.

## Practical Notes and Dhow Fishing Traditions

Oman has relatively relaxed regulations for recreational sea fishing from boats, but a commercial fishing licence is required for netting. The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Water Resources (the ministry known locally as wazarat al-tharwa al-zira'iya wal-samakiya wa mawwarid al-miyah) sets seasons and size limits for key commercial species. For visiting anglers, the most straightforward approach is to book through established charter operators based in Muscat, Khasab, or Salalah. Traditional dhow fishing excursions can be arranged through local tour operators — these typically combine slow-trolling for kingfish with handline fishing for reef species, following methods unchanged since the Portuguese era.
