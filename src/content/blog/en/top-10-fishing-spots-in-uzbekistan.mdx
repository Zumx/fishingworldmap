Uzbekistan is not a country most anglers associate with fishing, yet its waters tell a rich and complicated story. The Aral Sea disaster of the twentieth century devastated the lower Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers, but the upper stretches of both systems remain productive, and the mountain tributaries draining the western Tian Shan and Pamir-Alay ranges into Uzbek territory hold trout, marinka, and endemic species in their cold, clear headwaters. The Charvak Reservoir outside Tashkent is the centrepiece of Uzbek sport fishing — a vast highland lake with clear blue water, surrounded by mountains, that holds pike-perch of genuine trophy size and large, powerful carp.

## Charvak Reservoir: Pike-perch and Carp Paradise

The Charvak Reservoir was created in 1970 by the Charvak Dam on the Chirchiq River, at the point where three mountain tributaries — the Pskem, Koksu, and Chatkal — converge in the western Tian Shan. The reservoir covers approximately 37 km² and sits at around 920 m above sea level, surrounded by pine-forested ridges rising to over 2,000 m. The water is startlingly clear and cold, fed year-round by glacial melt from the higher ranges. Pike-perch up to 8 kg are caught regularly by jig anglers working the submerged channels and rock structures in 10–20 m of water. Common carp of 12–18 kg are present throughout the main basin and can be targeted with boilies and natural bait. Local fishing spots include venues marketed as Навес (a shaded fishing platform) and Беседка (a gazebo-style lakeside fishing hut).

## The Chatkal River: Mountain Trout

The Chatkal River, which feeds Charvak from the east before the dam was built and still enters the reservoir as a cold tributary, is one of Uzbekistan's finest trout rivers. The Chatkal National Nature Reserve protects the upper catchment, where the river flows through narrow forested gorges at 1,500–2,500 m. Brown trout introduced in the Soviet era have established a wild population in the cold, fast reaches above the reservoir. A special permit from the reserve administration is required to fish within protected zones, but the lower reaches outside the reserve are accessible to licensed anglers and hold fish up to 40 cm.

## The Pskem River: Remote Marinka and Trout

The Pskem Valley, accessible from the town of Chorvoq near Charvak, is a long, narrow river valley cutting deep into the western Tian Shan. The Pskem River flows fast and cold through stands of walnut, apple, and wild cherry forest — one of the most distinctive landscapes in the region. Marinka (Schizothorax species) are the native species here, along with brown trout in the upper reaches. The valley is best accessed by 4x4 from May to October, and guesthouses in the villages offer basic accommodation for anglers.

## The Syr Darya: Endemic Species and Carp

The Syr Darya — the ancient Jaxartes river — enters Uzbekistan from Tajikistan and flows across the Fergana Valley before turning north-west through the desert. The upper Syr Darya in the Fergana region still holds a diverse fish community including large common carp, catfish, and several endemic Central Asian species that have survived despite significant water extraction. The river around Fergana City and Kokand is the most productive stretch for coarse fishing. Traditional fishing from wooden boats using long-lines is still practised by local families.

## Айдаркул (Aidarkul) Lake: Desert Fishing Surprise

Lake Aidarkul (also written Aydarkul) in the Kyzylkum Desert, formed accidentally in the 1960s when excess water from the Syr Darya irrigation system was diverted into a natural depression, has grown to cover over 4,000 km². It now holds massive populations of carp, sazan (wild carp), catfish, and bream — all from natural colonisation via the connecting channels. The lake is remote and sees very little fishing pressure. Local fishing camps at the lake's edge offer basic accommodation and boat access. Carp to 15 kg and catfish to 30 kg are realistic targets.

## Лодка and Daryo Fish Venues near Tashkent

Within easy reach of Tashkent, several commercial fishing venues operate along the Chirchiq River below Charvak. Venues such as Лодка (Russian for "boat") and Daryo Fish (TCT Fish Cluster) offer pay-to-fish sessions with stocked rainbow trout, carp, and channel catfish. While they are managed commercial operations rather than wild fisheries, they provide accessible sport for residents and visitors and serve as reliable options when river conditions are unsuitable.

## The Kokand Area on the Syr Darya

The stretch of the Syr Darya around Kokand in the Fergana Valley is one of the most traditionally important fishing areas in Uzbekistan. Large bream, carp, and occasional catfish are the main species. Venues like DARYO BOLIQ (a local fishing point name translating roughly to "river fish") and MAXMUD BOLIQXONA represent the informal local network of established fishing spots along the river, known to local anglers and accessible via informal arrangement.

## Chashma-Ayub and Bukhara Oasis Waters

The ancient oasis city of Bukhara is surrounded by an extensive irrigation and canal network dating back thousands of years. These channels and artificial ponds hold carp and catfish that have thrived in the warm, productive water for generations. The fishing is low-key and informal, but productive for anyone who takes the time to explore the canal banks with light tackle. The contrast of fishing within sight of Bukhara's ancient mud-brick architecture is an experience unique to Central Asia.

## The Nurek Reservoir (Tajikistan/Uzbekistan boundary)

While primarily in Tajikistan, the Nurek Reservoir on the Vakhsh River influences fishing along the lower Amu Darya system, which forms the Uzbek border downstream. Migratory species including large asp and catfish move through the connected river system, and Uzbek anglers working the Amu Darya near Termez report exceptional catfish sessions.

## Рыбхоз (Fish Farm) Channels near Tashkent

The Рыбхоз (rybkhoz — fish farm) system that operates in many of Uzbekistan's irrigated lowlands supports a network of managed fish-production channels and ponds. Many of these are accessible for day fishing with permission and hold large carp and grass carp. The channels in the Tashkent Region and Fergana Valley are the most productive and easiest to access for visiting anglers.

## Best Seasons and Practical Tips

Uzbekistan's best fishing is between April and October. The summer months can be very hot in the lowlands — the Fergana Valley regularly reaches 40°C in July — so the mountain venues at Charvak, Chatkal, and Pskem are preferable in summer. Spring and autumn are excellent everywhere. Fishing permits are required for most official waters and can be obtained through the Committee for Nature Protection. Many local anglers fish without formal permits on informal stretches; hiring a local guide is the safest way to navigate the regulations.
