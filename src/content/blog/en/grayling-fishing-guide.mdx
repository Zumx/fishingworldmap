When the last of the autumn leaves have gone and most coarse fishing rivers are running cold and slack, the European grayling — Thymallus thymallus — is at its finest. This elegant salmonid with its iridescent dorsal sail and faint scent of thyme thrives in clear, cold, fast-flowing water and feeds with increasing confidence as water temperatures drop through October and November into full winter. On the Test in Hampshire, the Itchen in the Meon Valley, the Traun in Austria, or the Soca in Slovenia, skilled fly fishers are watching indicator nymphs tick down through crystalline pools while most other anglers have packed away their gear entirely. The grayling's willingness to feed in water as cold as 2°C makes it the ultimate winter quarry.

## Why Winter is the Best Time for Grayling

Grayling feed on aquatic invertebrates throughout the year, but their behaviour changes in winter in ways that benefit the angler. As water temperatures cool, nymphs and larvae move down through the gravel and concentrate in the deeper slots and seams of the riverbed. Grayling respond by forming compact shoals holding tight to the bottom in these productive feeding lanes — and crucially, they feed persistently and competitively when in these shoals. Brown trout, which compete for the same nymphs in summer, are largely inactive in cold conditions and pose no distraction. On chalk streams like the Kennet in Berkshire, grayling are actively targeted by fly fishers from October through February, and bag returns on controlled beats show that winter days regularly produce 15 or 20 fish to a competent nymph angler.

## Understanding Grayling Lies

Grayling are bottom feeders and hold differently to trout. While a brown trout often stations behind a boulder or weed bed in mid-water, grayling prefer to lie directly on the gravel riverbed in moderate to fast currents, using the slight dead zone just above the stones. Look for channels between weed beds where clear gravel is exposed, the fast seams at the head of pools, and deeper slots where the current accelerates slightly over a submerged ridge. On alpine rivers like the Inn in Tyrol or the Salzach in Salzburg, grayling concentrate in the gravel runs and glides between boulder fields, which are easy to spot in the clear mountain water.

## French Leader Technique

The French leader, or long-leader technique, revolutionised nymph fishing for grayling across Europe over the past two decades. The system uses a very long, monofilament leader — typically 8 to 12 metres — attached directly to the fly line with no intermediate braided section, so the leader can be kept almost entirely off the water surface. This dramatically reduces drag and allows nymphs to sink quickly and drift freely through the exact current lane where the fish are holding. Two or three small tungsten-bead nymphs are tied on short droppers; sizes 14 to 18 are typical. The method requires upstream or across-and-up casting followed by a high rod-hold to keep the leader elevated. Strikes are detected visually through a brightly coloured sighter section built into the leader material, typically in fluoro-yellow or orange, placed about 50 centimetres above the top dropper.

## Duo Method: Dry Fly and Nymph Combined

The duo method — sometimes called the New Zealand rig or the klink-and-dink — suspends a nymph below a buoyant dry fly. The dry fly serves both as a presentation fly and as a visual indicator. For grayling in autumn, a size 14 Elk Hair Caddis or a para-shuttlecock with a 40 to 60 centimetre drop to a pheasant tail nymph or PTN variant is highly effective. The dry must be properly waterproofed with Mucilin or a silicone spray; grayling takes are often delicate, with the dry merely dipping rather than being pulled under, and a firm but controlled strike is needed. On rivers like the Wylye in Wiltshire or the Kennet at Hungerford, this method accounts for large numbers of fish from September through November before water temperatures make the pure nymph approach more productive.

## Best European Waters for Grayling

The UK's chalk streams — the Test, Itchen, Kennet, Wylye, and Bourne — provide some of the finest and most accessible grayling fishing in Europe. The Test at Stockbridge and the Itchen above Winchester hold substantial populations accessible through syndicate membership or day-ticket bookings. Continental Europe offers extraordinary alternatives. The Traun in upper Austria between Gmunden and Bad Ischl is considered by many European fly fishers to be the finest grayling river on the continent. The Soca in northwestern Slovenia produces exceptional grayling alongside its famous marble trout. The San River in southeastern Poland holds grayling throughout its Bieszczady mountain reaches, as does the Dunajec along the Polish-Slovak border. In Scandinavia, the Glomma in Norway and the Klarälven in Sweden have significant grayling populations in their upper reaches.

## Tackle and Equipment

A single-handed rod of 9 to 10 feet rated for a 3 or 4 weight line is ideal for French leader fishing; the lighter weight improves line control during the high-rod hold. Fluorocarbon tippet in diameters of 0.10 to 0.14 millimetres is standard for the point fly on chalk streams; its density helps nymphs sink quickly. Tungsten-bead nymphs are indispensable — a jig-hook nymph with a slotted tungsten bead in sizes 12 to 16 settles through the water column more cleanly than a conventional hook and is less likely to snag. Essential patterns include the Hare's Ear nymph, the Pheasant Tail, the Perdigon in its various colour combinations, and the Zebra Midge for very cold conditions when midges are the primary food item.

## Conservation and Regulations

Grayling have a complex relationship with fishery management. Trout-focused river managers have historically regarded grayling as a pest species because they compete with trout for food and spawning gravel, and many chalk stream fisheries removed them aggressively in the twentieth century. Contemporary understanding is more nuanced: grayling are a native salmonid with genuine ecological value, and their populations on many rivers are now specifically managed and protected. Most chalk stream beats issue strict bag limits and encourage catch-and-release. Regulations vary considerably by country and river; always check current rules before fishing.
