There is no off-season in world fishing — only anglers standing in the wrong hemisphere. While Northern Europe's rivers close for spawning in November, Patagonia's brown trout season is just opening; while August heat sends Mediterranean fish deep, Iceland's salmon rivers are at their absolute prime. The planet runs a continuous relay of runs, hatches, spawns and migrations, and a traveller who understands the calendar can fish twelve peak months a year. This planner walks through the year season by season, naming the windows that guides book out twelve months in advance and the shoulder periods where bargains hide.

## January to February: the southern summer and the ice

Deep winter up north is high summer below the equator. Patagonia is at its peak: the Limay and Chimehuin in Argentina fish superbly for big browns, and Tierra del Fuego's Rio Grande holds the world's most famous run of sea-run brown trout, with fish averaging 4 to 7 kilograms. New Zealand's South Island offers its legendary sight-fishing for wild browns in gin-clear water — January and February bring stable flows and cicada falls that pull big fish to the surface. Meanwhile the northern hemisphere offers its own cult disciplines: ice fishing for perch, pike and Arctic char across Scandinavia and the Great Lakes, and Florida's winter season, when sailfish stack off Stuart and big seatrout feed in the shallows of Mosquito Lagoon.

## March to April: the northern wake-up

As northern waters warm past 8 to 10 °C, the year's first great hatches begin. In Europe, the trout season opens in mid-March in much of the UK and April elsewhere; large dark olives and March browns bring the first proper dry-fly days on rivers like the Eden and the Usk. April is prime time for Czech and Slovak grayling waters reopening, and Dutch and German pike fishing closes as spawning begins — a reminder to check closed seasons carefully. In the salt, the Bahamas and Cuba hit their bonefish prime with mild winds, and Australia's Northern Territory enters the run-off: March to April after the wet season is the storied window when barramundi feed in flooded creek mouths.

## May to June: arguably the world's richest window

If you can travel in only one period, choose late May to June. Europe's mayfly carnival peaks — the famous Duffer's Fortnight on English chalkstreams like the Test and Itchen, and the spectacular Tisza mayfly bloom in Hungary. Slovenia's Soča valley opens fully for marble trout. Iceland's sea trout and early salmon stir, and Norway's salmon rivers — the Gaula, the Orkla, the Alta — open in June with the heaviest fish of the year running on high water. Across the Atlantic, Alaska's king salmon arrive in June, and the bonefish-permit-tarpon grand slam season peaks in the Florida Keys, where the tarpon migration rolls through in May and June. The Indian Ocean adds its trump card: the Seychelles outer atolls fish brilliantly for giant trevally before the southeast trades strengthen.

## July to August: high summer strategies

High summer divides the world into early risers and travellers. In southern Europe, heat pushes river fish into dawn and dusk windows, and responsible anglers carry thermometers — above roughly 19 to 20 °C, trout fishing should stop. The smart moves are north and up: Lapland and northern Norway enjoy 24-hour daylight with grayling and char feeding around the clock, Iceland's salmon season peaks in July, and Alpine lakes above 1500 metres fish all day. Mongolia's taimen season runs strongly from June and pauses in mid-summer heat on some rivers, resuming in September. The salt thrives: Cape Cod's striped bass blitz, European bass along Atlantic coasts, bluefin tuna off Ireland and in the Skagerrak, and the dorado run through Mediterranean late summer.

## September to October: the golden months

Autumn may be the finest all-round travel season. Brown trout feed hard before spawning — the last weeks before closures in Europe and the eastern United States produce the year's largest river fish. Mongolia's taimen fishing peaks in September and October as water cools, with fish over 120 centimetres taken on surface mouse patterns. British Columbia's steelhead enter the Skeena system, and Atlantic Canada's Miramichi runs with autumn salmon. Northern pike across Scandinavia and the Baltic archipelagos go on their pre-winter binge — October in the Stockholm archipelago is a metre-fish lottery. In the tropics, the Seychelles re-open as trades ease in October, and Costa Rica's Pacific coast builds toward its sailfish winter.

## November to December: closing one book, opening another

As northern rivers close to protect spawning trout and salmon, attention swings south and to the sea. New Zealand and Patagonia open in earnest — November on the Tongariro or an early-season Chilean spring creek offers fresh, uneducated fish. The Indian Ocean enters its calm-season prime: November to March is the classic window for Christmas Island bonefish and Maldives flats. Big-game crews converge on Central America, with Guatemala's sailfish numbers from November onward among the highest on the planet. In Europe, sea fishing carries the flag: winter cod from Norwegian shores, and the famous skrei migration building toward its January-to-March peak around Lofoten. Zander and pike fishing on lowland rivers and lakes stays excellent until ice.

## How to use the calendar like a guide

Three rules turn this calendar into successful trips. First, book the peak, but consider the shoulders: the week before a famous window often fishes nearly as well at half the price and a fraction of the pressure. Second, always cross-check local closed seasons and licence rules — a country can be in peak season while a specific river is closed, and spawning closures shift year to year with water temperatures. Third, build in weather slack: any trip shorter than five days in a migratory fishery is a coin flip, so plan six to eight days where runs and tides, not luck, decide the result. The calendar gives you the odds; trip design collects on them.
