In June, for a few evenings only, the Tisza River erupts: millions of long-tailed mayflies hatch at once in the famous tiszavirágzás, the blooming of the Tisza, and every fish in the river feeds like it is the last meal on earth. Hungary is a landlocked country that thinks about fish constantly — fisherman's soup has protected cultural status, the word for angler (horgász) is everywhere, and its day-ticket lakes have produced carp at the very edge of the world record books. For visiting anglers it is cheap, organised and seriously underrated.

## Lake Balaton: fogas, the noble zander

Central Europe's largest lake (592 km², 77 km long) is Hungary's inland sea, and its silky, milky-green water grows the country's most celebrated fish: the Balaton zander, sold under the protected name fogas. Anglers troll and jig the deeper northern basins off Tihany and Badacsony, while shore anglers fish bream and carp from hundreds of public piers and the classic hotel stages — the stég is a Balaton institution. Spring and late autumn are zander prime time; a national ticket plus Balaton permit covers it all.

## The Tisza: catfish river of the Great Plain

Hungary's second river is its wildest, looping through the Alföld plain past Szeged and Szolnok. It holds the country's best wels catfish stocks — fish over 80 kg are caught most seasons — alongside zander, asp and barbel in the faster bends. The June mayfly blooming is both a natural wonder and a feeding trigger anglers plan trips around. Riverside camps near Tiszafüred, Csongrád and Mindszent cater specifically to catfish hunters with boats and bank swims.

## Lake Tisza: the flooded forest labyrinth

Created by the Kisköre dam in 1973, Lake Tisza (127 km²) is a mosaic of channels, dead arms, water-lily fields and drowned timber — closer to a temperate Everglades than a reservoir. It is arguably Hungary's most exciting pike and zander water and a bass-style playground for lure anglers in kayaks and small boats. Carp anglers target the deeper bays around Abádszalók and Poroszló, where the modern visitor centre and bird reserve make it a genuine family destination too.

## Maconka reservoir: where Hungarian carp records live

The Maconkai víztározó at Bátonyterenye, in the Mátra foothills, is the most famous big-carp day-ticket water in the country, run with strict rules from its lakeside halőrház (bailiff house). It has produced common carp far beyond 30 kg, national-record class fish, and its competition pedigree draws teams from across Europe. Swims are bookable, sacks and unhooking standards are policed, and the surrounding hills make it one of Hungary's prettiest sessions.

## Euro Aqua and the trophy-lake scene

Hungary's private specimen lakes rewrote world carp fishing: Euro Aqua near Nemesvita, a strictly limited syndicate-style water, produced a 51.2 kg common carp in 2018 — a world record. Around it operates a whole ecosystem of bookable trophy waters and friendly local lakes alike, from the Benta horgászpark south-west of Budapest to village waters like the Moby Dick horgásztó and the Joker Horgásztó, where a family can fish for dinner-sized carp beside specimen hunters. The horgásztó — the angling pond — is a Hungarian institution in every county.

## The Danube: from Budapest's embankments to the Szigetköz

The Hungarian Danube runs 417 km, and you can catch zander in sight of the Parliament building — urban anglers fish the Pest embankments and Kopaszi dam area year-round. Upstream, the Szigetköz branch system near Győr is a 375 km maze of side arms holding pike, asp and huge ide; downstream, the warm shallows of the Ráckeve-Soroksár Danube arm (RSD) form a managed 57 km fishery famous for carp and catfish. Asp boils at dawn around bridge pillars are a Budapest summer spectacle.

## Lake Velence and Palotaváros: city-easy carping

Shallow, warm Lake Velence (26 km²) between Budapest and Székesfehérvár may be Hungary's most accessible carp water, with bookable platform swims at Agárd and Gárdony and 40 minutes of motorway from the capital. In Székesfehérvár itself, the Palotavárosi lakes — including the Palotavárosi Felső-tó — show the Hungarian urban model: tidy, club-managed ponds with serious carp inside a city park. Both venues suit anglers who want bent rods without an expedition.

## The Körös rivers: quiet giants of the south-east

The Hármas-Körös and its tributaries near Gyula and Békésszentandrás are connoisseur water: slow, tree-lined rivers with outstanding zander and catfish fishing and far fewer visitors than the Tisza. Backwaters like the Kákafoki holtág (oxbow) at Szarvas — kilometres of protected dead arm — fish like rich lakes for carp and pike. This is where Hungarian match and feeder specialists quietly train, which tells you everything about the bream and crucian stocks.

## Reservoir trout and grayling: the hills have streams too

Hungary is plains country, but the Bükk and Mátra hills hide cold streams — the Szinva and the managed sections of the Garadna valley near Lillafüred hold brown trout, with a famous trout farm and fly-fishing stretch below the Hámori lake. These are small, technical waters, more about charm than size, and they pair perfectly with the wine cellars of Eger half an hour away. A speciality detour rather than a destination — but a lovely one.

## Practicalities: tickets, seasons and etiquette

Visitors need the national fishing licence (állami horgászjegy) plus a water-specific area ticket, all purchasable online through the federation system; commercial lakes simply sell day tickets on site. Closed seasons protect zander and pike in early spring and catfish in May-June on natural waters, with size and bag limits enforced by bailiffs who do check. Hungarian anglers are formidably skilled and generally welcoming — learn the word fogas, accept the offered pálinka, and information flows.
