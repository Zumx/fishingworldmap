Spain's fishing diversity is one of the great underappreciated secrets of European angling. A country of continental scale — 504,000 km² — with a geography that swings from Atlantic-facing Galicia and the Basque Country to the semi-arid Meseta and the warm Mediterranean coast, Spain hosts freshwater and saltwater species in an almost bewildering range. The Ebro River system has produced wels catfish world records; the Pyrenean rivers hold wild brown trout of exceptional genetic purity; the Strait of Gibraltar sees one of the Atlantic's most dramatic bluefin tuna migrations; and the Cantabrian coast runs salmon rivers that rival their Scottish counterparts. Regulations have tightened in recent years to protect declining stocks, but this has also improved the quality of the fishing that remains.

## River Ebro — Wels Catfish Capital of Europe

The Ebro is Spain's most voluminous river, rising in the Cantabrian Mountains and flowing east for 930 km to the Mediterranean near Tarragona. The middle and lower Ebro — particularly the stretch between Zaragoza and Mequinensa — has become the world's premier destination for wels catfish (*Silurus glanis*). The species was introduced in the 1970s, found the warm, deep, productive water ideal, and grew to astonishing sizes. Fish over 2 metres and 100 kg have been caught; the current European rod-caught record was set on the Ebro. Local guides operate from Mequinensa (known as the Catfish Capital) and Caspe, running session fishing with night rods, belly rigs, and pellet groundbait methods developed specifically for Ebro conditions. Species diversity is also high: barbel, carp over 20 kg, pike, and zander all inhabit the system.

## Pyrenean Trout Rivers — Brown Trout in Mountain Clarity

The upper Pyrenean valleys on the Spanish side harbour some of Europe's most pristine wild brown trout fishing. The rivers Noguera Pallaresa, Gallego, Aragón, and Bidasoa all rise from permanent snowmelt and glacial springs, running crystal-clear through limestone and granite gorges at altitudes from 600 to 1,400 m. Brown trout in these rivers are predominantly wild genetically, averaging 25–35 cm with occasional fish over 50 cm in the larger pools. The season typically runs from March through October; the classic technique is upstream dry fly or nymph, though wet fly works on the Bidasoa and Aragón after spring high water. Several sections carry conservation status and fish-only designations requiring single-barbless-hook fishing.

## Strait of Gibraltar — Atlantic Bluefin Tuna

The narrow passage between Spain's Punta de Tarifa and the Moroccan coast is the scene of one of the Atlantic's most extraordinary annual fish migrations. Atlantic bluefin tuna (*Thunnus thynnus*) pour through the Strait of Gibraltar from late May through July, moving from the Atlantic into the Mediterranean to spawn. Fish typically weigh 80–200 kg in this run, with giants over 300 kg not uncommon. Sport fishing for bluefin in the Strait is regulated under Spanish and EU quotas; most visiting anglers fish on tag-and-release trips that count against the quota but return the fish alive. Charter boats from Algeciras, Tarifa, and La Línea de la Concepción specialize in this fishery; heavy stand-up or chair tackle is used with live or dead mackerel, sardine, and calamari.

## Cantabrian Coast Salmon Rivers — The Narcea and Sella

The green, rain-drenched rivers of Asturias and Cantabria hold Atlantic salmon runs that are among the last healthy ones on the Iberian Peninsula. The Rivers Narcea, Sella, Pas, and Nansa all receive spring and summer salmon runs from May through August. The Narcea near Tineo is particularly renowned; its lower beats around Cornellana and Salas are managed with high conservation standards and limited rod days. The traditional method is fly fishing with tube flies or singles, though spinning is permitted outside the fly-only periods. Sea trout (*reo*) run the same rivers alongside salmon and are considered almost equally prized by Spanish river anglers.

## Balearic Islands — Dentex, Grouper, and Amberjack

The island waters of Mallorca, Menorca, Ibiza, and Formentera rise above a rocky Mediterranean seabed studded with submarine peaks and underwater caves that shelter dentex (*Dentex dentex*) — a powerful, deep-bodied predator that fights far harder than its equivalent size in northern European waters. Fish of 4–8 kg are caught by Balearic boats jigging or using live bait over the deep-water pinnacles, sometimes called *baixes*. Grouper (*Epinephelus marginatus*), now protected and recovering from historical depletion, is catch-and-release only; amberjack (*Seriola dumerili*) up to 30 kg are genuinely common from spring through autumn.

## River Segura — Carp and Barbel in the South-East

The Segura, flowing through the semi-arid Murcia region, hosts some of Spain's best carp fishing in its reservoir chain — particularly on the Embalse de Cenajo and the Embalse de Camarillas in the upper Segura valley. Common carp here average 6–10 kg; fish over 15 kg are caught on boilies, particles, and pack pellets fished on flat-bed method feeder rigs. The clear, warm water and the lack of angling pressure make these reservoirs genuinely productive from February through November. Native Iberian barbel (*Barbus guiraonis*) also inhabit the same reservoirs and river sections.

## Ría de Vigo and Galicia — Turbot and Sea Bass

Galicia's rias — the long drowned valleys on the Atlantic coast — are among Europe's most biologically productive estuaries. The Ría de Vigo and Ría de Arousa shelter enormous populations of shellfish but also quality sport fish: turbot (*Scophthalmus maximus*) inhabiting the sandy channels to 5 kg, sea bass (*Dicentrarchus labrax*) hunting the mussel buoy lines, and occasional European flounder. Shore fishing with sandeel or live prawn takes bass up to 4 kg; boat fishing over the sandy channel beds targets turbot with live sandeel. The Ría de Vigo is most productive from October through March when turbot concentrate in the deeper runs.

## Sierra Nevada Streams — Wild Trout at Altitude

The streams descending from Spain's highest mountain range, the Sierra Nevada in Granada, hold wild brown trout in gorges at 1,200–2,000 m altitude. The rivers Genil (in its upper sections), Dílar, and Monachil all carry designated trout zones. Fly fishing with dry flies and small nymphs in the classic upstream style is the preferred approach; the short season (typically March through August) and the wilderness setting make these small granite-sided streams among Spain's most atmospheric trout fisheries despite relatively small individual fish.

## Lake Bolarque — Big Pike and Perch

Lake Bolarque, formed by the Bolarque Dam on the Tagus River in the province of Cuenca, is one of central Spain's finest pike and perch fisheries. Northern pike reach 10 kg here in the cold winter months; perch to 1.5 kg are taken in late spring and autumn. The reservoir's irregular shoreline with sunken woodland and numerous rocky points creates ideal ambush habitat. Pike fishing with large jerkbaits and dead bait produces most trophy fish from November through February; perch respond to drop-shot rigs and small blade baits worked over gravel bars in the arms of the reservoir.
