The Parana Delta north of Buenos Aires spreads into a vast labyrinth of channels and islands where the water turns amber-brown with suspended sediment. At dawn, a 10-pound golden dorado hangs motionless in six inches of water behind a sedge bank, its golden flank catching the first horizontal light. The guide whispers, the cast is made, and a fly no bigger than a sparrow hits the surface three feet ahead of the fish. What follows in the next thirty seconds is why anglers travel to Argentina from across the world to fish for *Salminus brasiliensis*, the golden dorado.

## Anatomy of a River Tiger

The golden dorado (*Salminus brasiliensis*) is not a dorado in the saltwater dolphinfish sense, nor a salmonid despite its fighting style, but a member of the family Characidae — related to piranha and pacu. It occupies river systems throughout the Parana-Paraguay basin, the São Francisco in Brazil, and smaller Atlantic-draining rivers in southern Brazil and Uruguay. Males rarely exceed 8 kg; females grow substantially larger, with specimens over 20 kg recorded regularly and the confirmed IGFA record standing at 27.5 kg, caught in Argentina. Its golden-orange colouring, reflective scales, and heavy jaw loaded with sharp, recurved teeth make it visually unlike any other fish.

## The Parana System: Heart of Dorado Country

The Upper Parana in the Corrientes and Misiones provinces of northeastern Argentina holds the highest density of trophy dorado accessible to visiting anglers. The city of Posadas in Misiones and the town of Goya in Corrientes are the two main gateways. Esteros del Iberá — a vast subtropical wetland covering 13,000 square kilometres — feeds the Parana system and produces exceptional mid-sized dorado in its channels and lagoons. Lodges like Pira Lodge and Nervous Waters operate on the Corrientes drainage with dedicated guides and 24-hour access to productive water.

In Bolivia, the Río Grande and Mamoré systems in Santa Cruz department hold some of the largest dorado on earth. Untouched river systems with minimal fishing pressure produce fish that regularly approach 20 kg. Operations like Tsimane Lodge and Pluma Lodge offer fly-in expeditions to the Bolivian Amazon tributaries between May and October.

## Fly Fishing for Golden Dorado

Fly fishing is the preferred method for most travelling anglers, and the dorado obliges with a fighting style that perfectly suits the single-handed rod. A 9-weight rod with a tropical fly line handles the standard Argentine scenarios; Bolivia's larger fish and stronger currents call for a 10- or 11-weight with a sink-tip or intermediate head. Leaders of 30–40 lb fluorocarbon turn over large flies without difficulty.

Streamer patterns dominate. The Gurgier (a large foam-headed streamer that pushes a wake on the surface), the EP Minnow, rabbit-strip deceiver variations, and large articulated tube flies in orange, yellow, and white are proven patterns. Dorado are aggressive and relatively undiscriminating about pattern; the key variables are size (10–18 cm), presentation speed (fast is better), and placement close to structure, banks, and current seams.

Topwater fishing with large poppers and surface flies is effective in clear, low-water conditions, particularly in the Esteros del Iberá system where fish can be sight-fished. The visual take — a dorado clearing the water to engulf a surface fly — is one of freshwater fishing's most dramatic moments.

## Spinning and Conventional Tackle

Spinning tackle produces dorado at least as effectively as fly gear and is the preferred method for many guides on the Parana. A 7–8 ft medium-heavy rod (15–60 g) with a 3000–5000 series reel and 20–30 lb braid handles all situations. Large metal jigs (20–40 g) in silver, gold, and chartreuse, worked with fast, aggressive retrieves, produce excellent results. Soft-plastic swimbaits and large minnow lures in 10–14 cm are also effective. Dorado smash lures with force that frequently dislodges treble hooks; many guides file hooks to razor sharpness or replace factory hooks with premium alternatives. Wire traces are unnecessary — dorado teeth are sharp but their mouths are relatively small relative to body size, and strong 40–60 lb fluorocarbon leaders survive most fights.

## Bolivia vs Argentina: Choosing Your Destination

Argentina offers easier logistics, better infrastructure, and consistent fishing in established river systems. A 5-day trip to Corrientes costs USD 2,500–4,000 per person all-inclusive. The fish average 3–6 kg with occasional fish to 12 kg. Bolivia offers the prospect of fish in the 15–22 kg range with a genuine shot at a 25-pound fish, but requires fly-in logistics, significantly higher costs (USD 4,500–7,500 for a 7-day trip), and tolerance for frontier camp conditions. The Bolivian season runs May through October when rivers are clear and fish are concentrated.

## Seasons and Conditions

Dorado are most active in water temperatures between 18 and 26 °C. In Argentina, the season runs year-round with peaks in spring (September–November) and autumn (March–May). Summer heat above 30 °C pushes fish deep. The Parana floods in late summer (February–March) and fish disperse; October is consistently cited by guides as the single best month on the Argentine Parana. In Bolivia and Brazil's Mato Grosso, the dry season from May to October is optimal.
