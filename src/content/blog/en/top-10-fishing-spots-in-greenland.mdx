Greenland is the world's largest island and one of its most remote fishing destinations. Its 44,087 km of coastline — much of it deeply indented by fjords that extend hundreds of kilometres inland — frames a landscape of ice, tundra, and crystal-cold water that has supported both Inuit subsistence fisheries and, increasingly, international sport fishing for decades. The Arctic char (Salvelinus alpinus) here are genetically distinct from populations found anywhere else in the world: living in deep fjord lakes and glacial rivers, they grow slowly in the frigid water and accumulate the deep red-orange flesh characteristic of their kind. Greenland halibut (Reinhardtius hippoglossoides) — not a true halibut but a member of the righteyeflounders — are taken commercially in vast numbers on the continental shelf at depths of 300 to 1,000 m, and recreational catches of fish exceeding 40 kg from nearshore deep-water stations are documented. Sea trout (Salmo trutta) in Greenland's few river systems are among the most unspoiled stocks in the world.

## Kangerlussuaq and the Akia Peninsula

Kangerlussuaq (Søndre Strømfjord), a 190-km-long fjord on the west coast, is the main international gateway to Greenland by air and the starting point for the country's most accessible Arctic char fishery. The Watson River, which flows from the ice sheet edge through Kangerlussuaq town into the fjord, holds extraordinary concentrations of char — particularly in late July and August when the ice sheet meltwater peaks and fish stack at the river mouth. Cast from the shore at the river delta; char averaging 1 to 2 kg take dry flies, egg patterns, and small spinners with equal enthusiasm. The nearby Ferguson Lakes and Tasersuatsiaq Lake hold the region's largest sedentary char population, with fish to 5 kg documented.

## Nuuk Fjord System

Nuuk, Greenland's capital at 64°10'N, is surrounded by one of the most complex fjord systems on the west coast — the Nuuk Fjord system covers over 4,000 km² and includes hundreds of named lakes and rivers. The Kapisillit River, accessible by boat 50 km from Nuuk, is the premier fly-fishing river in the southern fjord system. Sea trout of 2 to 4 kg run the river from early July, followed by char and occasional Atlantic salmon. The outer fjord arms hold excellent sea fishing for cod (Gadus morhua) — though populations have fluctuated dramatically with warming — and for lumpfish and Greenlandic wolffish in the rocky sections. Char fishing in the fjord itself, around the freshwater outflows of rivers and streams, is productive from June to September.

## Maniitsoq and the Søndre Isortoq

Maniitsoq on the mid-west coast is the gateway to some of Greenland's most spectacular scenery and most productive sea-trout rivers. The Søndre Isortoq river drains a high lake system in the hills behind the town; its lower reach is pure sea-trout water from late June through August. Sea trout here average 1.5 to 3 kg with exceptional fish to 5 kg taken on intermediate fly lines and small tube flies. The river is fast and technical — wading is essential and falls should be scouted with care. The fjord below Maniitsoq holds Greenlandic halibut in deep slots accessible by local boat.

## Sisimiut and the Ikertooq Fjord

Sisimiut (Holsteinsborg), at 66°56'N just north of the Arctic Circle, represents the transition between sub-arctic and true arctic fishing. The Amerloq Fjord and Ikertooq complex northeast of the town are accessible by ATV or snowmobile depending on season and hold numerous lakes with large, slow-growing Arctic char. Fish caught through the ice in late winter (February to April) are often the largest, as char concentrate in the deepest lake basins over winter. Lake Tasersuatsiaq near Sisimiut holds char to 6 kg, among the largest recorded for the species in western Greenland.

## Ilulissat and the Disco Bay Char Runs

Ilulissat (Jakobshavn), home of the Sermeq Kujalleq glacier and the UNESCO World Heritage Icefjord, is best known for tourism, but its surrounding rivers provide char fishing of rare quality. The Ilimanaq River, 20 km south accessible by boat, drains a lake plateau and holds char from July through September. Icebergs — some the size of apartment blocks, calved from the nearby glacier — drift past the river mouth while you fish. Ice fishing for char through the frozen fjord in January and February, using traditional Greenlandic jigging methods with small metal lures, is a cultural experience unlike any other.

## Disko Island (Qeqertarsuaq)

Disko Island, the large volcanic island in Disko Bay, has a unique geological and biological character: its dark basalt landscape and relatively mild microclimate support unusual plant communities and fish populations. The rivers on the island's eastern coast, including the one draining Lyngmarkssø lake, hold Arctic char to 3 kg in accessible pools. The fjord around the island's southern shore is particularly good for Greenlandic halibut in the autumn; local fishers from Qeqertarsuaq town regularly catch halibut of 10 to 25 kg by jigging in 80 to 150 m of water.

## East Greenland: Ammassalik District

The Ammassalik (Tasiilaq) district on the east coast is one of the most isolated inhabited areas on Earth, accessible only by helicopter or ship. This isolation has left its fish populations in a genuinely pristine state. The Tasiilaq River and the numerous lakes of the fjord district around Sermiligaaq and Kuummiut hold Arctic char populations that have never been subjected to significant fishing pressure. Char of 3 to 6 kg are common, and fish of 8 to 10 kg have been reported by researchers working in the area. The east coast fjords also hold ringed seal, beluga whale, and Greenlandic halibut at depth — a wildlife context for fishing that is unmatched anywhere else in the North Atlantic.

## Continental Shelf: Greenland Halibut

The Greenland continental shelf — especially the Davis Strait between Greenland and Canada — is one of the world's most productive fishing grounds for Greenlandic halibut (Reinhardtius hippoglossoides). While commercial fishing at depths of 500 to 800 m requires industrial vessels, sport anglers fishing from the communities of Maniitsoq, Nuuk, and Paamiut have access to nearshore populations at depths of 80 to 200 m. Heavy jigging with metal lures on braided line at these depths produces halibut of 5 to 20 kg on a regular basis, with exceptional individuals above 40 kg caught near the steep drop-offs of the inner fjords.

## Narsarsuaq and the South Greenland Rivers

South Greenland, around Narsarsuaq and Nanortalik, has a comparatively mild climate and supports the island's most productive Atlantic salmon and sea-trout rivers. The Qooroq River north of Narsarsuaq holds small but genuine runs of Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) in August and September — a historically significant fish population that once supported Norse settlements in medieval times. Sea trout average 1 to 3 kg in the glacial rivers south of Narsarsuaq. The landscape of the south, with its fjords, Norse ruins, and occasional glacier, provides the most historically layered angling context in Greenland.

## Ice Fishing on the Frozen Fjords

From December to April across much of west and south Greenland, the fjords freeze sufficiently to support ice fishing. The traditional method is jigging through a small hole with a hand line and a weighted metal lure — an Inuit technique refined over thousands of years for Arctic char and cod. Modern sport anglers have adapted the method with ice fishing rods and electric drills, but the fundamentals are unchanged. Ice fishing near the mouths of rivers where char concentrate in winter produces the largest fish of the year: individuals of 4 to 7 kg are reported from ice fishing stations near Sisimiut and Kangerlussuaq.
