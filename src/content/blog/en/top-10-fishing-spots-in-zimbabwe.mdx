Zimbabwe's fishing heritage is inseparable from the Zambezi River. From the impoundment that created Lake Kariba — one of the world's largest man-made lakes by volume — to the remote wilderness of Mana Pools World Heritage Site, the Zambezi and its floodplain offer fishing experiences that place Zimbabwe among the elite addresses in African sportfishing. The tigerfish — Hydrocyanus vittatus — dominates conversations at every camp on the Zimbabwean shore of Kariba, but the country's fishing story goes well beyond one species. Largemouth bass introduced to highland impoundments, the enormous vundu catfish of Mana Pools, and the surf-rod bream of the Zambezi's braided channels all deserve equal billing.

## Lake Kariba Tigerfish

Lake Kariba stretches 226 kilometres along the Zambezi Valley between Zimbabwe and Zambia, filled with tigerfish that average 2 to 5 kilograms and occasionally reach 12 kilograms. The Zimbabwe shore between Kariba town and Bumi Hills is the most intensively fished and best serviced. Tigerfish take lures with extraordinary aggression — surface walkers, bladed spinners, and diving crankbaits all work. The period from September through November, when water levels are at their lowest and fish concentrate on rocky points and submerged trees, produces the highest catch rates. Kings Camp Fishing Village, near the town of Kariba, is a long-established base for visiting anglers.

## Mana Pools Bream and Bass

Mana Pools National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site 120 kilometres downstream of Kariba on the Zimbabwe bank, is famous for wildlife but its fishing is equally remarkable. The park's floodplain pools — Long Pool, Chine Pool, and Chitake Pool — fill seasonally from the Zambezi's rise and drain back to leave isolated bodies of water holding large populations of redbreast bream, greenhead bream, and the occasional largemouth bass that have colonized several pools from upstream. Fishing is permitted from the bank only, without motor boats, which preserves the wild character of the experience.

## Tiger Bay on Lake Kariba

Tiger Bay, between Charara Safari Area and Sanyati Gorge on the Zimbabwe side of Kariba, is the lake's most productive tigerfish zone. The submerged trees of flooded jesse bush and mopane woodland provide habitat complexity that concentrates fish year-round. A community-run fishing camp operates in the area and Zimbabwe National Parks regulations apply: all tigerfish caught must be released unless held as a licensed trophy. The Sanyati Gorge itself, where the gorge meets the main lake, produces the lake's largest tigerfish each October.

## Zambezi River at Victoria Falls

The stretch of the Zambezi above the Victoria Falls at the town of Victoria Falls is accessible to guided fishing trips operating from several licensed operators along Park Way. The river above the falls holds large tigerfish, African pike (Hepsetus odoe), and bream of various species in the quiet eddies behind mid-river rock outcrops. Guides recommend 20-kilogram nylon leaders for tigerfish given their razor dentition. The Falls section is best fished from July through October when water is clear and low.

## Bumi Hills Fishing

The Bumi Hills area on the southwestern shore of Lake Kariba, adjacent to Matusadona National Park, provides perhaps the lake's most spectacular setting. The mountains rise steeply from the lake shore, elephant regularly come to drink at the water's edge, and the submerged acacia woodland in the bays below the hills holds strong populations of both tigerfish and bream. The Bumi Hills Safari Lodge operates exclusive boat fishing sessions; prior booking is essential as daily boat numbers are capped to minimize pressure.

## Kyle Dam (Lake Mutirikwi)

Lake Mutirikwi — formed by Kyle Dam near Masvingo — was the first Zimbabwean impoundment to receive largemouth bass, introduced in 1952. The lake now holds a self-sustaining bass population that produces fish averaging 1.5 to 2.5 kilograms with occasional specimens to 4 kilograms. Fishing access is through the Kyle Recreation Park, managed by Zimbabwe Parks and Wildlife Management Authority. The dam also holds tilapia, barbel, and the endemic Zimbabwe bream (Oreochromis mossambicus) in its shallow reed-fringed bays.

## Mazvikadei Dam

Mazvikadei Dam, north of Chinhoyi in Mashonaland West, is Harare's premier day-trip bass venue. The dam was stocked with bass in the 1970s and now produces fish up to 3 kilograms consistently through the May-to-September dry season. Local anglers favour plastic worms rigged Texas-style on the weed edges, while visiting anglers often use spinner baits along the rocky dam wall. A Zimbabwe fishing licence is mandatory for all anglers over 16 years of age.

## Manyame Lake

Manyame Lake (formerly Lake Robertson), southwest of Harare, is the capital city's closest impoundment and a productive venue for bream, bass, and occasional tigerfish that push up from the Manyame River system during flood periods. The lake holds the largest bass population in Zimbabwe's Midlands region, with fish in the 1 to 2.5 kilogram range standard. Shore fishing from the lake's northern shore produces good catches of redbreast bream on earthworm bait through the April-to-August cooler months.

## Gonarezhou National Park Rivers

The rivers of Gonarezhou National Park in southeastern Zimbabwe — the Save, the Runde, and the Mwenezi — flow through remote sandveld terrain and hold tiger fish, bream, and barbel in their pools. Fishing here is integrated with walking safaris and canoe trips; no motor boats are used. The sandstone cliffs of the Chilojo Cliffs area are backdrop to some of the most remote freshwater fishing in southern Africa. The park's dry season from May to October makes river pools accessible by foot.
