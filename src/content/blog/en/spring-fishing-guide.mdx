There is a moment in late March or early April when everything on the water changes. The willows show their first green, a hatch of small olives lifts from the surface of a chalk stream, and in the depths below, the fish begin to stir with an urgency they have not felt for months. Spring fishing is unlike any other season precisely because it combines recovering fish with triggering biology — the pre-spawn window is one of the most reliable feeding frenzies in the natural world, and it rewards the angler who understands why it is happening.

As water temperatures rise from the winter trough toward spawning temperature, the metabolism of nearly every freshwater species accelerates sharply. They need to restore body condition and, in the case of females, develop energy reserves for spawning. That combination of need and warming temperatures produces feeding intensity that summer and autumn rarely match.

## Reading the Spring Temperature Ladder

Water temperature is the master variable in spring. Different species activate at different thermal thresholds, creating a successive series of fishing opportunities that runs from March through to June in most temperate regions.

Grayling begin feeding aggressively from around 5°C and are often at their best in March on rivers like the Derbyshire Wye, the Eden, or Austria's Traun before the trout season opens. Perch and pike reactivate at 6-8°C, making them excellent spring targets in stillwaters. Brown trout start feeding confidently at 8°C, while rainbow trout in reservoirs can be targeted throughout the winter on lures but become surface-active on emerging midges from March. Carp and tench — classic late-spring species — don't reach peak activity until water temperatures consistently exceed 12°C, typically from mid-April in England and from late April in Scotland or Scandinavia.

## Pre-Spawn Feeding Frenzies Explained

The pre-spawn phase triggers the year's most intense feeding for most species. For pike, this means the period from late January through February on southern English rivers, where fish as large as 30 pounds push into the shallows to feed heavily before spawning in the marginal reeds. By March, the females have largely spawned and recover slowly, but the jack pike — smaller males — remain aggressive and active into April.

For perch, the pre-spawn frenzy runs from late February through March on lakes and slow rivers. Schools of perch weighing 1 to 2 kg concentrate at the same depths as the previous autumn, and a small drop-shot rig worked slowly near sunken branches or pontoon legs will provoke multiple takes from the same spot. Ireland's Lough Derg and Scotland's Loch Lomond both offer outstanding spring perch sessions.

Brown trout pre-spawn feeding is a subtler affair since they spawn in autumn, but spring sees fish recovering condition after spawning. Rivers like the Test and Kennet see their trout intensely focused on emerging olives and grannom caddis from late March, with dry-fly sport at its most consistent through April and May.

## Tactics for Spring Trout

The grannom hatch — Brachycentrus subnubilus — is one of the most reliable events in the spring fly fisher's calendar. It typically runs from late March to mid-April on chalk streams and slower limestone rivers across southern England, France, and Central Europe. During a grannom fall, trout become intensely selective, and a size 14 or 16 elk-hair caddis fished in the film can be devastatingly effective.

For early-season nymphing before significant hatches begin, a duo rig — a buoyant dry fly with a point nymph suspended 30-50 cm below — covers both sub-surface feeding fish and any opportunistic risers without switching between tactics. On the Itchen, this approach from the first of April produces remarkably consistent results.

In reservoirs like Grafham Water, Chew Valley, and Loch Lomond, spring brings rainbow trout to the surface as water temperatures rise and buzzers — chironomid midges — begin hatching. Suspending a small black buzzer under a bung at depths between 1 and 3 metres, positioned near the windward shore where food concentrates, is the definitive reservoir spring technique.

## Spring Pike and Perch

Post-spawn pike in April and May are recovering fish — their behaviour is less aggressive than in winter but they still need to eat. The most reliable approach is downsizing: a 15 cm rubber lure worked slowly on a 28-30 g jig head through the edges of weed beds accounts for a disproportionate number of spring pike. On the Norfolk Broads, the River Thurne corridor from Potter Heigham to Martham consistently produces pike through May.

Perch spawn among weed and structure in March and April on most English stillwaters, and by May the adults are back in feeding mode. Light drop-shot rigs with a 7 cm natural perch-pattern lure fished to 4-6 metres depth near the dam wall or inlet streams on reservoirs like Farmoor or Draycote produce double-figure sessions in May.

## Coarse Fishing Spring Windows

The river season reopens on 16 June in England and Wales, but stillwater coarse fishing continues year-round. In spring, tench and bream begin their pre-spawn feeding from mid-April on lakes with rich feeding margins. A simple method feeder loaded with groundbait and hair-rigged sweetcorn or a 10 mm boilie fished at dawn on Lincolnshire or Cambridgeshire fenland lakes targets tench at their most catchable.

Chub in rivers are active from early spring when water fines down after winter floods. A lobworm fished under a stick float in 2-3 metres of water along the inside of a bend on rivers like the Severn or the Wye is a classic early-season approach that rarely fails.

## Gear Adjustments for Spring

Spring fishing calls for lighter, more versatile tackle than the winter months. A 9 ft 5-weight fly rod covers everything from early-season dry fly to buzzers and small nymphs. For lure fishing, a 7 ft medium-action spinning rod in the 10-30 g range handles both perch and pike searches effectively. Line choice matters: fluorocarbon mainline in 6-8 lb straight through, or as a 2 m leader on a braid main line, has the low visibility that crystal-clear spring water demands.

Weather remains unpredictable in spring. Rain showers can colour a river within hours, and late frosts can set feeding activity back by days. Always check temperature trends over 72 hours rather than just the morning reading; a sustained warming trend is worth twice as much as a single warm day.
