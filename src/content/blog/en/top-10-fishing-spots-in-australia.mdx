Australia is less a fishing destination than a continent of them. The tropical north runs on barramundi time, its rivers swelling and shrinking with the monsoon; the east coast hosts the most famous black marlin fishery on Earth; the Murray-Darling basin hides golden perch and Murray cod in slow brown water; and Tasmania, far to the south, fishes like a wilder New Zealand with highland lakes full of wild browns. Each state writes its own rules and seasons, distances are enormous, and the species list runs from 1-metre barramundi to half-tonne marlin. These ten spots sketch the outline of what an angling traveller can expect.

## The Daly River — Top End barramundi royalty

The Daly, southwest of Darwin, is the Northern Territory's most celebrated barramundi river. The prime window is the run-off, roughly February to April, when floodwater drains off the plains and baitfish funnel out of every creek mouth — barramundi stack up at the colour changes and hit trolled and cast lures with violence. Metre-plus fish are caught every season. Guided boats out of the Daly River crossing handle the logistics, including the saltwater crocodiles that make bank fishing a bad idea.

## Kakadu and Yellow Water — billabong fishing in a World Heritage park

Inside Kakadu National Park, the Yellow Water billabong and the South Alligator River system offer barramundi fishing framed by paperbark swamps, lotus flowers and more crocodiles per kilometre than almost anywhere in Australia. Casting shallow divers at snag piles from a guided boat is the standard method; the dry season from May to September gives reliable access, while the build-up months of October and November can produce the hottest bite. Licensed operators run from Cooinda.

## Cairns and the Ribbon Reefs — giant black marlin

From September to December, the outer edge of the Great Barrier Reef between Cairns and Lizard Island hosts the world's premier aggregation of giant black marlin. Fish over 450 kilograms — the fabled "grander" — are caught here every season on big skip baits, and the heavy-tackle fleet out of Cairns has run this fishery since the 1960s. It is expensive, spectacular and unique. Light-tackle days inside the reef add sailfish, Spanish mackerel and yellowfin for mortals.

## Tasmania's Central Highlands — wild brown trout lakes

A thousand metres up on Tasmania's Central Plateau, lakes like Arthurs Lake, Great Lake and Little Pine Lagoon hold self-sustaining brown trout descended from fish shipped from England in 1864. The polaroiding here is famous: stalking tailing trout across flooded marshes in summer, or matching mayfly hatches from a drifting boat. The Western Lakes wilderness, reached on foot, offers thousands of tarns where a 2-kilogram wild brown in ankle-deep water is the prize. Season runs roughly August to April.

## The Murray and Murrumbidgee — cod country

Australia's inland giants live in slow, snag-lined rivers. Murray cod — moody, territorial and capable of exceeding 1 metre — are cast for with huge swimbaits and surface lures around timber in the Murray, while the Murrumbidgee around Carrathool and Gundagai mixes cod with golden perch. On the South Australian Murray, camp-and-fish reaches like Hogwash Bend near Cadell are classic spots to set up under the river red gums. Cod season closes September to November for spawning; check each state's rules.

## Exmouth and Ningaloo — flats fishing meets big blue water

On Western Australia's North West Cape, Exmouth compresses an absurd range of fishing into one town: bonefish and permit on the Ningaloo flats, giant trevally in the lagoon, sailfish and marlin within sight of land, and coral trout on the reef. The continental shelf sits minutes from the boat ramp. April to October brings the most settled weather, and the gulf side stays fishable when the ocean side blows.

## Port Phillip Bay and Victoria's snapper run

Every spring, big snapper flood into Port Phillip Bay near Melbourne, and from October to December thousands of trailer boats work the mud basins at dawn for fish to 9 kilograms. It is Australia's most democratic big-fish event — a modest boat and a flesh bait will do. Land-based anglers fish the piers at Mornington and Rye, and in Gippsland to the east, surf beaches such as Pettmans Beach near Lakes Entrance produce gummy shark and salmon for beach casters.

## The Gold Coast and its city fishing

Southeast Queensland proves you can catch serious fish between high-rises. The Gold Coast Seaway and Nerang River system hold mulloway, flathead, mangrove jack and giant trevally, with land-based regulars fishing the bridges — Sundale Bridge at Southport is a known mark — and the seaway walls. Offshore, 50-fathom reefs give snapper and pearl perch, and the FADs hold mahi-mahi in summer. Flathead tournaments each spring treat the estuary like a bass circuit.

## Munglinup and the wild south coast of WA

Western Australia's south coast, from Esperance west past Munglinup Beach, is 4WD salmon country. In autumn, schools of Australian salmon — hard-pulling, surf-loving fish to 9 kilograms — run the white beaches in visible black masses, and casters work metal lures from the sand. The same coast gives mulloway after dark and herring year-round. It is remote fishing: carry recovery gear, watch the swell, and never turn your back on the Southern Ocean.

## Seasons, licences and the long drive

Fishing licences are state business: NSW and Victoria require a general recreational licence, Tasmania licenses inland fishing, WA licenses specific activities, while Queensland and the NT are largely licence-free for recreational anglers. Seasons matter enormously — barramundi is closed in Queensland around the summer spawn, cod closes in spring, and the marlin run is strictly seasonal. Distances deceive: Cairns to the Daly is a 2,500-kilometre drive. Fly between regions, hire guides for crocodile water, and respect marine park zoning on the reef.
