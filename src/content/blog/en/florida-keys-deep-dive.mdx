The Overseas Highway runs 113 miles from Key Largo to Key West, stitching together more than forty bridges and a chain of islands that anglers have treated as holy ground for a century. On one side lies Florida Bay and the backcountry; on the other, the Atlantic drops away toward the Gulf Stream. Nowhere else in the United States can you sight-cast to a tailing permit at dawn, jig a bridge channel for snapper at noon, and fight a sailfish in 200 feet of blue water before sunset. This is a deep dive into how the Keys actually fish — season by season, species by species.

## The grand slam: tarpon, bonefish and permit

The flats grand slam — a tarpon, a bonefish and a permit in a single day — was practically invented in the Keys, and Islamorada still calls itself the Sportfishing Capital of the World on the strength of it. Tarpon are the headline act: fish from 60 to well over 150 pounds pour through the channels between the islands from March through July, with the peak migration in May and June. Bonefish in the Keys run bigger than almost anywhere on earth; fish over 10 pounds are caught every season on the flats of Islamorada and Biscayne-side banks. Permit are the heartbreakers — spooky, sharp-eyed, and addicted to crabs — and the flats around the Lower Keys and the Marquesas hold some of the best permit fishing in the hemisphere. A slam requires luck, but the Keys are the one place where all three species feed on the same tides.

## Tarpon season, bridge by bridge

When the spring migration is on, the bridges become tarpon highways. The Seven Mile Bridge at Marathon, the Bahia Honda channel, Long Key bridge and the Channel 2 and Channel 5 bridges near Islamorada all concentrate fish as they move with the tide. Guides stake out up-tide of the spans and drift live mullet or crabs back into the shadow lines; fly anglers work the ocean-side flats at first light before the boat traffic builds. A tide change around dawn or dusk is the prize draw. Expect company in May — this is the most famous tarpon fishery in the world — but the fish keep coming in waves, and a quiet weekday morning at Bahia Honda can still feel like a private show.

## Reading the flats

Keys flats are a mosaic of turtle grass, white sand and coral marl, and the fish use them on a schedule set by the tide. Bonefish push onto a flooding flat to root for shrimp and crabs, often revealing themselves by tailing in inches of water. Permit prefer slightly deeper edges, two to four feet, where a black sickle tail or a nervous push of water gives them away. The skill is in seeing fish before they see you: polarized glasses, a high sun between roughly 9 a.m. and 4 p.m., and a poled skiff are the standard toolkit. Wading is possible on firmer ocean-side flats around the Lower Keys. Long, accurate casts matter more here than anywhere — Keys bonefish have seen every fly ever tied.

## When the wind blows: bridges and channels

The flats game collapses in a 20-knot blow, but the Keys never stop fishing. The bridge channels hold mangrove snapper, mutton snapper, grouper, tarpon, sharks, and Goliath grouper that test tackle to destruction. Anglers without a boat have genuine options: the old Seven Mile Bridge section and the piers at Bahia Honda State Park give walk-up access to moving water, and many bridge catwalks throughout the islands are designated fishing platforms. Live shrimp on a jighead, fished on the down-tide side of the pilings, is the universal Keys bridge rig. Night tides produce the biggest snapper, especially around the summer full moons when mangrove snapper school to spawn.

## Bluewater: the reef edge and the Stream

From Key Largo to Key West, the reef line runs four to six miles offshore, and beyond it the bottom falls fast toward the Gulf Stream. Winter is sailfish season — December through March, when cold fronts push bait down the reef edge and kite-fishing crews out of Islamorada and Key West rack up multiple releases a day. Spring brings blackfin tuna to the Islamorada humps, underwater seamounts that rise from 1,000 feet to under 300 and concentrate everything from tuna to amberjack. Mahi-mahi flood the weed lines from April through summer, and deep-dropping for swordfish has become a Keys specialty, with daytime fish hauled from depths around 1,500 feet.

## Key West and the Marquesas

The end of the road deserves its own chapter. Key West offers the most varied fishing day in the Keys: tarpon in the harbor, permit on the Lakes flats to the west, and a short run to the Atlantic reef. Twenty-five miles further west lie the Marquesas Keys, an uninhabited coral atoll ringed by flats that hold permit, bonefish and tarpon with far less pressure. The crossing of the Boca Grande Channel is weather-dependent and best left to experienced skippers, but a calm-day trip to the Marquesas is the closest the Keys come to wilderness. Beyond that again, 70 miles out, the Dry Tortugas hold spectacular reef fishing around Fort Jefferson for those who plan a multi-day trip.

## Logistics, licences and rules

Florida requires a saltwater fishing licence for most anglers, available online in minutes, though fishing from a licensed charter is covered by the boat. The Florida Keys National Marine Sanctuary protects the entire island chain, and rules have teeth: numerous no-take Sanctuary Preservation Areas dot the reef, lobster and grouper have closed seasons, and tarpon over 40 inches must stay in the water. Bonefish and permit are catch-and-release in Florida, with permit subject to extra protection in the Special Permit Zone covering the Keys. Book flats guides six months or more ahead for May and June tarpon. Marathon makes a practical mid-Keys base; Islamorada has the deepest guide bench; Key West offers the most fishing options per day on the water.
