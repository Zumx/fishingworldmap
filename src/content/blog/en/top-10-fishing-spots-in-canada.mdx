Somewhere on the Fraser River, an angler is right now attached to a white sturgeon that may be older than Canada's confederation — fish over three metres and a hundred years old are caught and released near Chilliwack every season. Canada holds two million lakes, three oceans of coastline and rivers that drain half a continent, so picking ten waters is an exercise in glorious unfairness. These are the spots, from Pacific salmon rivers to walleye cities, that combine world-class fish with realistic access for a travelling angler.

## Fraser River, British Columbia: sturgeon and salmon bars

The lower Fraser between Mission and Hope is the world's premier white sturgeon fishery, a catch-and-release program where fish between two and three metres are weekly events. The same gravel bars that anglers fish for sturgeon — locally named spots like Two-bit Bar, Airport Bar, Big Sky Bar and One-Mile Bar near Chilliwack — fill with bar-fishing rods each fall when millions of pink and chinook salmon push upriver. A licensed guide handles the heavy 130 lb-class tackle and the regulations; expect multiple sturgeon hookups on a good day, every one released at the boat.

## Skeena system, British Columbia: steelhead of a lifetime

The Skeena and its tributaries — the Babine, Bulkley, Kispiox and Morice — produce the largest wild steelhead on earth, with fish over 20 lb realistic every autumn. September and October are prime, swinging flies through glacial-green runs near Smithers and Terrace. The Kispiox alone has produced steelhead approaching 30 lb. Classified-water rules require non-residents to fish certain rivers with a guide or on restricted days, so plan early: lodge space for peak weeks books out a year ahead.

## Great Bear Lake, Northwest Territories: record lake trout

Straddling the Arctic Circle, Great Bear is the world's eighth-largest lake and the undisputed capital of giant lake trout — the 72 lb all-tackle world record came from these waters, and fish over 40 lb are caught every summer at Plummer's lodges. The season is brutally short, mid-June to late August, with 20-plus hours of daylight. Arctic grayling in the Tree River fly-out add a second world-class species. This is expedition fishing: float planes, barren-ground caribou on the shorelines and water so cold trout live for decades.

## Lake Mistassini, Quebec: the wilderness brook trout factory

Quebec's largest natural lake, 161 km long in Cree territory south-east of James Bay, Mistassini is legendary for outsized brook trout to 8 lb, lake trout and northern pike. Fishing on Lake Mistassini is managed with the Cree Nation of Mistissini, whose outfitters run the camps and know the rocky points where giant speckles cruise after ice-out in June. The Rupert River flowing out of the lake offers wild river fishing for the same species in vast, road-free country.

## Miramichi River, New Brunswick: Atlantic salmon heritage

The Miramichi has defined North American Atlantic salmon fishing for over a century, with runs entering from June through October and famous pools around Doaktown and Blackville. All salmon fishing is fly-only and live release, and hiring one of the river's storied guides — many families have guided for five generations — is part of the experience. Early-morning sea-run brook trout in June are a superb bonus fishery while you wait for a grilse to roll.

## Campbell River, Vancouver Island: the Tyee tradition

Campbell River calls itself the Salmon Capital of the World, and the Tyee Club — founded in 1924 — still requires members to hook a 30 lb-plus chinook from a rowed boat on light tackle in Discovery Passage. Modern anglers troll the same tidal rips for all five Pacific salmon species, jig for halibut and lingcod, and fish the Quinsam-fed river itself for autumn coho. Stand on the gravel at the estuary in September and you will see why Roderick Haig-Brown wrote his books here.

## St. Lawrence River, Ontario and Quebec: walleye, pike and muskie

From Kingston's Thousand Islands to Trois-Rivières and Lac Saint-Pierre, the St. Lawrence is an urban-accessible giant. The Fleuve St-Laurent shoreline around Trois-Rivières — including easy public spots near Notre-Dame street and Île Saint-Quentin — gives bank anglers genuine shots at doré (walleye) and brochet (pike) minutes from downtown. Downstream of Montreal, Lac Saint-Pierre is a walleye factory; upstream, the Thousand Islands hold muskellunge over 50 inches and some of North America's best smallmouth bass.

## Lake of the Woods, Ontario: 14,552 islands of multi-species water

Sprawling across the Ontario-Manitoba-Minnesota borders, Lake of the Woods offers 105,000 km of shoreline and arguably Canada's most complete freshwater menu: walleye on every reef, trophy muskie in the Northwest Angle, smallmouth on the rock piles and crappie in the bays. Kenora is the hub, houseboat rentals let you live on the fishery, and the winter ice season for walleye and giant pike is just as famous as summer.

## Bow River, Alberta: blue-ribbon trout below Calgary

Flowing out of the Rockies and straight through Calgary, the Bow's tailwater section downstream to Carseland is rated among North America's finest brown and rainbow trout fisheries, with around 2,000 trout per mile and fish averaging 16–18 inches. Drift-boat season runs June to October; the famous hopper fishing of August and the Blue-Winged Olive hatches of September are the peaks. Few cities of 1.6 million anywhere have wild two-foot browns rising within sight of their skyline.

## Saskatchewan's north: Tobin, Reindeer and trophy pike country

Northern Saskatchewan's Canadian Shield lakes — Reindeer, Wollaston, Cree and the Churchill River system — are the heartland of trophy northern pike, where fly-in lodges release fish over 50 inches every June when big females sit in warming bays. Tobin Lake near Nipawin adds road-accessible fame: its tailrace below the E.B. Campbell dam produced a 18.3 lb world-line-class walleye and remains one of the continent's best big-walleye waters, especially through autumn and the hard-water season.
