Colombia is the only country on Earth where you can fight a sailfish in the Pacific in the morning, fly to the Llanos by afternoon, and wake up the next day casting for peacock bass in a blackwater tributary of the Orinoco. Two oceans, three Andean cordilleras, and a slab of Amazon rainforest squeeze an absurd diversity of fisheries into one passport stamp. For decades security concerns kept anglers away; today the lodges of Vichada and the pangas of Bahía Solano are booked months in advance. Here are the ten places that explain why.

## Río Tomo and the Vichada tributaries — peacock bass heartland

The eastern department of Vichada, where the Tomo, Bita and Vichada rivers drain toward the Orinoco, is Colombia's answer to Brazil's famous pavón fisheries. From December through March the water drops and clears, concentrating Cichla temensis — the speckled three-bar peacock — in lagoons and sandbar pockets. Fish over 8 kg are caught every season, and the violence of a topwater strike at first light is something no angler forgets. Most operations fly clients from Bogotá to Puerto Carreño, then run upriver by boat. Bring 50 lb braid; these fish head straight for timber.

## Bahía Solano — Pacific sailfish and roosterfish

On the rain-soaked Chocó coast, the fishing town of Bahía Solano sits within panga range of an underwater shelf where sailfish, yellowfin tuna, wahoo and dorado stack up between April and September. Closer to the rocks, roosterfish patrol the surf lines off Playa Huina and El Valle, and cubera snapper haunt the pinnacles. The town is reached by a one-hour flight from Medellín, and the whole operation feels refreshingly small-scale — local captains, hand-tied teasers, whales breaching behind the boat in July and August.

## The Caribbean flats and tarpon of Barú and the Canal del Dique

South of Cartagena, the mangrove labyrinth around Barú and the Canal del Dique holds resident tarpon from 10 to 60 kg, plus snook in the creek mouths. The Caribbean here is not a classic flats fishery like the Bahamas, but baby tarpon on fly in a Colombian mangrove tunnel, with colonial Cartagena an hour away, is a combination few destinations can match. Permit and bonefish appear around the San Bernardo archipelago for those willing to explore.

## Río Inírida and the Guainía blackwaters

Near the famous Cerros de Mavecure — three granite domes rising 250 m out of the jungle — the Inírida river system offers peacock bass, payara (the vampire fish with its 10 cm fangs) and giant catfish in water the colour of dark tea. The town of Inírida has daily flights from Bogotá, and indigenous Puinave guides know every lagoon. The dry-season window runs roughly January to March.

## Leticia and the Amazon — arapaima and giant catfish

Colombia's southern tip touches the Amazon proper at Leticia. From here anglers reach oxbow lakes holding arapaima (locally pirarucú), plus piraíba and redtail catfish in the main channel that exceed 100 kg. Lake Tarapoto, about two hours upriver near Puerto Nariño, is famous for pink dolphins and evening peacock bass sessions. Regulations protect arapaima in many waters, so catch-and-release with a licensed guide is the rule.

## Lake Tota — rainbow trout at 3,015 metres

High in Boyacá, Lake Tota is Colombia's largest natural lake — 55 square kilometres of cold, clear Andean water at over 3,000 m elevation. Rainbow trout were introduced in the 1930s and thrive both wild and in cages. Trolling spoons from Playa Blanca or fly fishing the rocky points at dawn produces fish to 3 kg, and the surrounding onion fields and páramo scenery are a destination in themselves.

## The trout streams of the coffee axis

The cordilleras around Manizales, Salento and the Cocora Valley hide cold mountain streams and a dense network of stocked fisheries — the famous trucheras. Waters such as Truchera Arroyo Hondo and Truchera El Vergel let families fish for rainbow trout that go straight to the kitchen, while the Río Blanco reserve above Manizales offers wilder water and cloud-forest birdwatching between casts. It is low-key fishing, but quintessentially Colombian.

## Girardot and the Magdalena valley

The Magdalena, Colombia's great central artery, has suffered from overfishing, yet the stretch around Girardot and Honda still produces bocachico runs during the subienda migration of January and February, plus catfish like the prized bagre rayado. Sport-fishing clubs in Girardot have promoted catch-and-release tournaments to rebuild stocks. Recreational ponds in the warm valley — places like Pesca deportiva Mampay — fill the gap with cachama and tilapia for a relaxed afternoon.

## San Andrés and Providencia — Caribbean blue water

Five hundred kilometres off the mainland, the islands of San Andrés and Providencia sit on the third-largest barrier reef on the planet. Wahoo, blackfin tuna, mahi-mahi and blue marlin cruise the drop-offs, while bonefish tail on the flats of the McBean Lagoon in Providencia. The Seaflower Biosphere Reserve imposes strict rules — local guides know the zones, and fishing with them keeps money in island hands.

## The Orinoco mainstem at Puerto Carreño — payara and lau lau

Where the Meta meets the Orinoco at Puerto Carreño, raudales (rapids) churn over granite, and this oxygen-rich water is payara territory. Casting heavy spoons into the seams below the rocks produces these fanged silver missiles to 7 kg, while nighttime bait sessions target lau lau — the piraíba catfish — at weights that test 80 lb gear. The season again favours the December–March dry months, when the river drops as much as 10 m from flood levels.

## Practical notes — seasons, licences and logistics

Colombia's fisheries follow water, not temperature. Orinoco and Amazon basins fish best in the low-water dry season (roughly December to March); the Pacific peaks April to September; the Caribbean is workable year-round outside October's rains. Recreational fishing requires a permit from AUNAP, usually arranged by lodges and guides. Fly via Bogotá (El Dorado) for the east and south, Medellín for the Pacific, Cartagena for the Caribbean. Malaria prophylaxis is advised for jungle basins, and yellow fever vaccination is required for many regions.
