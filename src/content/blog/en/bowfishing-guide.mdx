Bowfishing occupies a unique position in the spectrum of fishing methods: it is simultaneously an archery discipline, a fishing technique, and, for many practitioners, a conservation tool. In freshwater systems across North America, invasive common carp and bighead carp have degraded water quality and displaced native species to the point where state fisheries agencies actively encourage bowfishing as a management strategy. In Texas, Louisiana, and Arkansas, guided bowfishing tours on illuminated flatboat platforms have become a thriving commercial enterprise, bringing new participants into both archery and fisheries conservation simultaneously.

The core equipment is relatively simple: a recurve or compound bow of modest draw weight (30–50 lbs), a reel of heavy line, an arrow with a barbed bowfishing tip, and a retrieval system that allows the arrow to be recovered and the fish to be pulled to the boat or bank. But the specific knowledge required to succeed — understanding how light refraction distorts a fish's apparent position, how to approach close enough for a clean shot, and which rigs work in current versus still water — separates consistent bowfishers from casual participants who spend most of their time missing.

## Bow Selection and Setup

A compound bow set to a draw weight of 40–50 lbs with a 60–70% let-off gives bowfishers the option to hold at full draw while repositioning or waiting for a better angle — an advantage over recurve bows when targeting moving fish. Draw length matters less than usual because bowfishing shots are short (typically 3–7 metres) and arrows are much heavier than standard hunting arrows; a slightly longer draw than ideal is manageable.

The reel mounts directly to the bow above the grip. Three types are common: hand wrap reels — essentially a plastic spool mounted to the bow with no mechanical mechanism — are the cheapest and most reliable choice for beginners. Bottle reels allow the line to flow freely from an open-top container and are popular for shots at active, fast-moving fish. Spin-cast reels, identical to fishing reels, offer the most control and the fastest line retrieval for shooting in rivers where a fish can travel quickly after being struck.

Polarised sunglasses are not optional equipment; they are the single piece of gear that most improves daytime bowfishing success. Without polarisation, surface glare renders fish almost invisible in water deeper than half a metre. With quality polarisation, fish in three metres of clear water become clearly visible and the targeting calculation becomes manageable.

## Understanding Light Refraction

The most fundamental technique in bowfishing is accounting for light refraction — the bending of light as it passes between water and air. Because of this optical phenomenon, a fish that appears to be 30 centimetres below the surface is actually sitting approximately 40 centimetres below it, and a fish that appears to be one metre away horizontally is actually two to three metres away in real space. The refraction angle is steeper for deeper targets and shallower for targets directly below the bow.

The practical rule taught by guides on the Atchafalaya Basin in Louisiana and the Snake River in Idaho is to aim at least 15 centimetres below the apparent position of the fish at most bowfishing ranges. Some guides simplify this further: aim at the bottom edge of the fish you can see. At very short range — under two metres — refraction is less significant and the correction shrinks to a few centimetres.

## Target Species: Carp, Gar, and More

Common carp are the primary bowfishing target across most of North America and Europe. They are large, abundant in shallow weedy areas, and have no conservation protection in most jurisdictions — making them an ideal species for new bowfishers. Grass carp, silver carp, and bighead carp are targeted in heavily invaded river systems in the Mississippi and Missouri drainages, where they leap clear of the water when startled by boat motors, sometimes presenting almost vertical targets.

Longnose gar and alligator gar are popular targets in the southern United States; alligator gar in the Trinity River in Texas and the Sabine River in Louisiana grow to over 60 kg and provide a genuinely trophy-level bowfishing challenge. Freshwater drum, bowfin, and suckers round out the common target list. In coastal areas, rays and certain shark species are occasionally taken on bow, though regulations vary significantly by state and country.

## Night Fishing with Lights

Night bowfishing on illuminated flat-bottomed boats is the dominant form of the sport for most North American enthusiasts. A 12-volt LED light array suspended over the bow illuminates the shallows to depths of two to three metres, attracting both baitfish and the predators that follow them. Carp, gar, and drum move actively at night in summer temperatures, and the combination of their visibility in the light cone and reduced pressure from daylight boat traffic makes night sessions often three to four times more productive than daytime equivalents.

Platform height matters. A stable shooting platform elevated 60–90 centimetres above the water surface reduces the refraction error at most shooting angles — the higher you stand above the water, the shallower the refraction angle becomes and the less adjustment is required. Guide boats on the Atchafalaya and Red River systems in Louisiana often carry six or more hunters simultaneously, with separate shooting positions assigned to each participant.

## Shot Placement and Retrieval

For clean kills and minimal arrow damage, aim for the fish's body just behind the head, targeting the shoulder area. A shot through the midsection often passes through soft tissue without immediately incapacitating the fish, resulting in a struggling fish that may tear free. Shots through the back work well for larger species such as gar, whose tough scales and lateral line position make a spine hit practical.

After the shot, allow the line to run freely as the fish moves, then begin retrieving steadily. Never wrap the line around your hand; a hard run from a large carp or gar can cause rope burns or worse. Use the reel mechanism or feed the line in loops to the deck. Once the fish is alongside the boat, dispatch it quickly with a priest or a sharp blow to the head — a thrashing carp in a small boat is a safety hazard.

## Legal Considerations

Bowfishing regulations vary enormously by location. In most US states, bowfishing is legal for rough fish — carp, gar, drum, suckers — without a separate permit, but targets such as gamefish (bass, walleye, trout) are prohibited. In the United Kingdom, bowfishing is not a recognised method and is effectively illegal under freshwater fisheries law. In France and Germany, it falls into a regulatory grey area; always confirm local rules before setting up a bow on any water.

Night bowfishing from a motor vessel typically requires a boating licence and adherence to navigation light regulations even on inland waters.
