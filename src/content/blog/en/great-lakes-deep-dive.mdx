The five Great Lakes hold about 21 percent of the world's surface fresh water — 244,000 square kilometres of it, enough to feel and behave like an ocean, with shipwreck storms, surf beaches, and horizons with no far shore. What swims in that water today is one of the strangest and most successful fishery experiments ever attempted: Pacific salmon stocked into Midwestern seas, steelhead running rivers two thousand kilometres from any ocean, and native lake trout slowly reclaiming the depths. For travelling anglers, the result is a fishery of absurd scale where a week can include 15-kilo king salmon, chrome steelhead in waist-deep gravel runs, and smallmouth bass fishing as good as anywhere on earth.

## The crash and the gamble

The modern fishery was born from disaster. By the 1950s, sea lampreys — invaders that arrived via shipping canals — had gutted the native lake trout, and alewife, a small invasive herring, exploded unchecked until die-offs piled silver windrows of dead fish on Chicago and Milwaukee beaches. In 1966, Michigan fisheries chief Howard Tanner made a decision he later summarized as wanting to do something « spectacular » : he stocked coho salmon from Oregon into Lake Michigan tributaries. Chinook followed in 1967. The Pacific salmon found an inland ocean stuffed with alewife and grew at astonishing rates — and a sport fishery now worth billions of dollars a year was born almost overnight. Lampreys, meanwhile, were brought under control by a targeted lampricide program run jointly by the United States and Canada, one of the great quiet successes of fisheries management.

## Kings, coho and the trolling fleet

Today Lake Michigan and Lake Ontario host the headline salmon fisheries. Charter fleets out of ports like Ludington, Manistee, Sheboygan, Milwaukee and Frankfort on Lake Michigan, and Oswego, Olcott and the Niagara Bar on Lake Ontario, troll spoons, flasher-fly rigs and cut-bait behind downriggers in 20 to 60 metres of water. Chinook — kings — average 7 to 11 kilos with 15-kilo-plus fish every season, and when a hot king strips 100 metres of line off a downrigger rod the ocean comparison stops being a metaphor. Late August and September bring staging fish to river mouths, and then the runs begin: tributaries like Michigan's Pere Marquette and Muskegon and New York's famous Salmon River at Pulaski fill with thousands of spawning kings and coho, and with anglers swinging flies and drifting beads shoulder to shoulder.

## Steelhead: the lake-run obsession

For many, the deeper addiction is steelhead — Pacific-strain rainbow trout that treat the lakes as their ocean and run the rivers from autumn through spring. The variety is enormous: the gravel runs of the Pere Marquette, the long drifts of the Muskegon, the Lake Erie « steelhead alley » tributaries of Ohio and Pennsylvania — Conneaut Creek, Elk Creek, Walnut Creek — where numbers can be staggering, and Ontario's Georgian Bay rivers like the Bighead and Beaver. Lake-run steelhead average 2.5 to 5 kilos, with double-digit-pound fish common, and they are caught on swung flies with two-handed rods, drifted egg patterns, centre-pin floats and small spoons. Winter fishing, with guides chipping ice from rod guides between chrome fish, is its own culture. Lake-run brown trout — including true giants over 10 kilos in the Lake Ontario and Lake Michigan harbours — fill the gaps between runs.

## The deep natives: lake trout and the recovery story

Beneath the stocked glamour species swims the original apex predator. Lake trout, the slow-growing char of the cold depths, have staged a genuine comeback: Lake Superior's populations are essentially restored and self-sustaining, and Lake Huron and parts of Michigan now see significant natural reproduction. Jigging for them in 30 to 80 metres with heavy spoons and tube jigs has become a fashionable light-tackle game, especially around Superior's Apostle Islands, Isle Royale and Stannard Rock — a mid-lake reef 40 kilometres from shore that produces trophy lakers over 15 kilos. Superior also offers a wilder, lonelier version of the whole fishery: coaster brook trout along the Nipigon shore, where the world-record brookie was caught in 1915, and steelhead rivers that see more bears than anglers.

## Bass, walleye and the everyman fisheries

The Great Lakes are not only a salmonid story. Lake Erie is arguably the best walleye fishery on the planet — its western basin around the Bass Islands and Maumee Bay holds tens of millions of fish, with spring jig bites and summer trolling producing limits of 2- to 5-kilo « eyes ». Smallmouth bass fishing is world-class nearly everywhere: the flats of Lake St. Clair, the rocky shoals of Georgian Bay, Door County in Wisconsin, and the Niagara River drift all produce 2-kilo-plus bronzebacks in numbers that tournament pros build careers on. Yellow perch fries, muskellunge in the St. Lawrence and Detroit rivers, and a growing trophy carp scene round out a fishery where literally every port town has a reason to fish.

## Seasons, licences and the practical map

The fishery is genuinely year-round: ice fishing for walleye and perch in January, river steelhead through winter and spring, walleye and smallmouth from May, offshore salmon trolling peaking July to September, river salmon runs September-October, then back to steelhead. Each state and province sells its own licence (non-resident annual or short-term, generally 15 to 60 US dollars), and Great Lakes salmon and trout often require an additional stamp; charter captains handle the paperwork on guided trips. Charters typically run 600 to 1,200 dollars for a six-hour trip for up to six anglers — among the best value in big-water sportfishing anywhere. One caution: check consumption advisories before eating your catch, as legacy contaminants still limit how much lake trout and big salmon should be eaten.
