Azerbaijan occupies the southern flank of the Caucasus, where the Kura and Aras rivers converge before emptying into the Caspian Sea — the world's largest inland body of water. That Caspian connection defines Azerbaijani fishing: species that exist nowhere else on earth, migration patterns shaped by the closed sea's peculiar chemistry, and a sturgeon heritage that was once the envy of the fishing world. Today, sustainable fishing for kutum, asp, pike-perch, and barbel offers outstanding sport, while the country's reservoirs provide year-round access for coarse anglers.

## The Kura River — Kutum and Barbel in the National River

The Kura (Mtkvari in Georgian, Kür in Azerbaijani) is the longest river in the Caucasus and Azerbaijan's defining waterway. Its lower reaches below Mingachevir are the principal habitat for the Caspian kutum (Rutilus frisii kutum), a large member of the carp family that migrates from the Caspian into the river to spawn each spring between February and April. Kutum can reach 60 cm and 3 kg, and they fight with impressive strength for a white-fish. Local anglers target them with bottom rigs baited with worm or crayfish tail fished in the gravel runs below the city of Mingachevir. Barbel of similar size share the same habitat and provide year-round sport when kutum are absent.

## Mingachevir Reservoir — Pike-Perch and Asp

The Mingachevir Reservoir on the Kura, created in 1953 and covering roughly 605 km², is Azerbaijan's largest body of freshwater and its most productive angling destination. Pike-perch (known locally as sudak) are the premier quarry, growing to 3–5 kg in the productive, relatively shallow waters. Trolling with small perch-coloured minnow lures or jigging with soft plastics over the submerged river channel are the most reliable techniques. Asp — a speciality of Caspian basin rivers — are taken on surface lures and slim metal spoons fished through the reservoir's inlet areas in the early morning, when they chase small roach and bleak near the surface with explosive speed.

## The Aras River — Border Fishing for Barbel and Chub

The Aras River forms Azerbaijan's border with Iran and Turkey along much of its length, and its middle reaches in the Nakhchivan Autonomous Republic hold excellent stocks of Caspian barbel, chub, and the occasional brown trout in the colder tributaries descending from the Zangezur Range. The Aras flows through semi-arid country where red cliffs meet the river on both banks, creating a dramatic backdrop for a day's fishing. Feeder fishing with groundbait for barbel produces reliable results in the slower pools below Julfa; chub take small spinners and weighted flies worked through the faster glides between pools.

## The Caspian Sea — Shore Fishing at Baku and Sumgait

Azerbaijan's Caspian coastline stretches nearly 800 km from the Russian border to the Iranian frontier. Shore fishing from the rocky breakwaters around Baku and the industrial port of Sumgait produces gobies — small but plentiful and edible — along with occasional Caspian roach, bream, and the small Caspian anchovy (kilka) taken on tiny sabiki rigs at night when the fish are attracted to lights. The sea wall at Biləcəri, north of Baku, is a popular spot for local anglers. Boat fishing further offshore for larger Caspian species including bream schools of 1–2 kg and zander is productive in the shallow southern Caspian where depths rarely exceed 30 m.

## The Kura-Aras Lowland — Wetland Fishing for Carp and Bream

Where the Kura and Aras rivers converge near Saatli and Sabirabad, their floodplains spread into a complex of oxbow lakes, seasonal wetlands, and irrigation channels that hold impressive stocks of common carp to 10 kg, bream to 3 kg, and tench. This area is not set up for visiting anglers in any formal sense, but local permission can be obtained and the fishing quality can be exceptional in the right conditions. Late spring and early autumn produce the best sport; midsummer heat and algal blooms reduce water quality significantly. Bread-and-sweetcorn paste fished on a simple ledger rig produces the carp.

## The Goychay and Girdimanchay Rivers — Trout in the Sheki Foothills

The rivers descending from the Greater Caucasus into the Sheki and Gakh districts of north-western Azerbaijan — including the Girdimanchay, Goychay, and Kürmükchay — hold populations of Caucasian brown trout in their upper reaches at elevations above 600 m. These are relatively small streams, typically 10–20 m wide and knee-deep in summer, but the trout are wild and not heavily pressured. Spinning with ultralight tackle and size 0–2 Mepps blades produces consistent results on a summer morning. The surrounding landscape of temperate rainforest in the Hirkan National Park area, with ancient Oriental beech trees and a lush understorey, makes these rivers among the most beautiful in the Caucasus.

## Lake Hajigabul — Carp and Pike in the Lowlands

Hajigabul Lake in the Shirvan lowlands east of Baku is a shallow, moderately saline lake that provides habitat for common carp, pike, and bream. It is one of several lakes in the Shirvan National Park area where fishing is possible with permits. The lake edges are fringed with reed beds that shelter spawning carp in May and June, and predatory pike up to 6 kg hunt the transitions between open water and reed. Surface lures worked along the reed edge at dawn produce pike strikes; carp respond to boilies and sweetcorn fished on hair rigs along the drop from the shallow shelf.

## Sturgeon Heritage — The Caspian Legacy

Although commercial sturgeon fishing is heavily restricted across the Caspian basin due to devastating population declines, Azerbaijan has invested in beluga sturgeon aquaculture facilities near Neftchala on the lower Kura delta. Visitors can tour these operations and observe the fish — beluga that once reached 6 metres and 1,500 kg, now rare and protected. The cultural memory of sturgeon fishing permeates Azerbaijan's relationship with the Caspian; the Shirvan lowland place names Vətəgə and Balıq vətəgələri both literally mean fish station or fishing ground, reflecting centuries of organised Caspian fishery.
