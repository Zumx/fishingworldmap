Lake Huron is the second largest of the Great Lakes by surface area, covering 59,596 square kilometres, and it is divided into two dramatically different fishing environments by the Bruce Peninsula. To the east of the Peninsula, Georgian Bay spreads across 15,000 square kilometres of sheltered water, its eastern and northern shores lined with the ancient Precambrian granite of the Canadian Shield. To the west, the main Huron basin opens to the full sweep of the lake with its trolling runs for lake trout and salmon. Further south, the St. Clair River — the connecting river between Huron and Lake Erie — hosts a muskellunge fishery that is counted among the finest in North America.

## Georgian Bay Smallmouth Bass

Georgian Bay's smallmouth bass fishing is arguably the finest in Canada. The bay's shoreline is a continuous maze of granite islands, submerged rock humps, sand flats between boulder fields, and weed-lined channels — exactly the habitat complexity that smallmouth demand. Fish averaging 1.5 to 2.5 kilograms are present in extraordinary numbers from the Thirty Thousand Islands archipelago near Parry Sound south to the Midland area and north to the French River Delta. The peak season runs from mid-June through September. In June the fish are in spawning mode and aggressive, concentrated in 1 to 3 metres of water on the sunny sides of granite points. By July they move deeper — typically 4 to 8 metres — over sand and gravel near weed edges. Tube jigs, drop-shot rigs with finesse worms, and topwater lures such as the Rapala Walk the Dog produce excellent sport. Fly anglers find that a 6-weight rod with a medium sinking line and a large Clouser Minnow swung around granite boulders is a method of extraordinary effectiveness.

## North Channel Walleye

The North Channel of Lake Huron runs between the northern shore of Manitoulin Island — the world's largest freshwater island — and the Ontario mainland, forming a sheltered waterway stretching nearly 130 kilometres. Walleye are the primary target here, with the shallower bays around Espanola, Little Current, and Massey producing fish averaging 1 to 3 kilograms from April through June. The post-spawn period (mid-May through June) is the most productive, when walleye spread across gravel flats in 3 to 6 metres of water and readily take jigs tipped with minnows, blade baits, and slow-rolled spinners. The North Channel's limited recreational boat traffic compared to Georgian Bay proper makes it possible to fish productive areas without competition — a significant advantage on summer weekends.

## Trophy Muskellunge of the St Clair River

The St. Clair River at the southern end of Lake Huron's jurisdiction — connecting the lake to Lake St. Clair before Lake Erie — is one of the premier muskellunge rivers in North America. The river is 64 kilometres long and flows at a significant pace, with current speeds that push large lures at productive angles without angler effort. Muskellunge (Esox masquinongy) averaging 10 to 14 kilograms are present through the river, with fish exceeding 18 kilograms taken annually near the city of Algonac, Michigan, and around the Walpole Island First Nation on the Ontario side. The standard approach is casting large bucktail jigs, oversized swimbaits, or jointed crankbaits while drifting downstream; the fast current means lures are always moving regardless of retrieve speed. Catch-and-release is strongly encouraged by the Michigan DNR and Ontario MNRF, and most serious musky anglers practice it exclusively.

## Lake Huron Salmon and Lake Trout Trolling

The main Huron basin on the Michigan side — particularly the area between Tawas Bay and Rogers City — supports a productive chinook salmon and lake trout trolling fishery from May through August. Fish are typically found at depths of 20 to 45 metres along the thermocline, with downriggers and wire-line setups presenting large spoons and flasher-fly combinations. Cheboygan, Alpena, and Oscoda are the primary charter ports. Lake trout averaging 2 to 5 kilograms are the most consistently caught species, with chinook running 5 to 12 kilograms in peak summer months.

## French River Delta Bass and Walleye

Where the French River empties into Georgian Bay near Pickerel, a vast delta of channels, bays, and islands creates one of the most productive mixed-species fisheries in Ontario. Walleye congregate at the mouth from mid-May through June, then disperse into the bay. Smallmouth bass are present throughout the river system and the delta islands from June through September. The Upper and Lower French River lodges (operated for generations as outpost camps) provide structured access to water that requires a boat to fish properly, and their combination of comfort, isolation, and consistent fish makes them worth the investment for visiting anglers.

## Access, Licenses, and Logistics

Georgian Bay is primarily accessible from the Ontario towns of Parry Sound, Midland, and Penetanguishene. Ontario fishing licenses are required for all anglers in Canadian waters; annual sport fishing licenses for non-residents cost approximately C$57 with applicable species tags. Michigan waters of Lake Huron require a Michigan fishing license; annual non-resident licenses cost approximately $75. The St. Clair River straddles the international boundary, and anglers need to be aware of whether they are in Canadian or American water — the shipping lane midpoint is the general boundary guide.

## When to Go

Georgian Bay smallmouth: mid-June through September, peak in late June and July. North Channel walleye: May through June for post-spawn fish, again in September and October. St. Clair River musky: June through November, with the September-October period peak. Lake Huron trolling: May through August, with July being the most productive month for chinook.
