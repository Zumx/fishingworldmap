Bosnia and Herzegovina contains some of the most untouched river fishing in Europe. The country's mountainous interior feeds dozens of rivers with constant, cold spring water filtered through karst limestone. The Neretva flows south to the Adriatic, threading through canyons of startling turquoise. The Una marks the northwestern border with Croatia, cascading over natural travertine barriers and through a national park of beech and fir. Smaller rivers — the Trebišnjica, the Pliva, the Vrbas — each support distinct fish populations shaped by their geology and geography. Access infrastructure is less developed than in western Europe, which means that many of the best stretches remain genuinely wild, with very little fishing pressure compared to comparable waters elsewhere on the continent.

## Neretva Huchen (Danube Salmon)

The Neretva is the defining river of Herzegovinian fishing culture, and the huchen — locally called mladica — is its defining fish. Hucho hucho reaches 1.2 metres and exceeds 30 kg in the Neretva's deeper pools, and the river is considered one of the three most important remaining huchen rivers in the Balkans alongside the Drina and the Sava. The season opens in December and runs through February, when post-spawning fish feed aggressively. The section below Jablanica reservoir, between the villages of Jablanica and Čapljina, contains the most productive pools. Large diving lures, 15 to 22 cm articulated jerkbaits in silver and blue-white, are cast across the current and worked through the pool tails.

## Marble Trout in the Upper Neretva

Above the Jablanica dam, the upper Neretva above Konjic holds the native marble trout (Salmo marmoratus × S. trutta hybrid), sometimes referred to locally as mramorka. This fish is genetically distinct from brown trout, characterised by its irregular marble patterning of grey, green, and gold, and it is found wild only in the upper Adriatic river systems of the western Balkans. A carefully managed catch-and-release fly fishery operates on the Neretva above Konjic, issued through the local angling association in Konjic town. Fish average 0.5 to 1.5 kg, but occasional specimens to 5 kg are recorded. The river here is a narrow, rocky freestone stream fished with a 9-foot 4-weight rod and small dry flies or Czech nymphs.

## Una River Fly Fishing at Kulen Vakuf

FF ribolovni revir Kulen Vakuf is a formally managed fly fishing reserve on the Una River, located in the Una-Sana Canton in northwestern Bosnia near the village of Kulen Vakuf. The Una here is a wide, braided river of exquisite clarity, running over white limestone gravel with a gradient that produces a constant, lively current. Brown trout of 300 to 800 grams are present throughout, and grayling are particularly abundant in the flat pools above the road bridge. The revir operates under fly-only, barbless hook, catch-and-release rules. Day permits are issued at the local fishing house; rod numbers are limited to eight per day on the most sensitive beats.

## Revir Krušnice

The Krušnica is a short spring-fed tributary of the Una near Ključ in central Bosnia. Revir Krušnice is a managed trout fishery operating on a 3-kilometre section of the river, with the spring creating a constant, clear flow that supports excellent brown trout and rainbow trout densities year-round. The river is small — some sections are only 4 to 6 metres wide — and it demands accurate short-range casting. Upstream dry fly and short-line nymphing techniques are most effective. The fishery is one of Bosnia's best-kept secrets among European fly fishing travellers; the water quality is equivalent to the finest Austrian chalk streams but the permit costs are a fraction of the price.

## Vrbas River Grayling

The Vrbas flows north from the highlands above Kupres through Jajce and Banja Luka before joining the Sava. The middle Vrbas, between the waterfalls at Jajce and the town of Banja Luka, runs through a deep forested gorge and holds grayling of exceptional quality. Fish averaging 400 to 700 grams respond to upstream nymph techniques and dry flies, and the river's moderate width — 15 to 30 metres — suits both wading and bank fishing. The Jajce angling association manages several kilometres of river beats and issues day permits at the association office. The waterfall at Jajce, where the Pliva joins the Vrbas in a spectacular 17-metre cascade, is a short walk from the best fishing pools.

## Trebišnjica Chalk Stream Trout

The Trebišnjica in eastern Herzegovina is one of the longest sinking rivers in Europe — it disappears underground repeatedly through the limestone karst before emerging at surface springs. The sections where it runs above ground near Trebinje hold brown trout and chub in crystal-clear water, visible against the pale limestone bottom from 10 metres above. Sight fishing to individual trout with small dry flies — size 16 to 20 parachutes and comparaduns in grey and olive — is the preferred technique. The river runs through a semi-arid landscape of fig trees, vineyards, and white rock very different from the lush northern rivers, giving fishing here a distinctly Mediterranean character.

## Ribnjak Semešnica Stocked Trout

Ribnjak Semešnica, near Mrkonjić Grad in central Bosnia, is a structured trout fishery operating on spring-fed ponds with stocked rainbow and brown trout. The fishery caters primarily to families and recreational anglers wanting a reliable fishing experience, and it offers a catch-and-keep model with fish of 0.5 to 3 kg available on a per-kilogram basis. While not a wild fishery, Semešnica's spring water quality produces firm, well-conditioned fish, and the surrounding landscape of central Bosnian mountains makes the visit worthwhile. Rod hire and bait are available on site; no prior permit is required.

## Pliva Lakes Brown Trout

The two Pliva lakes above Jajce, formed by travertine barriers on the Pliva River, are among the most beautiful bodies of water in Bosnia. The larger lake covers 1.27 square kilometres and reaches 40 metres in depth. Wild brown trout inhabit the deeper cold layers, and they feed near the surface in the evening in July and August when sedge and mayfly hatches occur. Dry fly fishing from a small rowing boat is permitted under a day licence issued by the Jajce municipality. The famous water mills on the lower lake — a row of wooden structures overhanging the river — are a UNESCO heritage site visible from the fishing area.

## Bistrica River at Foča

The Bistrica joins the Drina at Foča in eastern Bosnia, a small but exceptionally clear river flowing from the Zelengora mountain plateau. The Bistrica's population of wild brown trout is managed by a local association, and the river is one of the few in the region with a genuine grayling population as well as trout. The mountain setting at 350 to 600 metres elevation means the river remains fishable throughout summer without warming. Early morning sessions in July, when low-angle light illuminates the gravel beds and dew sits on the bankside grass, are considered the finest dry fly opportunities in eastern Bosnia.
