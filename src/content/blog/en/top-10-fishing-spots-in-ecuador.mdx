Ecuador is one of the world's most biodiverse countries by area, and its fishing reflects that biodiversity with almost shocking generosity. The narrow Pacific coast drops to the Galápagos platform, where sailfish, mahi-mahi, and tuna accumulate in waters of extraordinary productivity. The Andes spine runs north to south dividing the country into three distinct zones: the coastal lowlands, the highland páramo where cold Andean rivers hold rainbow trout, and the Amazon basin — the *Oriente* — where jungle tributaries carry golden dorado, payara, and peacock bass. A visiting angler can change species entirely by driving four hours in almost any direction.

## Amazon Headwaters — Golden Dorado

The upper Amazon tributaries in Ecuador's Oriente region — rivers like the Napo, Pastaza, and Curaray — flow through primary rainforest at elevations of 200–800 metres. The golden dorado (*Salminus brasiliensis*) found in these headwaters is among the most powerful freshwater fish on earth for its size: a 5-kg fish fights with the energy of a 15-kg salmon, erupting from the water repeatedly and changing direction with terrifying speed. The best dorado fishing occurs in the dry season (November through February) when water levels drop and fish concentrate in the clear pools and runs below falls. Fly fishing with large streamer patterns in orange, gold, and chartreuse is the primary technique; spinning with surface lures at dawn also produces explosive takes.

## Andean Rainbow Trout — Highland Rivers

Rainbow trout were introduced to Ecuador's highland rivers in the early 20th century and have naturalized in dozens of Andean streams at elevations from 2,500 to 4,000 metres. The rivers feeding into the Quilotoa Crater Lake area, the Pastaza tributaries in Baños canton, and the cold río Papallacta northeast of Quito all hold self-sustaining trout populations. Local trout farms in the area — among them Truchas El Pedregal, El Alizal Pesca Deportiva, and Pesca Deportiva El Corazón — supplement wild fishing and allow anglers to book day sessions on private stocked pools. Wild trout in the mountain rivers average 25–35 cm; genuine wild fish over 40 cm are taken on small nymphs and dry flies during the evening rise. The scenery, fishing against a backdrop of active volcanoes and cloud forest, is unlike anywhere else.

## Galápagos Islands — Deep Blue Pelagic Fishing

The Galápagos archipelago, 1,000 km west of the Ecuadorian mainland, sits in the convergence of three major Pacific currents — the Humboldt, the Panama, and the Cromwell — creating a marine environment of extraordinary richness. Wahoo, dogtooth tuna, yellowfin tuna, and Pacific blue marlin are all found in abundance. Sport fishing is tightly regulated in Galápagos waters to protect the UNESCO World Heritage ecosystem; catch-and-release is mandatory for all billfish and most large pelagics. The Santa Cruz Island (Isla Santa Cruz) is the primary base for offshore charters, which run to the "exclusion zone" edges where fishing is more permissive. Mahi-mahi (dorado) is caught in large numbers around the floating debris lines.

## Pacific Sailfish off Santa Cruz

The Pacific sailfish (*Istiophorus platypterus*) is probably the most sought-after species in Ecuadorian offshore sport fishing. The Humboldt Current upwelling that occurs along the Ecuadorian coast concentrates baitfish — particularly sardine, flying fish, and squid — that in turn attract sailfish in numbers that rival the famous fisheries of Guatemala and Costa Rica. The high season runs from December through April. A typical day in good conditions off the Santa Cruz and Manta coastline can produce 5–15 sailfish releases per boat; fish averaging 35–45 kg but with individuals past 60 kg not uncommon. Trolling with rigged ballyhoo or live sardines under outriggers accounts for most fish; light-tackle fishing on 20-lb class tackle produces the most dramatic fights.

## Río Napo — Payara and Peacock Bass

The Napo River — one of the main Amazon tributaries in Ecuador, draining the eastern slopes of the Andes — is a world-class destination for two of the Amazon's most aggressive sport fish: the payara (*Hydrolycus scomberoides*), also known as the vampire fish for its two long curved fangs, and the peacock bass (*Cichla monoculus*). The Napo's clear tributary streams and oxbow lagoons hold peacock bass that average 1–2 kg with fish to 4 kg taken regularly; payara of 3–5 kg inhabit the faster rocky runs and are best targeted with large diving lures or rigged fish early in the morning. The combination of jungle setting, wildlife (pink river dolphins, giant otters, and macaws are regular sightings), and extraordinary fish makes the Napo basin one of South America's most memorable fishing destinations.

## Baños — Trout in the Gateway to the Amazon

The city of Baños de Agua Santa in Tungurahua Province sits at 1,800 m elevation at the point where the Andean highlands drop toward the Amazon basin. The rivers descending from the active Tungurahua Volcano — particularly the Pastaza and its affluents — carry cold, oxygenated water ideal for trout. Several operations including Brisas Amazónicas and Pesca Deportiva outlets in the canton offer guided sessions and private lake fishing. The canyon scenery around Baños — waterfalls, basalt columns, cloud forest on the slopes — provides one of the most dramatic backdrops for trout fishing in South America. Anglers fishing the río Chambo and Ambato tributaries above Baños at 2,200–3,000 m find genuinely wild fish in less accessible gorges.

## Lago Agrio — Amazon Lowland Bass

The Lago Agrio area in Sucumbíos Province in the far northeast of Ecuador borders Colombia and represents the deep lowland Amazon at its most pristine. The Aguarico River, a major tributary of the Napo, runs through primary forest where deforestation is minimal and the fish populations are largely undisturbed. Peacock bass dominate the ox-bow lakes along the Aguarico; arowana (*Osteoglossum bicirrhosum*) patrol the surface of flooded forest sections in the wet season (May–August). Catfish species — including goliath catfish (*Brachyplatystoma filamentosum*, locally called *salton*) — inhabit the deep channels and can exceed 100 kg in the main river, though catching one requires heavy jigging tackle and a knowledgeable local guide.

## Coastal Estuary — Snook and Tarpon

Ecuador's Pacific coast estuaries — particularly around the Guayas River delta south of Guayaquil and the mangrove systems of Muisne and San Lorenzo — hold fat common snook (*Centropomus undecimalis*) and occasional Pacific tarpon (*Megalops atlanticus* subspecies). These tidal systems are best fished from small canoe or kayak, working topwater lures, large streamers, or live mullet among the mangrove roots at dawn and dusk. Snook to 5 kg are taken regularly in the Guayas estuary from June through September when the dry season drops river turbidity and the fish move to feed on schooling mullet. Local operators at Brisas Amazónicas and Club Social de Caza y Pesca coordinate trips along the coast with bilingual guide services.

## Cotapaxi Area — High-Altitude Trout

The páramo wetlands surrounding Ecuador's famous Cotopaxi Volcano (5,897 m) drain into small rivers and natural lagoons at 3,200–4,000 m that hold wild rainbow trout in cold, clear water. The Limpiopungo Lagoon inside Cotopaxi National Park is catch-and-release only and a genuine cold-water fishery; outside the park, tributaries of the Pita River and the upper Toachi River carry wild fish accessible on foot. Fishing at this altitude requires warmer clothing than the equatorial climate implies; water temperatures stay below 12 °C year-round. Small dry flies on light (#5 weight) rods in the classic upstream method take the best fish.
