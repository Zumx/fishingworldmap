Stand on the gravel bank of the Vorderrhein at dawn and you can watch a brown trout tilt upward through two metres of glass-clear water to sip a mayfly off the surface. Switzerland packs an astonishing variety of fishing into 41,000 square kilometres: lakes more than 300 metres deep holding Arctic char, chalk-clear rivers sliding out of the glaciers, and reservoirs perched above 2,000 metres. The catch — quite literally — is that every canton writes its own rules, so the paperwork is half the adventure.

## How Swiss fishing permits actually work

There is no single national fishing licence. Each of the 26 cantons issues its own permits, and the differences matter. In Graubünden you buy day, week or month patents covering whole districts of rivers and lakes; in Bern you can fish many large lakes free from the bank with a simple "free fishing" right but need a patent for rivers; Ticino sells a tourist-friendly seasonal pass. Since 2009, anyone buying a licence valid longer than one month needs the SaNa certificate (Sachkunde-Nachweis), a short course on fish handling and welfare. Day tickets usually skip this requirement, which is why most visiting anglers stack day permits. Always check closed seasons: brown trout rivers typically close from October to February or March, and minimum sizes vary canton by canton.

## Lake Thun and Lake Brienz — deep-water char and lake trout

The two lakes flanking Interlaken are classic alpine deep-water fisheries. Lake Thun drops to 217 metres and holds Arctic char (locally Seesaibling), lake trout that occasionally pass 80 centimetres, whitefish and pike. Locals troll with downriggers along the steep drop-offs near Spiez and Oberhofen, or jig vertically for char in 40 to 80 metres of water. Lake Brienz, fed directly by glacial rivers, has that surreal turquoise tint and a famously nutrient-poor ecosystem — fish are fewer but the lake trout run large and fight in cold, oxygen-rich water. Spring and late autumn are the prime trolling windows.

## The Vorderrhein gorge — Switzerland's grayling cathedral

The Vorderrhein between Ilanz and Reichenau in Graubünden carves through the Ruinaulta gorge, a canyon of white limestone cliffs reachable in places only by the RhB railway or on foot. This is arguably central Europe's finest grayling water, with fish over 45 centimetres rising freely to dries on summer evenings. The Graubünden patent system covers generous stretches, and the wading is honest — slippery, pushy, glacier-cold even in August. Brown trout share the runs, and the scenery alone justifies the train ticket from Chur.

## The Doubs — wild zebra trout on the French border

Along the Jura border near Saint-Ursanne, the Doubs loops through limestone farmland like a river designed by a dry-fly purist. Its native "zebra" brown trout, with bold vertical parr-like bars, are a distinct local strain, and the grayling fishing can be superb during mayfly hatches in May and June. The river has suffered from pollution episodes, so regulations are strict — catch-and-release zones, barbless hooks in places — and the canton Jura permit spells out each beat. Fish fine tippets; the water is slow, clear and merciless.

## Lake Geneva and Lake Neuchâtel — perch and the table tradition

Lake Geneva (Lac Léman) supports a genuine commercial fishery, and its perch fillets are a regional dish. For sport anglers, the drop-offs off Lausanne, Morges and Lutry produce perch in numbers, plus pike, whitefish (féra) and deep-dwelling char. Lake Neuchâtel, the largest lake entirely within Switzerland at 218 square kilometres, offers similar fishing with less boat traffic — vertical jigging for perch over 15 to 25 metres is the local method from September onward. Boat rental harbours in Estavayer-le-Lac and Neuchâtel make access easy.

## High reservoirs and mountain tarns

Above the treeline, dams and tarns hold trout and char in spectacular settings. Lago di Luzzone and Lago Ritom in Ticino, Lac de Moiry in Valais and the Engadine's chain of lakes around Sils and Silvaplana all fish well from ice-out in June to September. On the Austrian frontier, the Silvretta region's high reservoirs show what these cold impoundments can produce: slow-growing but strikingly coloured fish. Expect short seasons, afternoon winds, and char that key on tiny midges. A 3-weight rod and a box of size 18 dries cover most situations.

## Ticino — Mediterranean light, alpine water

South of the Gotthard, the Ticino canton mixes palm-lined lakeshores with savage mountain valleys. Lago Maggiore and Lago di Lugano hold pike, perch, black bass and big chub along their Swiss shores — the Portofino-style waterfront promenades of Ascona hide surprisingly good evening pike fishing. Inland, the Verzasca and Maggia rivers are famous for emerald pools between sculpted granite; their trout are spooky and the canyon hiking is real, but landing a wild brown in the Verzasca's gin-clear water is a badge of honour. The cantonal tourist patent covers an enormous amount of water for the price.

## Stocked ponds and family-friendly options

Switzerland also has a network of pay ponds where no licence or SaNa is needed — useful for children or a low-key afternoon. Angelparadies Güfel near the Liechtenstein border and the Pêcherie de la source in Romandie are typical examples: stocked rainbow trout, rental rods, and a grill on site. Several piscicultures in Valais, such as the trout farm at Ardon, sell fresh fish and run fishing ponds beside the breeding raceways. They are not wilderness, but they are an honest gateway drug to the cantonal patent waters.

## Seasons, tactics and what to bring

Plan rivers for mid-March to September, with June grayling and trout fishing at its peak after the snowmelt drops. Lakes fish year-round where canton rules allow, with char deepest in summer and shallow at ice-out. Bring a 9-foot 4-weight for rivers, a spinning or trolling outfit for the big lakes, and studded boots for the glacier rivers — Swiss wading is slick. Carry your passport-format photo: several cantons print it on the patent. And budget time for the paperwork; a Graubünden week patent is roughly CHF 100 to 180 depending on zone.
