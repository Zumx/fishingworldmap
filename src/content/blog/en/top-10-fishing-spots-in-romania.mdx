Romania holds the last great wetland fishery in Europe. The Danube, after crossing a continent, unravels here into a delta of 3,400 km of channels, 400 lakes and reed islands that float free in the wind — a labyrinth where wels catfish grow to legendary sizes and pike hunt rudd through crystal bays. Behind it rise the Carpathians, sheltering the huchen, Europe's largest salmonid, in rivers most western anglers have never heard of. Add vast reservoirs, lowland carp lakes and a Black Sea coast, and Romania might be the continent's most complete and least crowded fishing country.

## The Danube Delta — Mila 23 and the heart of the labyrinth

The fishing village of Mila 23, reachable only by boat from Tulcea, is the classic base for the central Delta. From here, guides thread the channels toward lakes Fortuna, Trei Iezere and Bogdaproste, where pike average solid and ten-fish mornings are normal, perch school under the lily pads, and zander hold along deeper edges. May-June and September-October are prime; July's weed makes some lakes unfishable but concentrates fish at channel mouths. Staying in a pension in Mila 23 or Crișan, eating fish soup made with Danube water, is half the experience.

## The Sfântu Gheorghe arm — wels country

The southernmost Delta arm, running 109 km from Tulcea to the sea at Sfântu Gheorghe, holds the deep holes where the giant wels live. Local fishermen tell of fish beyond 100 kg, and verified catches in the 70-90 kg class come every season, typically on clonk gear over 20-30 m holes or on cut bait drifted at night. The same water gives freshwater bream, asp and big zander, and where the arm meets the Black Sea, shore anglers pick up Danube shad in spring.

## Lake Razim and the lagoon complex — pike and zander at sea scale

South of the Delta proper, the Razim-Sinoe lagoon system spreads across more than 1,000 km² of brackish, shallow water. Zander are the signature fish — the lagoons supplied much of Romania's commercial catch historically — with pike and carp around Sarichioi, Jurilovca and the ruins of the Byzantine citadel at Enisala. Wind decides everything on this open water: pick sheltered banks and fish jigs slow along the drop to 3-4 m.

## The Vișeu and Maramureș — huchen in the far north

In Maramureș, against the Ukrainian border, the Vișeu river and the border Tisa hold one of Europe's healthiest huchen populations, protected by strict regulations and a short season (generally autumn through early spring; check current rules, as huchen fishing is tightly licensed). A metre of Danube salmon in a snow-dusted Carpathian valley, with wooden churches and horse carts in the villages, feels like fishing a century back in time. Local guides in Vișeu de Sus arrange permits.

## The Bistrița and Moldavian trout rivers

East of the Carpathian ridge, the Bistrița and its tributaries tumble from the Rodna and Călimani mountains with wild browns, rainbows and grayling. The upper river above Lake Izvorul Muntelui fishes beautifully in June, and the huge reservoir itself — 33 km long beneath the Ceahlău massif — holds lake trout and big pike. In Vrancea county further south, the Lepșa valley pairs mountain trout water with the well-known Păstrăvăria Lepșa trout farm, where a family lunch of grilled trout is mandatory culture.

## Lake Vidraru and the big mountain reservoirs

Dammed in 1966 beneath the Făgăraș mountains, Vidraru's cold, deep water (155 m at the dam) grows lake-run brown trout, perch and huge pike along its drowned forest edges. Boat fishing is the way — the banks are steep and wooded. The Transfăgărășan road, one of the most spectacular drives on the planet, delivers you straight to the dam wall; combine a fishing day with the pass crossing and Bâlea Lake at 2,034 m.

## The Iron Gates — Danube giants at the Cazane gorge

Where the Danube saws through the Carpathians between Romania and Serbia, the Cazane narrows plunge to depths beyond 80 m — the deepest stretch of the entire river. This is authentic monster water: wels catfish of record class, zander stacked on the rock ledges in winter, and asp hammering bleak schools at the surface in summer. Orșova and Dubova make good bases, with the giant rock sculpture of Decebalus watching over your drift.

## Snagov and the Bucharest lake belt

Forty minutes north of Bucharest, Lake Snagov — 16 km of tree-lined, monastery-guarded water — offers the capital's classic escape: carp, pike, zander and huge stocks of crucian. The wider belt of lakes around the city, including Căldărușani with its island monastery, supports a busy weekend culture of float anglers and carp camps. It is the easiest possible introduction to Romanian fishing if your trip starts with a city flight.

## The commercial carp lakes — bălți culture

Romania's lowlands are dotted with hundreds of managed pay lakes — bălți — where carp fishing has boomed into a national passion. Venues around Bucharest, the Bărăgan plain and Transylvania stock commons and mirrors well beyond 20 kg, with night sessions, boilie tactics and competitions mirroring the Western European scene at friendlier prices. For a traveling angler, a balta is the reliable plan B when the Delta wind blows or time runs short.

## The Mureș and the western lowland rivers

Flowing 760 km from the Carpathians to the Tisa, the Mureș is Transylvania's workhorse river: wels in the deep clay holes below Arad and Lipova, zander and asp on the gravel runs, barbel in the faster water upstream around Alba Iulia. Its quieter cousin, the Someș, and the southern Jiu and Olt add endless under-fished kilometres. Floating the Mureș by boat in September, casting to timber, is one of Eastern Europe's sleeper trips.

## Practical notes — permits, seasons and access

Recreational fishing requires a national permit issued through ANPA or affiliated associations; the Danube Delta Biosphere Reserve needs an additional permit, easily arranged by guesthouses and guides in Tulcea. The general closed season protects spawning from spring for roughly two months (dates set annually); huchen and trout have their own strict windows. Delta logistics run through Tulcea — there are no roads inside the reserve core, so plan boats ahead. Spring (May-June) and autumn (September-October) are the universal sweet spots.
