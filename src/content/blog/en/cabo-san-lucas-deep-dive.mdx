Stand on the Cabo San Lucas marina at 6:30 in the morning and you will watch one of the most concentrated sportfishing fleets in the world file out past Land's End, the granite arch where the Pacific Ocean meets the Sea of Cortez. Within twenty minutes of leaving the harbor, many of those boats are already fishing in water over 300 meters deep. That absurd proximity of abyssal structure to a full-service tourist town is the engine behind a statistic local captains repeat with a straight face: more marlin are caught off Cabo San Lucas, per boat and per day, than anywhere else on the planet.

## Why the geography stacks the deck

The tip of the Baja California Peninsula is not a gentle continental shelf. The San Lucas Submarine Canyon begins practically inside the bay, plunging toward depths beyond 2,000 meters within a few kilometers of the beach. Around it sit a series of famous high spots — the Golden Gate Bank and San Jaime Bank to the west on the Pacific side, the Gordo Banks (Outer and Inner) about 10 nautical miles off San José del Cabo on the Cortez side, and the 95 and 1150 spots in between. Cold, nutrient-rich upwelling from the Pacific collides here with the warmer, calmer Sea of Cortez, concentrating sardines, mackerel and squid. Billfish, tuna and dorado simply follow the groceries, and the fleet follows the billfish. The result is a fishery where a half-day trip is a legitimate shot at a marlin, something almost unheard of elsewhere.

## The striped marlin machine

Striped marlin are the bread and butter of Cabo, and the cooler months are their show. From roughly November through March, stripers stack up on the Pacific banks in numbers that produce the famous double-digit days. The classic technique is slow-trolling live caballito or green mackerel, but the most exciting development of the past two decades has been sight-casting: spotting tailing or sleeping marlin on the surface, then pitching a live bait or even a fly in front of them. On the better days around the Golden Gate Bank, crews may raise 15 to 30 fish. Most striped marlin here run 50 to 90 kilograms — not giants, but on 30-pound stand-up tackle they are spectacular, spending half the fight in the air. Mexico's regulations allow one billfish per angler per day, but the Cabo fleet releases the overwhelming majority, and circle hooks are now standard practice on live bait.

## Blues, blacks and the summer heavyweights

When the water warms past 26 °C from June into November, the character of the fishery changes. Blue marlin in the 100 to 300 kilogram class move onto the banks, and genuine giants over 400 kilograms are hooked every season, usually on big trolled lures pulled at 8 to 9 knots. Black marlin, scarcer but stockier, tend to hold near structure like the Gordo Banks, often eating a live skipjack slow-trolled just off the high spot. This is also prime time for yellowfin tuna — football-sized fish under the porpoise schools, with 100-kilo-plus cows showing at the banks in late summer — plus dorado that pile up on any floating debris and wahoo that haunt the ledges in October and November. The Bisbee's Black & Blue tournament in late October turns the marina into a carnival, with team payouts that have exceeded four million US dollars.

## Roosterfish: the beach game nobody expects

Cabo's secret is that you do not need a boat at all. From roughly May through September, roosterfish — pez gallo, with their wild seven-rayed dorsal comb — patrol the surf line along the East Cape beaches and the stretches near San José del Cabo. Anglers walk the sand at first light throwing big surface poppers and ranger-style lures on 50- to 80-gram spinning gear, watching for the unmistakable sight of a comb slicing through a panicked sardine school in knee-deep water. Fish from 5 to 25 kilograms are realistic, and the take, often in less than a meter of water, is one of the most violent visuals in shore fishing. Jack crevalle, ladyfish and the occasional snook share the same beaches. Roosterfish have almost no food value and are released as a matter of near-universal ethic.

## Inshore variety: the underrated half of Cabo

Between the surf and the banks lies a rich middle game. Panga fishing along the Pacific side rocks produces sierra mackerel in winter — superb for ceviche — along with various snappers, amberjack and the hard-pulling Pacific dog snapper around structure. The Cabo San Lucas bay itself holds yellowtail in the cooler months, when 10- to 20-kilo fish crash bait around the famous arch at Land's End. Light-tackle anglers who tire of trolling can spend a morning jigging the canyon edges for grouper and pargo, or drifting squid strips at the Gordo Banks for the chance of a wahoo or a big amberjack. This variety is what separates Cabo from one-trick billfish ports: a blown-out marlin day still has a productive plan B.

## Seasons, money and how the fleet works

Cabo fishes twelve months a year, and the planning question is really about target species. December through March: peak striped marlin, sierra, yellowtail, whale watching as a bonus. April and May: transition, still good stripers, dorado building. June through August: blue and black marlin arrive, roosterfish peak inshore, tuna everywhere. September and October: arguably the best all-around window — all three marlin species, wahoo, big dorado — though it overlaps hurricane season. Charters range from pangas at 250 to 400 US dollars a half day to 35-foot-plus cruisers at 800 to 2,000 dollars. Every angler needs a Mexican sportfishing license (FONMAR), typically arranged by the boat, and crews work hard for the customary 15 to 20 percent tip. Book directly with established operations at the marina or in advance online; the dock hustlers selling discounted trips are a lottery.

## Conservation and the catch-and-release culture

The Cabo fishery has flirted with its own success for decades. Striped marlin in the eastern Pacific are managed under Mexican law that reserves billfish, by decree, for sportfishing within 50 miles of the coast, and the release ethic — over 95 percent of billfish released in most of the fleet — is genuinely strong. Pressure remains from long-lining outside the protected zone and from inconsistent enforcement, which is why organizations and tournaments here push circle hooks, careful boat-side handling and tag-and-release science. Visiting anglers can help: insist on circle hooks with live bait, keep marlin in the water for photos, and choose crews that resuscitate fish properly before release.
