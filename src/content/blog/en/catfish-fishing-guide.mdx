The Mississippi River drains 3.2 million km² of North America, and at the bottom of every deep hole along its 3,700 km main channel, something old and heavy is waiting. Blue catfish that have lived for 20 years and weigh more than 40 kg are not legend in the rivers of the American South; they are biology. Understanding what drives their behaviour — water temperature, dissolved oxygen, barometric pressure, seasonal forage migrations — is the difference between an accidental giant and a systematic trophy fishery.

## The Three Major Species: Know Your Target

**Channel catfish** (Ictalurus punctatus) are the most widely distributed and most popular catfish species in North America, found from the Saint Lawrence-Great Lakes basin to northern Mexico. They grow to around 15 kg in exceptional cases but average 1-3 kg across most waters. Channel cats are opportunistic feeders that respond well to stink baits, dip baits, chicken liver, and prepared punch baits. They are the ideal starting species for new catfish anglers.

**Blue catfish** (Ictalurus furcatus) are the giants of the family. The IGFA all-tackle world record stands at 51 kg, caught in Kerr Lake on the Virginia-North Carolina border in 2011. Blues are the primary target for serious trophy anglers on large rivers including the James, Roanoke, Missouri, and Mississippi. Unlike channel cats, blue catfish are aggressive active hunters of live and freshly dead shad, herring, and skip jack.

**Flathead catfish** (Pylodictis olivaris) are the lone wolf of the catfish world. They are almost exclusively ambush predators that eat live fish, crustaceans, and occasionally large crayfish. Flatheads haunt rocky logjam areas and undercut banks in rivers from the Great Lakes basin to the Gulf Coast. The challenge with flatheads is the bait: live bullheads, sunfish, or large creek chubs are far more effective than any cut or prepared bait.

## Reading River Structure for Trophy Catfish

Catfish are not randomly distributed. Blue catfish in particular follow the same hydraulic logic as salmon: they congregate where energy is conserved and food is delivered. The classic holding structure in a large river is the deep hole immediately downstream of a wing dam or dike, where current velocity drops sharply and shad killed by turbine passage or wing-dam turbulence accumulate.

On the Mississippi below St. Louis, anglers routinely find blue catfish stacked in 10-18 m holes downstream of major current deflection structures. The upstream lip of the hole — where current transitions from fast to slow — is the key feeding zone. Fish positioned here face into the slow current and let the river deliver food to them with minimal energy expenditure.

In reservoir settings, blue cats follow the thermocline and the baitfish that cluster along it, particularly in late summer when dissolved oxygen below the thermocline becomes insufficient to support fish life. Ledges and points adjacent to the old river channel typically hold the highest concentrations.

## Cut Bait: The Blue Catfish's Weak Spot

The most consistently effective bait for large blue catfish is fresh cut skipjack herring (Alosa chrysochloris) or gizzard shad (Dorosoma cepedianum). Both species release an oily, amino-acid-rich cloud in the current that blue cats can detect at distances of 100 m or more using their highly sensitive lateral line and olfactory organs. The cut must be fresh — bait that has been sitting in a cooler for more than 24 hours loses a significant portion of its attractant quality. Many serious trophy catfish anglers go out at night specifically to snag fresh shad as bait for the following day's session.

The best cuts are the head section — where blood and oil concentration is highest — and midsection belly cuts that expose the oily body cavity. Tail sections and fillets are the least effective. Hook size for large blue catfish should be in the 10/0 to 12/0 range on a heavy wire circle hook, which dramatically increases hook-up rate and reduces gut-hooking for catch-and-release.

## Rigs for Catfish: Simplicity Wins

The three-way rig, the Carolina rig, and the slip-sinker rig account for the vast majority of catfish caught on cut bait. The three-way rig uses a three-way swivel with the main line on one eye, a sinker dropper on the second, and the hook leader on the third. This keeps the bait slightly off the bottom where current can carry the scent downstream. Sinker weight should be just enough to hold position without dragging — typically 60-140 g in moderate river current.

The slip-sinker rig, also called a Carolina rig in its most basic form, allows a fish to pick up the bait and move without feeling the weight of the sinker. This is particularly useful in still-water reservoir settings and for channel catfish that are more likely to drop a bait under resistance.

## Night Fishing Tactics

Catfish, particularly flatheads and channel cats in rivers, are predominantly nocturnal feeders. Water temperature above 20°C amplifies nighttime feeding activity, making summer nights the prime season on most rivers. On the Wabash River in Indiana, the Tennessee River in Alabama, and the Red River between North Dakota and Manitoba, night sessions in July and August regularly produce the year's best catches.

Key nighttime tactics include positioning on the upstream edge of structure rather than directly in the hole — feeding fish move onto the flats and ledges at night when they feel safe — and using a rod holder setup that allows you to monitor multiple rods simultaneously. The bite on cut bait at night is typically a slow initial movement followed by a run as the fish mouths the bait, then a second, decisive run when it commits. Set on that second run.

## Flathead Secrets: Live Bait and Patience

Flathead catfish in excess of 30 kg are genuine river monsters, but they require an entirely different approach. They do not respond to cut bait or prepared baits. The most effective approach is a large live bream (sunfish, bluegill) hooked lightly through the back behind the dorsal fin, suspended 30-60 cm off the bottom near a large woody debris pile, root wad, or logjam in a river with moderate current.

Flatheads are notoriously reluctant to bite on bright days or during high-pressure weather systems. The best fishing is typically on overcast nights with falling barometric pressure — the same conditions that trigger feeding across most freshwater predator species. The Green River in Kentucky, the Neosho River in Kansas, and the Guadalupe River in Texas are among the premier flathead destinations in North America.

## Seasonal Patterns and Spawning Behaviour

All three major catfish species spawn in late spring to early summer when water temperatures reach 21-24°C. During pre-spawn, catfish concentrate in predictable staging areas close to spawning habitat — typically protected coves, undercut banks, and logjams. This is the single best time of year for trophy catfish of all species, as large fish are actively feeding to build energy reserves before the metabolic cost of spawning.

Post-spawn, catfish disperse and can be harder to locate. By late summer, falling water temperatures and improved dissolved oxygen concentrations pull fish deeper and onto main-channel structure where they remain through winter in a reduced metabolic state.
