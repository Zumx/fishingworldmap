Japan's relationship with fishing runs deeper than sport or sustenance: it is woven into the cultural and ceremonial fabric of the nation. The country's varied geography — volcanic mountain ranges, 30,000 km of deeply indented coastline, and offshore islands scattered into the subtropical Pacific — produces fishing environments of remarkable contrast. An angler who spends a week fly-fishing for cherry trout (yamame) in a Nagano prefecture gorge and then boards a shinkansen south to Kagoshima for offshore yellowfin tuna will barely feel they have visited the same country. That diversity is Japan's greatest angling asset.

## Hokkaido: Ice Fishing for Wakasagi on Lake Abashiri

When temperatures on Hokkaido's Okhotsk coast drop to minus 20°C in January, ice fishers drill holes through 60–80 cm of frozen surface on Lake Abashiri and lower tiny tanago jigs for wakasagi (Japanese smelt). The fish average only 10–15 cm but are caught by the bucketful; combined with the ice huts (kamakura-style shelters rented on the lake) and a finish of freshly fried smelt, it becomes a complete winter experience. Lake Tofutsu, just east of Abashiri, is another excellent wakasagi venue. Larger sport fish — including brown trout introduced to Hokkaido's rivers and rainbow trout in Lake Shikotsu — provide year-round options for rod-and-reel anglers.

## Hokkaido: Salmon Fly Fishing on the Tokachi River

The Tokachi River system, draining eastward from the Daisetsuzan mountain range to Tokachi Bay, supports sizeable runs of pink salmon (karafuto masu) in late summer and Masu salmon from April through May. The Uktotsunai tributary and the Satsunai River, both fed by snowmelt from peaks exceeding 2,000 metres, offer fast, boulder-strewn water ideal for swinging flies and using Spey-cast techniques. Pink salmon here average 1.5–2.5 kg and fight hard in cold current. Bear encounters are a genuine possibility — local anglers carry bells and fish in pairs, and some hire licensed bear-watch guides.

## Nikko and Tochigi: Ayu Fishing on the Daiya River

The ayu (sweetfish, Plecoglossus altivelis) is the most culturally significant freshwater fish in Japan. Every June, the ayu season opens on rivers throughout Honshu, and the Daiya River below Nikko's famous shrines draws experienced tenkara and ayu-specific anglers from across the Kanto region. Wild ayu of 20–25 cm are caught using the traditional tomozuri technique — live-bait ayu on a rig that exploits the fish's territorial aggression to provoke a territorial bite. Regulations require a river licence purchased from the local fisheries cooperative; season runs June through October.

## Nagano: Mountain Trout and Iwana in the Japan Alps

The Japanese char (iwana, Salvelinus leucomaenis) inhabits the highest and coldest headwaters of the Japan Alps in Nagano and Gifu prefectures. Tributaries of the Nakatsu River in the Oku-Tanzawa area and the Mimosa headwaters in Nagano hold populations of wild iwana in gin-clear water flowing over granite boulders at elevations between 1,200 and 2,000 metres. The fish rarely exceed 30 cm but are delicate, challenging targets on tenkara rods with #2–4 flies. Access requires a hiking approach of 2–5 hours; fishing is strictly regulated with catch-and-release zones on many upper reaches.

## Tokyo Bay: Squiding and Eeling within Sight of the Skyline

Tokyo Bay, enclosed by the Boso and Miura peninsulas, may not look like prime fishing territory from a distance, but its rich shipping lanes and oyster-culture areas create diverse habitats. Night squid fishing (ika fishing) from the jetties of Yokohama and from dedicated party boats out of Yokosuka is enormously popular from July through December; anglers use light jigs called egi to attract Japanese flying squid. Conger eel (anago) fishing from anchored boats in the bay's muddy shallows is a Tokyo tradition reaching back centuries; anago caught in Tokyo Bay appear on the menus of the finest Edo-style sushi restaurants in the city.

## Kanagawa: Yellowtail and Sea Bream on Sagami Bay

Sagami Bay, south of Tokyo, opens onto Pacific swell and supports excellent offshore fishing for Japanese amberjack (hamachi/buri), red sea bream (madai), and from autumn through winter, Pacific bluefin tuna. The madai fishery is Japan's most culturally loaded: the red snapper is the symbol of celebration and good fortune. Party boats depart daily from Hayama, Misakiguchi, and Manazuru harbours; madai are caught on slow-pitch jigging and live-bait rigs at 30–80 metres depth over sandy ground. Autumn hamachi runs, with fish averaging 3–6 kg, are some of the most exciting light-tackle sport available on the Shonan coast.

## Kochi, Shikoku: Katsuo (Bonito) Pole-and-Line Fishing

Kochi Prefecture on Shikoku's south coast is synonymous with katsuo — Pacific bonito. The century-old pole-and-line fishery here targets bonito schools driven to the surface by feeding tuna and dolphins; no-barb hooks are used so fish flip free instantly, allowing a single skilled angler to catch hundreds per hour during a school blitz. While commercial pole-and-line boats are not open to sport anglers, charter vessels out of Tosa Bay offer sport bonito fishing from May through October. Bonito tataki — seared over straw, rubbed with sea salt, sliced and served with ponzu and ginger — is Kochi's defining dish, and the flavour difference between a fish caught that morning and one bought at a supermarket is unmistakeable.

## Kagoshima: Offshore Yellowfin and GT Around Yakushima

Yakushima Island, a UNESCO World Heritage site 60 km south of Kagoshima, is surrounded by warm Kuroshio Current water that concentrates pelagic species year-round. Yellowfin tuna averaging 30–50 kg are targeted by jigging and popping over the island's underwater cliff walls on the east and south coasts. Giant trevally of 20–40 kg are caught on large poppers at dawn on the exposed northwest coast. Charter boats operating from Miyanoura and Anbo harbours cater specifically to sport anglers; October through December is peak season as Kuroshio intrusions bring the warmest, clearest water.

## Okinawa: Flats Fishing for Triggerfish and Bonefish

The Okinawan island chain, stretching 1,000 km southwest toward Taiwan, has shallow coral lagoons ringed by barrier reefs that give the waters a Caribbean quality. The flats around Ishigaki Island support a growing sport fishery for bonefish (sōda-katsuo and related species) and titan triggerfish on fly and light spinning gear. Permits (for fishing, not the fish) are available from the Okinawa Prefectural Government; the best wading season is April through June before typhoon season begins. Local guides operate out of Kabira Bay on Ishigaki's northwest coast.

## Ogasawara Islands: Deep-Sea Jigging and Dogtooth Tuna

The Ogasawara (Bonin) Islands, 1,000 km south of Tokyo and reachable only by a 25-hour ferry, offer some of Japan's most remote and rewarding deep-sea fishing. Dogtooth tuna exceeding 60 kg are caught on knife-edge jigs dropped to 150–200 metres along the walls of seamounts north of Chichi-jima. Amberjack, grouper, and giant trevally complete a typical species list. Because the islands are accessible by ferry only every six days, most visiting anglers combine a week of fishing with snorkelling on reefs that have never been subjected to commercial fishing pressure.
