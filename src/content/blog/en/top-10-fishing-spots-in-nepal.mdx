Few anglers think of Nepal, and that is exactly the point. While trekkers queue for Everest Base Camp, the rivers draining the highest mountains on Earth carry one of the world's great freshwater gamefish: the golden mahseer, a barbel-relative with armour-plate scales that can pass 25 kilograms and pulls like a freight train. Add snow trout in the high tributaries, catfish in the Terai lowlands and rainbow trout farms perched on Himalayan hillsides, and Nepal becomes a genuine angling destination hiding behind a trekking brochure.

## The Karnali — Nepal's wildest mahseer river

The Karnali is Nepal's longest river, rising near Mount Kailash in Tibet and cutting through the remote far-west before spilling into the Terai plains. Its lower gorges, particularly around Chisapani where the river leaves the hills, are the classic golden mahseer beat. Expeditions float multi-day raft-supported stretches, camping on white sand bars and casting heavy spoons and plugs into the seams where green water meets the boils. February to April and October to November, either side of the monsoon, are the windows — during the monsoon itself the river is an unfishable brown torrent. Fish over a metre long are caught most seasons.

## The Babai — protected water inside Bardia National Park

The Babai flows through Bardia National Park, and the stretch below the Babai bridge is one of the few places where mahseer fishing is managed with permits issued through the park. The reward for the paperwork is extraordinary: you fish pools patrolled by mugger crocodiles and visited by wild elephants, with a park guide mandatory at your side. Catch-and-release is the rule, single barbless hooks are expected, and the fishing pressure is close to zero. Bardia's lodges in Thakurdwara arrange the permits and the armed escort — this is angling as safari.

## Pokhara's lakes — Phewa, Begnas and Rupa

Pokhara is Nepal's second city and its lake district. Phewa Tal, with the Annapurna massif and the fishtail peak of Machhapuchhare reflected in its surface, supports a local fishery for carp, catfish and the introduced tilapia; wooden doonga boats are cheap to hire and fishing from them is the standard method. Quieter Begnas Tal, 15 kilometres east, is better for serious anglers — less boat traffic, established cage-culture operations, and good margins for carp at dawn. The Nepali word pokhari simply means pond, and you will see small village pokhari everywhere; many are community fish ponds stocked with carp.

## The Trishuli — roadside access on the Kathmandu highway

The Trishuli runs beside the Prithvi Highway between Kathmandu and Pokhara, making it the most accessible mahseer water in the country. Rafting companies dominate the river by day, so fish early and late, targeting the deep confluence pools — the junction with the Budhi Gandaki near Benighat is a known holding area. Mahseer here run smaller than in the far west, but a roadside fish of 5 to 10 kilograms is realistic, and you can be back in Kathmandu for dinner.

## Snow trout in the high tributaries

Above roughly 1,500 metres, the asala or snow trout (Schizothorax species) takes over. It is not a true trout but behaves like one, holding in pocket water and taking small spinners and nymphs. The upper Seti, the Modi Khola below the Annapurna Sanctuary and the upper Kali Gandaki around Tatopani all hold asala. Locals fish for them with hand lines and cast nets; a light spinning rod or a 4-weight fly rod is a luxury the fish have rarely seen. Treat them gently — populations are under pressure from damming.

## Rainbow trout farms — a Himalayan surprise

Since the 1990s, Nepal's fisheries agencies have built a genuine rainbow trout industry in the cold hill districts. Around Kathmandu's rim and in the Kakani and Nuwakot areas, farms like the Murali Khola trout farm and a string of operations along the Helambu road raise rainbows in concrete raceways fed by spring water at 10 to 15°C. Many double as restaurants where you pick your fish or catch it from a pond, and they make an easy half-day from the capital. On the Phulchoki hillside above Godawari, trout ponds sit among oak forest at over 2,000 metres. It is not wild fishing, but the fish are superb on a plate of jhol curry.

## Rara Lake — the remote jewel of the far west

Rara, Nepal's largest lake at 10.8 square kilometres and 2,990 metres elevation, sits inside Rara National Park in Mugu district — a flight to Talcha airstrip and a few hours' walk away. Three endemic snow trout species live here and nowhere else on Earth. Fishing is heavily restricted and at times prohibited outright, so check current park rules before carrying a rod; for many visitors Rara is a place to watch fish rise across mirror-calm water beneath blue pines rather than to cast. Few bodies of water anywhere feel this untouched.

## The Terai ponds and the eastern rivers

The flat southern Terai is Nepal's aquaculture belt. Around Tarahara near Dharan, government fisheries research ponds and dozens of private farms raise carp, and the eastern rivers — the Koshi system above the barrage — hold catfish, gounch (the giant devil catfish, Bagarius yarrelli) and mahseer in the cold months. Integrated farms such as the Panauti operation in the mid-hills combine fish ponds with livestock in the traditional way. Village fish hatcheries supply fingerlings across the country, and many welcome visitors who ask politely.

## Permits, seasons and practicalities

Nepal has no nationwide rod licence, but national parks (Bardia, Chitwan, Rara) require fishing permits and guides, and local districts may charge fees on specific rivers. The dry seasons — October to early December and February to April — are prime; winter water is cold and clear, and pre-monsoon April fishing can be superb as fish feed hard. Bring your own tackle: quality lures, 20 to 30 lb braid and spare trebles are nearly impossible to buy outside Kathmandu. A raft-supported multi-day trip on the Karnali books months ahead through Kathmandu-based outfitters.
