No river on Earth carries a greater weight of human meaning than the Ganges. Rising from the Gangotri Glacier in the Garhwal Himalaya at an elevation of roughly 3,892 metres, it descends through sacred gorges, past pilgrimage cities, and across the vast Indo-Gangetic Plain before emptying into the Bay of Bengal some 2,525 kilometres from its source. For over a billion Hindus it is Ganga Ma — Mother Ganges — a living goddess. For naturalists and anglers, the upper river is something else: one of the last refuges of the golden mahseer, a fish of mythological proportions in the angling world.

## The Golden Mahseer: A Fish of Legend

Tor putitora, the golden mahseer, is often described as the ultimate freshwater sport fish of the Indian subcontinent. It is a large cyprinid — a relative of carp and barbel — capable of reaching 45 kg or more in its optimal mountain river habitat. The fish are bronze-gold in colour, heavily scaled, and fight with a power and endurance that experienced anglers compare to atlantic salmon on a far heavier scale. Nineteenth-century British colonial sportsmen elevated mahseer fishing to an art form, and the records they published — fish of 40, 50, and in some disputed accounts 60 kg — created a legacy that still draws anglers to the Himalayan foothills.

## The Upper Ganges: Rishikesh to Haridwar

The most accessible mahseer fishing on the Ganges is in the stretch between Rishikesh and Haridwar in Uttarakhand state, where the river emerges from its final mountain gorge onto the plains. The water here is still clean, fast, and cold by comparison with the middle and lower river, and mahseer populations — though dramatically reduced from historical levels — persist in the rapids and deep pools of this stretch. Fishing is conducted almost exclusively from the bank or by wading; motorised boats are prohibited in the ecologically sensitive zone near Rajaji National Park, which encompasses this reach. The best months are October through February, when water levels are low and the fish are concentrated.

## Jim Corbett Country: The Ramganga

A few hours east of Rishikesh, the Ramganga River flows through the core zone of Corbett National Park — India's oldest wildlife reserve, established in 1936 and named for the legendary hunter-turned-conservationist Jim Corbett. The Ramganga holds both the golden mahseer and its relative the khursa mahseer, and the park's strict protection has meant that fish populations here are among the healthiest in India. Fishing inside the core zone is not permitted, but the buffer zone and the Corbett Ramganga reservoir hold fish that can be targeted on day trips from the resorts at Ramnagar, roughly 230 kilometres northeast of Delhi.

## Conservation and the Mahseer Crisis

The golden mahseer's future is genuinely precarious. The IUCN Red List reclassified Tor putitora as Endangered in 2018. Causes include dam construction that blocks migration routes, pollution from the massive urban centres downstream, sand mining that destroys spawning gravels, and historic overfishing. The Mahseer Trust, a UK-based conservation charity founded by angler and naturalist Adrian Pinder, has been instrumental in changing fishing practices on key rivers. Their philosophy — total catch-and-release, no treble hooks, barbless flies and lures only — has become the standard for responsible mahseer angling on the upper Ganges and its tributaries.

## The Spiritual Dimension

Fishing the Ganges is inseparable from the river's spiritual context. The ghats of Haridwar and Rishikesh see thousands of pilgrims daily; evening aarti ceremonies — where lamps are offered to the river goddess — draw enormous crowds at both cities. Anglers fishing near these zones are immersed in a sensory environment unlike any other: incense, chanting, and bells drifting downstream as heron work the margin pools. Many anglers report that this combination of natural and spiritual grandeur makes the Ganges an experience that transcends the fishing itself.

## Reaching the Upper Ganges

Haridwar and Rishikesh are both well connected by road and rail from Delhi, roughly 250 kilometres to the southwest. Haridwar has a railway station on the main Northern Railway network; Rishikesh is a short taxi ride from Haridwar. The nearest airports with significant connectivity are Jolly Grant Airport at Dehradun (35 km from Rishikesh) and Indira Gandhi International in Delhi. Fishing guides and outfitters operate from Rishikesh and from eco-camps along the river; advance booking is advisable for the October–February peak season.

## Other Mahseer Rivers of the Himalayan Foothill Zone

The upper Ganges tributaries offer additional opportunities. The Kali (Sharda) River on the Nepal border, the Bhagirathi above Tehri Dam, and the Song River below Rajaji National Park are all worth exploring for anglers seeking fewer crowds. Further afield, the Cauvery River in Karnataka holds a separate subspecies — the Hump-backed mahseer, Tor remadevii — in a warm-water context entirely different from the Himalayan foothills, with established lodges near Coorg providing guided fishing on a catch-and-release basis.
