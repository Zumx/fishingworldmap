The water is the first shock. The River Test does not look like other rivers: it runs gin-clear over bright gravel and waving ranunculus weed, fed not by rain but by chalk aquifers that filter every drop for months before releasing it at a near-constant 10 to 11 °C. Brown trout hang in the current like mobiles, visible at thirty paces, examining every mayfly with the scepticism of fish that have seen artificial flies for two centuries. Rising near Ashe in the Hampshire downs and flowing roughly 64 km through Whitchurch, Stockbridge and Romsey to Southampton Water, the Test is the most famous trout stream on Earth — the river where modern dry-fly fishing was codified, argued over and turned into something close to a religion.

## What a chalk stream actually is

There are around 200 to 260 chalk streams in the world, and roughly 85 percent of them are in England — a genuinely rare habitat, rarer than rainforest. Rain falling on the chalk downs percolates underground, sometimes for months, and emerges as spring water of remarkable clarity, stable temperature and high alkalinity. That chemistry grows dense weed, which grows freshwater shrimp and olive nymphs in extraordinary densities, which grow fat, free-rising trout. It also means the river barely floods and barely drops: the Test fishes through summer droughts that kill spate rivers. The price of all this is fragility — abstraction and pollution threaten chalk streams everywhere, which is why the Test's keepers guard their water so fiercely.

## Two centuries of history

The Test's modern history begins at the Houghton Club, founded in 1822 and headquartered at the Grosvenor Hotel in Stockbridge, whose members have fished the same 21 km of water ever since and recorded every trout in handwritten leather-bound ledgers. In the late nineteenth century, F. M. Halford fished the Test and the neighbouring Itchen while writing the books that defined upstream dry-fly purism; G. E. M. Skues answered with the case for nymph fishing, and their courteous, decades-long feud still shapes how the river is fished. Lord Mountbatten's Broadlands estate at Romsey hosted royal fishing parties on the lower river; Mottisfont Abbey's beats, now in National Trust hands, appear in centuries of angling literature. Walking a Test beat, you are fishing through a library.

## The rules of the game

Test etiquette is strict and the same on almost every beat: upstream presentation only, to a fish you have seen rising or spotted in the water — no blind flogging. Dry fly only on many beats; dry fly and unweighted nymph on others, with the nymph usually permitted only after July when the main hatches fade. You wade rarely or never — most beats are fished from the bank, kneeling or crouching, using the bankside sedges as cover. Each beat is yours alone for the day, an exclusivity that explains the price. Barbless hooks are increasingly required, and most beats now run full catch-and-release for wild fish. Ask the keeper what the river expects; on the Test, the keeper's word is law and his advice is gold.

## Mayfly time and the rest of the season

The trout season generally runs from April or May to the end of September. Its crescendo is Mayfly — two to three weeks from late May into June when Ephemera danica hatches in clouds and even the oldest, wariest browns lose their composure. The locals call it duffers' fortnight, which is unkind but not untrue: it is the easiest time to catch a big Test trout and the hardest time to book a beat. Before it come dark olives and hawthorn flies in April and May; after it, smaller olives, sedges in the evenings, and terrestrial patterns through high summer. From October to early spring, many beats open for grayling — wild, abundant, and the affordable back door to fishing this river.

## How to actually get a beat

Here is the practical secret: the Test is not closed to you. Day rods on famous water — Mottisfont, Kimbridge, Timsbury, Bossington, Broadlands, Wherwell, Leckford and dozens of others — are sold through agencies, of which Fishing Breaks (founded by Simon Cooper, who writes the river's best-known newsletter) is the largest, alongside estate offices and hotels like the Greyhound on the Test in Stockbridge. Expect roughly 100 to 200 pounds per rod-day on carrier and middle-river beats, climbing to 300-plus for prime Mayfly dates on storied water; grayling days in winter can cost a third of that. Book Mayfly six to twelve months ahead. You will also need an Environment Agency rod licence, bought online in minutes. A guide is worth the money for a first visit — reading water this clear is a skill of its own.

## Tackle and tactics for glass-clear water

Leave the heavy gear at home. The classic Test outfit is an 8 to 8.5-foot rod for a 3 or 4-weight line, a 12 to 15-foot leader tapering to 5X or 6X, and a box built around olives: CDC duns, Parachute Adams in 16-18, Kite's Imperial, hawthorn and black gnat patterns for spring, Grey Wulff and French Partridge mayflies for the great hatch, and small sedges for dusk. Degrease your tippet, dry your fly often, and cast less than you think — the standard Test mistake is casting at the river instead of at a fish. Watch the water for ten minutes before your first cast: find a feeding fish, work out its rhythm, and put the fly 60 cm above its nose. One good presentation beats fifty hopeful ones.

## Beyond the riverbank

Stockbridge makes the perfect base: one wide street with tackle shops including an Orvis store, galleries, bakeries and the Houghton Club's headquarters at the Grosvenor — plus the Mayfly pub at Testcombe, whose garden sits directly on the river. Romsey offers its Norman abbey and the gates of Broadlands; Winchester, with its cathedral and Izaak Walton's grave, is twenty minutes away and pairs naturally with a day on the Itchen, the Test's equally storied sister stream. The Test Way footpath follows the old Sprat and Winkle railway line down the valley for walkers. London is barely ninety minutes by car or train from Winchester — the Test is one of the world's great fishing day-trips.
