The Irrawaddy rises in the glacial peaks of the Hengduan Mountains in Yunnan, China, where its two main headwaters — the Mali Kha and N'Mai Kha — converge near the town of Myitkyina in northernmost Myanmar. From there the river flows roughly 2,170 kilometres southward through the length of Myanmar, crossing broad alluvial plains, threading between ancient pagoda-crowned hills, and eventually spreading into a vast delta before entering the Andaman Sea. For much of human history the Irrawaddy has been Myanmar's economic and cultural spine, and the fishing cultures that have grown along its banks represent some of the least documented traditional angling practices in Asia.

## The Irrawaddy Giant Catfish

The most storied fish in the river is Pangasianodon gigas — the Irrawaddy giant catfish, closely related to the Mekong giant catfish and similarly imperilled. Confirmed catches historically documented weights of over 200 kg, and traditional accounts from Buddhist chronicles speak of specimen exceeding 300 kg. The fish is now critically endangered: dam construction, intensive gillnetting, and habitat destruction have reduced populations to near-undetectable levels in the main stem. However, sporadic captures near Bhamo in Kachin State and in the upper river near the Chinese border confirm the species persists. Critically, the fish has special religious significance in Burmese Buddhism; temples along the river traditionally maintained pools for ceremonially released giant catfish, and local fishermen who catch them often release them voluntarily.

## Humpback Mahseer and the Upper Irrawaddy

The upper Irrawaddy and its fast-flowing tributaries — particularly the Mali Kha and Taping Rivers — hold populations of humpback mahseer (Tor sinensis and related species). These fish are smaller than the famous golden mahseer of the Ganges, typically 2–8 kg, but fight with the explosive energy characteristic of all mahseer in heavy current. The forested gorge country between Myitkyina and the confluence with the Chindwin at Pakokku is virtually unfished by sport anglers, partly due to the long period during which Myanmar was closed to tourism and partly due to ongoing conflict in Kachin and Shan states that affects parts of the upper watershed. The fishing potential here is, by any reasonable assessment, extraordinary.

## Traditional Burmese Fishing Cultures

The Irrawaddy is a working river in a way that few others in Asia still are. Between Mandalay and Pyay, a distance of roughly 400 kilometres, the river supports a continuous string of fishing communities. Intha fishermen of Inle Lake — famous for their distinctive leg-rowing technique that frees both hands for conical fish traps — are among the most photographed fishing peoples in the world, though Inle Lake is a tributary basin rather than part of the main Irrawaddy system. On the main river, cast-netting from dugout canoes, stationary trap-net weirs, and long-line fishing for the various catfish and carp species represent the backbone of subsistence and commercial fishing. Visiting these communities provides an unparalleled window into traditional riverine life.

## Fishing near Mandalay and Bagan

The cities of Mandalay and Bagan, on the middle Irrawaddy, are the most accessible angling bases for international visitors. The river near Mandalay is broad, muddy, and relatively slow, holding large populations of common catfish (Wallago attu and related species), as well as several species of large cyprinid. Evening fishing from the riverbanks near Sagaing, where dozens of pagodas crown a horseshoe ridge above the river, offers perhaps the most atmospheric angling setting in all of Southeast Asia. Bagan, with its extraordinary plain of 2,000-plus ancient temples and pagodas, provides similar access to the river roughly 160 kilometres downstream.

## The Delta and Sea Fishing

The Irrawaddy delta, a vast lowland of mangrove-bordered channels stretching from Pathein to Bogalay, is a productive zone for brackish and inshore marine fishing. Barramundi (Lates calcarifer) enter the lower delta channels from the sea, and their fast, acrobatic fighting makes them a prized target on light lure gear. Several species of snapper, grouper, and trevally also penetrate the outer delta from the Andaman Sea, and small-boat charter fishing is available from Pathein, the delta's main city. The season for barramundi peaks between October and February following the monsoon.

## Access and Practical Considerations

Myanmar remains a complicated destination for international visitors. Following the military coup of February 2021, most Western governments issued travel advisories recommending against non-essential travel, and the tourism infrastructure that existed before that date has been significantly disrupted. Visitors who do travel face limited choices of accommodation in smaller towns, fuel supply issues for charter boats, and the need to avoid conflict-affected zones in the north. The most straightforward access is via Yangon, with domestic flights available to Mandalay and Bagan. Fishing licenses are theoretically required but the system is poorly formalised for foreign anglers.

## Conservation Challenges

Beyond the giant catfish, the Irrawaddy faces the full suite of pressures experienced by all major Asian rivers: dams (the Myitsone Dam project, though suspended, remains controversial), gold mining in the headwaters, dynamite fishing in remote sections, and increasing agricultural chemical runoff. The Irrawaddy River dolphin (Orcaella brevirostris) — one of the river's most charismatic residents, concentrated around Kyaukmyaung between Mandalay and Bhamo — is a critically endangered flagship species whose health broadly reflects the river's ecological condition.
