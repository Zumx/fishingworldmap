Stand at the edge of an estuary channel as the tide turns and you feel it before you see it: a subtle quickening in the current, a ripple line forming where the fresh water pushes against the salt. Baitfish scatter at the surface. A cormorant lifts and repositions downstream. These transition zones — where rivers bleed into the sea — are among the most productive fishing environments on the planet, and understanding their rhythms is the difference between blank sessions and memorable catches.

Estuaries hold sea bass, thick-lipped mullet, flounder, sea trout, and in many systems eels, thin-lipped mullet, and even the occasional school of mackerel that push surprisingly far upriver on a big spring tide. Each species has its own relationship with the tide, the temperature, and the salinity gradient, but all of them use the estuary as a food highway rather than a residence. Read the water correctly and you are positioning yourself at the buffet.

## Understanding the Tidal Cycle

The tidal range determines everything about an estuary. A spring tide in the Bristol Channel can exceed 13 metres, completely transforming the same stretch of river within hours. A neap tide in a sheltered Scottish sea loch might shift the water level by barely a metre. Both create fishing opportunities, but through entirely different mechanisms.

On a big spring tide, water floods kilometres inland, carrying bait, nutrients, and salt into channels that were freshwater streams at low tide. As it ebbs, that same water drains back hard through constrictions and bends, creating seams and eddies that concentrate food and position predators. The rule of thirds applies: the tide floods for six hours but roughly a third of the total water volume moves in the first two hours, a third in the middle two, and a third in the final two. The middle two hours — peak flow — are typically the prime window for predators working hard water.

## Targeting Sea Bass in Estuaries

Sea bass are the premier target in European estuaries and their relationship with moving water is almost mechanical. They hold in the eddy behind a structure — a bridge abutment, a sandbank, a mooring post — and face into the current, waiting for prey to be swept toward them. Position yourself so that you can cast upstream and retrieve a lure or bait through that eddy, and the take can be savage.

Soft plastic lures worked on a 10–20 gram jig head are devastatingly effective in two to four metres of water during the ebb. Choose natural colours — sand, pearl, or pale olive — when the water is clear. Switch to darker profiles, chartreuse, or black in coloured water. In very low light, a surface popper walked across a calm channel mouth can draw explosive strikes. Bass feed confidently through the night in estuaries, particularly where street lights or boat lights create a lit/dark boundary that baitfish orient along.

## Mullet Tactics: The Estuary's Most Frustrating Fish

Thick-lipped grey mullet are everywhere in productive estuaries and almost universally ignored by anglers who consider them unteatable or uncatchable. Both verdicts are wrong. Mullet up to 4 kg roam estuaries from late April through October, feeding on algae, organic detritus, and small invertebrates stirred up by the tide. They can be caught on light float tackle with small pieces of bread flake or mackerel flesh, but only with patience and a feeder approach to loose-feeding that keeps them occupied and off-guard.

The key is to identify a regular patrol route — mullet often follow the same path along a quay wall or channel margin on every tide — and to have bait in the water before they arrive. Leader material should be no heavier than 6 lb fluorocarbon and the float must be adjusted so that the bait drifts at exactly the depth the fish are feeding. A mullet mouthing bread 20 cm too high will ignore a bait at 60 cm; get the depth wrong and the session is over before it begins.

## Flounder: Tide-Table Fishing at Its Simplest

Flounder are the perfect introduction to estuary fishing because their behaviour is almost entirely governed by tide and temperature rather than complex habitat preferences. They follow the tide onto mudflats and sandbanks to feed on worms, small crabs, and shrimp, and they drop back into the channel as the water ebbs. A running ledger rig baited with ragworm and cast onto a flat that is covered by a metre of water at mid-tide will consistently produce flounder from September through February.

The best tactic is deliberate movement: cast, wait ten minutes, reel in a few metres, wait again. This mimics the natural movement of a worm across a feeding flat and the vibration of the line dragging across the bottom often triggers a take. Flounder also respond to a small bright attractor — a single red or orange bead above the hook — which improves visibility in coloured water.

## Sea Trout After Dark

Sea trout are nocturnal hunters in estuaries, and fishing for them requires a fundamentally different mindset from daytime species work. They move with the tide, pushing upriver at night, and they are acutely sensitive to light and noise. Approach the water quietly, wear dark clothing, and resist the temptation to use a head torch except when absolutely necessary.

A single-handed fly rod of 9–10 feet, loaded with a floating or intermediate line, is the preferred tool. Tube flies, waddlers, and large hairwing patterns in sizes 4–10 are worked across and down through pools and runs on a slack-to-medium current. The takes can be hard and immediate or a barely-noticeable resistance that requires a steady sweep of the rod rather than a sharp strike. Fish from May through September, with July and August typically peak months across most British and Scandinavian estuaries.

## Night Tactics for Mixed Species

The hours between midnight and first light, during a rising tide in summer, produce a remarkable mix of species in productive estuaries. Warm water, high baitfish populations, and reduced boat traffic combine to push predators into surprisingly shallow water. A soft plastic on a light jig, or a slowly retrieved shallow-running lure, can encounter bass, sea trout, thin-lipped mullet, and the occasional garfish in a single session.

Noise discipline is critical. Estuary fish in shallow water at night are extremely alert; a dropped tackle box, a splash from a waded step, or even the crunch of gravel underfoot can clear a pool immediately. Work methodically from the downstream end of a pool upward, covering the water thoroughly before moving.
