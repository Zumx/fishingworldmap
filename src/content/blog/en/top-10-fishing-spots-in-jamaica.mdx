Jamaica sits at the centre of the Caribbean Sea with a dramatic underwater geography that makes its fishing uniquely varied. The island's north coast faces the deep Cayman Trench — at 7,686 m the deepest point in the Caribbean — which pushes cold, bait-rich water to within a few kilometres of the shoreline. Blue marlin of 200 to 400 kg have been caught trolling within 10 nautical miles of Montego Bay. The south coast is shallower, protected by the Pedro Bank, and produces excellent yellowfin tuna and wahoo during the winter months. Inland, the Black River drains the island's largest wetland: the Black River Morass, a 122-square-kilometre swamp of black water and dense reeds where Atlantic tarpon have lived since before European contact. Jamaica's reef systems — though under pressure — still produce jacks, barracuda, snapper, and the Caribbean's signature grouper species on the right day.

## Black River Morass: Tarpon of the Great Morass

The Black River empties into the sea at Black River town on the island's south coast, but the fishing begins 20 km upstream where the river widens into the Great Morass. This extensive wetland, fed by dozens of springs seeping from the Cockpit Country limestone to the north, holds a permanent population of Atlantic tarpon (Megalops atlanticus) ranging from juvenile fish of 5 kg to adults exceeding 65 kg. The water is naturally tea-coloured from tannins, and tarpon cruise the open channels between reed beds in a pattern that rewards patient observation. Fly fishers use 10-weight rods with large Deceiver-style flies; conventional anglers do well with large swimbaits retrieved slowly along the edge of the vegetation. The best season is April to August when spawning fish move into the morass.

## Negril and the Westernmost Reefs

Negril, at Jamaica's western tip, is famous for its beach sunsets but less known for its fishing — which is a shame, since the reef systems extending north and south from West End hold excellent concentrations of large snapper, horse-eye jack, and barracuda. The coral at "The Canyon" drop-off west of Negril begins in just 9 m of water and falls away to over 30 m in a wall heavily populated with Nassau grouper (Epinephelus striatus), which are protected but occasionally encountered. The nearby Booby Cays, uninhabited islets 15 km offshore, produce dorado and wahoo at the edge of the oceanic current that sweeps past their southern tip.

## Montego Bay Offshore: Blue Marlin Country

Montego Bay has hosted the Jamaica International Game Fishing Tournament for decades, and the blue marlin fishing here is world-class. The submarine canyon of the Cayman Trench comes within 8 nautical miles of the Montego Bay waterfront, and the current shear along the canyon edge produces blue marlin throughout the year, with the peak from October through January. Fish of 200 to 400 kg are encountered regularly; grander fish above 450 kg have been weighed. White marlin and sailfish also occur in these waters from December to March. Charter boats operate from Montego Bay Marina and from Club Montego.

## Port Antonio and the Blue Mountains Runoff

Port Antonio on the northeastern coast receives the runoff of the Blue Mountains, Jamaica's highest range, via the Rio Grande and several smaller rivers. This mixture of mountain freshwater and Caribbean salt creates a productive nearshore zone where African pompano, cobia, and king mackerel school in late autumn and winter. The Blue Hole (Blue Lagoon), an inland saltwater lagoon connected to the sea by an underwater channel 10 km west of Port Antonio, holds juvenile tarpon, snapper, and barracuda in an almost surreal setting — turquoise water surrounded by forest. Night fishing the Blue Hole channels for snapper can be spectacularly productive.

## Kingston Harbour and the South Coast Banks

Kingston Harbour, despite its urban setting, is a functional fishing location particularly for bottom fishing on the limestone pinnacles in 20 to 40 m of water south of the harbour entrance. Red snapper (Lutjanus campechanus) and mutton snapper (Lutjanus analis) are taken here year-round by local fishers. The Pedro Bank, a shallow-water carbonate platform 80 km southwest of Kingston, is one of the most productive fishing grounds in the entire Caribbean: yellowfin tuna of 40 to 80 kg school over the bank from November to February, and large wahoo patrol the bank edge through winter.

## Hellshire Hills Nearshore Reefs

The Hellshire Hills to the west of Kingston back a system of nearshore reefs extending to the Wreck of the Hartenstein — a cargo vessel sunk in 12 m of water that now hosts an extraordinary concentration of bar jack, lane snapper, and French grunt. Locals fish the wreck at night with hand lines and come home with coolers full of snapper and small grouper. The nearby Lime Cay, a tiny uninhabited island accessible by 20-minute boat ride from Port Royal, provides clear water reef fishing for snapper and barracuda.

## Ocho Rios and the North Coast Shelf

Ocho Rios, on the north central coast, sits at the foot of the Blue Mountains where they meet the sea. The shelf here is narrow — deep water of 100 m is only 5 km offshore — and sport fishing has traditionally focused on the reef systems at 15 to 30 m depth. Yellowtail snapper (Ocyurus chrysurus) are caught in considerable numbers on small jigs fished at reef edges at dusk. Dolphinfish (dorado) run along the coast from March to June when the trade winds push floating weed lines to within a few kilometres of shore. Trolling colourful skirted lures along these weed lines produces consistent action.

## Negril and Sav-La-Mar Estuary: Tarpon at Night

The small estuary of the Cabarita River near Savanna-la-Mar provides a secondary tarpon fishery on the south coast that is almost unknown outside Jamaica. At night, tarpon of 20 to 50 kg roll and crash in the brackish tidal reaches below the bridge, feeding on the large mullet schools that run the estuary from September to November. A single light casting large needlefish-pattern lures from the bridge deck can hook fish that explode through the surface and cart-wheel across the dark water.

## Falmouth and Discovery Bay: Reef and Pelagic Mix

Falmouth, a beautifully preserved Georgian port 35 km east of Montego Bay, gives access to a section of the north coast reef system where the shelf drops steeply and creates a convergence zone for both reef and open-water species. Cero mackerel (Scomberomorus regalis) school here in winter; large horse-eye jack patrol the reef edge at dawn. Discovery Bay to the east was the site of Christopher Columbus's first landing on Jamaica in 1494, and the bay's protected waters still hold excellent jack and snapper populations in the reef system around the eastern headland.
