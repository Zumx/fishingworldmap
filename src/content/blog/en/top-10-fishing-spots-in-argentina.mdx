The first time a golden dorado hits a streamer on the Paraná, most anglers swear the fish has been plugged into the river's hydroelectric grid. Salminus brasiliensis is all muscle, gold plate and bad temper, and Argentina holds the best of it. Three thousand kilometres further south, the same country offers sea-run brown trout in Tierra del Fuego that average heavier than almost anywhere on earth. Between those two extremes lie Patagonian spring creeks, enormous reservoirs, and a fly-fishing culture that has been refining itself since British rail engineers stocked the first trout over a century ago. Here are the ten places that define it.

## 1. The Middle Paraná at Paso de la Patria

Paso de la Patria, a small town in Corrientes Province, calls itself the national capital of the dorado, and every August its fishing festival backs the claim. The Paraná here is a braided giant, several kilometres wide, with rock bars and confluence seams where dorado from five to twenty-plus pounds ambush sábalo baitfish. Local guides run wooden tracker boats into the "zona de dorados", drifting live bait or working big streamers and surface plugs along the drop-offs. The prime season runs from September to April; strict catch-and-release rules now apply on much of the Corrientes shore, a conservation shift that has visibly improved the average size of fish.

## 2. The Upper Paraná and Iberá marshes

Upstream towards Ituzaingó and the Yacyretá dam tailrace, the river narrows and accelerates, and this is where fly anglers chase trophy dorado in fast, clear water. Nearby, the Esteros del Iberá — one of South America's largest wetlands at roughly 13,000 square kilometres — offers a completely different game: sight-fishing for dorado, pirá pitá and tararira among floating islands of vegetation, with capybaras and caimans as an audience. Lodges around Colonia Carlos Pellegrini combine both fisheries. The wetland is a provincial reserve, so single barbless hooks and full release are standard practice.

## 3. Río Grande, Tierra del Fuego

The Río Grande is, by broad consensus, the best sea-run brown trout river in the world. Browns stocked in the 1930s found their way to the South Atlantic, grew enormous on the marine buffet, and now return each season in runs estimated at tens of thousands of fish averaging 8 to 10 pounds, with twenty-pounders landed every week of the season. The river winds through windswept estancia country near the city of Río Grande, and access is controlled by famous lodges such as those on the Menéndez and José María sections. January to April is prime; bring sinking tips, small nymphs and a tolerance for 60 km/h wind.

## 4–5. The Limay and the rivers of Junín de los Andes

Flowing out of Lake Nahuel Huapi near Bariloche, the Limay is a big, clear tailwater system holding browns and rainbows that regularly top ten pounds. The middle Limay below the Pichi Picún Leufú dam is float-trip water, fished from rafts over long gravel runs, while the upper river offers wade fishing within an hour of Bariloche's airport. Migratory browns push up from the reservoirs in autumn — March and April — and that is when locals throw enormous articulated streamers after dark. The scenery is steppe and poplar gallery forest, condors overhead.

Junín de los Andes is Argentina's self-declared trout capital, and the Chimehuín, born at the Boca where it leaves Lake Huechulafquen under the Lanín volcano (3,776 m), is its sacred pool. The Boca produced the legendary 24-pound brown that put Argentine fly fishing on the map in the 1960s. Around town, the Malleo — arguably the country's finest dry-fly river — and spring creeks on Mapuche land offer technical fishing for selective rainbows. The season runs November to May, with the willow-lined Malleo fishing best during summer evening hatches.

## 6. Lago Strobel — Jurassic Lake

In the remote Santa Cruz steppe, the Barrancoso River feeds Lago Strobel, a mineral-rich closed-basin lake where stocked rainbows grew absurdly fast on scud and snail beds. The result is the planet's most reliable trophy rainbow fishery: fish average around ten pounds and twenty-pounders are landed weekly. There is no outlet, so the entire fishery concentrates at the river mouth and along rocky bays. Getting there means hours of gravel track from Gobernador Gregores, and the handful of lodges book out a season ahead. November, December and March are the kindest weather windows.

## 7. The lower Paraná delta and Entre Ríos

Closer to Buenos Aires, the Paraná delta around Rosario, Victoria and Esquina offers accessible dorado, surubí catfish that can exceed 40 kilos, and hard-fighting tararira in the lagoons. Public access points like the Bajada de los Pescadores in Rosario see weekend crowds drifting live bait, while guided skiffs work the arroyo mouths — the entrance of the Arroyo El Talar is a known dorado ambush point where clear tributary water mixes with the main river's milky flow. Closed seasons protect spawning fish in early summer on several stretches, so check provincial rules before travelling.

## 8–9. The San Matías Gulf coast and the lakes of Chubut

Patagonia's Atlantic shore offers superb shore and boat fishing in the warm, clear waters of the San Matías Gulf. Around Las Grutas and San Antonio Este, marked rock platforms such as Punta Perdices and Punta Villarino give shore anglers access to sandperch, silversides, sea bass and the occasional shark on big tides — the gulf has one of the largest tidal ranges in Argentina, approaching nine metres. November to March is the season; locals fish two hours either side of high water with squid or crab baits.

The Trevelin–Esquel corridor in Chubut Province packs in the Río Grande de Futaleufú, Arroyo Pescado spring creek and dozens of lakes inside and around Los Alerces National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage site protecting 2,600-year-old alerce trees. Further south, the Río Pico district is brook trout country — fish over five pounds from wind-scoured lakes such as Lago 3 — alongside browns and rainbows in intimate creeks. This is Argentina's quiet corner: fewer lodges, gravel roads, and big fish that see few flies.

## 10. The Uruguay River at Concordia — La Zona

Below the Salto Grande dam on the Uruguay River, the tailrace pool known simply as La Zona is the most famous trophy dorado beat anywhere. Access is restricted to a handful of boats per day, fishing turbulent water directly beneath the turbines where dorado over 40 pounds patrol. Bookings are made through a single concession on the Argentine side at Concordia, Entre Ríos. Even outside the restricted zone, the river around Federación fishes well from August through April for dorado in the 5–15 pound class.
