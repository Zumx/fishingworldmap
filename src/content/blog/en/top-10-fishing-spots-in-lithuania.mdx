Lithuania sits at a geographical crossroads where the rivers of inland Europe drain toward the Baltic Sea through a landscape of glacially carved lakes, oxbow meadows, and sandy floodplains. The country is small — roughly the size of Ireland — but it contains some 3,000 lakes and an exceptional network of rivers that support asp, zander, pike-perch, bream, and brown trout. The Nemunas, Lithuania's longest river, has a reputation among Baltic sport anglers that stretches well beyond the country's borders. The Curonian Lagoon, a vast brackish body of water separated from the Baltic by a narrow sand spit, holds bream in numbers that produce records. And the clear glacial lakes of the Aukštaitija National Park are among the finest perch and pike-perch waters in the Baltic states.

## Nemunas River for Asp and Zander

The Nemunas runs 937 kilometres from its source in Belarus to its delta at the Curonian Lagoon, and its middle Lithuanian reaches around Kaunas are the national heartland for asp fishing. Asp (Leuciscus aspius) are surface predators, and on warm summer mornings between May and August they herd small baitfish to the surface in spectacular boils that can be heard from 50 metres away. Light spinning rods of 7 to 9 feet with surface lures — walk-the-dog stickbaits and pencil poppers in silver and white — are the local technique. Zander up to 8 kg inhabit the deeper channels below the Kaunas reservoir, best fished with vertical jigging from a boat on the evening drift.

## Curonian Lagoon Bream Records

The Curonian Lagoon stretches 97 kilometres from the Lithuanian-Russian border to the port of Klaipėda and averages only 3.8 metres in depth. This vast, brackish shallows is one of Europe's most productive bream habitats. Lithuanian anglers regularly land fish exceeding 4 kg, and the lagoon record stands above 5 kg. Ground baiting with hemp, maize, and pellets on a fixed marker draws shoals within range of method feeders and waggler rigs fished at 30 to 60 metres. The eastern shore near Ventė and the sheltered bays around Minija are the most consistent marks. Night fishing is permitted in most zones, and the lagoon's calm summer mornings make for some of the most pleasant sessions in the region.

## Aukštaitija National Park Lakes

Lithuania's first national park, Aukštaitija in the northeast, encompasses over 120 lakes connected by short rivers. The largest, Lake Tauragnas at 40 metres deep, holds wild brown trout, pike-perch, and perch, and is one of the very few Lithuanian lakes where trout are naturally self-sustaining. The network of paddling routes allows anglers to access remote lake arms by canoe, targeting pike up to 12 kg in the reed margins and perch in mid-water. Fishing permits are issued at the park office in Palūšė. The season opens on the first Saturday of May and closes at the end of March, with a brown trout closed season from October through April.

## Neris River Above Vilnius

The Neris enters Lithuania from Belarus and curves through Vilnius before joining the Nemunas at Kaunas. Above the capital, in the sections between Nemenčinė and Kernavė, the river runs over gravel and holds one of Lithuania's few self-sustaining populations of grayling. Fly fishing with size 14 to 18 dry flies — parachute Adams, elk hair caddis, and local variants — produces consistent sport from April to September. The river also holds asp from late May and chub through most of the year. Access is open along most of the river with a national freshwater licence, which costs around 15 euros for visitors.

## Lake Drūkšiai for Pike-Perch

Lake Drūkšiai, in far northeastern Lithuania on the border with Belarus and Latvia, is the country's largest lake at 44.8 square kilometres. The warm-water outflow from the Ignalina Nuclear Power Plant cooling system elevated the water temperature for decades, creating an anomalous environment that produced outsized zander and catfish. Since the plant closed in 2009 the lake has slowly returned to ambient temperatures, but it still holds exceptional pike-perch. Boat fishing with drop-shot rigs, jigging with 5 to 10 gram shads, and trolling deep-running lures in 6 to 10 metres are the standard approaches. Charter boats operate from the village of Palūšė.

## The Minija for Roach and Bream

The Minija flows through the western Samogitia lowlands into the Curonian Lagoon delta and is a classic coarse fishing river. Wide, slow bends hold roach up to 1.5 kg and bream to 3 kg in summer. Float fishing with red maggot and sweet corn over a pre-baited bed produces sessions that regularly exceed 20 kg of fish in a three-hour window. The river is navigable by small boats in most sections and the bankside access paths are well maintained. The Šilutė district, where the Minija joins the Atmata channel, is the most densely fished area and the epicentre of Lithuanian coarse fishing culture.

## Žuvintas Biosphere Reserve

Lake Žuvintas in southern Lithuania is designated as a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and is the largest natural lake in the country. Angling access is restricted to the buffer zone, but the permitted fishing areas on the southern shore produce tench, crucian carp, and bream in exceptional numbers through June and July when the marginal vegetation draws fish into shallow, weedy bays. Float fishing with bread flake and pellet over white groundbait is the most effective approach. The reserve's bird life — breeding white-tailed eagles, great white egrets, and bitterns — makes a session here an experience that goes well beyond the rod.

## Baltic Sea Coastal Spinning at Palanga

The Baltic coast at Palanga in western Lithuania offers surf fishing for sea trout (Salmo trutta) from mid-October to January, when post-spawning fish move through the shallow surf zone. Weighted minnow lures in silver and herring patterns cast into the swells from the sandy beach attract fish averaging 1.5 to 3 kg. The pier at Palanga extends 470 metres into the sea and is also a productive station for garfish in summer. No sea fishing licence is required for fishing from the shore, only the national freshwater licence covers the beach runs where the sea trout enter the river mouths.

## Lake Tauragnas Trout Fishing

At 40 metres, Tauragnas is Lithuania's deepest lake and the only one with a permanent thermocline cold enough to support self-sustaining brown trout year-round. The trout feed primarily on smelt and small perch and average 0.5 to 1.5 kg, with occasional specimens to 3 kg. Trolling with small spoons in 8 to 15 metres of water in early morning and evening is the recommended technique. Fly fishing is not generally productive due to the depth at which the trout hold, but on warm evenings in June and July surface rises do occur near the inlet streams. Access is through the national park permit system.
