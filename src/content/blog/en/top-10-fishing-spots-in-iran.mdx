Iran rarely appears on fishing-travel lists, which is precisely what makes it interesting. The country fronts two seas with completely different characters — the brackish, sturgeon-haunted Caspian in the north and the hot, fish-rich Persian Gulf and Gulf of Oman in the south — while between them rise the Alborz and Zagros ranges, holding cold rivers, high dams and wild brown trout at altitudes above 2,500 metres. Add a deep domestic angling culture, from kutum netsmen on the Caspian shore to trout-farm ponds in every mountain valley, and you have a fishing nation in full working order that almost no foreign angler has explored. These ten spots map its range.

## Bandar Anzali and the Caspian lagoon

The Anzali Lagoon in Gilan, separated from the Caspian by a sandbar carrying the busy port of Bandar Anzali, is northern Iran's great wetland: reedbeds, lotus fields and channels holding pike, carp, catfish and seasonal runs of Caspian fish. Local boatmen run shallow skiffs through the lily channels, and the lagoon's pike fishing in the cooler months is the closest thing Iran has to classic European esox water. The adjoining coast is the heart of the commercial kutum fishery that defines Gilan's food culture.

## The kutum run — rivers of Gilan and Mazandaran

The Caspian kutum, called mahi sefid — the white fish — is Iran's national fish in everything but name. Each spring, roughly March to May, mature fish push from the Caspian into rivers like the Sefidrud, Iran's second-longest river, to spawn, and bank anglers line the lower reaches with light rods and dough or worm baits. The Sefidrud delta near Astaneh-ye Ashrafiyeh and the river mouths of Mazandaran around Babolsar are focal points. Strict seasonal rules protect the spawning run, so the legal angling happens on the shoulders of the migration.

## Lar Dam and the Lar plateau — wild browns under Damavand

The Lar National Park, a high treeless plateau at about 2,500 metres beneath the 5,610-metre cone of Mount Damavand, holds Iran's most famous wild brown trout water. The Lar River and the reservoir behind Lar Dam grow strong, golden-flanked browns, and access is permit-controlled through the park's environmental office, with a short summer season — roughly June to September — once the snow leaves. Fly fishers who make the trip describe sight-fishing in treeless, wind-scoured streams with Damavand filling the sky.

## The Alborz dams — Amir Kabir and Taleghan

Within two hours of Tehran, the big Alborz reservoirs give city anglers their weekend fix. Amir Kabir Dam (Karaj Dam) holds rainbow trout and carp in deep, cold water between rock walls; Taleghan Dam adds pike and carp against a backdrop of mud-brick villages. Both are busy on Fridays, the Iranian weekend, when families line the accessible banks. Renting local knowledge matters: water levels swing hard with irrigation demand, and the productive points move with them.

## Zagros trout streams — Khorramabad to Chaharmahal

The Zagros range carries surprising trout water. Spring-fed rivers in Lorestan and Chaharmahal va Bakhtiari — around Khorramabad, Shahrekord and the Koohrang headwaters that feed the Zayandeh Rud — hold both wild browns in the colder reaches and rainbows around the many trout farms. The mahi saraye trout-farm ponds of the Zagros valleys, found near nearly every spring, double as pay-fishing venues where a rod, a bag of pellets and a streamside kebab make the standard family outing. It is unglamorous, genuine and everywhere.

## The Zayandeh Rud — Esfahan's river

The Zayandeh Rud rises in the Zagros and flows through Esfahan beneath some of the most beautiful bridges on Earth. Its upper reaches around Saman and the Zayandeh Rud Dam hold carp, and in the colder months the river above the reservoir fishes for stocked rainbow trout. Water abstraction means the lower river sometimes runs dry — a reminder that fishing in Iran tracks the country's water politics — so the dependable angling sits upstream of the dam.

## Qeshm and Hormuz — Persian Gulf jigging

Iran's south coast faces some of the richest warm-water fishing in the Middle East. Around Qeshm, the largest island in the Persian Gulf, and the Strait of Hormuz, local dhows and modern skiffs jig and bottom-fish for grouper (hamour), trevally, queenfish, barracuda and king mackerel. The mangrove channels of the Hara Forest on Qeshm's north shore add light-tackle casting for queenfish and snapper among the roots. October to April is the comfortable season; summer heat is brutal.

## Chabahar — the Gulf of Oman frontier

Far to the southeast, Chabahar on the Makran coast opens onto the deep Gulf of Oman. This is Iran's big-game water: yellowfin tuna, sailfish, dorado and giant trevally work the upwellings within reach of the port, and the rocky headlands toward Beris and the dramatic Martian Mountains give shore anglers cliff-casting for trevally and barracuda. The fishery is essentially unexploited by travelling anglers, with local fishing companies in the free-trade zone beginning to offer charters.

## Caspian surfcasting and the northern piers

Along the 650-kilometre Iranian Caspian shore from Astara to Gorgan Bay, surfcasting is a winter institution. Anglers fish long rods from the beaches and breakwaters of towns like Ramsar, Nowshahr and Babolsar for kutum, Caspian salmon (a protected rarity to be released), mullet and shemaya. Gorgan Bay, sheltered behind the Miankaleh peninsula — a biosphere reserve thick with migrating birds — offers calmer water and boat fishing for carp and mullet. Dress for wind and rain: the Caspian winter is grey, mild and productive.

## Rules, culture and practical notes

Recreational fishing in Iran is administered through the fisheries organisation (Shilat) and environmental offices; protected species — sturgeon above all, plus Caspian salmon — must never be kept, and spawning-season closures on the kutum rivers are enforced. Foreign visitors will find tackle shops in Tehran, Rasht and Bandar Abbas, very low costs, and extraordinary hospitality; an invitation to share tea or a riverside meal is near-inevitable. Women anglers should note local dress codes apply on the water. The pay-ponds and trout farms everywhere make easy fishing days; the wild fisheries reward patience with paperwork.
