Few states can match Texas for sheer fishing variety. In a single week, an angler can wade the spartina marshes of the Laguna Madre for speckled trout at dawn, spend the afternoon casting topwater lures for largemouth bass on Sam Rayburn Reservoir, and board a party boat out of Port Aransas for red snapper and amberjack before the week is out. The state spans multiple ecological zones — Gulf Coast tidal flats, pine-forested East Texas impoundments, limestone Hill Country spring creeks, and the open blue water of the Gulf — and each demands a different approach. That diversity, combined with a 365-day season and water temperatures that keep fish active year-round, is why Texas holds more licensed anglers than any other US state.

## Galveston Bay Complex

The Galveston Bay system — comprising Trinity Bay, East Bay, West Bay, and the Houston Ship Channel margins — is the largest estuary on the Texas coast and one of the most productive coastal fisheries in North America. Speckled trout and redfish are the primary targets; both species can be found year-round, though autumn produces the largest concentrations of big trout. Wade-fishing the grass flats of the East Galveston Bay shoreline on an early October morning, when water temperatures drop below 20°C and baitfish are pushed into the shallows, regularly produces trout over 50 cm. Wading with a soft plastic on a jig head along the grass edge is the definitive technique.

## Sam Rayburn Reservoir

With 45,000 ha of surface area, Sam Rayburn is the largest reservoir entirely within Texas. It is a power fishery for largemouth bass, particularly in spring when fish move onto shallow timber and spawning flats. The lake record stands at over 7 kg, and five-fish tournament bags over 12 kg are registered regularly. Deep-running crankbaits along the submerged timber corridors between Rayburn and the San Augustine area work well in summer; topwater lures on the grassy coves at dawn produce action from March through May. The lake also holds exceptional white bass populations, with run fish stacking below the Jasper-area bridges in March.

## Lake Fork

Lake Fork in Wood County has a legitimate claim to being America's best largemouth bass lake. It has produced more fish in the 7 kg-plus class than any other lake in Texas history, including the state record of 8.03 kg caught in 1992. Hydrilla beds, submerged timber, and standing trees create a complex structural environment that supports outstanding year-round largemouth fishing. The lake is heavily regulated: only 5 bass per day can be harvested, and fish over 40 cm must be immediately released. Flip a jig into the base of standing timber in a 3–4 m depth range on a cloudy November day, and a 5 kg bass is a genuine possibility.

## Laguna Madre

The Lower Laguna Madre, stretching from Port Mansfield to the Mexican border near Brownsville, is arguably the finest sight-fishing bay in North America. Water clarity on a calm morning can exceed 3 m of visibility, allowing anglers to spot large redfish and speckled trout tailing on shallow flats. Redfish here feed aggressively in water as shallow as 30 cm; the sight of a 5 kg redfish with its tail breaking the surface as it roots for crabs in less than half a metre of water is an image that defines Texas coastal fishing. Fly fishing for tailing reds on the Laguna is a world-class experience comparable to bonefish flats in the Bahamas.

## Sabine Lake

Straddling the Texas-Louisiana border, Sabine Lake and its extensive marsh system produces some of the finest speckled trout fishing on the Gulf Coast. The Sabine River and Neches River deltas create a nutrient-rich mixing zone where trout accumulate in autumn around jetty rocks, channel edges, and grass flat drop-offs. The Toledo Bend spillway on the Sabine River above the lake is an extraordinary winter fishery for white bass and striped bass; during the spring run, anglers line the banks shoulder to shoulder and fill coolers with fish in the 1–2 kg range. Local guides rate October and November as peak months for trophy specks on Sabine Lake proper.

## South Padre Island Waters

The jetties at Port Isabel, the ship channel, and the nearshore Gulf reefs accessible from South Padre Island offer superb fishing for a mixed bag of species. Flounder concentrate at the mouths of passes in autumn, reaching weights over 2.5 kg. Spanish mackerel, ladyfish, and jack crevalle run the beach troughs in summer. The nearshore artificial reefs — some deployed just 10 km offshore — hold red snapper, grouper, and gag grouper in federal waters during the open season from June through early August. The Mansfield Channel cut between South Padre and northern Kenedy County is a reliable producer of large speckled trout, particularly in winter.

## Canyon Lake and the Guadalupe River

Canyon Lake is the only Texas impoundment with a year-round cold-water trout stocking program. Texas Parks and Wildlife Department releases rainbow trout into the Guadalupe River tailwater below Canyon Dam from November through March, creating a put-and-take fishery that is unique in the southern United States. The Guadalupe River below the dam, flowing through a cypress-shaded gorge, is the setting for one of Texas's most scenic fishing experiences. Beyond the stocked trout, Canyon Lake itself holds excellent populations of largemouth and smallmouth bass, white bass, and striped bass in the cold, clear, limestone-filtered water.

## Aransas Bay and Rockport

The Aransas Bay complex between Port Aransas and Rockport-Fulton is a classic Texas coastal system with extensive seagrass meadows, oyster reefs, and tidal creek networks that support large populations of redfish and trout. Whooping cranes winter in the adjacent Aransas National Wildlife Refuge; watching a crane stalk the same shoreline you're wading is an experience unique to this stretch of Texas coast. The Aransas Pass jetties are legendary for flounder in autumn and for nearshore red snapper and king mackerel in summer. A guided wade trip along the grass flats of Aransas Bay in October is one of the finest coastal fishing experiences Texas offers.

## Port Aransas and the Gulf Blue Water

Port Aransas is the departure point for some of the finest offshore fishing in the western Gulf of Mexico. The 60–80 m depth contour lies only 30–50 km offshore, and beyond it the blue water holds wahoo, yellowfin tuna, mahi-mahi, blue marlin, and white marlin. The Port Aransas Tarpon Roundup, though the tarpon run that once defined the port has been diminished by decades of pressure, remains a cultural institution. Modern offshore trips target yellowfin tuna at structure around the Flower Garden Banks National Marine Sanctuary, a coral reef system 190 km offshore that is one of the most biologically diverse sites in the Gulf.
