Jigging is in many ways the most intimate connection between angler and fish. There is no float watching, no waiting for a bite indicator to move — just the direct feedback of a weighted lure through a thin line to your rod hand, and the moment of conscious decision-making about when to lift, when to pause, and how fast to work the next retrieve. It is also an extraordinarily adaptable technique: the same mechanical principle — dropping a weighted lure and retrieving it through the water column in a pattern that triggers predatory instincts — works equally well in a mountain lake at 5 m depth for perch, at 60 m in the North Sea for cod, or at 200 m in the Pacific for amberjack and yellowtail.

## What Is a Jig?

In its simplest form, a jig is a hook with a weighted head — usually a ball or cylinder of lead, tungsten, or iron — that causes it to sink with a specific falling action. A round-head jig with a rubber paddle-tail swims horizontally during a retrieve and falls head-first on the pause. A metal blade jig — a hammered or cast slab of lead or tin — sinks in a fluttering, side-to-side motion that mimics a dying baitfish. A bucktail jig — a round or tapered head dressed with deer hair, marabou or synthetic fiber — creates a pulsating, breathing action on the fall that is irresistible to fish feeding at depth.

Weight and depth are directly linked. For freshwater perch at 5–10 m, jigs of 3–7 g work effectively; for cod at 30–50 m in moderate current, 60–120 g metal jigs are necessary. The rule of thumb in vertical jigging is to use the lightest jig that maintains contact with the bottom or stays in the strike zone without excessive drift — this maximises the lure's action time and maintains the sensitive connection needed to feel takes.

## Rod Choice for Jigging

A dedicated jigging rod differs from a general spinning rod in one critical way: it must have both sufficient power to lift a heavy jig and enough sensitivity in the tip to transmit the subtle take of a perch or the hesitant tap of a flounder. The tip section should be relatively soft — in the top 15–20% of the blank — while the middle and butt sections provide lifting power. A blank that is too stiff through the tip will fatigue the angler and mask takes; one that is too soft through the middle will not recover fast enough for the rapid jerk-retrieve needed in high-speed jigging styles.

For light freshwater jigging with rubber lures up to 10 g, a 1.8–2.1 m rod rated for 5–20 g in medium-light or medium action is ideal. Pairing it with a 2500-size reel loaded with 8–12 lb braided line of 0.10–0.12 mm diameter covers most European perch, zander and trout jigging scenarios. For deeper saltwater jigging — cod, pollock, coalfish in the 30–80 m range — a 1.8 m boat rod in medium-heavy action rated for 50–150 g jigs, paired with a 3000–4000 size reel loaded with 0.16–0.18 mm braid, is the standard configuration.

## Metal Jigs: Shapes and Falling Actions

Metal jigs come in an almost bewildering variety of shapes, each designed to fall and swim in a distinct manner. The most important distinction is between slow-fall jigs and high-speed or speed jigs.

Slow-fall jigs — the Shimano Ocea Slow Jerk, Major Craft Slow Style, or Cultiva Slow Flicker are representative examples — have a wide, flat profile that catches water on the fall, causing them to rock, flutter and drift in an almost horizontal plane as they sink. This action triggers strikes from fish that are following without committing, particularly in slow currents or when the target fish are lethargic. The retrieve is minimal — a single slow crank and pause, or simply lowering the jig and allowing the current to work it.

High-speed or speed jigs — narrow, elongated shapes like the Majorcraft Jigpara Short, Daiwa Samurai Jig, or Savage Gear Seeker — are designed to be worked fast, with sharp upward strokes of the rod of 60–90 cm and then an immediate drop back on a slack line. The falling action is a tight, rapid flicker. This style is optimal for pelagic species like yellowtail, amberjack and tuna that respond to fast-moving prey.

## Reading the Sonar

Modern sonar — whether the Lowrance Elite-FS series, Garmin ECHOMAP UHD, or Humminbird Helix — transforms jigging from a blind exercise into a tactical one. The key information is the position of fish marks relative to the bottom and the depth of the bait school. Perch and zander in freshwater often appear as a distinct arch or cluster of arches just above the bottom — typically in the first 2–3 m off the seabed or lakebed. Targeting these requires lowering the jig to the bottom, feeling the contact (a light double-tap on the rod tip or a sudden slackening of the line), then lifting just 1–2 m and beginning the retrieval sequence.

Suspended fish — those appearing at mid-water depth rather than near the bottom — require a different approach: lower the jig past the fish level, then retrieve it up through the school. Most takes come as the jig falls through the school on the controlled-slack drop between strokes.

## Rubber Lures for Jigging: Paddle Tails, Grubs and Shads

Rubber or soft-plastic lures mounted on a jig head have become the dominant technique for perch and zander in European freshwater, and for bass and pollock in inshore saltwater. The three main body types are the paddle tail (a flat, beaver-like tail that kicks and vibrates on both the retrieve and the fall), the shad body (a larger, fish-shaped soft body with a prominent curved tail), and the grub (a short, curled or ribbed body that gives erratic action at slow retrieves).

The Keitech Fat Swing Impact and Savage Gear Cannibal Shad are among the most consistently productive freshwater jig trailers for zander in rivers like the Rhine, the Danube, and the Thames. The Fiiish Black Minnow has become the go-to inshore saltwater jig in France, Spain and the UK for bass and pollock in 10–25 m depths. Hook exposure matters — in rubber-lure jigging, the hook must clear the body cleanly on the strike or every bite becomes a missed fish.

## Freshwater Jigging Scenarios

In a river like the Elbe or the Meuse, zander can be found lying in deep channel bends — 4–10 m of water with a gravel or clay bottom — during daylight hours, becoming more active at dawn and dusk. A 7–10 g rubber paddle-tail in 7 cm length, fished on a light-wire 3/0 jig head, worked with short 20–30 cm rod lifts and two-second pauses, is the standard approach. In still lake water targeting perch, a smaller 3–5 g jig with a 5 cm grub or swimbait fished on the drop — lowering it slowly and watching the line for sideways movement indicating a take as the lure falls — is highly productive.
