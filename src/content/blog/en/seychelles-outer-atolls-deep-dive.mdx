There is a moment that has become the signature image of modern saltwater fly fishing: an angler standing on a hard coral flat in ankle-deep water, somewhere in the outer Seychelles, watching a giant trevally the size of a car tyre charge a baitfish fly with its whole back out of the water. Thirty years ago almost nobody fished these atolls. Today, names like Alphonse, Astove, Cosmoletro, Farquhar and Providence are spoken among travelling anglers the way climbers speak of eight-thousanders — and the fishery they hold is unlike anything else on the planet.

## The geography of remoteness

The Seychelles is not one place but 115 islands scattered across 1.4 million square kilometres of the western Indian Ocean. The granite inner islands — Mahé, Praslin, La Digue — are the postcard Seychelles. The fishing legends live far to the southwest, in the coralline outer islands. The Alphonse Group, including St. François lagoon, lies about 400 kilometres from Mahé. The Amirantes chain holds Poivre and Desroches. Farther still are the raised fossil atolls of the Aldabra Group: Cosmoledo and Astove, roughly 1,000 kilometres out, neighbouring Aldabra itself — a UNESCO World Heritage Site sheltering 150,000 giant tortoises. Providence and Farquhar sit between. Getting there means a light-aircraft hop from Mahé to an island airstrip, or a liveaboard mothership; there is no casual way to fish these places, and that isolation is precisely why the fishing has stayed extraordinary.

## Giant trevally: the gangster of the flats

Caranx ignobilis is the species that built this fishery's reputation. Elsewhere in the world GTs are mostly a bluewater popping target; in the outer Seychelles they hunt across shallow flats and lagoon edges where they can be stalked on foot and cast to with a 12-weight fly rod. Cosmoledo is widely regarded as the best GT flat-fishing destination on earth — weekly catch tallies there routinely run to dozens of fish, with specimens over a metre and the occasional giant pushing 50 kilograms. The fishing is brutal and visual: a black or silver shape appears on the white sand, the guide hisses a clock direction and distance, and you get one or two casts with a 6/0 brush fly before the fish either eats in a shower of spray or evaporates. Hooked GTs head straight for coral, so guides preach maximum drag, straight-line pressure and 130-pound leader. Every fish is released.

## Bumpheads, milkfish and the strangest flats targets anywhere

What makes the outer atolls more than a one-species wonder is the supporting cast. Bumphead parrotfish — green, blunt-headed brutes up to 30 kilograms that crunch live coral with fused beaks — tail in schools across the flats of Providence and Farquhar and will, improbably, eat a crab fly. Milkfish, vegetarian and seemingly uncatchable, sip algae in chum lines and current seams off Alphonse and Astove; hooked on tiny flies, they run like bonefish scaled up to 10 kilos and fight for an hour. Then come the triggerfish: moustache and yellowmargin triggers tailing on coral shores, maddening, parrot-beaked, capable of biting through hooks. Indo-Pacific permit, the golden ghost, haunts the sand of Providence. Add bonefish in extraordinary numbers — St. François lagoon may hold the healthiest bonefish population in the Indian Ocean — and a flats week here can produce ten species on foot.

## Bluewater steps from the reef

The atolls drop into deep ocean within casting distance of the surf line, so every fishing day carries a bluewater option. Sailfish ride the current lines off Alphonse; dogtooth tuna and yellowfin hold along the drop-offs at Astove, where the wall plunges hundreds of metres almost from the beach. Popping and jigging the outer reef produces GTs of a different class, plus grouper, jobfish and wahoo. Several operations run dedicated bluewater days with teasers for sailfish on fly — and the Alphonse fishery has recorded numerous « flats slams » combined with billfish in the same week, a combination available almost nowhere else.

## How a week actually works

The logistics follow one of two models. Land-based: Alphonse Island and Astove and Farquhar host small lodges — Alphonse with comfortable bungalows and a resident guide team, Astove a more rustic eight-angler camp. Liveaboard: Providence and Cosmoledo trips have historically run from motherships or, at Cosmoledo, an eco-camp of converted pods. Days run on tides, not clocks: skiffs and tenders drop pairs of anglers with a guide onto whichever flats the tide is flooding, and you walk — often 8 to 10 kilometres a day on coral, so sturdy flats boots are non-negotiable. The season runs roughly October to May, avoiding the strong southeast trade winds of the austral winter; November-December and March-April are prime. Weeks are sold through specialist agencies, typically 7 nights and 6 fishing days, and demand is such that Cosmoledo and Providence often book a year or more ahead.

## Tackle that survives coral

The outer atolls destroy soft equipment. The standard battery is a 12-weight rod with a fully sealed-drag reel and 130-pound fluorocarbon bite leader for GTs; a 9- or 10-weight for triggers, bumpheads and permit; and an 8-weight for bonefish. Flies are simple but stout: brush flies and NYAP poppers for GTs, alphlexo crabs for triggers and permit, small shrimp patterns for bones. Hooks must be the strongest made — GTs straighten ordinary saltwater irons. Guides recommend bringing doubles of everything, because the nearest fly shop is an ocean away. Sun protection matters as much as tackle: the equatorial sun on white sand is relentless, and a buff, gloves and high-SPF sunscreen are standard uniform.

## Conservation: a fishery built on protection

The outer islands fishery survives because it is tightly controlled. Catch-and-release is mandatory for all flats species, fishing pressure is capped at a handful of rods per atoll per week, and operators fund the Islands Conservation Society and the Alphonse Foundation, which place conservation officers and researchers on the atolls. Aldabra itself is fully protected — no fishing at all — and serves as a giant larval source for the surrounding fisheries. Tagging studies on GTs and bonefish, turtle monitoring and seabird censuses are funded directly from anglers' conservation levies. It is one of the clearest cases in world angling where high-value, low-volume sportfishing pays for the protection of an entire ecosystem.
