Italy hides its fishing the way it hides its best trattorie: in plain sight, behind more famous attractions. The Po valley grows some of the largest freshwater fish in the world, the Alps drop trout streams straight out of postcards, two volcanic lakes south of Tuscany fish like private estates, and 7,500 km of coastline offer everything from squid at dusk to bluefin tuna. Add regional licences that are cheap and increasingly easy to buy online, and the peninsula becomes one of Europe's most underrated angling destinations. Here are the ten waters to build a trip around.

## 1. The Po and its delta

Italy's longest river is Europe's wels catfish capital alongside the Ebro. In May 2023 a catfish of 2.85 metres — a world length record — was landed near Mantua, and the stretches around Cremona and the braided delta below Porto Tolle produce fish over two metres every season. Guides fish vertical baits in the deep holes and stalk clonking runs along the sandbars; spring and autumn are best. The delta lagoons add sea bass and gilthead bream where fresh water meets salt.

## 2. Lake Garda

Italy's largest lake — 370 square kilometres under the Monte Baldo ridge — is a deep, clear arena for lake trout, big pike, perch and carp that cruise the olive-lined eastern shore. The endemic carpione, Garda's own salmonid, is strictly protected, but trolling for lake trout at depth and light-rod perch fishing around the harbour moles of Bardolino and Garda town are open to all with the provincial licence. Winter shore fishing for pike is a quiet local speciality.

## 3. The Sesia at Varallo, Piedmont

Tumbling off Monte Rosa, the Sesia around Varallo is one of the spiritual homes of European fly fishing — the valley pioneered no-kill river management in Italy, and its pocket water suits the local Valsesiana fixed-line style that anglers now travel from abroad to learn. Brown trout, marble trout hybrids and grayling hold in green pools between cobbled villages. The season opens in late winter at valley level and runs into October on regulated stretches.

## 4. Trentino's rivers and alpine lakes

Trentino sells a guest fishing permit system that opens hundreds of kilometres of water: the Sarca beneath the Brenta Dolomites, the Noce — a world fly-fishing championship venue — and the Adige's cold side channels. Above them sit glacial lakes such as Tovel and Cornisello where char and brown trout rise short alpine summers long. Local rules are serious: barbless hooks and daily catch logs are standard, and the fishing is better for it.

## 5. The Nera River, Umbria

Below the 165-metre Marmore falls, the Nera runs cold, fast and absurdly rich through a green canyon — spring-creek chemistry in a mountain-river body. Its regulated stretches around Arrone and Ferentillo hold brown trout in densities few Italian rivers match, and the water stays fishable nearly year-round thanks to stable spring flows. Rafting traffic owns midday in summer, so fish early, late, or in the quieter months.

## 6. Lake Bolsena, Lazio

Europe's largest volcanic lake is a crater of clear water 146 km north of Rome, ringed by black-sand beaches. It fishes wonderfully for pike, perch, big whitefish (coregone) and eels that have fed the lakeside towns since Etruscan times. Rowing-boat trolling at dawn off Marta or Capodimonte is the classic method, and the lake's two islands, Bisentina and Martana, shelter productive drop-offs. A regional licence and modest local rules cover it.

## 7. Lake Trasimeno, Umbria

Shallow, warm and reed-fringed, Trasimeno is the opposite of the alpine lakes — average depth barely 4 metres — and it is one of Italy's best carp and tench waters, with black bass and pike along the reedlines. Float fishing from the piers of Passignano and Castiglione del Lago is delightfully old-fashioned, and the island of Isola Maggiore still hosts a fishing community centuries old. Summer evenings, when the lake glows pink, are the time.

## 8. The Tuscan coast and Argentario

Mediterranean shore fishing comes into its own along the rocky headlands of Monte Argentario and the Maremma beaches: spinning for bluefish and barracuda at dawn, surfcasting for gilthead bream after autumn storms, and eging for squid on winter evenings from the harbour walls of Porto Ercole and Porto Santo Stefano. No licence is required for recreational sea fishing in Italy beyond a free online registration, which keeps the barrier wonderfully low.

## 9. Sardinia: Carloforte and the southern banks

The island of San Pietro off southwest Sardinia faces some of the central Mediterranean's richest big-game grounds. Carloforte — a town built on centuries of tuna culture — is now a catch-and-release bluefin base, with giants over 200 kg raised on trolled lures and live bait from June to September. Inshore, the granite coves of the Costa del Sud offer amberjack, dentex and barracuda for boat and kayak anglers willing to work the dawn shift.

## 10. The Strait of Messina and the deep south

Between Sicily and Calabria, currents squeeze through a 3-km gap above canyons hundreds of metres deep, creating a freakishly rich corridor: Mediterranean spearfish, dolphinfish in late summer, big amberjack on the banks, and a centuries-old harpoon swordfish tradition aboard the strange high-masted passerelle boats. Shore anglers do well too — the rocks near Scilla and the beaches of the Costa Viola produce bonito, bluefish and squid within sight of two regions at once.

## Licences and seasons

Freshwater fishing is managed by region or autonomous province: a licence for visitors typically costs little and is increasingly issued online, with extra daily permits on club-managed and no-kill stretches; trout seasons broadly run from late February or March to September or October. Sea fishing needs only the free national recreational registration, with rules on minimum sizes and a bluefin quota system for tuna. Always check the local provincial regulations — Italy delights in regional exceptions.
