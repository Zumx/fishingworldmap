The barometer falls, the sky darkens over the hills, and rain begins to dimple the surface of the river. Most people retreat. The angler who knows what that falling pressure and rising water is doing to the fish behaviour picks up the rod and heads out. Rain is not the enemy of good fishing. Approached correctly, a wet weather session is often the most productive of the entire season.

The science behind this is real and well-documented. Rainfall changes water chemistry, increases oxygen content, alters temperature gradients, and creates food-bearing run-off that moves fish from holding lies into active feeding positions. Understanding what happens at each stage of a rain event — before, during, and after — lets you choose exactly when to fish and what to target.

## What Happens to Fish When It Rains

As rain falls on the catchment of a river, dissolved oxygen levels at the surface increase. Aquatic insects are disturbed and washed in. Terrestrial insects — earthworms, beetles, ants — fall into the water from bankside vegetation. For surface-feeding trout and chub, this is a sudden abundance of natural food, and feeding activity spikes accordingly.

Rivers colour up as suspended particles enter from fields and bankside topsoil. A touch of colour — pale tea to amber — is ideal for many species. Barbel become bolder as visibility decreases, moving away from their usual deep-water refuges and feeding confidently in shallower, faster water. Chub follow washed-in food items downstream. Pike use the murky water as cover to ambush prey.

In stillwaters, prolonged rain on a warm surface can create a temperature inversion layer that pushes bream and tench into shallower, better-oxygenated water near the margins. Coarse anglers who switch to close-in tactics during and after rain often report extraordinary catches.

## Before the Rain: The Pre-Front Window

The period just before a front arrives — typically 6 to 12 hours before rain begins — often produces a burst of feeding activity. Barometric pressure begins to fall, and many species respond by feeding more aggressively. On stillwaters like the Norfolk Broads or on chalk streams such as the River Kennet, carp and trout have been observed showing the most surface activity in the hour before rain arrives.

Plan your fishing session to coincide with this pre-front period whenever possible. On a river like the River Wye or the Tweed, a session starting mid-afternoon on the day before predicted overnight rain can yield excellent results before the water changes.

## During the Rain: Staying Comfortable and Catching Fish

Staying warm and dry is the only prerequisite for productive wet-weather fishing. A breathable waterproof jacket — Gore-Tex or similar — rated at least 20,000 mm hydrostatic head, waterproof over-trousers, neoprene or rubber boots, and a brimmed cap or hood that keeps rain off your polarised lenses. Without the polarised glasses, reading the water becomes impossible.

Keep electronics and reel mechanisms dry. A reel bag or a simple cloth in a jacket pocket can wipe a reel that gets splashed. Rod rings can ice in cold rain or clog with debris; carry a cotton bud to clear them.

Adjust bait presentation for turbid water. Scent-based baits — boilies, paste baits, cheese paste, prawns — become proportionally more important than visual attractants. In coloured river water, a large bunch of lobworms on a 6 hook leger fished in the crease between fast and slow water is one of the most reliable barbel and chub methods in British and European freshwater angling.

## Protecting Tackle in Wet Conditions

Electronics — fish finders, bite alarms — should be kept under a rod-rest umbrella or covered with a cloth when not actively watched. Rods themselves need no special protection during rain, but reels benefit from a light wipe-down after exposure to rain, particularly if the water is salty or peaty.

Waterproof tackle boxes with foam-seal lids protect hooks and small components. Zip-lock bags cost almost nothing and keep floats, split shot, and swivels organised and dry inside a damp tackle bag. A small towel clipped to the outside of your bag is essential for drying hands before retying rigs in the rain.

## After the Rain: The Post-Flood Window

The period immediately after rain often produces the finest fishing of all. As a river begins to drop and clear after a flood event, fish that were pushed into slack water or off the main river channel begin to move back into feeding positions. A river coming off the peak, with light amber colour and rising water temperature, is precisely when specimen barbel and chub start feeding with real intensity.

For migratory fish, a spate on a salmon or sea trout river triggers movement. A fresh flood on the River Dee, the Tay, or the River Erne after weeks of low summer water can see dozens of salmon push upriver within hours of the first flood pulse reaching the lower beats. Anglers on the right beat at the right time during a spate experience days that become stories retold for years.

## Rain and Sea Fishing

Offshore, rain has little direct effect on saltwater fish — they are already wet. But rain on a sea-fishing day often signals the passage of a depression, which brings onshore wind and a swell that stirs up the bottom and pushes food items — crabs, worms, small fish — inshore. Bream, bass, and flatfish often feed more actively in the hours after a weather system passes and conditions begin to calm.

Inshore, rain washes fresh water into estuaries, altering salinity and pushing mullet and sea bass into the lower reaches. On rivers like the Exe or the Mawddach, post-rain conditions attract large bass that follow the freshwater tongue.
