The first fish caught from a stand-up paddleboard was almost certainly an accident. Some paddler somewhere let a line trail behind them and came tight to something unexpected. But the sport of SUP fishing has evolved far beyond accidental encounters in the two decades since those early experiments. Boards are now engineered specifically for fishing, with multiple rod holders moulded into the deck, integrated cooler hatches, and scupper-plug systems that prevent water ingress without compromising the hull's lateral stability. SUP fishing sits at the intersection of stealth, fitness, and angling — and it is growing rapidly.

## Choosing the Right Board for Fishing

Not all stand-up paddleboards are suitable for fishing. The key specification is width: a fishing SUP should be at least eighty-two centimetres wide and ideally between eighty-five and ninety-five centimetres. This extra beam dramatically increases the platform's stability, allowing the angler to stand, turn, cast, and fight fish without fear of capsizing. Length matters too. A board of between ten feet six inches and twelve feet six inches (approximately 320 to 380 cm) provides enough straight-line tracking to cover water efficiently while remaining manoeuvrable in tight spots.

Popular dedicated fishing SUPs include the Hobie Mirage Eclipse, the BIC Sport Tough-Tec, and the Rogue Fishing SUP, all of which offer pre-installed rod holders, D-ring anchor points, and non-slip EVA deck pads. Inflatable SUPs — iSUPs — have become extremely popular for their portability and ease of car-top transport, but they trade some rigidity for convenience. Brands like Red Paddle Co and Fanatic produce inflatable fishing models that perform surprisingly well in calm conditions.

## Paddles and Propulsion Options

The paddle is the second most important piece of SUP fishing equipment. A carbon fibre shaft with an adjustable length of between 170 and 200 centimetres keeps the blade low and controlled, minimising water splash on lures and lines. Some SUP anglers prefer a single-blade kayak paddle seated on a low seat mounted to the deck — a hybrid approach that eliminates the balance challenge entirely for calmer-water scenarios. Hobie's pedal-drive systems can also be installed on certain large fishing SUPs, freeing both hands for casting while the angler pedals forward.

## Rod Holders and Storage

A fishing SUP without rod holders is merely a paddleboard. The best fishing boards come with between two and six pre-installed flush-mount rod holders positioned along the rail and at the nose and tail of the board. Aftermarket solutions — such as RAM Mount fishing rod holders that attach via cam-strap to paddle board handles — can supplement or replace factory fittings. A milk-crate bungee system lashed to the deck via D-rings is popular among recreational SUP anglers for storing tackle boxes, a small cooler, pliers, and a landing net.

Watertight dry bags of five to ten litres clipped to D-rings provide waterproof storage for phones, keys, and licences. A PFD — personal flotation device — is mandatory for SUP fishing in most jurisdictions, and a leash attaching the angler to the board is equally essential.

## Anchor Systems and Holding Position

Holding position in wind and current is the primary tactical challenge of SUP fishing. A small stakeout pole — a fibreglass or carbon spike of about 120 cm in length — pushed into a sandy or muddy bottom keeps the board motionless in shallow water up to about one metre in depth. In deeper water, a micro-anchor system of one to two kilograms with a clip-release carabiner attached to a front D-ring holds the board oriented into the current while the angler targets downstream structure.

A drift anchor or drift sock dragged from the stern slows the board's movement in moderate wind without fixing it to the bottom — useful when targeting a section of current seam or a reef edge with a drifting presentation. Many SUP anglers rig a hand-line spike that can be poled into a weedbed or seagrass flat, anchoring the board in less than thirty centimetres of water for targeting tailing redfish, bonefish, or flats-dwelling flathead.

## Casting and Fighting Fish on a SUP

Casting from a standing position on a moving platform requires a more compact casting stroke than boat fishing. Overhead casts are effective in open water but risk lure tangles in wind. Side-arm roll casts and flip casts are more reliable for targeting structure with accuracy. A seven-foot medium-action spinning rod with a 2500- to 3000-series reel is the most versatile SUP fishing setup — light enough for extended one-hand paddling, but capable of handling most inshore species.

When a fish is hooked, the key to staying upright is to lower the centre of gravity immediately by bending the knees into a slight squat position, feet shoulder-width apart. Resist the impulse to lean heavily over the rail — a large fish pulling at ninety degrees to the board's axis is the most likely cause of a swim. Use the paddle as a counterbalance when side pressure is intense. Many experienced SUP anglers actually kneel to fight large fish, sacrificing some line-of-sight for stability.

## Target Species and Locations

SUP fishing excels in shallow, calm water environments that larger boats cannot access. Estuaries, tidal flats, seagrass meadows, mangrove creek mouths, and freshwater lakes all produce excellent SUP fishing. In salt water, bream, flathead, and whiting are ideal SUP targets in Australian estuaries; redfish, speckled trout, and snook on the shallow flats of Florida and the Gulf Coast; bass and zander in European rivers and stillwaters. In the Florida Keys and the Bahamas, permit and bonefish on the flats have attracted a dedicated community of SUP fly-fishers who value the ability to pole silently within casting range of tailing fish.

## Safety Essentials

A leash from the ankle or calf to the board is non-negotiable in open or tidal water. A whistle and a mirror for signalling are low-cost safety additions. Sunscreen, polarised sunglasses for spotting fish, and neoprene booties for rocky launches round out the essential kit. Keep a VHF radio or a waterproof phone in a dry bag when fishing in exposed coastal waters.
