Kazakhstan is the world's ninth-largest country, yet it barely registers on the angling map — which is precisely the appeal. Between the Caspian Sea and the Altai Mountains stretch steppe rivers, half-saline lakes and reed deltas where carp die of old age and wels catfish reach weights that would make headlines anywhere in Europe. Infrastructure is thin, distances are enormous, and a fishing trip here still feels like an expedition. For anglers who measure a destination by how few boot prints they find on the bank, these ten waters are hard to beat.

## The Ili River delta

Where the Ili unravels into Lake Balkhash, it forms one of Central Asia's great wetlands: hundreds of square kilometres of reed channels, lagoons and shallow lakes. This is the country's flagship fishery. Wild carp — the local sazan — average 3 to 8 kg with far bigger fish present, wels catfish exceed 50 kg, and aggressive asp smash lures at every confluence. Access is by boat from villages such as Bakanas or through fishing bases that house guests in simple cabins. Spring and autumn are prime; in high summer the delta swelters and the mosquitoes take command at dusk.

## Lake Balkhash

Balkhash is a hydrological curiosity: roughly 600 km long, fresh in its western half and brackish in the east, divided by the narrow Saryesik strait. The western basin around the city of Balkhash and the Saryshagan shore fishes best, with wild carp, pike, zander, asp and wels all present in numbers. Locals fish from shore with feeder rods or run small boats along the reed lines. The lake's pale turquoise colour against the ochre steppe is unforgettable, and summer evenings bring blistering topwater asp action where baitfish gather off rocky points.

## Kapshagai Reservoir

An hour north of Almaty, the 100-km-long Kapshagai Reservoir on the Ili is the country's most accessible big-fish water. Zander and asp dominate the rocky shorelines, carp roam the bays, and the dam area holds serious wels. Because it is close to the city, weekend pressure exists, but a short boat ride leaves the crowds behind. Many Almaty guides offer day trips here, making Kapshagai the logical first stop before committing to the delta. Ice fishing for zander and perch is popular from December through February when the reservoir freezes solid.

## Bukhtarma Reservoir and Lake Zaysan

In the far east, the Irtysh river system feeds the giant Bukhtarma Reservoir and, upstream, the ancient Lake Zaysan. Together they form Kazakhstan's richest predator fishery: pike to double figures, zander in absurd numbers, ide, perch and large wels. The Zaysan basin's eroded badlands shoreline looks Martian, and summer boat camps let anglers fish dawn to dark for days. Locals jig the old riverbed channels; visitors who time the June zander feed can catch fish until their arms tire. Winter brings a major ice-fishing scene served from the town of Serebryansk.

## The Altai rivers of East Kazakhstan

Around Katon-Karagay National Park, near the borders with Russia and China, the Bukhtarma river and its mountain tributaries run cold and clear out of the Altai. This is grayling and lenok country — the lenok here is the prized local salmonid related to the famous uskuch of nearby Lake Markakol. Expect walk-and-wade fishing through larch forest at 1,000 to 1,800 metres elevation, with small spinners and dry flies both effective from June to September. The region demands time — the drive from Oskemen takes most of a day — but rewards with genuine mountain wilderness.

## The Syr Darya and the southern steppe

The Syr Darya crosses southern Kazakhstan on its long run toward the Aral basin, and its turbid waters hold wels, sazan, asp and — a modern twist — introduced northern snakehead, which have spread through the basin's canals and lakes and hit surface lures ferociously. The river towns of Kyzylorda and Turkistan make workable bases, and the side lakes and irrigation reservoirs are often better than the main stem. This is hot, dusty, utterly untouristed fishing, best in April-May and September-October when the steppe heat relents.

## Trout streams and lakes around Almaty

The Tian Shan foothills behind Almaty hide quick, cold streams such as the Turgen and Issyk, where introduced rainbow and brook trout hold in pocket water beneath 4,000-metre peaks. Several trout farms in the gorges offer guaranteed action and lakeside dining — a relaxed half-day when travel fatigue hits. Higher up, the Big Almaty Lake area is closed to fishing as drinking-water catchment, but the valleys eastward toward Issyk Lake offer legal water in alpine scenery. Combine a morning stream session with an afternoon in the mountains and you understand why Almaty people love their weekends.

## The Lepsy, Alakol and the rivers of Zhetysu

The Zhetysu region — the land of seven rivers — sends streams like the Lepsy, Aksu and Karatal down from the Dzungarian Alatau into Balkhash's eastern basin. Their lower reaches and confluences hold sazan, wels, asp and pike, and see almost no visiting anglers. Further east, Lake Alakol — a slightly saline lake popular with summer bathers — produces surprisingly good perch and marinka fishing away from the beaches. Gravel roads, basic guesthouses and helpful locals define a trip here; bring spares for everything and translate your fishing phrases in advance.

## Seasons, licences and getting it done

Recreational fishing in Kazakhstan requires only inexpensive regional permits for most public waters, typically arranged through local inspectors, fishing bases or guides — letting an outfitter handle paperwork is the practical route. Closed seasons protect spawning fish roughly from April to June on many waters, with exact dates varying by region. The continental climate is extreme: plan delta and steppe trips for May-June or September-October, mountain trips for July-August, and pack for cold nights even in summer. Russian-language fishing terms go far; English is rare outside Almaty and Astana.
