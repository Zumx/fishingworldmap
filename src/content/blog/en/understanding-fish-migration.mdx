In late October on the River Dee in Wales, the water runs cold and tannin-dark after autumn rain, and in the deeper pools something is happening that has been happening since before humans put words to it. Atlantic salmon that entered the river in January after a two-year ocean feeding migration are completing a journey that began in the egg-sized gravel beds of the upper tributaries above Bala. They have not fed since they crossed from salt water to fresh. Their bodies have been fuelled entirely by reserves accumulated in the Norwegian Sea and the waters west of Greenland, and now they are digging redds in precisely the same gravel sections where they hatched, where the water temperature and oxygen content and substrate size meet requirements hard-coded into their biology over hundreds of thousands of years. The act of witnessing it — even at a distance — is one of the most compelling things in outdoor life.

## The Science of Anadromy: How Salmon Navigate

Atlantic salmon are anadromous fish: they hatch in freshwater, spend their juvenile period in rivers, migrate to the sea to feed and grow, and return to freshwater to spawn. The return navigation is extraordinary. Tagging studies by the North Atlantic Salmon Conservation Organisation and national fisheries agencies have confirmed that the majority of returning salmon enter the same river system, and within that system make their way to within metres of their natal tributary. The mechanism has been studied intensively: juvenile salmon imprint on the unique chemical signature of their home river during the downstream migration as smolts, using their olfactory system to encode this as a homing signal. At sea, they use a combination of the Earth's magnetic field and celestial cues to navigate across ocean distances. The details remain incompletely understood despite decades of research.

The Atlantic salmon's feeding migration takes them to the Norwegian Sea and the waters around the Faroe Islands, where they chase capelin, sand eels, and krill. Satellite tagging of post-smolt salmon leaving Irish and Scottish rivers has documented journeys of over 5,000 kilometres before the fish turn and begin the homeward run, typically after one or two years at sea. Multi-sea-winter fish that survive two feeding migrations can grow to over 20 kilograms — the large spring fish that make rivers like the Tweed, the Wye, the Moy, and the Gaula in Norway legendary for trophy catches.

## Pacific Salmon: Six Species, One Continental Migration

The Pacific coast of North America hosts six salmon species, each with their own migration timing and life history. Chinook (king) salmon, the largest of the six, migrate the furthest inland: the Yukon River run in Alaska ascends over 3,000 kilometres to spawning grounds near the Yukon-British Columbia border, making it one of the longest freshwater migrations of any fish. Sockeye salmon staging for the Fraser River system in British Columbia congregate in such numbers in late summer that the river runs visibly red in the lower tributaries when the run peaks.

All Pacific salmon species are semelparous — they spawn once and die, their decomposing bodies releasing marine-derived nutrients including nitrogen and phosphorus into the freshwater systems. The carcass load from a large salmon run can measurably increase the nitrogen content of streamside trees within 100 metres of the bank, cycling ocean nutrients into the forest. In years with poor salmon returns, the impact on riparian vegetation is measurable. This nutrient transfer makes salmon not just a target for anglers and predators but a structural component of northern Pacific and Atlantic coastal ecosystems.

## European Eels: The Most Mysterious Migration

If the salmon's migration is extraordinary, the European eel's is almost incomprehensible. Anguilla anguilla spawns somewhere in the Sargasso Sea east of the Bahamas — the exact spawning ground has never been directly observed by scientists, despite nearly a century of searching. Larval eels, called leptocephali, drift on Atlantic currents for between six months and three years, gradually crossing the ocean before metamorphosing into glass eels and entering European river systems as transparent, finger-length juveniles.

Once in freshwater, the eel spends between 5 and 20 years feeding and growing in rivers, lakes, and estuaries across Europe. Eels have been found in watercourses as far inland as Bavaria and the Swiss plateau. When they reach sexual maturity — triggered by a combination of age, size, and environmental cues that are not yet fully understood — they undergo a dramatic physical transformation: the eye enlarges, the body darkens to a silver-black, the gut degenerates as they will not feed again. They then migrate downstream and out to sea, swimming approximately 6,000 kilometres back to the Sargasso Sea to spawn, after which they die. The complete round trip, from birth to death, has never been observed by a human researcher.

## Steelhead and Sea Trout: The Anadromous Relatives

Sea trout in Europe and steelhead in North America are anadromous forms of the same species — brown trout (Salmo trutta) in Europe, rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) in North America — that express a migratory life history in certain populations. Not all individuals in a population migrate; in most rivers, sea-run individuals coexist with non-migratory residents. The trigger for migration appears to be partly genetic and partly related to density and food availability in the juvenile habitat.

Sea trout runs in rivers like the Orkla in Norway, the Shetland voes, and the chalk streams of southern England are among the most coveted sea fishing targets in European freshwater. Unlike salmon, sea trout commonly return to sea after spawning and may complete multiple migrations. Large sea trout of over 10 kilograms have been caught in rivers including the Wye in Wales and the Eman in Sweden.

## Coarse Fish Migration and Local Movements

Not all migration is oceanic in scale. Freshwater coarse fish species undertake shorter migrations that are ecologically significant and directly relevant to anglers. Barbel in English rivers like the Kennet and the Hampshire Avon make predictable upstream movements in autumn to winter spawning grounds, and downstream movements to summer feeding areas in the deeper slower reaches. Pike and perch make regular movements between shallow weedy summer margins and deeper winter holding areas. Understanding these seasonal patterns is as important to the angler as understanding the larger migrations of salmon and eels.

Bream in stillwaters like Windermere and Grafham Water make daily movements between deep-water resting areas and shallower feeding zones, often following the thermocline as oxygen levels shift through the day. These movements can be tracked using acoustic telemetry tags, and the resulting data has practical implications: anglers who understand where bream will be at a given time of day are fishing efficiently rather than searching blind.

## Why Fish Migration Matters for Anglers

Fish migration determines where fish are, when they are catchable, and what state they are in physically when they arrive. Understanding migration science helps anglers make better decisions about timing, location, and technique. It also underpins the conservation case for maintaining migratory corridors free of barriers, for controlling water abstraction that reduces flows during critical migration periods, and for protecting sea feeding areas from overfishing. An angler who understands why salmon enter a river when they do is an angler who can advocate coherently for the conditions that allow the run to continue.
