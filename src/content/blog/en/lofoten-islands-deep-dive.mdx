The Lofoten wall rises straight out of the Norwegian Sea a hundred kilometres above the Arctic Circle: granite peaks to 1,000 metres with fishing villages pinned to their feet on stilted wooden wharves. For more than a thousand years, this archipelago has lived on a single annual event — the arrival of the skrei, the great migration of Barents Sea cod that swims south every winter to spawn in the Vestfjord. The Vikings dried this fish on wooden racks and traded it across Europe; the racks still stand, the smell of drying stockfish still rides the March wind, and the fishing is still, by any global measure, extraordinary.

## The skrei: a migration measured in millions

Skrei are not ordinary coastal cod. They are the northeast Arctic cod stock — the largest cod population left on earth — which lives in the Barents Sea and returns each winter, from roughly February to mid-April, to spawn along the Lofoten banks. Mature skrei run from 5 to over 20 kilos, hard-muscled from a 1,000-kilometre swim. For anglers, the season means big fish in relatively shallow water: spawning concentrations stack between 30 and 80 metres, easily reached by jigging with pirks and large soft baits from small boats. On a good February day off Henningsvær or Ballstad, double-digit catches of fish averaging 8 to 12 kilos are normal, and every season produces cod past 25 kilos — within sight of snow-covered peaks.

## A thousand years of Lofotfisket

The winter fishery — Lofotfisket — is the foundation of coastal Norway. By the Middle Ages, dried Lofoten cod (tørrfisk) was Norway's most valuable export, shipped via Bergen to Catholic Europe; even today, Italy buys much of the production for baccalà. The fishing villages preserve this world intact: Henningsvær spread across its islets, Nusfjord's perfectly kept 19th-century harbor, Å and Reine at the road's dramatic end, and Kabelvåg, where the fishery's history fills the Lofoten Museum. Visitors sleep in rorbuer — the red fishermen's cabins built on poles over the water — many converted to comfortable lodging that keeps boats, fish-landing quays and cod-liver-oil history a few steps from bed.

## The World Cod Fishing Championship

Every March, Svolvær hosts the Verdensmesterskapet i skreifiske — the World Championship in cod fishing — when several hundred competitors in everything from open skiffs to charter boats jig the Vestfjord for the heaviest bag. It is equal parts serious competition and Arctic festival, with the harbor full, prizes for the biggest skrei, and a town that triples in energy for a weekend. For a travelling angler, championship week is the perfect storm: peak skrei season, charter availability organized around visitors, and the rare chance to fish a world championship for the price of an entry ticket.

## Halibut from small boats

Cod made Lofoten famous, but Atlantic halibut keep many anglers coming back. The sand flats and channel edges around the archipelago — and the banks toward Værøy and Røst at the chain's tip — hold halibut from hand-sized to genuinely huge; Norwegian waters have produced rod-caught fish well over 200 kilos, and every Lofoten season delivers fish past the magic 100-kilo mark to small-boat anglers. The game is patient: drifting big shads and dead baits across sand in 20 to 60 metres, often in summer when the midnight sun allows fishing at 2 a.m. in full light. A large halibut beside a 17-foot boat is one of angling's serious moments — guides carry harpoons and strict procedures, and most big females are released to protect the slow-growing stock.

## Beyond cod: the supporting cast

The same waters hold coalfish (saithe) that hunt in ravenous schools and pull like tuna on light tackle, haddock over sand, wolffish with their crab-crushing jaws, redfish in the deeps, and pollack along the kelp edges. Shore fishing is genuinely good — harbor moles, rock points and beaches produce cod and coalfish through winter, a rarity in modern Europe. Inland, the islands hide small trout lakes and sea-trout streams in the valleys between peaks, though the salt is the show. In summer, plumb the deeper channels; in autumn, the coalfish schools boil on the surface around the fjord mouths.

## Seasons, light and weather

Lofoten fishes year-round, but it offers two different worlds. Winter, January to April, is the skrei spectacle: short days growing fast, northern lights overhead by night, hard cold and real weather — gales can lock boats in harbor for days, so build slack into any trip. Summer, May to August, brings the midnight sun, halibut and coalfish, and family-friendly conditions. The Gulf Stream keeps harbors ice-free at a latitude that freezes solid in Canada. Pack for wind always: the Vestfjord can turn from mirror to malice in an hour, and good operators simply wait it out.

## Practicalities and rules

Saltwater fishing in Norway requires no licence for hand-held tackle — a freedom worth celebrating — but rules still matter. Tourists may export a limited amount of fish fillets (currently 18 kilos, twice per year) only when fishing through registered tourist-fishing businesses; sell nothing, ever. Minimum sizes apply, cod among them, and the deep national instinct for sustainable harvest deserves respect. Getting there: fly to Bodø and ferry to Moskenes, fly directly to Svolvær or Leknes, or drive the E10 the length of the chain. Winter anglers should book registered operators with proper sea-rescue equipment; the Arctic forgives little improvisation.
