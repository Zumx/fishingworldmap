The first run of a Mekong giant catfish feels less like a fish and more like a passing truck that has somehow hooked itself to your braid. Thailand is one of the very few countries on earth where an angler can battle fish over 100 kg in the morning, eat street food for lunch, and be trolling for sailfish off a limestone island by the next sunrise. Between the famous monster-fish lakes around Bangkok, the jungle reservoirs of the west, and the Andaman Sea's blue-water grounds, the kingdom packs an absurd variety of fishing into one tropical country.

## Bungsamran Lake, Bangkok

Bungsamran, on the eastern edge of Bangkok, is probably the most famous monster-fish lake in Asia. Dug as a commercial pond decades ago, it now holds Mekong giant catfish that commonly run 30 to 80 kg, with genuine 100 kg-plus fish hooked every season, alongside huge striped catfish and Siamese carp. Most visitors fish from bamboo bungalows over the water, using a method ball of fermented rice bran packed around the hook. Fights on heavy float gear regularly last 30 to 45 minutes, and arms ache by mid-afternoon. Day tickets, tackle hire and guides are all arranged on site, which makes it the easiest serious fishing day in the country.

## Gillhams Fishing Resorts, Krabi

Set against jungle-covered karst cliffs near Krabi town, Gillhams is the address for arapaima in Thailand. The lake has produced arapaima well past 150 kg, plus Siamese carp approaching world-record size, alligator gar and redtail catfish. This is a full resort operation with lakeside villas, strict barbless-hook rules and guides on hand for every fight, because an arapaima's bony mouth and violent head-shakes demand technique. It is expensive by Thai standards, but anglers chasing a genuine fish of a lifetime in comfortable surroundings rarely leave disappointed.

## Jurassic Mountain Resort and the Hua Hin lakes

Three hours south of Bangkok, the area around Hua Hin has become Thailand's stocked-lake capital. Jurassic Mountain Resort and Fishing Park holds more than 40 species, including Siamese carp over 100 kg, arapaima, chao phraya catfish and giant freshwater stingray. Smaller venues such as the Hua Hin Fishing Lodge offer relaxed half-day sessions ideal for families or anglers travelling with non-fishing partners, since the beach and night markets are minutes away. November to February brings the most comfortable temperatures for long days on the bank.

## Phuket's fishing parks and Chalong charters

Phuket is the island where freshwater and saltwater Thailand meet. In the interior, Phuket Fishing Park and Chalong Fishing Park offer pondside shelters where beginners can hook hard-fighting pacu, catfish and barramundi within minutes — Big Game Kathu 89 near the old tin-mining district is another local favourite. Down at Chalong Bay's piers, dozens of charter boats head out daily toward Racha Yai and Racha Noi islands, where trolling produces wahoo, dorado, Spanish mackerel and sailfish, and jigging over the drop-offs turns up giant trevally and dogtooth tuna.

## Cheow Lan Lake, Khao Sok National Park

Cheow Lan is the most beautiful place you will ever cast a lure: a 165 square-kilometre reservoir flooded in the 1980s, studded with limestone towers that rise hundreds of metres straight from the water. Anglers stay in floating raft-house bungalows and fish dawn and dusk for giant snakehead, hampala barb and jungle perch around drowned timber. Casting poppers against the cliffs as gibbons whoop in the forest is the signature Thai jungle-fishing experience. Local boatmen know the productive arms of the lake; access is from the Ratchaprapha Dam pier near Khao Sok.

## Srinakarin Dam and Khao Laem Reservoir

Kanchanaburi province, west of Bangkok along the Myanmar border, hides two enormous jungle reservoirs. Srinakarin Dam is Thailand's best-known giant snakehead water, where fish over 10 kg ambush topwater lures around flooded forest, and the same trips often produce jungle perch and hampala. Khao Laem, further north near Sangkhlaburi, fishes similarly but sees far fewer anglers. Both are multi-day expedition fisheries — you sleep on raft houses, run long-tail boats at first light, and cast until your wrists give out. The dry season from December to April offers the most stable water levels.

## Pattaya and the eastern seaboard

Pattaya is better known for nightlife, but its fishing scene is real. Jomtien Fishing Park, just south of the main beach strip, stocks Mekong giant catfish, striped catfish and barramundi in a relaxed pondside setting with restaurants at your back. Offshore, charters run to the islands of Ko Larn and beyond toward Ko Si Chang for cobia, queenfish, grouper and barracuda. Because the Gulf here is calm for most of the year, this coast suits anglers who want a saltwater day without the longer Andaman crossings.

## The Andaman big-game grounds

From November to April, the Andaman Sea off Phuket, Khao Lak and Ko Lanta is Southeast Asia's most accessible big-game arena. Multi-day liveaboard trips push out toward the Similan Islands, where seamounts hold dogtooth tuna, yellowfin, sailfish and the occasional black marlin. Closer banks around Phi Phi and the Racha islands fish well on day trips. Heavy popping and jigging gear is standard, and reputable charters supply quality tackle. The flat seas of the high season also make these trips comfortable for mixed groups who want snorkelling between bites.

## Seasons, rules and practicalities

Thailand has no national rod licence for recreational anglers; commercial ponds and parks charge day rates that usually include tackle, while national-park waters such as Cheow Lan require park entry fees and a local boatman. The cool, dry season from November to February is prime almost everywhere, the hot season from March to May suits early starts, and the southwest monsoon from May to October mainly affects the Andaman coast while Gulf and freshwater venues keep producing. Nearly every serious venue enforces catch-and-release and barbless hooks for the giant species — treat the fish well and the photos take care of themselves.
