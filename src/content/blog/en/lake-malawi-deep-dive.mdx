Stretching nearly 600 kilometres along Malawi's eastern border and shared with Mozambique and Tanzania, Lake Malawi holds a distinction no other body of freshwater can claim: it contains more species of fish than any lake on Earth. Estimates vary, but somewhere between 800 and 1,000 species have been identified in its waters, the overwhelming majority of them cichlids and the overwhelming majority of those found nowhere else. The United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organisation recognised this uniqueness in 1984 when it declared Lake Malawi National Park a World Heritage Site — the park protecting the lake's southern end where fish diversity is most extraordinary.

## Cichlid Paradise: The mbuna and Utaka

Two ecological guilds of cichlid dominate the lake's ecology. The mbuna — a local Tonga word meaning "rock-dwelling fish" — occupy the rocky shoreline zones in jaw-dropping densities and a kaleidoscope of colours. Labeotropheus, Pseudotropheus, and Melanochromis species pile into reef-like rock piles, each defending territories no larger than a dinner plate. The utaka, by contrast, are open-water cichlids that school in massive midwater aggregations and form the basis of the lake's artisanal fishery. For anglers, mbuna are not sport targets in the conventional sense, but watching hundreds of these fish — some in colours that seem too vivid to be real — while fishing from an anchored boat is an experience in itself.

## Mpasa: The Lake's Unique Salmon Analogue

The mpasa (Opsaridium microlepis) is perhaps the most extraordinary fishing target in the lake. This large riverine barb — sometimes called the Lake Malawi salmon — spends most of its life in the lake but makes epic upstream spawning migrations into feeder rivers, particularly the North Rukuru and South Rukuru Rivers that drain the Nyika Plateau in northern Malawi. Anglers who time their visit to coincide with these runs (typically March through May) can intercept fish of 3–6 kg on fly gear or light spinners in the relatively accessible river pools near Chintheche and Nkhata Bay. The Bua River further south offers a similar opportunity in different terrain.

## Tiger Fishing in the Southern Arm

The Shire River drains the southern end of Lake Malawi, connecting it to the Zambezi. The shallow southern arm of the lake, near the town of Mangochi and the lake's exit into the Upper Shire, holds a productive tiger fish population (Hydrocynus vittatus) that rarely appears in international fishing literature. Fish of 3–7 kg are regularly taken by spin-fishing anglers working rocky points and river mouths in this zone. The season peaks between July and September when water temperatures drop slightly and fish concentrate in predictable feeding lanes.

## The Nankumba Peninsula and Monkey Bay

The Nankumba Peninsula juts into the lake roughly 150 kilometres north of Mangochi, forming the northern boundary of Lake Malawi National Park. The rocky headlands and offshore islands of this peninsula — particularly Thumbi West Island, Boadzulu Island, and Otter Point — are the lake's most famous diving and snorkelling spots, but the angling for catfish and large predatory cichlids like the kampango (Bagrus meridionalis) is equally good. Monkey Bay, at the peninsula's base, serves as the main logistical hub for the lake's southern section, with road connections to Blantyre and Lilongwe.

## The Northern Lake: Nkhata Bay and Likoma Island

Northern Lake Malawi is wilder and sees fewer tourists. Nkhata Bay is a natural harbour fringed by forested hillsides with guesthouses ranging from backpacker lodges to small boutique hotels. The fishing here for mpasa during the run months and for the lake's large synodontis catfish is excellent. Likoma Island, a Malawian enclave surrounded on all sides by Mozambican waters, has a cathedral dating to 1903 that would not look out of place in an English county town, and the waters around it hold some of the lake's clearest visibility and most abundant rock cichlid communities.

## Practical Access and Accommodation

Malawi is straightforward to visit by regional African standards. Kamuzu International Airport near Lilongwe and Chileka Airport near Blantyre receive connections from Nairobi, Johannesburg, Addis Abeba, and Dar es Salaam. The M1 highway runs the length of the country parallel to the lake, and a network of tarred lakeshore roads connects most major resorts. Accommodation spans a wide range, from rustic campsites at Chitimba in the north to well-appointed lodges at Cape Maclear and at Mvuu Camp in Liwonde National Park. A fishing permit is required and available from local fishing cooperative offices.

## Conservation and the Future

Lake Malawi's biodiversity faces serious pressure from overfishing of the artisanal fleet, particularly the use of fine-mesh nets that remove juvenile fish. The introduction of the ncheni (Nile perch) to parts of the lake has also created localised extinction pressure on endemic cichlids in affected bays. Several international conservation organisations and the Malawi Department of Fisheries are working on stock assessment and community-based management programmes. Sport anglers can support this work by using catch-and-release practices and engaging with responsible licensed operators.
