The wind hits you first. Stand on the bank of the Limay or the Río Grande in January and a forty-knot westerly will flatten the grass, rip line off your reel and remind you that the nearest landmass upwind is New Zealand, some 9,000 kilometres away. Patagonia is not a polite fishery. It is a vast, half-empty triangle of steppe, beech forest and Andean snowmelt shared by Argentina and Chile, where brown and rainbow trout introduced barely a century ago have grown into some of the largest, wildest salmonids on Earth. This deep dive covers both countries: the spring creeks of Neuquén, the lake districts around Bariloche and Puerto Varas, the remote Carretera Austral, and the sea-trout rivers of Tierra del Fuego at the literal end of the world.

## How trout conquered the Southern Cone

There were no trout in Patagonia before 1904. That year, Argentine authorities — advised by the American fish culturist John Titcomb — began stocking brook trout, browns and rainbows into the rivers draining the Andes. Chile followed soon after. The fish found cold, food-rich, almost competitor-free water and did what trout do: they took over. The Río Grande's sea-run browns descend from a 1935 stocking by John Goodall, and that single introduction now supports what is statistically the best sea-trout fishery on the planet, with annual averages around 4 to 5 kilos and fish over 12 kilos landed every season. The season across most of Patagonia runs from November to mid-April or May, the reverse of the northern hemisphere — which is precisely why so many anglers travel here in January.

## Northern Patagonia, Argentina: Junín de los Andes and the Chimehuín

The town of Junín de los Andes in Neuquén province calls itself the trout capital of Argentina, and the claim is defensible. The Chimehuín river leaves Lago Huechulafquen beneath the perfect cone of Volcán Lanín (3,776 m) at the famous Boca, a pool where fly-fishing legends like Bebe Anchorena once landed double-digit browns. Within an hour's drive you have the Malleo — a willow-lined freestone stream that fishes like a dry-fly classroom during the January dragonfly hatches — plus the Aluminé, the Quilquihue and the Collón Curá. The Malleo flows through Mapuche land and a day permit is required on the upper beats. Expect rainbows of 40 to 50 cm rising freely, and browns considerably larger hugging the willow roots.

## Bariloche, the Limay and the boca culture

Further south, San Carlos de Bariloche on Lago Nahuel Huapi is the most practical base in Argentine Patagonia, with direct flights from Buenos Aires. The Limay river, leaving the lake on its long journey to the Atlantic, is the country's heavyweight brown-trout water; its middle section below the Alicurá dam — fished by drift boat — produces migratory browns of 60 to 80 cm with humbling regularity. The local style is the boca: fishing the mouth where a river enters or leaves a lake, often at first and last light, where cruising trophy fish patrol the current seam. The boca of the Correntoso, which claims to be one of the shortest rivers in the world at roughly 200 m, is famous for exactly this game.

## Chilean Patagonia: rainforest spring creeks and the Carretera Austral

Cross the Andes and the climate flips. Chilean Patagonia is wetter, greener and mossier — Valdivian rainforest instead of dry steppe. Around Coyhaique in the Aysén region, the Río Simpson and Río Ñirehuao offer classic dry-fly water, while the milky-turquoise Río Baker, Chile's biggest river by volume, holds rainbows that eat streamers the size of small birds. The Carretera Austral, the gravel highway threading 1,240 km from Puerto Montt to Villa O'Higgins, is arguably the world's finest road trip for an angler with a rented 4x4: every bridge crosses fishable water, and lakes like Yelcho near Chaitén produce browns and rainbows over 5 kilos. Further north, the Puerto Varas and Pucón areas mix volcano scenery with the Petrohué and Liucura rivers.

## Tierra del Fuego: the sea-trout pilgrimage

The Río Grande crosses the Argentine half of Tierra del Fuego through treeless, wind-scoured sheep country, and nothing about it looks special until a fish takes. Roughly 70,000 sea-run brown trout enter the river each season, and lodges such as Kau Tapen and Villa María have made the valley a pilgrimage site. The technique is humbling: two-handed rods, heavy sink-tips, small nymphs or leeches swung across the wind at dusk, repeated until the line stops. Neighbouring Chilean Tierra del Fuego offers the quieter Río Penitente and the remote fjords of the Magallanes region. Average fish here would be the catch of a lifetime in Europe; the river is entirely catch-and-release on the prime water.

## Seasons, tactics and the wind problem

Timing matters enormously. November and December bring high, cold water and aggressive post-winter fish; January and February are peak dry-fly months in the north, with beetle, hopper and dragonfly patterns dominant; March and April offer the biggest migratory fish in the Limay and Río Grande as autumn cools the water. The wind is a constant: practise your double haul before you come, bring a fast-action 6-weight for general work and an 8-weight or a 12-foot switch rod for sea trout. Sun protection is non-negotiable — the ozone layer is thin this far south, and a buff, gloves and SPF 50 are as essential as tippet.

## Licences, access and getting around

Argentina sells a single Patagonian fishing permit covering Neuquén, Río Negro, Chubut, Santa Cruz and Tierra del Fuego, available online and in tackle shops, with daily, weekly and seasonal options. Chile requires a separate national licence from Sernapesca, easily bought online. Access culture differs: Argentine rivers are legally navigable public space but crossing private estancia land often needs permission; in Chile, riverbanks are public but reaching them can mean negotiating with farmers — a smile and a few words of Spanish open most gates. Rental cars cross the border with notarised paperwork arranged in advance. Many anglers simply split the trip: fly to Bariloche or San Martín de los Andes for a week, then to Balmaceda/Coyhaique or Ushuaia for the second.

## Beyond the fishing

Patagonia rewards the rest days. From Bariloche you can hike the Refugio Frey granite spires; from El Calafate, watch the Perito Moreno glacier calve into Lago Argentino; from Puerto Natales, walk beneath the towers of Torres del Paine. Asado culture means lamb roasted over open coals at every estancia, paired with Malbec from Mendoza. The distances are huge — Patagonia is the size of Western Europe with the population of a mid-sized city — so build slack into any itinerary and treat the journey as part of the catch.
