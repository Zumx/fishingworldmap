There is a persistent and entirely mistaken prejudice among some river fly fishers that stillwater trout fishing — casting from a bank or a drifting boat into a reservoir or man-made lake — is somehow a lesser pursuit. In reality, stillwater fly fishing on the great UK reservoirs demands tactical awareness, entomological knowledge, and casting skill every bit as refined as dry-fly fishing on the Test or the Itchen. The quarry — rainbow and brown trout stocked by the fishery, or in some cases wild fish in natural lakes — feed selectively and learn quickly from angling pressure. Reading the water, understanding the wind, selecting the right fly and line, and presenting the pattern at the correct depth and retrieve speed are skills that take seasons to develop. This is fly fishing of the highest order.

## The Great UK Stillwater Fisheries

The UK's network of purpose-built and reservoir trout fisheries is unmatched in the world. Rutland Water in Leicestershire — at 3,100 hectares the largest man-made reservoir in England — has been a national institution since it opened to fly fishing in 1977. Its depth, clarity, and consistent rainbow and brown trout stocking make it the benchmark against which other stillwater fisheries are measured. Grafham Water in Cambridgeshire, Draycote Water in Warwickshire, Bewl Water in Kent and East Sussex, and Chew Valley Lake in Somerset are all major venues that have produced numerous record fish and multiple national fly-fishing competitions. In Scotland, natural lochs like Loch Leven (famous for its wild brown trout) and Loch Fitty in Fife provide a different, wilder context. In Wales, Llyn Brenig on the Denbigh Moors and Llyn Brianne in Carmarthenshire offer upland reservoir fishing.

## Lines and Their Applications

The single greatest decision in stillwater fly fishing is which fly line to use. The choice determines at what depth your fly fishes and how quickly it gets there.

**Floating line (WF7F or DT7F)**: Essential for dry-fly fishing and for the buzzer-under-an-indicator technique. Also used with weighted nymphs fished on long fluorocarbon leaders of 20–27 feet, allowing the fly to sink to 1–3 m without a dedicated sinking line.

**Intermediate line** (sinking at roughly 2.5 cm per second): The most versatile of all stillwater lines, retrieving through the upper 1–2 m of the water column where trout feed most actively between April and October. Works with lures, buzzers on a slow figure-of-eight retrieve, and mini-lures.

**Slow-sinker (Di-3)**: Reaches depths of 2–4 m. Useful in windy conditions when a floating or intermediate would otherwise ride up in the wave action.

**Fast-sinker (Di-5 to Di-7)**: For deep fishing in early spring (when trout hug the thermocline at 4–7 m) and when pulling large lures at depth in competition fishing.

## Dry Fly on Stillwater

Dry-fly fishing on reservoirs is one of the great pleasures of the fly-fishing calendar, but it is weather-and-conditions-dependent. Calm, warm evenings from May through September trigger hatches of midges (chironomids), sedge flies, and caenis — the tiny white flies that produce the famous "caenis madness" hatch in late summer when trout rise so selectively they can be almost impossible to catch. The most reliable stillwater dry-fly patterns include the Shuttlecock Buzzer (for midge adults in the film), the Elk Hair Caddis (for sedge), the F Fly (for caenis and emergers), and the Klinkhåmer Special (for emerging sedge on gentle ripple). Cast to rising fish, placing the fly 60–90 cm ahead of the last rise. Leader length is critical — use 12–15 feet of copolymer or fluorocarbon tapering to 5X (0.15 mm) or finer for leader-shy fish.

## Buzzer Fishing: The Core Stillwater Technique

The buzzer (the angling term for the pupal stage of the chironomid midge) is the most important food item for trout in UK stillwaters. Chironomid pupae spend prolonged periods hanging vertically in the surface film as they struggle to break through — during this time trout feed on them with minimal effort, sipping them in what appears to the observer as casual rises. The "washing line" method is the classic boat technique: a floating fly line, a long leader of 18–27 feet, and three flies at intervals — the top dropper within centimetres of the surface, the middle dropper at 60 cm, and the point fly at 1.2–1.8 m. Flies — black, red, green, or claret buzzers — hang motionless in the drift. The indicator variant uses a foam or deer-hair indicator to suspend buzzers from bank or boat, allowing real-time depth adjustment.

## Boat Fishing: Drifting and the Rudder

Boat fishing on large reservoirs opens up vast stretches of water that shore anglers cannot reach. The traditional Scottish drift technique — two anglers fishing side-by-side as the boat drifts broadside to the wind — is the bedrock of UK competition boat fishing. The boat is controlled with a drogue (a fabric sea-anchor deployed on the downwind side) to slow the drift to a productive pace of 0.5–1 knot. Anglers cast downwind at 45 degrees, then retrieve as the boat drifts over the line. The drift allows each cast to cover new water and, critically, to work the flies up through the water column at the critical angle that triggers takes. Boils and swirls at the surface reveal feeding lanes; anchor the boat 40–50 m upwind of a consistent rise form to work a fixed area systematically.

## Lure Fishing from the Bank

When trout are not showing at the surface, large lure patterns fished on an intermediate or sinking line from the bank can locate fish rapidly. The Viva (black with green tail), the Cat's Whisker (white with yellow bead), and the Montana (black chenille) are three iconic UK stillwater lures with decades of productivity behind them. Cast as far as possible along productive margins — dam walls, inlet streams, weed beds, and submerged features — then retrieve on a varied pace: a fast strip to trigger reaction takes, or a slow figure-of-eight that barely moves the fly and sometimes proves irresistible to following fish. Lure fishing is most productive in low light — the first hour of daylight and the final hour before dark are typically the most explosive sessions of the day.

## Reading Stillwater: Wind and Temperature

Understanding how wind shapes a stillwater fishery is essential. Wind pushes surface-drifting food — hatching midges, spent spinners, and terrestrial insects — to the windward bank. That leeward margin of food accumulation is where trout will be found patrolling. In warm summer months, surface temperatures may reach 20–22 °C in the shallows, pushing trout into deeper, cooler water. Inlets — where cool, oxygenated water enters the reservoir — become hotspots, particularly in high summer. Thermocline depth can be estimated with a temperature probe or by reading where birds (particularly terns and gulls) are working the surface — they follow the insect hatches, which in turn follow the thermocline.
