Every serious angler reaches a point in their development where the basic clinch knot and the blood knot — serviceable as they are — simply are not enough. Big-game fishing demands connections that can absorb the shock load of a 100 kg billfish without slipping; fly fishing for large trout or salmon requires leader systems that turn over smoothly and present delicately; saltwater lure fishing pushes fluorocarbon and braid combinations to their limits at the point of connection. The knots covered in this article represent the upper tier of practical angling connections — each one solves a specific problem, and each one is worth the time invested in mastering it.

## The Bimini Twist: The Foundation of Big-Game Fishing

The Bimini twist is not just a knot — it is a system for creating a double length of line that, when tied correctly, retains virtually 100 percent of the original line's rated breaking strain. This doubled section then serves as the base onto which all other connections are made: the hollow-splice to a wind-on leader, the loop-to-loop for a conventional heavy leader, or the double Uni knot for a fluorocarbon section. To tie: form a loop of approximately 60 cm in your main line and twist it 20 times. Spread the turns evenly along the loop, then hold the twists under tension while a partner (or your knee) holds the standing part of the loop. Roll the two strands back toward the original twist while maintaining even pressure until the turns lock. A half-hitch on each leg, then a final three-turn whipping knot completes the Bimini. Practice until you can tie it in under 90 seconds — in a pitching boat with a hooked billfish, that is precisely the time you will not have.

## The PR Bobbin Knot: Braid to Fluorocarbon

For modern lure fishing, connecting braided main line to a fluorocarbon leader is one of the most critical connections in tackle. The FG knot has been the industry standard for several years, but the PR Bobbin knot (named for the bobbin tool used to wrap it) is arguably superior in smoothness and consistent 100 percent strength when tested. The PR knot wraps the fluorocarbon around the braid in alternating half-hitches using a winding bobbin, then finishes with a series of locking half-hitches and a whip finish. The resulting connection is very slim — it passes through rod guides with minimal resistance, unlike the bulkier Albright knot or double Uni. In practice, most serious lure anglers tie their braid-to-fluorocarbon connections at home on a dedicated bobbin and keep 3–4 pre-tied leaders of different strengths ready for quick field change.

## The FG Knot: Field Alternative for Braid to Fluorocarbon

When a bobbin is not available — which in field conditions is most of the time — the FG knot (Fine and Good, or Fast Good, depending on whom you ask) is the go-to braid-to-fluorocarbon connection. To tie: hook the fluorocarbon around a finger or toe to create tension. Wrap the braid over and under the fluorocarbon in alternating half-hitches for 20 wraps per side — a total of 20 alternating turns. Then lock with 5–6 half-hitches in the same direction, followed by 2 half-hitches in the opposite direction, then a 4-turn half-hitch to finish. The completed knot should be approximately 3–4 cm long and pass freely through guides. The FG knot tests at 95–100 percent of braid strength in independent laboratory tests and is the connection of choice for tournament bass fishing, jigging, and inshore saltwater fishing worldwide.

## Loop-to-Loop Connections for Fly Fishing Leaders

In fly fishing, the loop-to-loop system allows the angler to change leaders in seconds — a critical advantage when a dry-fly session demands a 14-foot tapered leader while a nymph session requires a short, stiff 7-foot leader for indicator work. There are two components to this system: the loop at the fly line tip, and the loop at the thick end of the leader.

**Fly line loop**: Modern fly lines come with factory-welded loops, but these can become worn or break. A whip-finish with a fine thread builds a small loop from a doubled section of 10 lb monofilament, which is then epoxied for permanence. Alternatively, a Perfection loop tied in a short length of 20 lb monofilament can be attached to the fly line via a needle knot for a near-seamless connection.

**Leader loop**: The Perfection loop is the standard loop at the butt of a tapered leader. Form two overlapping loops in the thick monofilament butt section, then pass the tag end through and pull tight. A correctly tied Perfection loop is in-line with the main strand — offset loops are a sign of an incorrectly tied knot that will fail under load.

## The Needle Knot and Tube Knot

Attaching a monofilament leader butt directly to the fly line — rather than using loop-to-loop — gives the cleanest possible turnover for dry-fly fishing on flat water where even a small hinge at the connection causes presentation problems. The needle knot uses a thin needle heated and pushed through the core of the fly line tip; the monofilament is threaded through the hole and wrapped around the outside of the line in a five-turn clinch, then pulled through to lock. The tube knot achieves a similar result without penetrating the fly line: the monofilament is wrapped five times around the line and a small tube (a pen tube or cocktail straw), the tag end is threaded back through the tube, the tube removed, and the knot pulled tight. Both knots create connections of less than 3 mm diameter that slide through rod tip guides easily.

## Polyleader and Shooting Head Connections

Polyleaders — braided or extruded density-compensated leaders sold in lengths of 5–14 feet — have transformed modern reservoir and sea-trout fly fishing. They attach to the fly line via loop-to-loop, and to the tippet via a loop or blood knot. The key challenge is that polyleaders are often used with fluorocarbon tippets, and tying fluorocarbon to the polyleader loop reliably requires an improved clinch or a Non-Slip Mono loop rather than a standard clinch, which tends to slip under the lateral forces of casting and fish pressure.

Shooting heads — heavy 10-metre heads used for Skagit and Scandi casting — connect to the running line via loop-to-loop. The critical detail is that both loops must be of compatible sizes: too large a loop causes the connection to hinge and slap against the rod on the shoot; too small a loop prevents the connection from sliding through the rod guides. A 2 cm loop on the head to a 2.5 cm loop on the running line is the ideal dimension.

## The Non-Slip Mono Loop and Rapala Knot

Hooks and lures that benefit from free movement — jig heads, topwater plugs, and soft-plastic rigs — fish better when tied on a loop knot rather than a fixed clinch. The Non-Slip Mono loop (also called the Kreh loop after fly-fishing legend Lefty Kreh) creates a fixed-diameter loop that does not cinch down on the lure eye, allowing full three-dimensional movement. Tie an overhand loop in the main line approximately 12 cm from the tag end. Pass the tag end through the lure eye and back through the overhand loop, then wrap around the standing line 5 times for 20 lb mono (3 times for 50 lb, 7 times for 8 lb) and back through the overhand loop. Moisten and pull tight. The Rapala knot achieves the same effect with slightly different geometry and is widely used in Scandinavian freshwater fishing for predator lures.
