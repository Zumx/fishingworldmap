Panama occupies a narrow land bridge between two oceans, and that geographic accident produces a fishing portfolio of remarkable diversity. On the Pacific side, Coiba Island — once a penal colony, now a UNESCO World Heritage Site — sits amid offshore grounds that produce black marlin, sailfish, and wahoo in numbers that rival any location in the Eastern Pacific. The Canal Zone has created Gatun Lake, an artificial reservoir that now hosts one of the most accessible peacock bass fisheries in Central America. The Pacific flats from the Azuero Peninsula north to the Gulf of Chiriquí hold roosterfish, snook, and jack crevalle that take lures just a few kilometres from the beach. And on the Caribbean side, remote rivers and jungle canals shelter tarpon, snook, and giant snook (robalo) in wild, largely unfished water. Panama is underrated; its fishing, distinctly not.

## Coiba Island — Black Marlin Territory

The Isla Coiba national park in the Gulf of Chiriquí on Panama's Pacific side is the fishing highlight of Central America for big game enthusiasts. The island was used as a prison colony for most of the twentieth century — effectively keeping its waters from being fished — and the result is offshore grounds that feel genuinely pristine. Black marlin up to 900 kg have been recorded on the Hannibal Bank, a seamount about 25 kilometres south-west of Coiba. Blue marlin, striped marlin, sailfish, and wahoo are also abundant. The Coiba Ridge and the surrounding seamounts concentrate baitfish and, with them, billfish, tuna, and dolphinfish throughout the year. The Antiguo Club de Coiba — the former penal colony's administration building — has been converted into a basic lodge and represents one of the most historically atmospheric fishing bases in the world.

## Gatun Lake — Peacock Bass in the Canal

Gatun Lake was created between 1907 and 1913 by damming the Chagres River to supply water for the Panama Canal. The result is a reservoir of approximately 425 square kilometres sitting in the middle of one of the world's most biodiverse regions. Peacock bass (Cichla ocellaris) were introduced in the 1960s, accidentally via the aquarium trade, and have thrived spectacularly. Fish averaging 1–2 kg are abundant; specimens over 4 kg are regularly caught. The lake is accessible from Gamboa on the Canal's Atlantic side, a village surrounded by Soberanía National Park, and from marinas near Colón. Fishing is done from small boats working the flooded forest edges, submerged stumps, and channel margins. The lake also holds massive tarpon — occasionally seen rolling in the deeper sections — that enter from the Caribbean via the Canal.

## Gulf of Chiriquí — Roosterfish and Snook

The Gulf of Chiriquí, enclosed by the Azuero Peninsula to the east and the border region with Costa Rica to the west, is an exceptional inshore fishery. Roosterfish patrol the rocky points and sandy beaches along the mainland and around the Pearl Islands chain, taking large poppers and jigs with characteristic explosive surface strikes. Snook gather in the river mouths and estuary channels that drain into the gulf, particularly in the Boca Brava area near Remedios. The rainy season from May to December concentrates snook at river mouths as baitfish flush seaward with the current. Jack crevalle, cubera snapper, and Pacific needlefish round out the inshore species list.

## Gulf of Panama — Pacific Flats and Bay Fishing

The Gulf of Panama, east of Panama City, offers extensive shallow-water fishing on sandy and muddy flats that extend for kilometres toward the Pearl Islands and the Darién Peninsula. At low tide, these flats expose enormous areas where corvina (Pacific weakfish) feed in large schools, accessible by wading or from a shallow-draft skiff. Corvina up to 5 kg on light spinning tackle or fly gear are a routine pleasure here. Deeper sections hold large cubera snapper and jack crevalle. The bay also receives runs of ladyfish and jack crevalle during the dry season from December to April when the trade winds push baitfish toward the coast.

## Caribbean Side — Tarpon and Jungle Snook

The Caribbean coast of Panama, particularly the Bocas del Toro Archipelago in the far west and the Comarca Guna Yala to the east, offers tarpon and snook fishing in jungle-fringed waterways that see almost no fishing pressure. Tarpon enter the river systems from the Caribbean and hold in the deeper pools and canal junctions. The Changuinola River near Almirante and the rivers of the Bocas del Toro mainland are productive. Small-boat access from local guides in the Bocas del Toro town is straightforward. The added attraction is the stunning scenery: clear Caribbean water, overhanging jungle, and the quiet that comes only from genuinely remote locations.

## Hannibal Bank — Offshore Seamount

The Hannibal Bank, roughly 25 kilometres south-west of Coiba, is one of the most productive offshore fishing locations in the Eastern Pacific. This flat-topped seamount rises from deep water to approximately 60 metres below the surface, creating an upwelling that draws baitfish, tuna, wahoo, and billfish from the surrounding deep water. Yellowfin tuna in the 50–150 kg range are regularly encountered on the bank, and the billfish action here — particularly black marlin — has established Hannibal's reputation among serious big-game anglers worldwide.

## Darién — Remote Jungle Rivers

The Darién Province in eastern Panama, one of the most biodiverse and least explored regions of the Americas, harbours rivers that flow from the Colombian border to the Pacific through pristine jungle. Snook, tarpon, and large river-dwelling catfish inhabit these waterways. Access requires either light aircraft or multi-day boat journeys, and fishing is strictly catch-and-release by sensible expeditioners. The Tuira River, the largest in Panama, and its tributaries like the Chucunaque offer large populations of snook that rarely see a lure.

## David and Río Chiriquí — Inshore and River Fishing

The city of David in Chiriquí Province is the hub for fishing both the Gulf of Chiriquí offshore grounds and the Río Chiriquí and its highland tributaries in the Barú Volcano area. Rainbow trout stocked in the cold mountain streams above Boquete offer alpine fishing in contrast to the tropical coast below. Day trips from David can cover both — morning trout fishing in the mountains and afternoon roosterfish on the gulf flats.

## Pearl Islands — Offshore and Inshore

The Archipiélago de las Perlas in the Gulf of Panama, 65 kilometres south of Panama City, has been a fishing destination since pre-Columbian times. Today it offers a full range of species from reef-associated snappers and groupers to offshore sailfish and marlin just a few hours' run south into open water. The island of Contadora has airport access and several small hotels with fishing connections. The reefs around Mogo Mogo Island — familiar from the television show Survivor — hold large cubera snapper and concentrations of cabrilla (spotted grouper) in the 3–8 kg range.

## Bocas del Toro — Caribbean Reef and Lagoon

The Bocas del Toro Archipelago on Panama's Caribbean north-west coast is most widely known as a backpacker destination, but its bonefish flats and snook-filled mangrove lagoons make it a serious light-tackle destination. The flats around Bastimentos Island hold bonefish in the 1–3 kg range and occasional permit. The mangrove channels on the interior of the main island shelter snook that average 2–4 kg and concentrate at the channel entrances on the falling tide. A few fly fishing guides based in Bocas town offer specialised light-tackle and fly fishing packages.
