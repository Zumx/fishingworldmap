There are perhaps 1,300 islands scattered across Lough Corrib, depending on who is counting and how high the water stands, and almost every one of them has a name, a story, and a trout-holding shoreline. Ireland's second-largest lake — over 176 square kilometres of limestone-filtered water stretching 60 kilometres from Galway city toward the mountains of Connemara and Mayo — is not merely a fishery. It is a culture: of clinker-built boats, of whispered hatch reports in Oughterard pubs, and of a fortnight in May when much of the west of Ireland quietly stops working because the mayfly is up.

## A limestone machine for growing trout

Corrib's secret is geology. The eastern and central basins lie over Carboniferous limestone, which buffers the water to a rich alkalinity and fuels enormous productivity: dense beds of shrimp and snail, caddis, olives, and the burrowing nymphs of Ephemera danica, the green drake mayfly. The western arm, by contrast, runs onto acid granite toward Connemara, giving the lake a split personality — fertile, shallow, island-studded water in the east and deeper, darker, wilder water in the west. The lake averages only about 9 metres deep but plunges past 40 metres in places, and that mix of vast shallows over marl and sudden depths is exactly the architecture that grows both free-rising brown trout and the predatory giants that hunt beneath them.

## The mayfly: Ireland's great angling festival

From roughly the second week of May into early June, the green drake hatches in numbers that have to be seen to be believed. Trout that have fed on the bottom all spring suddenly look up, and the lake's surface comes alive. The traditional methods all get their moment: wet-fly fishing with teams of three on floating lines, dapping live mayfly on long telescopic rods with blow lines — an art form essentially unique to the great Irish and Scottish loughs — and dry-fly fishing with spent gnat patterns in the calm of evening, when the biggest trout of the year quietly mop up dying spinners. Buzzers (chironomids) in April, lake olives early in the season, and the murrough and other sedges of summer evenings complete the calendar. An average Corrib trout runs around a pound and a half; fish of 2 to 4 pounds are common in the mayfly, and every season produces wild browns over 6 pounds to the dry fly.

## Ferox: the lake's apex predator

Beneath the olive-sipping browns lives another animal entirely. Corrib holds a genetically distinct population of ferox trout — long-lived, fish-eating browns that abandon insects for the lake's vast shoals of roach and perch and grow to extraordinary sizes. Specialist anglers troll deep-diving lures and dead baits on downriggers and leaded lines along the drop-offs of the western lake, marking fish on sonar at 15 to 25 metres. Ferox over 10 pounds are caught most seasons, and the lake has produced fish well into the high teens of pounds. It is slow, patient, weather-beaten fishing — hours of trolling for one savage take — but a 15-pound wild brown trout in a net is one of European freshwater angling's rarest prizes. Most specialist ferox anglers now release these old fish, some of which are 15 years old or more.

## Pike, perch and the unsung second fishery

Corrib is officially managed as a wild brown trout fishery, but its pike fishing is quietly world-class. The reed-fringed bays of the lower lake and the weedy shallows around Annaghdown and Headford produce pike to well over 20 pounds every year, with fish over 30 pounds documented, taken on deadbaits, big soft plastics and fly tackle. Perch swarm the marl shoals and provide brilliant sport for children from the piers at Oughterard and Cong. There is even a salmon run passing through the short, powerful Corrib River in Galway city, where the famous Galway Weir fishery puts anglers onto fresh Atlantic salmon within sight of shopfronts and traffic.

## Boats, drifts and how the lake is actually fished

This is big, open water that demands respect: squalls arrive off the Atlantic with little warning, and rocks lurk inches under the surface miles from shore. Almost all trout fishing is done from 19-foot lake boats, drifting broadside to the wind while two or three anglers cast wet flies down the breeze. Local knowledge is everything — which shallows hold fish in April, which islands shelter a drift in a northerly — and hiring a boatman-guide from Oughterard, Cong, Clonbur, Cornamona or Greenfields for at least the first days is the single best investment a visitor can make. The trout season runs from mid-February to the end of September. A few essentials: lifejackets are legally required, engines should be treated and boats cleaned to stop invasive species like zebra mussels and Lagarosiphon weed spreading further, and no licence is needed for brown trout, though a share certificate supports the lake's management.

## Villages of the lake: where to base yourself

Oughterard on the western shore styles itself the angling capital of Corrib, with tackle shops, guides and boat hire concentrated along its main street. Cong, on the isthmus between Corrib and Lough Mask, doubles its fishing pedigree with film fame as the setting of The Quiet Man, and Ashford Castle's grounds meet the lake there. Headford and Greenfields serve the fertile eastern shore, Clonbur and Cornamona the wilder northwest. Galway city, at the lake's outflow, makes a lively base for non-fishing partners, with Connemara National Park, Kylemore Abbey and the Aran Islands all within day-trip range — a genuine advantage Corrib holds over remoter trout destinations.

## A fishery facing its pressures

Corrib's wild trout have survived arterial drainage, nutrient enrichment and the arrival of invasive species, thanks largely to vigilant lobbying by angling federations and management by Inland Fisheries Ireland. Water quality remains the existential issue — agricultural runoff feeds algal blooms in warm summers — while roach competition and the spread of Lagarosiphon weed have changed parts of the ecosystem. The angling community responds with stream-enhancement work on the spawning rivers, notably the Cregg and the Owenriff, and with a strong voluntary catch-and-release ethic for larger trout. Visitors help by following biosecurity rules and supporting local guides whose livelihoods depend on the lake staying wild.
