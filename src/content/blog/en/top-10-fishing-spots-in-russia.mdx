Russia spans eleven time zones, and its fishing matches the scale: the world's biggest remaining Atlantic salmon runs on the Kola Peninsula, taimen — the largest salmonid on Earth — in Siberian wilderness rivers, and a Volga delta so fertile that one season there spoils an angler for ordinary water. Travel logistics can be demanding and access rules change, so always verify current entry and permit conditions before planning. With that said, here are the ten waters that define angling across the world's largest country.

## 1. The Ponoi, Kola Peninsula

The Ponoi drains the eastern Kola tundra and is statistically among the most productive Atlantic salmon rivers on the planet — camp records have logged thousands of fish to rods in a single season. The river's wide, even tempo suits swung flies perfectly, and fresh fish enter from May ice-out into October. This is helicopter-access lodge water with strict catch-and-release, and weeks are booked far in advance.

## 2. The Varzuga and the southern Kola rivers

The Varzuga system on the White Sea side of the peninsula trades the Ponoi's average size for sheer numbers: in good spring weeks, double-figure salmon days are normal rather than exceptional. The river runs through sand-and-pine country dotted with centuries-old Pomor villages, and its smaller neighbours — the Kitsa and Pana among them — offer wilder, cheaper alternatives to the famous beats.

## 3. The Volga delta, Astrakhan

Below Astrakhan, the Volga splinters into hundreds of channels reeded like a jungle before reaching the Caspian. This is Russia's most famous mixed fishery: wels catfish that regularly pass 50 kg, zander stacked in the deep yamas, asp boiling on the current seams, and legendary spring runs of vobla roach that fill every drying rack in the region. April-May and September-October are the classic windows; float camps and lodge boats work the channels from dawn.

## 4. The Akhtuba floodplain

Between the parallel Volga and Akhtuba channels lies a 450-kilometre braid of lakes, side arms and sandbars — the Volga-Akhtuba floodplain. The area around Kharabali is a magnet for Russian anglers chasing trophy catfish on clonking gear, big pike in the spring-flooded meadows, and carp in the warm lakes of late summer. It is more affordable than the delta lodges and reachable by road, which makes it the country's great everyman fishing holiday.

## 5. Kamchatka's wild rivers

The Kamchatka Peninsula is the Pacific mirror of Alaska: all six Pacific salmon species, char and grayling, and — the main prize for travelling fly anglers — leopard-spotted wild rainbow trout that smash mouse patterns skated across the surface. Rivers such as the Zhupanova and Bystraya are reached by helicopter from Petropavlovsk-Kamchatsky, and float trips of a week or more drift anglers through volcano country where brown bears outnumber people. July to September is the season.

## 6. Taimen rivers of Siberia

The Siberian taimen is the world's largest salmonid — fish over 1.5 m have been verified — and it survives in remote rivers from the Yenisei tributaries of Tuva to the Vitim basin in Transbaikal. Taimen fishing is a patience game: long floats, big streamers and surface mouse flies, with strict release of every fish, since taimen grow slowly and are protected in much of their range. September, when fish feed hard before winter, is the prime month.

## 7. Lake Baikal and its tributaries

The world's deepest lake — 1,642 m — is better known to scientists than anglers, but the fishing around it is superb: black and white Baikal grayling along the rocky shores, lenok and taimen in tributaries like the Barguzin, and excellent pike in the shallow sors (lagoon bays). Local culture revolves around the endemic omul, best experienced hot-smoked in Listvyanka rather than on a hook, as omul stocks are under protection.

## 8. Ladoga and Onega, the great northwestern lakes

Europe's two largest lakes sit east of St Petersburg and fish like inland seas. Ladoga's skerries hold big pike and shoals of perch among thousands of granite islands, while both lakes carry landlocked salmon and brown trout for trollers working the open water. Winter turns the bays into ice-fishing cities; in summer, the Svir River between the lakes adds zander and ide to the menu.

## 9. The Rybinsk Reservoir and the upper Volga

Three hours north of Moscow, the Rybinsk Reservoir — the Rybinsk Sea, 4,500 square kilometres of flooded forest and old riverbeds — is central Russia's serious predator water: zander and big perch over the drowned channels, pike in the weedy margins, and famous rough-weather drifting for trophy fish around Bryeitovo. The whole upper Volga chain, including the Uglich and Ivankovo reservoirs, gives Muscovites their weekend fishing.

## 10. The Moscow region's organised fisheries

Around the capital, dozens of managed waters and rybkhoz fish farms sell day tickets for stocked carp, sturgeon and trout — the long-established Senezh fishery at Solnechnogorsk is a well-known example, and Lake Senezh itself is a beloved ice-fishing venue each winter. Purists may prefer the wild Oka River south of the city, but these pay waters are where urban Russia actually fishes after work, and a visiting angler learns more about local culture here than anywhere else.

## Seasons, permits and practicalities

Most Russian fishing on public water is free for hand-tackle, with regional rules on closed seasons, protected species (taimen, sturgeon and Atlantic salmon among them) and bag limits; salmon rivers and managed fisheries sell their own licences. The fishing year is sharply seasonal: open-water fishing peaks May-June and September-October, Kamchatka and the Kola run June-September, and from December to March the entire country switches to ice fishing. Remote trips depend on helicopters and outfitters, so book through established operators and confirm travel requirements early.
