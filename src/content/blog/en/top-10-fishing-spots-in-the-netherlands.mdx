No country in Europe has more water per square kilometre than the Netherlands: roughly a fifth of the nation is wet, stitched together by polder ditches, shipping rivers and the drowned geometry of land reclamation. The Dutch turned this into one of the world's most organised fisheries — a single VISpas membership opens tens of thousands of kilometres of bank, and predator fishing has become a national art form. Live baitfish are banned, lure fishing rules, and a metre-long pike from a ditch barely wider than your rod is a genuine possibility. These are the ten waters that show the Netherlands at its best.

## 1. Amsterdam's canals — urban zander

Amsterdam's ring of roughly 165 canals is Europe's most famous street-fishing arena. Zander hold against quay walls, bridge shadows and houseboat moorings, and locals work drop-shot rigs and small shads vertically along the brick. Winter is prime, when baitfish pack into the deeper grachten; a 70 cm zander between bicycles and canal cruisers is an entirely normal afternoon. Perch to 50 cm hammer creature baits around the houseboats in autumn. A VISpas plus the Amsterdam club's water list covers it all — and night fishing needs its own permit.

## 2. The Frisian and North Holland polders — pike on the fly

The polder grid of Friesland and Noord-Holland is the spiritual home of Dutch fly fishing for pike. From October to March, when the ditches run clear, anglers walk the dykes with 8-weights and bunny streamers, sight-casting to fish lying like logs over bare clay. Most pike run 60–80 cm, but every polder season produces metre fish from ditches barely three metres wide. It is walking-pace fishing — a thousand casts, a windmill on the horizon, and explosive takes in half a metre of water.

## 3. The Randmeren — giants in the chara weed

The chain of border lakes left between the old land and Flevoland's polders — Wolderwijd, Veluwemeer, Gooimeer, Eemmeer — has become the country's premier trophy pike water. Zebra mussels filtered the lakes clear, chara weed carpeted the bottom, and pike grew long and fat on abundant roach; fish over 110 cm come out every winter. Most fishing is done from boats with big softbaits and jerkbaits over 1.5–3 m of water. Harderwijk and Zeewolde are convenient bases, with rental boats and guides specialised in exactly this game.

## 4–5. Hollands Diep, Haringvliet and the Biesbosch

Where the Rhine and Maas braid toward the sea, the broad tidal basins of Hollands Diep and Haringvliet hold the country's heavyweight division: zander in huge numbers along the shipping channel edges, asp chasing bleak against rip-rap, and a booming population of wels catfish — fish over two metres are landed yearly. Next door, the maze of creeks in De Biesbosch National Park, one of Europe's last freshwater tidal wildernesses, offers pike and perch among flooded willow forests, ospreys and beavers included. Vertical jigging from a drifting boat is the local method on the deep water.

## 6. The IJsselmeer and Markermeer dams

The enclosed former Zuiderzee is now a 1,100-square-kilometre freshwater sea, and its basalt dams and harbour moles are perch and zander factories. The rocky stretches along the Houtribdijk between Lelystad and Enkhuizen, and the harbours of Urk and Stavoren, produce 40-plus-centimetre perch on dropshot and ned rigs, with zander stacking off the stones in autumn. Wind decides everything here: an onshore blow colours the water and kills the bite, so locals check the forecast before the alarm clock.

## 7. The big rivers — Waal, Lek and Maas

The shipping rivers are the wild card of Dutch angling. Groyne fields along the Waal — Europe's busiest waterway — hold asp that smash surface lures in the boils behind the stone fingers, plus barbel and ide on feeder tackle in the current. The Maas adds deep dredged stretches full of zander and increasingly large catfish. Fishing between passing barges takes nerve and sturdy tackle, but a 70 cm asp from the white water off a groyne tip is one of the great adrenaline moments of lowland Europe.

## 8. Zeeland and the Oosterschelde

The Netherlands turns salty in Zeeland, where the Oosterschelde — a national park of tidal channels up to 45 m deep — offers sea bass over oyster beds, flounder and whiting from the dams, and superb autumn squid fishing under the lights of the Zeelandbrug. The artificial island of Neeltje Jans, part of the Delta Works storm barrier, is a renowned shore mark for bass and garfish. Charter boats from Colijnsplaat run wreck trips for cod and ling further out in the North Sea.

## 9–10. The forelvijver circuit and the Vinkeveen lakes

Two stillwater experiences round out the list. The Dutch put-and-take trout pond — the forelvijver, found from Cothen in Utrecht to dozens of sites in Drenthe and Limburg — is an institution: stocked rainbows, family-friendly jetties, rental rods and a smoker for your catch. Entirely different are the Vinkeveense Plassen south-east of Amsterdam, flooded peat diggings up to 40 m deep with startling clarity, where freedivers and anglers alike pursue perch approaching the half-metre mark and pike that shadow the legendary sand flats. A day ticket covers the islands and the fishing.
