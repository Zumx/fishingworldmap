Nigeria is not the first country most anglers put on their bucket list, yet the fishing is sensational. The Niger River — the third longest in Africa at 4,180 km — drains a catchment the size of Western Europe before spreading into a 70,000 square kilometre delta on the Bight of Benin. Tigerfish (Hydrocynus vittatus) that average 3 to 5 kg patrol the fast rocky runs of the middle Niger near Baro and Lokoja. In the vast Kainji Reservoir, Nile perch reaching 80 kg cruise the submerged ghost-forest of drowned trees. Downstream, the mangrove-fringed tidal creeks of the Niger Delta — among the largest delta systems on earth — teem with tarpon, Atlantic jack, and snook-like species. The Gulf of Guinea beyond the bar offers barracuda, king mackerel, and cobia in quantities that would fill a logbook quickly.

## Kainji Reservoir: Giant Nile Perch

Kainji Lake, formed when the Niger was dammed in 1968, stretches 135 km long and 24 km wide in Kebbi and Niger States. It is Nigeria's premier freshwater fishery and holds Nile perch (Lates niloticus) to at least 80 kg; commercial netters regularly land fish above 40 kg and sport anglers have boated specimens over 60 kg using large swimbaits fished deep along the submerged channels. The dam's turbine outflow creates a constantly aerated tailwater where tiger fish, Nile tilapia, and large African catfish (Clarias gariepinus) concentrate. Fish between October and March, when the receding flood concentrates fish in the main reservoir basin.

## The Middle Niger near Baro

The town of Baro in Niger State sits on a rocky section of the Niger where the river is channelled between laterite outcrops into swift glides and deep pools. This is classic tigerfish water: fish them with fast-retrieved metal spoons in the 20-40 g range or hard jerkbaits worked through the current seam. African tigerfish here average 2–4 kg with individuals to 8 kg documented. The dry season from December to May keeps the river clear and comparatively low, making it the prime period for lure fishing. The confluence of the Niger and Kaduna River near Mureji, some 40 km north, produces additional holding spots.

## Lokoja and the Niger-Benue Confluence

Lokoja, Nigeria's first colonial capital, stands at the precise point where the Benue River meets the Niger. The mixing of two massive river systems creates a rich, complex current seam that holds enormous concentrations of predatory fish. Vundu catfish (Heterobranchus longifilis) reaching 50 kg have been taken on large cut-bait presentations, and African moonfish (Citharinus citharus) form dense schools in the slack water behind the confluence bar. October and November, when both rivers are running high and beginning to drop, represent the peak period.

## The Niger Delta's Tidal Creeks

The Niger Delta is one of the world's most complex estuarine systems: 14 main distributaries, 400 smaller channels, and thousands of square kilometres of mangrove swamp between Port Harcourt and Warri. Atlantic tarpon (Megalops atlanticus) averaging 20–40 kg stack in the tidal creeks year-round, with the peak rolling season from March to June. These fish are accessible on fly with 10-weight rods and large baitfish patterns, or on conventional tackle with lipped lures. The creeks around Buguma and Brass, where salt and fresh water mingle, are particularly productive. Night fishing the tidal surges near inlet mouths also produces spectacular snapper and barracuda action.

## Lagos Lagoon and Lekki Creek

Lagos Lagoon, attached to the ocean via a narrow inlet guarded by the famous Lagos Bar, covers 208 square km and receives the inflow of several rivers including the Ogun. Its brackish waters and extensive papyrus margins host enormous populations of snook-like fish, tilapia, and juvenile tarpon during wet season flooding. The Lekki Conservation Centre waterway to the east provides a relatively undisturbed fishery accessible by canoe. Lagos anglers also target the open bight outside the bar, where Spanish mackerel schools run close to shore from November through February.

## Cross River and Calabar

The Cross River in southeastern Nigeria drains a catchment that includes the biodiverse Obudu Plateau and the Korup Forest. By the time it reaches Calabar, it is a wide, slow-moving tropical river with impressive populations of Nile perch in the tidal reaches and large catfish above the tidal limit. The Cross River National Park, established to protect the region's forests, indirectly protects the river's fish populations: netting pressure here is lower than in most Nigerian rivers. Fishing the pool below Ikom is particularly productive for large African catfish and labeo (the African carp family) during the dry season.

## Lekki Offshore: Wahoo and Barracuda

The continental shelf off Lagos breaks sharply around 50 km offshore, and this edge — especially in the section between the 50-metre and 150-metre depth contours — holds excellent concentrations of wahoo (Acanthocybium solandri) from October to February. Trolling large wahoo lures and konahead rigged with flying fish produces fish averaging 12–18 kg, with individuals to 35 kg not uncommon. King mackerel and African bonito school in the mid-depth zone above the shelf. December and January see the combined influence of harmattan winds and cold upwelling pushing pelagics close to the surface.

## River Anambra and Flooded Floodplain

The Anambra River, a major left-bank tributary of the Niger draining Anambra and Kogi States, floods a vast clay floodplain between July and October. When the waters begin receding in November, they leave behind hundreds of temporary pools and oxbows choked with tilapia, African carp, and Synodontis catfish. Local fishers take thousands of tonnes from these seasonal pools. Sport anglers willing to walk the drying floodplain in December can wade-fish for large tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) in crystal-clear receding pools — an unusual and intensely satisfying experience.

## Oguta Lake, Imo State

Oguta Lake is a natural freshwater lake in Imo State connected to the Njaba River and, via a narrow neck, to the oil-stained Orashi River. The lake's 8 square km of open water and reed margins hold tiger fish, large Nile tilapia, and catfish. Local boat-fishing guides operate from Oguta town, and the area's relative isolation — compared to the heavily fished Niger and its major tributaries — means fish densities are comparatively high. The best fishing is from February through April.

## Gulf of Guinea Offshore: Cobia and Atlantic Sailfish

Beyond the 30-metre depth contour off Warri, Port Harcourt, and Calabar, cobia (Rachycentron canadum) are remarkably common by West African standards — fish of 15 to 35 kg are encountered on virtually every offshore trip between November and March. Atlantic sailfish appear along the outer shelf from December to February, and a patient angler trolling teasers can raise multiple fish in a day. Charter operations out of Port Harcourt's creek system, though small by international standards, are the most developed in the country.
