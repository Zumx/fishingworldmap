The Deep South is fishing country in the truest sense. It is a region where bass rods stand against the back doors of diners, where tackle shops outnumber hardware stores in towns along the Alabama-Georgia line, and where the knowledge of local water — which hollow holds spawning crappie in March, which tidal pass flushes with redfish on a falling tide — passes from generation to generation as naturally as a recipe. The fishing here is not just recreation; it is social fabric. And the water is extraordinary: the vast oxbow lakes of the Mississippi Delta, the cypress-lined impoundments of Alabama and Georgia, the lush coastal marshes of Louisiana, and the backwater bays of the Florida Panhandle combine to create one of the richest freshwater and inshore saltwater fisheries in North America.

## The Mississippi Delta Oxbows

The floodplain of the Mississippi between Vicksburg and Greenville is scattered with hundreds of oxbow lakes — crescent-shaped remnants of old river meanders that were cut off as the main channel shifted. Lakes like Deer Creek, Moon Lake, Lake Lee, and Lake Washington hold extraordinary populations of largemouth bass and crappie, but they are also known for outsized catfish, with blue catfish exceeding 20 kg not uncommon in the deeper channels. The water is dark and tannic, rich in structure from fallen timber and submerged vegetation, and the bass fishing peaks in spring when water temperatures climb above 16°C and fish move onto shallow flats to spawn. A float-tube or kayak is ideal for the narrow, tree-canopied passages between oxbow lakes.

## Weiss Lake, Alabama

Weiss Lake on the Coosa River near Centre, Alabama, has earned the nickname the Crappie Capital of the World, and the title holds up under scrutiny. The lake's 11,800 ha of surface water is characterized by extensive brush piles, submerged timber, and dock structures that hold crappie in numbers that astonish first-time visitors. Fish routinely exceed 400 g; catches over 700 g are common. The best fishing is in late February through April when crappie move into the brush in 0.5–2 m of water to spawn. Local guides use long poles to dip tiny jigs — 1/32 oz heads tipped with a 2.5 cm curly-tail in chartreuse or white — directly into the structure. Weiss also offers strong largemouth bass fishing along its rocky points and creek channel intersections.

## Lake Eufaula, Alabama-Georgia

Straddling the Alabama-Georgia state line on the Chattahoochee River, Lake Eufaula (also known as Walter F. George Reservoir) is one of the finest bass lakes in the Southeast. Its 18,000 ha include a mix of natural and artificial structure — bridge pilings, flooded timber, rock ledges, and hydrilla beds — that supports diverse largemouth bass populations through multiple seasons. Spring spawning flats along the upper Chattahoochee arm produce aggressive fish; summer finesse fishing on rocky ledges in 5–8 m depth requires a drop-shot rig or Carolina rig with straight-tail worm. The lake record stands above 5 kg, and tournament events are held here throughout the year.

## Louisiana Coastal Marshes — Redfish

Louisiana holds the largest coastal marshland complex in the contiguous United States: more than 4 million ha of brackish and saltwater marsh, tidal lakes, cheniers, and barrier islands. The red drum (redfish) thrives in this environment in extraordinary numbers — the state of Louisiana accounts for nearly 40 percent of all red drum caught in the US in a typical year. Grand Isle, the Delacroix marshes south of New Orleans, and the Lake Pontchartrain shoreline are classic venues. Wading the marsh edges on a rising tide, sight-casting to tailing reds in grass, or working a gold spoon through an inland marsh pond — all produce fish that typically run 2–5 kg, with "bull reds" over 10 kg taken from the surf of Grand Isle each autumn.

## Pickwick Lake, Tennessee-Alabama-Mississippi

Pickwick Lake occupies a 57 km stretch of the Tennessee River where it passes through the corner of three states. It is famous for its smallmouth bass, which use the rocky ledges and current seams of the river channel in a way that is unusual in the Deep South — most regional fisheries are largemouth-dominated. Smallmouth over 2 kg are common; fish above 2.5 kg are taken regularly. Spring is the classic time, when males guard nests in 1–3 m of clear water over gravel and rock. The Tennessee River's current keeps water oxygenated and clear, contrasting with the stained impoundments that dominate most of the region. Pickwick also produces outstanding spotted bass and striped bass.

## Toledo Bend Reservoir, Texas-Louisiana

Though part of Toledo Bend is in Texas, the Louisiana side is equally impressive. At 72,000 ha, Toledo Bend is the largest man-made lake in the South and holds one of the nation's most productive largemouth bass populations. The lake's standing timber, flooded creek channels, and abundant hydrilla create a structural complexity that produces big bass year-round. The slot-size regulation (fish between 35–45 cm must be released) has helped maintain the quality of the fishery. Toledo Bend has also produced significant numbers of largemouth above 5 kg; the lake record exceeds 7 kg. Sight-fishing for spawning bass in the timber-strewn coves in March and April is the lake's signature experience.

## Santee Cooper Lakes, South Carolina

The Santee Cooper system — Lake Marion and Lake Moultrie, connected by the Santee River diversion — is the largest lake system in South Carolina, totaling about 44,500 ha. It is best known for its striped bass fishery: the lakes hold a landlocked striped bass population that is self-sustaining without stocking, a biological rarity. Stripers over 10 kg are common; the lake record is above 25 kg. The fish concentrate in the spillway area between the two lakes and in the deeper channels in summer when surface temperatures exceed 26°C. Channel catfish over 10 kg are taken regularly, and the crappie fishing in the flooded timber is outstanding in spring.

## Mobile Bay and Mobile Delta

Mobile Bay, at the mouth of the Alabama River system, is one of the most productive estuaries on the Gulf Coast. It holds speckled trout, redfish, flounder, Spanish mackerel, and blue crabs in abundance. The Mobile Delta — the labyrinthine river delta that feeds the bay through the Mobile, Tensaw, and Spanish Rivers — is an extraordinary freshwater fishery for largemouth bass, crappie, and catfish in a setting of ancient Spanish moss-draped tupelo swamp. The Point Clear shoreline and Dauphin Island fishing piers are classic saltwater destinations, while the inland delta offers kayak-accessible bass fishing in some of the most beautiful water in Alabama.
