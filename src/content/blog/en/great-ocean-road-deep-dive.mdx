The Great Ocean Road corridor, stretching roughly 250 kilometres from Torquay to Allansford on Victoria's southwest coast, is one of Australia's most photographed coastlines. But behind the tourist buses and the lookouts above the Twelve Apostles, there is a serious fishing destination that rewards anglers willing to read the Southern Ocean's moods carefully. The combination of exposed rock platforms, sheltered river mouths, offshore seamounts, and the nutrient-rich waters of the Bonney Upwelling makes this a region where species diversity is genuinely impressive, and where big fish are caught from the shore.

## Australian Salmon: The Rock Ledge Specialist

Australian salmon — Arripis trutta, not related to Atlantic salmon — is the species most associated with Victoria's southern coast, and the Great Ocean Road is prime territory. These hard-fighting schooling fish average 1 to 3 kilograms but run larger in autumn, and they are found all along the exposed beaches and rock platforms from Jan Juc to Princetown. Aikenhead Point, south of Otway National Park, is a well-known mark where rock platforms extend into the water and the current creates reliable salmon holding ground. Surf casting with metal slugs and ganged hooks baited with pilchard produces consistent results on incoming tides. The fish are not considered table quality by most anglers, but their fight on light to medium surf gear is exceptional.

## Snapper on the Reefs

Offshore snapper fishing around the Port Campbell area and from Apollo Bay focuses on reef systems at 20 to 60 metres. Victorian snapper — Chrysophrys auratus — can exceed 10 kilograms, and the fish taken from the deepwater reef systems off the Great Ocean Road coast include some of the largest shore-accessible specimens in southern Australia. Boat fishing from Apollo Bay allows access to reefs not reachable from shore, and jigging with soft plastics in pink or natural colours works well in spring when snapper move into shallower water. Lake Nobelius near Lorne is a freshwater option for anglers seeking a change of pace, offering trout fishing in a scenic forested setting.

## Kingfish and Offshore Jigging

Yellowtail kingfish — one of Australia's premier light tackle offshore targets — are present along the Great Ocean Road coast in summer and autumn. Fish in the 5 to 15-kilogram range school around kelp edges and reef pinnacles, and vertical jigging with metal jigs in the 60 to 150-gram range produces electric strikes. The seamount systems further offshore, accessible by longer charter runs from Apollo Bay, hold larger kingfish. Live bait techniques using yellowtail scad (yakkas) jigged up from the reef and freestyled down on unweighted hooks are devastatingly effective when kingfish are active near the surface.

## Rock Fishing Safety and Technique

Rock fishing from the elevated platforms along the Great Ocean Road demands respect. The Southern Ocean produces powerful swells generated across thousands of kilometres of open water, and rogue sets regularly wash anglers from platforms that appear dry and safe. Life jackets — now mandatory in Victoria for rock fishing from certain defined locations — and footwear with felt or studded soles are minimum equipment. Julieannes Rock and Yellow Rock are marks that experienced local anglers fish on calmer days, using their knowledge of swell windows and tide heights. Newcomers should fish with experienced companions and never turn their back to the ocean.

## Beach Fishing: Surf Zones and River Mouths

The long surf beaches between Anglesea and Lorne, and again between Port Campbell and Peterborough, offer productive beach fishing for salmon, Australian herring, and in summer, good numbers of gummy shark. Gummy shark — Mustelus antarcticus — is a highly regarded table fish in Victoria, and the sandy beach gutters are prime territory. Squid-baited running sinker rigs fished after dark in the beach gutters produce the best results. River mouths, particularly the Erskine River at Lorne and the Gellibrand River near Princetown, concentrate bream and estuary perch in their tidal reaches. The Windermere Pontoon and Jetty at Lake Windermere offers a sheltered family fishing option close to the coast.

## Trout Fishing Inland: The Otways

The Otway Ranges immediately behind the coast hold a network of small to medium streams stocked with rainbow and brown trout. Barwon River headwaters, the Wye River, and the upper reaches of the Gellibrand hold wild brown trout that are notoriously selective. Fly fishing with small dry flies — elk hair caddis in size 14 to 16, Royal Wulff patterns, and parachute adams — produces fish in the 250 to 600 gram range on most visits. The streams are narrow and heavily canopied, requiring short roll casts and careful wading. Emerald Lake in the Otways is a popular trout destination with a more accessible fishing environment, as is Lake Wallace further inland near Wickliffe, which has an accessible fishing pontoon installed for anglers with mobility needs.

## Permits and Regulations

A Victorian Recreational Fishing Licence is required for anyone 18 and older fishing Victorian waters, including freshwater streams and the marine environment. Minimum sizes for snapper, Australian salmon, and most other species are set by the Victorian Fisheries Authority and are subject to annual review. The reef systems around the Twelve Apostles Marine National Park are closed to all fishing, and this no-take zone should be checked on current maps before launching any offshore trip. Bag limits for kingfish are set at five fish per person per day in Victorian waters.
