Rock fishing kills. In Australia alone, where the sport is enormously popular along the rugged Pacific coast from New South Wales through Queensland, it accounts for more drowning deaths annually than any other single recreational activity. In the United Kingdom, Italy, Japan, and South Africa, the statistics tell a similar story. The combination of beautiful, productive fishing locations and unpredictable, powerful waves creates a risk profile that demands the same respect afforded to mountaineering or open-water diving — activities that no sane person would attempt without planning, equipment, and training.

None of this means you should not fish from rocks. Coastal rock marks produce extraordinary fish — large bass and sea bream from the Atlantic headlands of Cornwall and Brittany, yellowtail kingfish from the cliff ledges of South Australia, groper and snapper from the granite shelves of the Cape Peninsula — and for many anglers the combination of spectacular scenery and challenging quarry makes it the most rewarding form of fishing available. But competence and preparation are not optional extras. They are what separate the angler who fishes the rocks for decades from the one who does not come home.

## Understanding Wave Behaviour

The most lethal misconception in rock fishing is the belief that because the sea looks calm right now, it will stay calm. Swells generated by storms thousands of kilometres away can arrive at a coast with no local wind warning whatsoever. A perfectly calm morning can produce a single wave — or a series of three or four waves arriving within minutes of each other — that sweeps a ledge clean without any prior indication.

These rogue sets are particularly dangerous on the west-facing coasts of Ireland, southern England, Portugal, and New Zealand's South Island, where Atlantic and Southern Ocean swells arrive with enormous energy even in apparently settled conditions. The practical rule is to watch any rock platform for at least twenty minutes before setting foot on it. Count wave sets, identify the highest wash line on the rocks (the darkest wet mark above the current wash), and never stand below that line.

## Footwear: The Foundation of Rock Safety

The single most impactful safety decision a rock angler makes is footwear. Smooth-soled shoes, sandals, and ordinary trainers are genuinely dangerous on wet rock, weed-covered ledges, and the barnacle-crusted platforms that characterise productive marks. Felt-soled wading boots, rubber-soled dive boots, or purpose-built rock fishing boots with tungsten-spiked soles provide dramatically better grip on the slick surfaces that cause most rock fishing falls.

In Japan, where rock fishing (iso-fishing) has a dedicated and safety-conscious following, studded rock fishing boots are considered as fundamental as a rod. The Japanese Standards Association certifies rock fishing footwear under specific grip-performance standards. In Australia, the NSW government now recommends that all rock fishers wear a life jacket; several high-profile rescues and fatalities have driven a campaign that has demonstrably improved survival rates.

## Life Jackets: Non-Negotiable on Exposed Marks

If you fish exposed headlands or low-lying ledges where the sea can reach you — and most productive rock marks involve both — a self-inflating life jacket (Type 150 or Type 275) is non-negotiable. Modern auto-inflating personal flotation devices are compact, comfortable, and barely noticeable to wear; the argument that they are cumbersome has not been valid for at least fifteen years.

In New South Wales, wearing a life jacket is mandatory on certain classified rock fishing locations following a series of fatalities, and this legislation has been effective. In Ireland, UK, and New Zealand, life jackets are strongly recommended by coast guard agencies and are mandatory for junior anglers at any organised rock event. The physics is simple: if a wave sweeps you in, an inflated life jacket keeps your airway above water while you recover or wait for help. Without one, the combination of shock, cold water, and heavy clothing makes self-rescue extremely difficult.

## Tide and Swell: Research Before You Go

Planning a rock session without checking the tide tables and swell forecast is equivalent to driving without checking the weather. The NSW Rock Fishing Safety website, Surf Life Saving Australia, and Windy.com all provide swell height, period, and direction forecasts that translate directly to rock fishing safety. A 1.5-metre swell with a 12-second period from the southwest will produce very different conditions from a 1.5-metre swell with an 8-second period from the northwest.

High tide combined with incoming swell is the most dangerous combination at most marks. The same ledge that sits four metres above the water at low tide may be awash at high tide, and waves arriving during a peak tide surge across it with little warning. If in doubt, do not fish it. There is always another session.

## The Buddy System and Communication

Never fish exposed rock marks alone. The statistics are stark: solo anglers who are swept in have almost no chance of rescue unless a witness is present. A fishing partner who is watching for waves, positioned above the immediate fishing zone, and equipped with a throw rope or a charged mobile phone can make the difference between a recovered angler and a fatality.

Before any rock session, inform someone not present of your location, the planned end time, and the nearest car park or access point. Coast guards in the UK (via 999 or VHF Channel 16), in Australia (via 000 or VHF 16), and most maritime nations take rock fishing distress calls extremely seriously. A waterproof case or dry bag for your phone ensures it functions even after an unexpected wetting.

## Reading Your Exit

Before you begin fishing, identify two exit routes from your position. Rocks look very different from the water than from above; what seemed like an obvious ladder-climb from the shore may be invisible from sea level in a surge. In several documented Australian rock fishing fatalities, recovered anglers had been unable to find a way back onto the rock face because they had not reconnoitred their exit in advance.

An emergency throw rope — a simple 15-metre buoyant line in a throw bag — stowed on the highest accessible point of your mark is cheap insurance that has saved lives.
