There is a particular kind of disorientation that strikes the first-time boat angler about forty minutes offshore. The coastline has shrunk to a thin smudge; the water around is no longer blue or green but a deep, moving grey; and the boat, which seemed substantial at the dock, now pitches and rolls in a way that makes standing feel precarious. This is normal. Every experienced skipper and sea angler went through it. The skills of boat fishing — reading a chart, choosing an anchorage, working a drift, preventing seasickness, and respecting the unwritten codes of behaviour afloat — are learnable, and the rewards of mastering them are enormous.

## Understanding Sea Safety Basics

Safety on the water is non-negotiable and must be understood before any rod is rigged. Every person aboard should wear or have immediate access to a properly fitted lifejacket or personal flotation device rated to at least 150 Newtons for open water use. A VHF marine radio on Channel 16 should be operational and switched on at all times; this is the international distress and calling channel monitored by the Maritime and Coastguard Agency in the UK and equivalent bodies worldwide. File a float plan — tell someone ashore where you are going, with how many people, and what time you expect to return.

Check the weather forecast from a reputable marine source — the UK Met Office Inshore Waters Forecast, NOAA weather radio in the United States, or Météo-France marine bulletins — and do not go afloat if wind forecast exceeds Force 4 on the Beaufort scale (approximately twenty-five kilometres per hour) until you are experienced. Force 5 and above generates wave heights that are hazardous for small boats. Look at the direction of the wind relative to the tide: wind against tide creates steep, short-period waves far more dangerous than wind-with-tide in the same wind strength.

## Boat Etiquette and Unwritten Rules

Most experienced anglers will help a newcomer gladly, but certain protocols exist that the beginner must learn quickly. Never anchor upwind or upcurrent of another anchored boat without leaving at least thirty metres of clear water — your anchor rope swings your vessel into their swim, potentially fouling rigs and lines. When approaching a charter boat already anchored, slow well before you reach it, approach from downwind or downtide, and don't moor within the fishable arc of their anglers.

On a shared charter or day boat, keep your rigs stowed when moving between marks. Swinging trebles and large hooks are a serious hazard at close quarters. Take turns placing your rig in the water: when bottom fishing in a group, allow the skipper to signal when lines can be released, and count in sequence to avoid tangles at the stern.

Never gut fish on the boat without asking the skipper first. Some vessels prohibit this; others have a designated gutting station. Dispose of bycatch, offcuts, and waste in a bucket, not over the side into a neighbour's swim.

## Anchor Positioning and Holding Ground

Anchoring correctly is one of the most important boat-handling skills. The anchor must be dropped at the correct distance upcurrent of the target mark so that at full scope — typically three to five times the water depth in the anchor rope — the boat sits directly over the fish. If the current is running at four knots and the water is eighteen metres deep, the anchor will need to be deployed at a distance of roughly sixty to ninety metres upcurrent of the intended position. Use a fishfinder or echo sounder to confirm the bottom is suitable: a sand or gravel bottom holds an anchor; rock may not, and weed will likely cause the anchor to drag.

Once anchored, assess the swing of the boat regularly. Wind and tide direction can change, rotating the boat on its anchor point and shifting the stern away from the intended position. A second anchor deployed from the stern prevents this rotation if you need to hold the boat precisely over a wreck or a tight reef.

## Drifting Techniques

Anchoring is not always the right approach. For targeting fish over large areas of featureless sand or in water too deep for a practical anchor scope, drifting is more productive. Allow the tide or the wind — whichever is the dominant force — to carry the boat across the mark, lowering lures or baited traces to the bottom and feeling for takes as you pass over fish-holding ground. A drift sock deployed from the bow slows the speed of drift when wind is faster than tide; this prevents lures from planing off the bottom at too shallow an angle.

Mark the position with a GPS waypoint each time a fish is caught during a drift, then return upcurrent and re-drift the same line. Fish on a sandy bottom over a gravel scour or a tidal eddy tend to hold station; repeated drifts over the same line will keep producing.

## Seasickness Prevention and Management

Seasickness is caused by the conflict between what the inner ear senses (movement) and what the eyes perceive (a stationary boat). Most cases can be managed or prevented. The most consistently effective over-the-counter remedy is cinnarizine (Stugeron), taken one hour before departure and every eight hours thereafter; it causes mild drowsiness in some individuals. Scopolamine patches applied behind the ear twelve hours before departure provide excellent protection for twenty-four to seventy-two hours. Acupressure wristbands (Sea-Bands) are genuinely effective for mild conditions.

On the boat, stay at the stern in the freshest air and keep your eyes on a fixed point on the horizon. Avoid going below, reading, or looking at a phone screen. Eat a light, bland meal before departure — an empty stomach worsens the sensation — and drink water steadily throughout the day. If someone in the group becomes severely sick, head for calmer water and anchor; attempting to fish through severe motion sickness is ineffective and potentially dangerous.

## Essential Gear for the First Trip

The first-time boat angler needs less than they think. A medium-weight rod of seven feet in a ten- to thirty-gram casting weight, matched to a 3000- to 4000-series spinning reel with ten pounds of braid as the main line, covers most inshore boat fishing situations. Add a selection of jig heads of ten to thirty grams, soft plastic shads of seven to twelve centimetres, a range of clip-top swivels, and a small assortment of hooks from size 2 to 2/0. A gaff or landing net, a set of pliers, sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, waterproof jacket and trousers, and enough water for the day round out the essentials.
