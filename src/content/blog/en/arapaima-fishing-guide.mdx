In the oxbow lakes and flooded forests of the Brazilian Amazon, a fish that looks like it belongs in the Cretaceous rolls at the surface every quarter-hour, takes a breath of air, and disappears. The arapaima (*Arapaima gigas*) — known as pirarucu in Brazil and paiche in Peru — is one of the largest freshwater fish on earth: verified specimens exceed 2.5 metres and 200 kg, and scientists believe fish approaching 3 metres existed before centuries of overharvesting reshaped populations. Fishing for arapaima is a unique experience that depends not on conventional casting technique but on patience, stealth, and the biology of a fish that must breathe.

## Understanding the Arapaima

The arapaima is an obligate air-breather: its swim bladder is highly vascularised and functions like a primitive lung, allowing it to extract oxygen from the atmosphere in the oxygen-poor waters of Amazonian floodplains. This respiratory requirement — surfacing every 10 to 20 minutes — is both the fish's vulnerability and the key to catching it. Arapaima are covered in large, tough, imbricate scales with a hard outer layer of hydroxyapatite that makes them remarkably resistant to piranha bites. They are apex predators of floodplain lakes and flooded forests, feeding primarily on fish but also taking birds and small mammals at the water surface.

## Conservation Status and the Role of Sport Fishing

The arapaima is classified as Data Deficient on the IUCN Red List, though regional assessments suggest populations are severely depleted across much of the species' range due to centuries of commercial netting and spearing for the dried-fish trade. Brazilian legislation prohibits commercial harvest in most states and requires that all sport fishing be catch-and-release. In the Mamirauá Sustainable Development Reserve (established 1996) near Tefé in Amazonas state, community management programs have allowed arapaima populations to recover dramatically — local communities count fish annually by monitoring surfacing events, and densities have increased tenfold since protection began.

Sport fishing for arapaima is directly linked to conservation outcomes: lodges that offer arapaima fishing pay community licensing fees that fund protection. Responsible arapaima fishing is a net positive for the species.

## Where to Fish for Arapaima

The most established sport fishery for arapaima operates in the floodplain lakes (várzeas) of Amazonas state in Brazil. The Mamirauá Reserve is the benchmark; the reserve operates Uakari Lodge, which includes arapaima fishing as part of its ecotourism program. The Amanã Reserve, adjacent to Mamirauá, also holds significant populations. Further upstream, the Juruá and Purus river systems in western Amazonas produce fish in várzea lakes accessible by riverboat from Eirunepé or Lábrea.

In Peru, arapaima (known locally as paiche) are found in the floodplain lakes of the Pacaya-Samiria National Reserve and the Madre de Dios river system. Iquitos is the gateway to Peruvian arapaima fishing; operators like Pisces Amazon Fishing and several Iquitos-based outfitters offer packages to lakes within the reserve. Peru's arapaima fishing is less developed than Brazil's but improving rapidly as regulations have stabilised.

## The Breath-and-Cast Technique

Catching arapaima on rod and reel depends entirely on the breath cycle. Anglers position their boat on a known lake or flooded forest bay and wait. When an arapaima surfaces — the roll is distinctive, often accompanied by a loud gulping sound — the guide estimates the fish's direction of travel and the angler makes a cast to intercept it at the next surfacing point. Casts must be accurate, delivered quickly, and the lure or fly must be in the water before the fish surfaces again.

Large surface lures — floating plugs, popper-style lures, and large foam-headed surface flies — are the primary options. Arapaima, when they take, hit surface lures with enormous force, then immediately run deep and thrash. They also occasionally breach completely on hookup, an event that is as alarming as it is impressive. The fish must be brought alongside the boat quickly to avoid exhaustion; handling must be done in the water, supporting the fish horizontally, and the revival process often takes 5 to 10 minutes for large individuals.

## Tackle for Arapaima

For spin fishing, a heavy action baitcasting rod of 8 ft rated to 100 g with a 300–400 series reel loaded with 60–80 lb braid is the minimum requirement. Hook-setting on an arapaima requires significant force — the bony, armoured mouth demands a firm, repeated strike on hookup. Many guides recommend striking three to five times in succession to ensure penetration. Replace all stock hooks on surface lures with premium single or double hooks in size 4/0 to 6/0; treble hooks tend to get caught in the fish's gills on removal.

For fly fishing, a 12-weight single-handed rod or a two-handed 10-weight is appropriate. Reels must have a smooth, powerful drag — a large arapaima can run 80 metres in its first panic. Backing of at least 300 metres in 50 lb is standard. Flies are enormous: foam or deer-hair poppers and large streamer-popper combinations on 10–16 cm tube frames in white, orange, and chartreuse. The fighting butt of the rod will get used heavily.

## Planning Your Trip

Most arapaima trips are arranged through specialist Amazon fishing operators. The Uakari Lodge at Mamirauá books through conservation travel agents and is often fully committed one year in advance. Independent operators in Tefé, Eirunepé, and Iquitos offer more flexible booking. The season in Brazil aligns with the falling-water phase (September through January) when fish are concentrated in lakes rather than dispersed through flooded forest. Peru operates May through November. A 7-day package with a quality lodge runs USD 3,000–6,000 per person depending on location and accommodation standard.
