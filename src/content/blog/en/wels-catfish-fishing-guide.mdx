Europe's largest freshwater predator slides through warm, turbid water without the fanfare of a salmon or the acrobatics of a trout. The wels catfish — Silurus glanis — is a fish of shadows, silt, and immense power. On rivers like the Ebro in northeastern Spain, guides with headlamps walk the bank before dawn, listening for the deep boil of a big cat rolling at the surface. Fish exceeding 2 metres and weighing more than 100 kg are genuinely plausible catches, and the European record, set on the Po in Italy, stands above 130 kg. This is not finesse fishing. It is an exercise in discipline, heavy tackle, and patience measured in hours rather than casts.

## Where Wels Catfish Live

The wels is native to central and eastern Europe, thriving in the Danube basin, the Ebro, the Po, the Rhône, and numerous reservoirs across Spain, France, and Italy. Spain's Ebro, particularly around Mequinenza and Caspe, has produced some of the most consistently large fish since catfish were introduced in the 1970s and the population exploded. Embalse de Ribarroja, a reservoir on the Ebro, routinely yields fish in the 50–80 kg bracket. In Italy, the Po and Ticino rivers around Pavia and Cremona harbour enormous fish in slow, deep pools. In Germany, the Rhine and Main hold catfish, and the Saône in France is a respected destination. Eastern European rivers — the Danube near Novi Sad in Serbia and the Tisza in Hungary — hold fish with less angling pressure and arguably bigger average sizes.

## Reading the Water

Wels catfish are opportunistic ambush predators that favour structure: deep holes beneath weir pools, undercut clay banks, submerged tree stumps, bridge pilings, and confluences where currents slow and food settles. Water temperature is critical. Activity peaks between 18°C and 28°C, making June through September the prime window on most rivers. Below 10°C, catfish become almost entirely nocturnal and feed infrequently. On the Ebro, daytime temperatures can exceed 35°C in July, pushing fish into deep, cooled pools before they resurface to hunt at dusk. A thermometer and a depth sounder are as important as the rods themselves.

## Deadbait Techniques

Stationary deadbait on the bottom remains the most reliable technique for targeting big wels. Large baits — whole roach, bream, or carp frames of 500–1,000 g — are presented on heavy monofilament or braid leaders with single 10/0–14/0 hooks. The bait is laid directly on the silt in or near a snag, and the angler waits. In river fishing, a lead of 200–400 g holds the bait in a specific spot in current. Twitching the rod tip every twenty minutes moves the bait slightly, disturbing the silt and triggering a response from fish that have been circling without committing. On the Ebro, guides often pre-bait a swim with ground pellets and chopped fish for two or three sessions before fishing it, building confidence in resident fish.

## Lure and Surface Fishing

Surface fishing for wels — known as clonking — is a highly visual and tactile technique borrowed from Eastern Europe. A wooden or rubber clonk is slapped rhythmically on the water surface, mimicking the sound of a frog or injured fish. A large soft-plastic lure or octopus bait hangs beneath it on a short leader. The angler rows or drifts slowly while clonking, and strikes the moment the rod tip pulls down. It is most effective at night on warm, calm water in June and July. Big rubber shads — 30–40 cm lures — fished slowly along the bottom on jig heads of 60–150 g also produce excellent results, especially in reservoirs where visual contact with structure is possible on a sonar.

## Fly Fishing for Wels

An emerging discipline on the Ebro and Rhône involves targeting catfish with streamer flies on heavy 10–12 weight rods. Oversized articulated patterns in black or brown, tied on 5/0 hooks and fished on sinking lines in the 400–600 grain range, are worked slowly through deep pools. Strikes are aggressive and the fight on fly tackle is punishing. It is not a mainstream approach, but specialist guides in Mequinenza now offer dedicated sessions.

## Tackle Requirements

Line class for serious wels fishing starts at 50 lb breaking strain and can reach 150 lb or more for fish in heavy snag-filled swims. Heavy duty baitrunner reels with generous spool capacity hold 300–500 metres of braided mainline, backed by a mono shock leader. Rods of 3.5–4 lb test curve and 3.6–4 m length provide the backbone needed to lever big fish away from snags. Landing is done by hand — a skilled guide will grip the lower jaw while the angler cradles the body — never with a gaff or net on fish above 30 kg, as the weight can injure both the fish and the angler. Unhooking mats, weigh slings, and antibacterial treatment for hook wounds are standard practice on responsible venues.

## Seasons and Regulations

Most Spanish catfish fisheries are open year-round, but June–September brings the best action. In France and Italy, river regulations vary by département and province; many Rhône and Po stretches require a specific licence endorsement for catfish. On managed Spanish lakes like Embalse de Mequinenza, foreigners can purchase a one-week licence at the local guardia forestal office. Germany has strict size limits and closed seasons on some rivers; check the Fischereigesetz of the relevant Bundesland before fishing. Catch-and-release is now the norm at virtually every serious destination, and large fish should be returned within ten minutes of landing.
