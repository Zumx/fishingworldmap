Peru is one of the few countries where an angler can chase three completely different target species in three completely different ecosystems within a single trip. The warm headwaters of the Amazon Basin teem with peacock bass and payara, the cold surface of Lake Titicaca shelters introduced rainbow and lake trout, and the deep Pacific swells off Cabo Blanco once produced a world-record black marlin. Add jungle rivers alive with catfish and dorado, mountain streams slicing through cloudforest, and coastal estuaries thick with corvina, and the picture becomes clear: Peru is a fishing destination that rewards adventurers willing to travel beyond the tourist trail.

## Amazon Peacock Bass — Iquitos and the Pacaya-Samiria Reserve

The city of Iquitos, reachable only by air or river, is the launching point for the finest peacock bass fishing in South America. Tucunaré — as locals call the species — grow to over 9 kg in the oxbow lakes and flooded igapó forests of the Pacaya-Samiria National Reserve. The season runs from June through November when water levels drop and the fish concentrate in predictable shallows. Live-aboard riverboats spend five to ten nights cruising side channels, and a day's catch of twenty or thirty fish is routine. Poppers and large jerkbaits draw explosive surface strikes that remain startling no matter how many times you witness them.

## Cabo Blanco — Black Marlin Capital

In the 1950s Cabo Blanco, a small port town on Peru's northern coast near Talara, was the most famous big-game fishing location in the world. Ernest Hemingway fished here in 1956 while researching what became associated with the character Santiago. A 707-kilogram black marlin was caught here in 1953, a world record that stood for decades. Today the fishery has recovered significantly under better management. Charter boats operating out of Cabo Blanco target black and striped marlin, broadbill swordfish, and large yellowfin tuna in the cold Humboldt Current upwellings. The best months are February through April.

## Lake Titicaca — Trout at Altitude

Straddling the Peru-Bolivia border at 3,810 metres above sea level, Lake Titicaca is the world's highest navigable lake and supports a productive introduced trout fishery. Rainbow trout were introduced in the 1940s and have thrived in the cold, clear waters, regularly reaching 2 to 3 kg. The Peruvian side around Puno and the island communities of Amantaní and Taquile offers guided shore and boat fishing. The Santuario de Truchas La Quebradita near Puno operates as a dedicated trout sanctuary where catch-and-release fishing is well established and the scenery — reed islands, totora boats, snow-capped Bolivian peaks across the water — is extraordinary.

## The Marañón River — Catfish and Payara

Rising in the Andes west of Huaraz and flowing north before turning east to join the Amazon system, the Marañón carries nutrient-rich water that supports enormous catfish. Dorado (the river fish, unrelated to the marine mahi-mahi) and payara — the ferocious fanged vampire fish — inhabit the faster sections. The river passes through remote gorges accessible only by local boat, and organised expedition fishing is growing. Catches of catfish exceeding 30 kg are not unusual in the deeper pools below rapids.

## Lago Sandoval and the Madre de Dios — Southern Amazon

Near Puerto Maldonado, close to the Bolivian border, Lago Sandoval sits inside the Tambopata National Reserve. This oxbow lake is reached by a 45-minute canoe journey through flooded forest. It holds peacock bass, large oscar cichlids, and several catfish species. The surrounding Madre de Dios river system is less pressured than the northern Amazon and provides exceptional fly-fishing for peacock bass on intermediate lines and large flashy streamers. Picigranja Pichari, a trout farm and recreational fishing site in the Apurímac valley, offers a contrasting highland experience within the broader southern region.

## Tumbes and the Pacific Estuary Fishery

The far north of Peru, where the department of Tumbes borders Ecuador, hosts mangrove estuaries and river mouths that harbour snook, corvina, and flounder. The Tumbes River supports large snook that are rarely fished by visiting anglers, making the area genuinely undiscovered by international standards. Light-tackle spinning and fly-fishing for snook in the mangroves is productive from December through March when the sea warms and baitfish concentrate.

## The Huallaga River — Golden Dorado

The Huallaga River flows north through the department of San Martín and is the most accessible golden dorado river in Peru. This Andean river species — distinct from the Amazon dorado — is a powerful predator that attacks lures with ferocity. The section between Tarapoto and Yurimaguas passes through partly protected forest and sees only a handful of serious fishing visitors each year. Guides from Tarapoto know the best pools, which are productive from May through October.

## Piscigranja Trout Farms — Highland Fly-Fishing

Throughout the Andean highlands, piscigranja (trout farm) operations have opened catch-and-release pools and stocked mountain streams to recreational anglers. The concept is popular with Peruvians and increasingly with visiting fly anglers. Many of these facilities — including Piscigranja operations in the Callejón de Huaylas valley near Huaraz — stock genuine rainbow trout in clear mountain streams and allow dry-fly presentation in beautiful surroundings. Huaraz, at 3,090 metres with the Cordillera Blanca rising to over 6,700 metres behind it, provides a stunning backdrop for a morning's fishing.

## Lake Llanganuco — High Andes Glacial Trout

At 3,860 metres in the Huascarán National Park, the twin glacial lakes of Llanganuco hold wild rainbow trout in water so clear that individual fish are visible from the shore. Fishing is permitted with a park entry fee and local guide. The trout here are relatively small — most fish run 200 to 400 grams — but catching them in the shadow of Chopicalqui and Huascarán peaks, the two highest summits in the tropical Andes, creates an experience that no weight of fish could improve.

## The Napo River — Upper Amazon Diversity

The Napo, entering Peru from Ecuador and flowing east through Loreto department, is lined with small fishing lodges and community tourism operations. The variety of species is remarkable: peacock bass, piranha, payara, aruana, and numerous catfish species all coexist in the braided channel. The town of Mazán near the Napo-Amazon confluence is a base for multi-day river expeditions. Water temperatures sit between 27 and 30°C year-round, and fishing is productive in both high- and low-water seasons, though the methods and target areas differ.
