New Zealand has no native trout. Every brown swimming its rivers descends from shipments of English and Tasmanian ova in the 1860s, and every rainbow from a single batch of California steelhead stock — yet nowhere else on Earth did the transplants prosper like this. Gin-clear backcountry rivers grow browns past ten pounds, Lake Taupo's tributaries run thick with spawning rainbows, and offshore, the warm Pacific currents that Zane Grey fished in the 1920s still carry marlin, kingfish and broadbill past the Northland coast.

## Lake Taupo and the Tongariro — the rainbow factory

Lake Taupo, a flooded supervolcano caldera of 616 square kilometres, anchors the North Island fishery. Each autumn and winter, its rainbows run up the Tongariro River to spawn, and pools with hundred-year-old names — the Major Jones, the Hydro, the Bridge Pool — fill with fish averaging 1.5 to 2.5 kilos. Nymphing the winter runs is a New Zealand rite of passage. The Taupo fishery has its own licence (separate from the national one), and the river fishes from Turangi, a town that exists substantially because of trout.

## The backcountry browns — Nelson and the West Coast

The South Island's upper rivers are the sight-fishing benchmark of the planet. In the Nelson Lakes region and the valleys radiating from Murchison, rivers hold modest numbers of very large browns — fish of 4 to 6 pounds are normal, doubles are real — in water so clear you spot them from 50 metres. The method is pure hunting: walk upstream, find a fish, make one good cast. Many rivers carry special regulations, and some prime waters use a beat or ballot system in peak season. Hire a guide for the first days; reading this water is a skill of its own.

## The Mataura — the world's best dry-fly river?

Southland's Mataura is a different game: not giants, but extraordinary numbers of free-rising browns in a gentle, weedy stream made for the dry fly. The famous "mad Mataura rise" during autumn mayfly hatches can carpet the river with feeding fish. Access is generous under Southland's tradition of anglers' access, the season runs October to April, and a 5-weight rod with a box of size 16 Adams and emergers covers most days. Nearby, the Oreti holds some of the largest browns in the country for those who want the trophy hunt instead.

## The Twizel canals — the strangest trophy water on Earth

The hydro canals between Lakes Tekapo, Pukaki and Ohau in the Mackenzie basin look like nothing — straight concrete-edged channels through tussock. Yet salmon farms in the canals leak feed pellets, and the trout below them grow to world-record sizes: browns and rainbows beyond 15 kilograms have been landed. It is a peculiar, crowded, utterly unique fishery — egg patterns and soft baits under floats, fished metres from your car at 700 metres elevation under the Southern Alps. Love it or hate it, the biggest trout of your life probably lives here.

## Bay of Islands — Zane Grey's El Dorado

In 1926, western novelist Zane Grey set up a big-game camp at Otehei Bay on Urupukapuka Island and broadcast the Bay of Islands to the world as the "Angler's El Dorado". A century later, the bay and the Northland coast up to the Three Kings Islands remain serious big-game water: striped marlin, kingfish around every reef and rock like the famous fishermen's marks off Cape Brett, snapper in the bays, and broadbill swordfish on the deep canyons. Charters run from Paihia and Russell, the latter once the lawless whaling port turned game-fishing capital.

## Rotorua's lakes — trophy rainbows in geothermal country

The crater lakes around Rotorua — Tarawera, Rotoiti, Okataina — grow some of the country's heaviest rainbows, fish that feed on smelt and reach 4 to 5 kilos. Winter jigging and trolling produce on the deep lakes, while stream mouths fish at night for anglers swinging lures into the drop-offs. The pumice-filtered water is astonishingly clear, and steam rises from the shoreline in places — fishing here feels mildly volcanic because it is.

## Fiordland and the far south — remote water, big rewards

Fiordland's rivers — the Eglinton along the Milford road, the Waiau below Lake Te Anau, the Clinton and Worsley at the lake heads — fish in scenery that needs no exaggeration. Browns and rainbows share the water, sandflies enforce humility, and boat access across Te Anau or Manapouri opens valleys that see a handful of anglers a year. Stewart Island, further south again, offers blue cod and groper fishing out of Halfmoon Bay in water that belongs to another century.

## Saltwater everywhere — snapper, kingfish and the kahawai armada

No New Zealander lives more than 130 kilometres from the sea, and the saltwater scene is correspondingly vast. The Hauraki Gulf off Auckland produces work-up fishing — birds, dolphins and whales herding baitfish — with snapper and kingfish underneath. Kingfish to 30 kilos haunt every headland from North Cape to the Marlborough Sounds; surfcasters take snapper off Ninety Mile Beach; and the humble kahawai, swarming every river mouth, may be the best light-tackle sport fish nobody brags about. No licence is needed for sea fishing — just size and bag limits, strictly enforced.

## Licences, biosecurity and the rules of the game

One national Fish & Game licence (roughly NZ$150 for a full adult season) covers all freshwater except the Taupo district, which sells its own. The season for most rivers runs the first Saturday of October to 30 April; many lakes and lower rivers stay open year-round. Biosecurity is taken deadly seriously: didymo invaded South Island rivers in 2004, so Check-Clean-Dry between waterways is both law and ethic. Felt-soled waders are banned. The exchange rate of effort to reward is simple here — walk further, fish bigger.
