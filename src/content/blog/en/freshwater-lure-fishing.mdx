The lure angler who carries only one type of artificial bait is like a carpenter with a single tool — capable of some work, but frustratingly limited. The modern freshwater lure fishing toolkit spans an extraordinary range of designs, each engineered to present an irresistible trigger to specific predatory fish in specific conditions. Understanding when to reach for a classic Mepps spinner, when to switch to a crankbait that runs at exactly 2.5 metres, and when to slow-roll a soft plastic shad along the gravel bottom is the core skill of lure fishing — and it transforms what feels like random success into repeatable, planned results.

## Inline Spinners: The Classic Starting Point

The inline spinner — most famously the Mepps Aglia — is the lure that introduced millions of anglers to predator fishing. The spinning blade creates vibration and flash that is detectable to the lateral line of perch, pike, and trout from considerable distances in coloured water. Blade geometry matters: a narrow willow-leaf blade spins at a tighter angle and sinks faster, better for deep-running or fast-retrieve fishing; a wide Colorado blade creates more lift and vibration at slower speeds, useful in cold water when predators are sluggish. Size selection should broadly match the prey fish in the target water: a size 1 Mepps Aglia (7 g) for small stream trout and perch; a size 5 (20 g) for pike in large rivers and lakes. Copper and gold blades excel in coloured or peaty water; silver blades outperform in clear conditions.

## Crankbaits: Depth Control and Constant Action

Crankbaits — also called plugs in UK terminology — are hard-bodied lures with a lip that causes them to dive to a specific depth range on retrieve. This depth control is their primary advantage: a crankbait rated to 2 to 3 metres will hunt through the thermocline band where perch and pike often station themselves in summer. Shallow divers (running to under 1 m) are ideal for targeting pike in shallow weedy bays in spring; medium divers (1 to 3 m) cover mid-water presentations on rivers and lakes; deep divers (over 4 m) reach fish holding in reservoir depths. Bill shape also affects action: a wide, flat bill creates a wide wobble; a narrow angled bill produces a tighter, more rapid action. For perch in UK rivers and lakes, a Rapala Countdown or an X-Rap in sizes 5 to 9 cm is a classic choice; for pike, a Salmo Slider or a Westin Ricky the Roach in 12 to 15 cm.

## Jerkbaits: Irregular Action for Reluctant Predators

The jerkbait is an elongated, lipless hard lure that relies entirely on rod-tip action rather than retrieve speed to generate movement. A sharp downward rod jerk causes the lure to dart sideways — either left or right, depending on the lure's design — and a pause allows it to glide and suspend. This jerk-pause action mimics a wounded or disoriented prey fish, one of the most effective triggers for pike and large perch. Glide baits take a side-to-side sweeping path, useful for covering a wide search arc; dive-and-rise baits dart forward and upward. Jerkbait fishing uses a short, stiff rod — typically 1.8 to 2.1 metres with a fast action — and a baitcasting reel spooled with low-stretch braid. The Salmo Slider and the Illex Nitro Shad are well-established European pike jerkbaits.

## Soft Plastic Lures: Versatility and Depth

Soft plastic lures — shads, paddle tails, curly tail grubs, and creature baits — are the most versatile category in the freshwater lure angler's box. Their appeal lies in lifelike texture and action combined with the ability to rig them on a wide variety of hook and weight combinations to fish virtually any depth and retrieve style. The Texas rig (weight at the nose, hook buried in the body) is weedless and ideal for fishing through dense weed and snags. The drop shot places the weight below the hook, keeping the bait at a constant depth as you work it — highly effective for perch holding over specific bottom features. The jig head — a hook with moulded-in weight — is the simplest and most universal soft plastic presentation, covering everything from slow bottom-crawling for zander to high-speed shad reeling for perch.

## Spinnerbaits and Bladed Jigs

The spinnerbait — a safety-pin frame lure with a wire arm carrying rotating blades above a lead jig head and rubber skirt — is a lure born in North America but increasingly popular for European pike fishing. Its weedless wire arm makes it ideal for fishing through surface vegetation and lily pads that would immediately snag a conventional lure. Chatterbaits and bladed jigs use a hexagonal blade attached directly to the hook eye, creating a vibrating action on a straight retrieve that is highly effective for late-autumn pike, perch, and large chub on rivers.

## Retrieve Strategies and Seasonal Adjustment

Lure selection without retrieve adjustment is only half the equation. In cold water under 8°C, every retrieve should be slowed significantly; predators in cold conditions will not chase a fast-moving target but will intercept a very slow, bottom-hugging presentation. The slow-roll — a near-static retrieve that barely keeps the lure swimming — is a critical cold-water technique. In warm summer conditions, the opposite applies: fast retrieves provoke competitive reaction strikes from aggressive pike and perch. Vary depth, speed, and cadence systematically on a new water rather than changing lure colour and type endlessly.

## Targeting Different Species

The lure approach varies significantly by species. Perch respond best to small finesse lures — a 5 to 7 cm paddle tail shad on a 5 to 7 gram jig head, fished around bridges, jetties, lock gates, and other hard structures. Zander prefer longer, slim shad profiles in natural colours, fished very slowly close to the bottom in low-light or murky conditions. Pike respond to a wide variety of lures but are particularly reliable on big swimbait profiles and blade lures during dawn and dusk. Chub and asp in river situations hit surface lures and high-speed cranks with great aggression.
