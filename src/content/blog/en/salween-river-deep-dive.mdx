The Salween River — Thanlwin in Burmese, Nu Jiang in Chinese — is one of Asia's last truly wild rivers. While its counterparts, the Mekong, the Irrawaddy, and the Yangtze, have been transformed by hydroelectric infrastructure and agricultural encroachment, the Salween has so far escaped large-scale damming. It flows nearly 2,800 kilometres from the Tibetan Plateau, through the deeply incised gorges of Yunnan Province in China, and into Myanmar where it curves south through Kayah State and Karen State before reaching the Andaman Sea at Moulmein. Along the Thailand-Myanmar border south of Mae Hong Son, the river is accessible enough to attract adventurous anglers who make the journey for some of Southeast Asia's most remarkable freshwater fishing.

## Giant Mahseer: The Freshwater Tiger

Giant mahseer — collectively referring to several large cyprinid species in the genus Tor — are the paramount target for serious sport anglers on the Salween. Tor putitora and the closely related Tor tambroides are present in the upper and mid-Salween, and fish exceeding 30 kilograms have been reliably reported from deep pools in the Myanmar border stretches. Mahseer have a reputation for powerful, sustained runs that test 30-pound tackle, and their enormous scales — each can be several centimetres across — give them an almost prehistoric appearance. Traditional fishing methods in the river communities use hand lines and traps, but visiting anglers use heavy spinning rods with braided mainlines and large hard-bodied lures or floating boilies as used in European specimen fishing.

## Snakehead: The Predator of the Shallows

Giant snakehead — Channa micropeltes — is the largest species in the Channa genus and reaches lengths over 1.2 metres and weights above 20 kilograms. In the flooded margins and backwater oxbows of the lower Salween and its tributaries in Karen State, snakehead occupy the apex predator niche in shallow water. The species breathes atmospheric air using a labyrinthine organ and can survive in anoxic water that would kill most fish, which makes it extraordinarily resilient. Surface lure fishing for snakehead is one of Thailand's most popular recreational pursuits, and the Salween tributaries along the Thai-Myanmar border — accessible from Mae Sot and Umphang — produce fish that dwarf the average Thai pond specimens. The strike, invariably from below at full speed, can launch a 5-kilogram snakehead entirely clear of the water.

## Unique Endemic Species

The Salween basin is a global biodiversity hotspot. Its geographic isolation — the river has been cut off from neighbouring basins by geological uplift for millions of years — has produced a high rate of endemism. The Salween spiny eel, several endemic loach species, and at least three endemic catfish species have been described from the river in the past two decades, and ichthyologists believe substantial numbers of species await formal description. For the angling naturalist, the Salween offers encounters with species not found anywhere else on Earth. Smaller cyprinids in the fast-water sections — Devario, Crossocheilus, and Garra species — will readily take small dry flies and nymphs, providing technical fly fishing for fish that have never seen an artificial fly.

## The Myanmar Border Reaches

Access to the Salween's most productive fishing stretches in Myanmar requires a combination of logistics that demands thorough planning. Flights from Yangon reach Kengtung in Shan State, and the river below Kengtung is accessible by vehicle and then foot or boat. Entry to the border zones requires permits from the Myanmar Ministry of Hotels and Tourism, and travel should be organised through licensed operators based in Yangon or Chiang Mai. Political instability in Myanmar since 2021 has complicated access, and current conditions should be carefully assessed before committing to travel. The reward for those who manage the logistics is fishing on a river where pressure is near zero, wildlife encounters — including fishing eagles, giant river otters, and Irrawaddy dolphins in the lower reaches — are routine, and the landscape is utterly wild.

## The Thai Side: Mae Hong Son and the Border Tributaries

For anglers unwilling or unable to enter Myanmar, the Thai side of the border offers accessible alternatives. Mae Hong Son Province, a mountainous region in northwestern Thailand almost entirely surrounded by Myanmar, has several rivers that drain eastward toward the Ping and ultimately the Chao Phraya, but its western slopes feed Salween tributaries. The Pai River above the town of Pai holds mahseer-family species (specifically Neolissochilus hexagonolepis, the chocolate mahseer) along with giant gourami in slower sections. Fishing lodges near Mae Hong Son town provide local guides and equipment, and the fishing culture here blends the serious sporting approach of visiting anglers with the subsistence traditions of the Karen and Shan communities.

## Fishing Methods and Ethics

Fishing the Salween and its tributaries requires adaptability. In fast-water gorge sections, heavy lure fishing with 25 to 35-pound mono mainlines and hard-bodied lures that dive to 2 to 3 metres works best for mahseer. In slower pools and margin habitat, floating boilies fished hair-rig style in the carp fishing tradition have produced the largest mahseer on record in the system. All responsible anglers and operators in the region practice catch-and-release for mahseer and other large species, both for conservation reasons and because the river communities depend on fish for protein and would rightly object to the removal of breeding stock.

## Conservation Pressures and the Dam Threat

Despite the Salween's current undammed status, hydroelectric development remains an ongoing threat. The proposed Hatgyi Dam on the Thai-Myanmar border and multiple upstream projects in China's Yunnan have been under discussion for decades. Environmental organisations including International Rivers have campaigned for the river's protection, supported by the Karen and Shan ethnic communities whose livelihoods depend on the river's natural flood cycle. Sport fishing tourism, however modest in scale, provides an economic argument for keeping the river wild — a free-flowing Salween is worth more to its watershed than any dam project.
