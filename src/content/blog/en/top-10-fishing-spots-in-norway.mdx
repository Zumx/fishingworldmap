Norway's coastline, if you straighten out every fjord and island, runs over 100,000 kilometres — longer than two laps of the equator. Along it swim some of the largest cod, halibut and coalfish in the Atlantic, while inland the rivers that carved the valleys still host Atlantic salmon runs that made British lords build fishing lodges here in the 1830s. Add free saltwater fishing for everyone — no licence needed in the sea — and Norway might be the most generous fishing country in Europe.

## The Gaula — Atlantic salmon without a dam

The Gaula, emptying into Trondheimsfjord south of Trondheim, is one of the last great undammed salmon rivers in Europe. Its season (June to August) regularly produces fish over 15 kilos, with the powerful early-summer run drawing fly anglers to famous beats around Støren and the Gaulfossen gorge. No hydropower, no stocking — every salmon is wild. Day cards on association water exist alongside exclusive beats, and the river's protection rules are strict: registered and disinfected gear, mandatory catch reporting, and quotas that change yearly with the run.

## Saltstraumen — fishing the world's strongest current

Near Bodø, four hundred million cubic metres of seawater force through a 150-metre-wide channel every six hours, creating whirlpools and a food conveyor that stacks fish like nowhere else. Coalfish (saithe) are the stars — fish over 10 and even 15 kilos taken on shore-cast lures — alongside big cod, halibut and wolffish. The fishing is free, the bridge and rocks give access, and the timing revolves around the tide tables: fish the hour each side of slack water when the current is workable.

## Lofoten — skrei, the migrating winter cod

Each January through April, mature Arctic cod — skrei — migrate from the Barents Sea to spawn around the Lofoten islands, a run that has fed Norway since the Viking age. From villages like Henningsvær, Ballstad and Å, charter boats jig cod from 10 to over 30 kilos within sight of the granite walls. Winter weather is serious, daylight short, and the fishing can be absurd: pirks dropped 40 metres come back with fish on every hook. The same harbours offer summer fishing for halibut, plaice and coalfish under round-the-clock light.

## The Alta — the queen of salmon rivers

In Finnmark, the Alta river is salmon royalty: a glacial-green river running through a canyon of 300-metre walls, producing more 20-kilo-plus Atlantic salmon than perhaps any river on Earth. Access is famously restricted — locals control most fishing, and visiting rods enter a lottery or pay for scarce, costly beats — but even a low-chance application is worth it for the angling experience of a lifetime. The midnight sun means fishing at 2 a.m. in full light, when the biggest fish often move.

## Sørøya and the halibut grounds of the far north

The island of Sørøya off Hammerfest has become the destination for trophy Atlantic halibut. The shallow banks on its western side hold fish from 20 to beyond 150 kilos, taken on jigged shads in 20 to 60 metres of water. Camps in Sørvær and Hasvik run modern boats with sonar and safety kit. June and July are peak months; in recent years, halibut over two metres long have been released here every season, since fish above 2 metres must by camp practice — and conservation sense — go back.

## Trondheimsfjord and the mid-coast — cod, pollack and the kelp edge

You do not need the Arctic for great sea fishing. The mid-Norway coast around Hitra and Frøya, where the Trondheimsfjord meets open sea, holds year-round cod, huge pollack along the kelp lines, ling and tusk in the deeps, and mackerel shoals all summer. Hitra's camps cater heavily to visiting anglers with rental boats on sheltered water — a forgiving place to learn Norwegian sea fishing before attempting the exposed northern banks.

## The Oslofjord and the southern skerries

Within an hour of the capital, the Oslofjord offers sea trout along its rocky shores — a classic cold-season fishery from ice-out to May — plus pollack, mackerel and recovering cod stocks (note: cod is protected in the inner Oslofjord; check the current rules). Small marinas and jetties from Drøbak south to the skerries of Hvaler give endless light-tackle sport. Local spots with names like Åros at the Sætre coast typify the fjord: a river mouth, a gravel beach, and sea trout cruising the drop-off at dawn.

## Mountain trout and char — the inland plateau

Norway's interior is a freckle-map of trout water. The Hardangervidda plateau, Europe's largest mountain plateau, holds thousands of lakes with wild brown trout; Femund and the rivers of Trøndelag offer grayling and trout in canoe country; and even city-adjacent lakes like Trollvann above Oslo are stocked and family-friendly. Inland fishing requires two things: the national fishing fee for salmon/sea trout rivers, and a local fiskekort (fishing card) for lakes and trout streams — most are bought in minutes via apps like Inatur.

## Seasons, rules and the Allemannsretten advantage

Sea fishing in Norway is free for recreational anglers — no licence, no fee — with a few key rules: a 15-kilo export limit on fish fillets (must be connected to a registered camp to export more), minimum sizes, and protected species including spiny dogfish and bluefin without permit. Rivers are another world: national fee, local cards, disinfection certificates for tackle used abroad. The Allemannsretten (right to roam) lets you walk, camp and fish the shoreline almost anywhere. Summer gives midnight sun north of the Arctic Circle; winter gives skrei and northern lights in the same trip.
