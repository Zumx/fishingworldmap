The Columbia River begins its 1,953-kilometre journey in the Rocky Mountains of British Columbia, Canada, collecting the drainage from a watershed of nearly 670,000 square kilometres — larger than France — before crossing into Washington State and eventually emptying into the Pacific Ocean near Astoria, Oregon. It is the largest river by volume flowing into the Pacific from North America, and for much of its length it is one of the continent's great fisheries. Five species of Pacific salmon run its waters seasonally, steelhead trout return from the ocean in numbers that still awe biologists, and the lower river holds the largest population of white sturgeon on Earth.

## The Pacific Salmon Runs

Five salmon species complete their life cycles in the Columbia basin: chinook (king) salmon, coho (silver) salmon, sockeye salmon, pink salmon, and chum salmon. Of these, the chinook is the most prized by anglers — adults can exceed 30 kg, and the Columbia spring chinook (which begin entering the river as early as March) are famous for their exceptionally high oil content and flavour. The summer and fall chinook runs extend the season through October. The Snake River, the Columbia's largest tributary, entering the river near the Oregon-Washington border, historically supported some of the longest salmon migrations on Earth — fish ascending over 1,400 kilometres from the sea to spawning grounds in Idaho's Sawtooth Mountains.

## Steelhead: The River's Greatest Prize

Many anglers regard the Columbia steelhead — the sea-run form of rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) — as the ultimate freshwater sport fish in North America. Unlike Pacific salmon, steelhead do not necessarily die after spawning and may return to the ocean and run rivers again in subsequent years. Summer steelhead enter the Columbia from July onward and run well into the winter months; winter steelhead runs peak from December through February. The Deschutes River in Oregon, entering the Columbia from the south near the town of The Dalles, is considered among the finest steelhead rivers in North America, its clear volcanic-rock channels accessible to wading fly fishers for the full length of its lower canyon.

## White Sturgeon: Living Dinosaurs of the Columbia

No fish in the Columbia excites quite the same sense of encounter with deep time as the white sturgeon (Acipenser transmontanus). Biologists have confirmed individual fish living more than 100 years, and the current IGFA all-tackle world record for the species — 212.28 kg (468 lb) — was caught in the Columbia. Fish of 100 kg or more are encountered regularly in the stretches between Bonneville Dam and the estuary. The sturgeon are bottom-feeders, targeted with heavy anchored gear and large natural baits — lamprey, salmon carcass, and smelt all produce. A catch-and-release ethic is now effectively mandatory for any fish over the "slot" size limit (between 44 and 54 inches in most Columbia stretches), protecting the largest breeding females.

## Dam Management and Fisheries Politics

No fishery in North America has been more profoundly shaped by political and engineering decisions than the Columbia. The river's 14 main-stem dams — including Bonneville, The Dalles, John Day, and McNary — generate enormous amounts of electricity for the Pacific Northwest but have blocked salmon passage to vast areas of historical habitat and raised water temperatures that stress cold-water species. The Snake River dams (Ice Harbor, Lower Monumental, Little Goose, and Lower Granite) became the subject of decades of legal battles. In 2024 the United States government and tribal nations reached a historic agreement to begin breaching the four lower Snake River dams, a decision with enormous implications for steelhead and salmon recovery.

## Fishing the Lower Columbia: Astoria and the Estuary

The estuary near Astoria, Oregon, where the Columbia meets the Pacific, is a fishing hub with a long history. Astoria — named for fur trader John Jacob Astor — was the first American settlement west of the Rocky Mountains. Today its waterfront hosts charter fleets targeting chinook salmon from May through September, halibut in summer, and bottomfish year-round. The Columbia Bar — the notoriously rough confluence of river and ocean — is one of the most dangerous waterway crossings in the world but also one of the richest for sea-run fish. Tillamook Bay and the nearby north Oregon coast offer salmon and steelhead fishing as a natural complement to the main river.

## The Upper Columbia and Canadian Reaches

Upstream of the US-Canada border, the Columbia flows through British Columbia's Rocky Mountain Trench, a vast landscape of glacial lakes, old-growth forest, and mountain peaks. Revelstoke, about 560 kilometres north of the border, sits at the junction of the Columbia and Illecillewaet Rivers. The reservoir behind Revelstoke Dam holds trophy rainbow trout and bull trout. Further north, the river passes through Arrow Lakes Reservoir and eventually to Columbia Lake, its headwater, near Fairmont Hot Springs — a journey through some of the most dramatic mountain scenery in North America.

## Seasons, Licensing, and Regulations

The Columbia system spans two countries and multiple states and provinces, each with their own licensing and regulation schemes. In Oregon and Washington, annual fishing licenses are required and are supplemented by salmon and steelhead punch-card systems that record retention. Tribal fishing rights — held by the Columbia River tribes including the Yakama, Nez Perce, Umatilla, and Warm Springs — predate statehood and coexist with state management in complex ways that anglers must understand before fishing. The best single resource is the Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife or Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife websites, which publish updated annual regulations.
