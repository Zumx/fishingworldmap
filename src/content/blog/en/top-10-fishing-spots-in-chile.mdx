Chile is 4,300 kilometres long and rarely more than 180 kilometres wide, which means almost every fishing spot in the country sits within an hour of either the Andes or the Pacific. In the south, German and British settlers stocked brown and rainbow trout over a century ago, and the fish found the cold, sterile rivers of Patagonia so agreeable that Chile now rivals New Zealand for trophy trout. In the north, the Humboldt Current pushes cold, nutrient-rich water along the desert coast, feeding everything from surf-zone corvina to offshore swordfish.

## The Futaleufú — turquoise water, muscular rainbows

The Futaleufú crosses from Argentina into Chile's Los Lagos region as one of the most beautiful rivers on the planet — an unreal glacial turquoise pouring through granite gorges. Beneath the whitewater that made it a rafting mecca, the slower braids and lagoons hold rainbow trout averaging 18 to 22 inches, plus browns that push beyond. The season runs roughly November to April; February and March bring the best dry-fly fishing with big attractor patterns. The town of Futaleufú has lodges and independent guides, and Wild River Chile Lodge is among the operations running float and walk-and-wade days on the river and its tributaries.

## Lago Yelcho and the Yelcho river — Patagonia's reliable giant

South of Chaitén on the Carretera Austral, Lago Yelcho is a vast, moody lake hemmed by hanging glaciers. It is famous for the late-summer dragonfly hatch, when big browns and rainbows charge surface patterns along the reed margins. The Yelcho river flowing out of the lake offers slow, deep pools where streamer anglers connect with fish in the 5 to 10 pound class. Lodges around Puerto Cárdenas serve the water, and the bridge over the outlet is a landmark every Carretera Austral traveller knows.

## Coyhaique and the Aysén heartland — Simpson, Ñirehuao and the steppe rivers

Coyhaique is Chile's trout capital. Within an hour of town you can fish the Río Simpson's wooded canyon (a national reserve), the grasshopper-banked Ñirehuao in the eastern steppe, and spring creeks like the Emperador Guillermo. February hopper fishing on the Ñirehuao is the stuff of legend: browns sip foam patterns tight to undercut banks all afternoon. The infrastructure is the best in Chilean Patagonia — guides, fly shops and estancia lodges — yet a ten-minute drive still puts you on water you will share with nobody.

## The Araucanía — Quepe, Leufucade and the volcano rivers

Around Temuco and Villarrica, rivers drain the snowfields of Villarrica and Llaima volcanoes through Mapuche farmland. The Río Quepe is a sleeper: the stretch around the "tres puentes" area near Vilcún gives up wild rainbows and browns in pocket water with the volcano smoking on the horizon. The smaller Río Leufucade near Lautaro is a willow-lined spring-fed stream where careful wading and small dries pay. The Toltén, draining Lake Villarrica, adds the chance of a lake-run fish over 60 centimetres. This region fishes earlier than Aysén — November and December are prime.

## Northern Patagonia's lakes — Llanquihue, Todos los Santos and Puerto Varas

The Lakes District around Puerto Varas and Puerto Montt is where Chilean trout culture began. Lago Llanquihue, Chile's second-largest lake, holds big rainbows and landlocked Atlantic salmon that are trolled near river mouths in spring. Emerald Lago Todos los Santos, inside Vicente Pérez Rosales National Park beneath Osorno volcano, offers boat-access fishing in scenery that justifies the trip alone. The Petrohué river below the falls is a powerful, boulder-strewn run where streamers move heavy rainbows and the occasional sea-run brown.

## Tierra del Fuego and Magallanes — sea-run browns at the end of the world

Chile's far south is less famous than Argentina's Río Grande, but the Chilean side of Tierra del Fuego and the Magallanes mainland hide sea-trout rivers of their own, along with lakes like Blanco on the big island holding brook trout and browns. The wind is constant and brutal — count on casting 30-knot gusts — and the season is short, December to March. Outfitters in Punta Arenas and Porvenir run the logistics. For the adventurous, king salmon (chinook), self-established since the 1990s, now run several rivers around the Strait of Magellan in January and February.

## The central reservoirs — Recoleta and the norte chico

In the semi-arid norte chico near Ovalle, Embalse Recoleta and its sister reservoir La Paloma are Chile's warm-water surprise: pejerrey (Argentinian silverside) and introduced largemouth-style fishing among cacti and vineyards. Local anglers fish floating baits and small lures from the banks at dawn before the desert sun flattens everything. It is a completely different Chile from Patagonia — and only five hours north of Santiago.

## The Pacific coast — surf, rocks and the offshore grounds

Chile's coast is one long fishing spot. Surfcasters target corvina and róbalo from the beaches of La Serena, Pichilemu and Constitución; rock fishermen pull jurel, sierra and the prized lenguado (flounder) from headlands the whole length of the country. Small asentamientos pesqueros — artisanal fishing coves with their wooden boats winched up the beach — dot the shore and are the best places to arrange a local boat trip. Offshore, the waters from Arica to Iquique are historic big-game grounds where swordfish and striped marlin ride the Humboldt's edge, and albacore runs still fire up the ports in summer.

## Licences, rules and timing

Chile requires a national recreational fishing licence (pesca recreativa), bought online through Sernapesca or in municipal offices, valid countrywide and cheap. The general inland season runs from the second Friday of November to the first Sunday of April, with year-round exceptions on some lakes and reservoirs; many premium rivers carry catch-and-release or single-hook rules, and several Aysén waters are fly-only. Didymo algae is established in Patagonia — clean and dry waders between rivers, and expect checks. For the south, book December to March; for the coast and the north, the austral summer is equally kind.
