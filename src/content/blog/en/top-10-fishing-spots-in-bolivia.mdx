Bolivia sits landlocked at the heart of South America, yet its fishing is anything but confined. From the snow-rimmed shores of Lake Titicaca at 3,810 metres to the steaming lowland rivers of the Beni and Mamoré departments, the country holds a diversity of freshwater species that surprises even seasoned anglers. The golden dorado — one of the world's most powerful fighting freshwater fish — reaches its greatest densities here in remote Bolivian rivers that see almost no fishing pressure. Peacock bass lurk in oxbow lakes fed by Amazon headwaters, and rainbow trout cruise the cold, shallow bays of the Titicaca shore. Bolivia asks effort from visiting anglers, but the rewards are genuinely extraordinary.

## The Mamoré River — Golden Dorado Country

The Mamoré is the centrepiece of Bolivian sport fishing. This broad, sandy river flows north through Beni department, fed by scores of clear-water tributaries draining the Andean foothills. Golden dorado in the 8 to 20 kg range are present year-round, but concentrate most reliably from June to October when water clarity peaks. The fish hold in eddies and current seams below rapids and surge at large surface poppers or fast-stripped streamers with explosive force. Several specialist lodges — operating near Trinidad and along the Ibare and Secure rivers — offer multi-day packages with skiff-based fishing in water that averages only light boat traffic.

## Lake Titicaca — High-Altitude Trout

Bolivia's shore of Lake Titicaca around Copacabana and the Isla del Sol hosts the same productive rainbow trout fishery found on the Peruvian side. Introduced in the 1940s, the trout have naturalised fully. Fish of 1 to 3 kg are regularly caught from small boats, and the rocky shoreline around Isla del Sol — Bolivia's most sacred Inca site — provides excellent structure for shore fishing. The altitude makes activity feel more demanding; mornings are cold and clear, and the light at 3,810 metres is intense, so polarised glasses are essential for spotting fish.

## The Iténez (Guaporé) River — Remote Dorado and Pacu

The Iténez river forms the border with Brazil in eastern Bolivia and is considered one of the most biologically diverse rivers in the world. Golden dorado here are less pressured than on the Mamoré, and large pacu — a cousin of the piranha that can exceed 30 kg — are targeted by local and expedition anglers. Pesque y Pague Don Diego and similar fishing ponds in Beni department were established to bring the region's angling closer to towns, but the wild Iténez corridor itself rewards those willing to travel. Access is by light aircraft to Trinidad followed by river boat.

## The Secure River — Fly-Fishing for Dorado

A tributary of the Mamoré rising in the Isiboro-Sécure National Park, the Secure River is a clear, fast-flowing stream ideal for sight-fishing and fly-fishing for golden dorado. The park's protected status limits access to licensed operators, which keeps fishing quality high. Wading anglers using 9-weight rods and large tungsten-bead streamers connect with dorado that fight far above their weight. Water temperatures run 24 to 28°C from April through November. The surrounding Isiboro-Sécure buffer zone holds jaguars, giant river otters, and an extraordinary bird list alongside the fishing.

## The Ichilo River — Peacock Bass and Dorado

The Ichilo drains the eastern Andean foothills near Cochabamba and flows into the Mamoré near Trinidad. Its gradient creates a mixture of fast runs and deep pools that hold both dorado and large peacock bass. Pesque y Pague Santa Clara and Pesque y Pague Mi Refugio near Cochabamba have tapped into local demand for accessible recreational fishing in stocked ponds, but the wild upper Ichilo is where serious anglers head, particularly from July to September.

## The Beni River — Jungle Diversity

Draining the vast highland plain of the Altiplano via the La Paz and Beni departments, the Beni River is one of South America's great lowland rivers. By the time it reaches Rurrenabaque it has descended from the cold highlands to warm jungle water at around 400 metres above sea level. Surubí catfish (a spotted tiger-patterned predator) exceeding 40 kg share the river with dorado, pacu, and large piranha. Pesque y Pague Quinta Paraíso and Pesque y Pague Hermanos Atta cater to local fishing culture in the department; the wild Beni itself draws expedition groups.

## The Chapare Region — Lowland Rivers Near Cochabamba

The Chapare is Bolivia's tropical zone east of Cochabamba, where several rivers — including the Chapare, Ichoa, and Isiboro — cut through dense forest on their way to the Mamoré. The climate here is hot and humid, averaging 28°C, and fishing is year-round though best in the dry season from May to September. Large surubí, pacu, and dorado inhabit the deeper pools. Pesque y Pague Chivé is one of several community-run fishing spots in the region, and access to wild rivers is improving as tourism infrastructure grows.

## The Pilcomayo River — South-Eastern Bolivia

Rising near Potosí at over 4,200 metres and falling through the Gran Chaco plain to the Argentinian border, the Pilcomayo is a geographically dramatic river. Its upper sections in the highlands hold small populations of torrent ducks and Andean catfish (bagres). Lower down where the river enters Chaco scrubland the species change dramatically — surubí and large dorado appear in the muddy current. Pesca Deportiva and Pesca Las Palmas in the southern departments cater to the growing local sport fishing culture in this often-overlooked corner of Bolivia.

## The Yacuma River — Wildlife and Fishing Combined

The Yacuma is a calm, clear-water river in Beni department that flows through the Barba Azul Nature Reserve. Its waters hold dorado, surubí, and peacock bass, and the surrounding pampas are among the best places in South America to see anaconda, capybara, caimans, and the giant river otter. Several eco-lodges including Pesque y Pague Rancho Matías cater to anglers who want wildlife encounters alongside their fishing. The Yacuma is productive from June to October and particularly good for fly-fishing with surface lures early and late in the day.

## Lake Rogaguado — Remote Northern Bolivia

North of Trinidad, Lake Rogaguado is one of the largest lakes in Bolivia's Beni department. Rarely visited by international anglers, it holds large peacock bass, pacu, surubí, and numerous smaller cichlid species. The lake is shallow — generally under 4 metres — and ringed by floating meadows and gallery forest. Access requires a river journey from Trinidad and the lake experiences minimal fishing pressure, making it an exceptional destination for anglers seeking wild waters away from any established circuit. Pesque y Pague Super Pacu near Beni town is a staging point for trips into this remote region.
