Rain falls on the Olympic Peninsula at over 3,500 mm per year in the wettest valleys, and that rain feeds something extraordinary: rivers that run green and cold and full of some of the most powerful migratory fish in the world. The Pacific Northwest is defined by water — the Pacific Ocean crashing against sea stacks and rocky headlands, the rivers threading through old-growth forest, the broad tide flats of Puget Sound where harbor seals follow the same salmon runs that humans have pursued for ten thousand years. Fishing here is not a casual pastime. It is a pursuit embedded in the landscape, the culture, and the biology of a region that has given the world a disproportionate share of its greatest angling legends.

## The Hoh River — Wild Steelhead Country

The Hoh River drains the western Olympics, dropping from glaciated peaks through ancient Sitka spruce and western hemlock rainforest before reaching the Pacific near Forks. It holds one of the last significant populations of wild winter steelhead on the continent — fish that average over 5 kg and have never seen a hatchery. Regulations on the Hoh's catch-and-release sections are among the most conservative in the state, reflecting the fragility of the wild population. Swinging flies — traditional spey casting with large wet flies — is the preferred method. January through March is prime, when the river runs at a fishable 80–120 cubic metres per second and chrome-bright fish fresh from the ocean hold in the deeper pools.

## The Sol Duc River

The Sol Duc drains the northern Olympics and is perhaps the most celebrated steelhead river on the Peninsula. It receives both hatchery and wild fish, allowing retention of hatchery steelhead and making it an accessible entry point for anglers not yet comfortable with pure catch-and-release fishing. The lower Sol Duc, accessible from Highway 101, holds fish from November through March. The upper canyon above the Sol Duc Hot Springs, where the river drops through a series of dramatic falls, holds summer steelhead from July through September. The Sol Duc's greener, more transparent water compared to the tannic Hoh makes it a spectacular visual experience.

## Puget Sound — Wild Chinook

Puget Sound is an inland sea of 2,600 km² extending from Admiralty Inlet south to Olympia, and its wild chinook salmon are among the most contested fish in American conservation debates. Populations have declined dramatically since the mid-20th century, and regulations change annually. When open, chinook fishing in Puget Sound — particularly around the Tacoma Narrows, the area between Kingston and Edmonds, and the waters off Point No Point — delivers encounters with fish averaging 8–10 kg, with individuals exceeding 20 kg each season. Mooching with fresh herring at depth is the traditional technique; downrigger trolling with flasher-and-plug rigs also produces consistent results.

## The Westport and Ilwaco Halibut Charters

The coast south of Grays Harbor and the mouth of the Columbia River is the launching point for Washington State's halibut charter industry. Pacific halibut are caught on the offshore banks — the Willapa Ridge, the Grays Canyon, and the Continental Shelf — at depths of 50–200 m. A typical charter departs Westport or Ilwaco at dawn and reaches the halibut grounds within two hours, fishing until the boat limit is reached. Fish average 5–12 kg, though specimens over 40 kg are taken every season. The Pacific halibut season in Washington typically runs from May through September, with quotas set by the International Pacific Halibut Commission.

## The Quinault and Queets Rivers

The Quinault and Queets are two of the most remote steelhead rivers on the Olympic Peninsula, running through the heart of the Olympic National Park's wilderness. The Quinault supports both winter steelhead and summer-run fish, as well as a spring chinook run of significance. The lower Quinault, flowing through the Quinault Indian Nation's territory, requires tribal permits for fishing. The Queets is accessible only by trail in its upper reaches; the hike in through old-growth forest three to four hours each way ensures solitude and native fish that have received almost no fishing pressure. Both rivers hold wild fish exclusively.

## The Columbia River Bar and Lower Columbia

The Columbia River's estuary and lower tidal section between Astoria and Bonneville Dam represents one of the most diverse fisheries in the Pacific Northwest. Spring chinook — fresh, bright, high in fat — stack below the Bonneville Dam fish ladder from March through May, drawing boaters and bank anglers from across the region. Fall chinook and coho follow in August through October. The tidal section from the Columbia bar to Longview holds striped bass, a non-native population that has thrived since mid-century introduction and now provides year-round sport fishing for fish up to 15 kg.

## The Methow and Wenatchee — Inland Steelhead

On the east side of the Cascades, tributaries of the Columbia River provide steelhead runs of a different character — drier landscapes, warmer summers, and fish that have migrated over 400 km from the ocean before they reach the upper Methow or Wenatchee valleys. The Wenatchee River above Leavenworth is particularly productive for summer steelhead from July through September, with fish showing in the 3–6 kg range. The Methow above Twisp holds both summer and fall steelhead. These eastern Washington rivers fish best with a floating line and a waking dry fly on warm August evenings — a dramatic contrast to the spey-rod tactics of the Olympic Peninsula.

## Hood Canal and the Dungeness Crab Season

Hood Canal, the narrow fjord-like arm of Puget Sound that lies between the Kitsap and Olympic Peninsulas, offers a dual fishery of coho salmon in autumn and Dungeness crab year-round. Hood Canal coho, which show from August through October, are known for their excellent table quality. The canal's near-landlocked nature means fish stack at the Hoodsport area for weeks before pushing upstream, creating a prolonged opportunity for bank and boat anglers. Dungeness crab, caught on ring nets or pots in the 5–12 m depth zone along the eastern shoreline, can be taken year-round and reaches eating sizes of 15–20 cm shell-width.

## Willapa Bay — Surf Perch and Dungeness

Willapa Bay on the southern Washington coast is one of the most pristine estuaries remaining in the contiguous United States, with over 7,000 ha of oyster habitat. It provides outstanding surf perch fishing along the open Pacific beach from the Long Beach Peninsula, with redtail surfperch averaging 35–45 cm caught in the surf zone from April through July. The bay itself holds Dungeness crab, flounder, and ghost shrimp. A less pressured alternative to the more famous Westport charters, Willapa Bay's tidal channels and oyster bars can produce excellent winter steelhead when the Willapa River runs clear after rain.
