In 1496, Wynkyn de Worde's press in Westminster printed the second edition of the Book of St Albans, and tucked inside it was the Treatyse of Fysshynge wyth an Angle — the first substantial work on sport fishing in the English language. Tradition credits it to Dame Juliana Berners, prioress of Sopwell Nunnery near St Albans. Whether or not a fifteenth-century nun really wrote it, the text describes braided horsehair lines, twelve artificial fly patterns matched to the months of the year, and rods of hazel, willow and aspen up to fourteen feet long. Anglers were already, five centuries ago, obsessing over the same things we argue about today.

## Fishing for pleasure before print

People fished for fun long before anyone wrote it down. Egyptian tomb paintings from Beni Hasan, around 2000 BC, show nobles angling with rod and line as leisure rather than labour. The Roman author Aelian, writing in the second century AD, described Macedonian anglers on the river Astraeus wrapping red wool and waxy feathers around hooks to imitate a local insect — the earliest clear account of fly fishing. In medieval Europe, monastic communities managed carp ponds and trout streams with remarkable care, and the fish pond became a fixture of abbey life from Burgundy to the English shires. The Treatyse simply gathered up this scattered knowledge and fixed it on paper, adding a moral argument that would echo for centuries: angling, it claimed, was the most virtuous of field sports, good for body and soul alike.

## Izaak Walton and the contemplative ideal

The book that made angling a literary subject arrived in 1653. Izaak Walton, a retired London ironmonger, published The Compleat Angler during the upheaval of Cromwell's England, framing fishing as a peaceful refuge from a violent age. The book is part instruction manual, part pastoral idyll, full of milkmaids' songs and riverside inns along the River Lea north of London. Walton's friend Charles Cotton added a section on fly fishing for trout and grayling in 1676, drawn from his home water, the River Dove in Derbyshire's Peak District, where his fishing temple of 1674 still stands at Beresford Dale. The Compleat Angler has never been out of print since — by some counts only the Bible and the Book of Common Prayer have seen more English editions — and its gentle motto, study to be quiet, still defines a whole school of angling temperament.

## Horsehair, gut and the rise of tackle makers

For most of angling history, tackle was homemade. Lines were plaited from horsehair — the Treatyse explains how to dye it — and a skilled angler tapered the braid from perhaps fifteen hairs at the rod down to two or three at the hook. Silkworm gut from Spain replaced horsehair at the business end during the eighteenth century, and oiled silk lines followed. The reel, first illustrated in English print by Thomas Barker in 1651, evolved from a simple wooden winch into precision brass machinery. By the Victorian era, tackle making was an industry: Hardy Brothers opened in Alnwick, Northumberland, in 1872 and their Perfect reel of 1891 became the most famous piece of fishing equipment ever made. Split-cane rods, built from six triangular strips of Tonkin bamboo, reached an extraordinary refinement in the hands of makers like Hiram Leonard in Maine and the Hardy workshop in England.

## The Victorian boom and the railway effect

Railways did as much for fishing as any rod maker. From the 1840s, trains carried clerks and factory workers out of London, Manchester and Birmingham to rivers they could never have reached on foot, and angling clubs multiplied by the hundred. Match fishing — competitive coarse angling for roach, bream and gudgeon — became a working-class institution across the English Midlands and the north, with whole trainloads of anglers decanting onto the banks of the Trent and the Witham on Sunday mornings. At the other end of the social scale, the chalkstreams of Hampshire became temples of expensive exactitude: the Houghton Club, founded on the River Test in 1822, remains perhaps the most exclusive fishing club on earth. Frederic Halford codified the upstream dry fly there in the 1880s, and G. E. M. Skues promptly started a civil war by championing the sunken nymph on the neighbouring Itchen.

## America, steelhead and the frontier rod

Across the Atlantic, angling took on a different character. The Catskill rivers of New York State — the Beaverkill, Willowemoc and Neversink — became the cradle of American fly fishing in the late nineteenth century, with Theodore Gordon adapting English dry flies to faster, brawnier water. In the West, anglers pushed into Montana, Wyoming and the steelhead rivers of Oregon and British Columbia. Bass fishing grew into a wholly American school with its own technology: the Kentucky-made multiplying baitcasting reels of George Snyder and the Meek brothers, from the 1810s onward, were precision instruments decades ahead of European equivalents. By the twentieth century the United States was the engine room of tackle innovation, from the Shakespeare level-wind to Pflueger hooks and, in 1948, the French-built Mitchell 300 spinning reel that sold by the tens of millions worldwide.

## Plastics, fibreglass and carbon fibre

The Second World War transformed tackle as it transformed everything else. Nylon monofilament, a DuPont invention of the late 1930s, replaced silk and gut almost overnight; suddenly lines were cheap, strong and nearly invisible. Hollow fibreglass rods appeared in the late 1940s and put a usable rod in every hardware store in the world. Then, in the early 1970s, carbon fibre arrived — developed for aerospace at the Royal Aircraft Establishment in Farnborough — and rod building changed forever. A carbon rod weighed half what cane did, recovered faster, and could be made longer without becoming a flagpole. Fixed-spool reels gained skirted spools and silky drags, echo sounders shrank from naval hardware to handheld units, and by the 1990s an ordinary angler carried more technology than a 1950s research vessel.

## Catch and release and the modern conscience

Perhaps the deepest change of the last half century is ethical. Lee Wulff's 1939 remark that a good gamefish is too valuable to be caught only once slowly grew into a movement, and when Ray Scott founded the Bass Anglers Sportsman Society in 1967 and later pushed catch-and-release tournament rules, live release became mainstream in America. In Europe, the picture diverged sharply: catch and release is the norm for UK coarse fishing and Scandinavian salmon rivers like Norway's Gaula impose strict release rules, while in Germany and Switzerland animal-welfare law actually restricts releasing fish that could be eaten. Today's debates — barbless hooks, keepnets, hatchery stocking, hook-and-cook versus release — are simply the latest chapter in a conversation about why we fish that Dame Juliana started in 1496.
