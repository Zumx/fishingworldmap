Bangladesh is the planet's largest delta, a country where land and water trade places twice a year. The Padma, Jamuna and Meghna — the combined outflow of the Ganges and Brahmaputra — braid across it in channels kilometres wide, the Sundarbans mangrove forest guards the seaward edge, and in the northeast whole districts vanish under the seasonal lakes called haors. Fish is not a hobby here but the national diet: machhe bhate Bangali, the saying goes — fish and rice make a Bengali. For the travelling angler that means giant catfish in big rivers, snakehead in village wetlands, mangrove estuaries thick with life, and a pond-fishing culture denser than anywhere on Earth. Here are ten places to understand it.

## The Padma at Rajshahi and Mawa — the great river

The Padma — the Ganges' main distributary — runs broad and tea-brown past Rajshahi and under the Padma Bridge at Mawa. This is the home water of the hilsa, the silver shad that is Bangladesh's national fish, netted on a scale that feeds the country, and of serious rod-and-line targets: long-whiskered rita and ayre catfish, freshwater sharks (boal) and carp along the sandbar edges. Local boatmen take anglers out to fish baited handlines and rods around the deep channels; the post-monsoon months, October to February, give the calmest water.

## The Sundarbans — fishing the world's largest mangrove forest

The Sundarbans, a UNESCO World Heritage labyrinth of tidal channels shared with India, holds an estuarine fishery like nowhere else: bhetki (barramundi), mangrove jack, catfish and mudskipper-flicking shallows, fished from wooden boats under the gaze of spotted deer and — unseen but present — the Bengal tiger. Access requires Forest Department permits and licensed boats from Khulna or Mongla, usually as multi-day live-aboard trips out of Hiron Point and Kotka. The dry season, November to March, is the practical window.

## Tanguar Haor — the wetland sea of Sunamganj

In the far northeast under the Meghalaya hills, Tanguar Haor floods into a hundred-square-kilometre inland sea each monsoon, then shrinks to a mosaic of beels (permanent lakes) in winter. It is a Ramsar site and the richest freshwater fishery in the country, holding well over 140 fish species, including rui, katla, snakehead and the prized chital featherback. Winter boat trips from Sunamganj or Tahirpur combine birdwatching — tens of thousands of migratory ducks — with fishing the deeper beels where the fish concentrate.

## Kaptai Lake — hill-country fishing at Rangamati

Kaptai Lake, created in the 1960s when the Karnaphuli River was dammed in the Chittagong Hill Tracts, spreads over 680 square kilometres of drowned valleys around Rangamati. It is Bangladesh's biggest sportfishing water: rui and katla carp, boal and ayre catfish and snakehead along the wooded margins, fished from country boats between floating markets and Buddhist monasteries on the islands. The lake closes to all fishing for roughly three months from May for spawning — plan around the monsoon and visit October to March.

## The Meghna estuary and Chandpur — hilsa country

Where the combined rivers meet the tide near Chandpur, the Meghna runs so wide the far bank disappears. Chandpur is the hilsa capital — its ghats roar at dawn as the night boats land — and the surrounding waters hold pangas catfish, giant river prawns and bhetki on the brackish edges. Anglers can arrange trips with local fishermen to drift the channels; the hilsa runs peak with the monsoon, but the river fishes year-round. Watch for the seasonal hilsa bans, when all netting and fishing pause to protect the spawn.

## Cox's Bazar and the longest beach on Earth

Cox's Bazar claims the world's longest natural sea beach — about 120 kilometres of sand running south to Teknaf. Off it, the Bay of Bengal gives surf and boat fishing for bhetki, queenfish, threadfin salmon, croakers and rays, with local trawler crews taking anglers out from the Bakkhali River mouth. Winter, November to February, brings the settled seas. The fish markets at the beach end are worth the trip alone: tuna, kingfish and pomfret stacked at sunrise.

## Saint Martin's Island — coral water in the far south

Ten kilometres off the Teknaf peninsula lies Saint Martin's, Bangladesh's only coral island. Its clear water is a different world from the silt-laden delta: snapper, grouper, trevally and barracuda hold around the rock and coral patches, and local boats handline them year-round. Conservation rules on the island have tightened — parts of the reef are protected and visitor numbers capped in season — so check current access before building a trip around it. November to February offers the calm crossings.

## The haors and beels of Sylhet — snakehead at village level

Beyond Tanguar, the whole Sylhet basin — Hakaluki Haor, Hail Haor and countless village beels — is snakehead country. Shol and gajar (great snakehead) ambush frogs in the weed, taken on surface lures and live bait, while koi (climbing perch) and magur fill the margins. This is intimate, walk-and-cast fishing among rice paddies and fishing villages, best arranged through local guides in Sylhet or Srimangal, the tea capital. The receding water of November and December concentrates everything.

## Dhaka's pond scene and the managed lakes

Bangladesh has several million ponds, and recreational angling on them is a national pastime. Around Dhaka, managed waters and market-side lakes — the Ashulia area on the city's northern edge is a long-standing hub — sell day tickets for stocked rui, katla, pangas and tilapia, with whole-day baited-rod competitions (ticket fishing) a weekend institution. In the northwest, districts like Naogaon and Joypurhat are blanketed with hatcheries and farm ponds where a polite request and a small fee often buy an afternoon's fishing.

## Seasons, ethics and practicalities

The monsoon, June to September, redraws the country — roads flood, rivers merge and most sportfishing pauses; October to March is the travelling angler's season. Respect the conservation calendar: hilsa fishing closes during spawning bans, Kaptai closes in early summer, and jatka (juvenile hilsa) must never be taken. There is no national rod licence, but protected areas — the Sundarbans above all — require permits and registered guides. Tackle is basic outside Dhaka, so bring your own lures, hooks and braid. Bargain boat hire politely, accept the tea, and expect an audience for every cast.
