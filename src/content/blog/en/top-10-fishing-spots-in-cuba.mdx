Ernest Hemingway kept a boat, the Pilar, in a fishing village east of Havana for two decades, and the marlin grounds he wrote about still run purple just offshore. But Cuba's real angling treasure is the shallows: thousands of square kilometres of flats, mangrove marls and cays where commercial netting has been excluded for decades. The result is the Caribbean as it looked a century ago — schools of bonefish in ankle-deep water, laid-up tarpon in the lagoons and enough permit to keep you humble.

## Jardines de la Reina — the crown jewel

Ninety kilometres off Cuba's southern coast, the Jardines de la Reina archipelago — the Gardens of the Queen — has been a marine protected area since 1996 and a no-take zone for nearly everything except a small catch-and-release sport fishery. Anglers reach it by liveaboard or land-based operation out of Júcaro, then spend a week poling white-sand flats and mangrove channels. The grand slam is a daily possibility: tailing bonefish at sunrise, a shot at a permit over turtle grass at noon, rolling tarpon in the channels at dusk. The fishery is strictly limited to a handful of skiffs across 2,170 square kilometres.

## Cayo Largo — permit factory of the south

Little Cayo Largo, at the eastern end of the Canarreos archipelago, fishes a marl-and-flat system that many guides rate as the best permit water in the Caribbean. The southern flats hold huge schools of bonefish, but it is the black-tailed permit cruising the white marl that anglers fly across the Atlantic for. The operation is small, the rotation of zones is disciplined, and the fish see very few flies. March to June offers the calmest wind and the biggest strings of permit.

## Zapata Peninsula — bonefish in a UNESCO biosphere

The Ciénaga de Zapata, Cuba's great southern swamp west of the Bay of Pigs, is a biosphere reserve where the flats of Las Salinas hold bonefish that have never seen a net. You wade firm white marl among flamingos, with fish from 2 to 8 pounds tailing in centimetres of water. The fishery is managed from Playa Larga, day-trip distance from Havana — making Zapata the most accessible serious flats fishing on the island. Tarpon stack in the Río Hatiguanico, the blackwater river crossing the swamp, where a floating line and a black-and-purple fly do the damage.

## Cayo Cruz and Cayo Coco — the northern flats

On the Atlantic side, the Jardines del Rey chain shields a lagoon system of seagrass and coral sand. Cayo Cruz, the newest fishery to open, has built a reputation for big permit and consistent oceanside bonefishing; Cayo Coco and Cayo Guillermo (Hemingway's wartime patrol grounds, name-checked in Islands in the Stream) offer flats and channel tarpon within sight of resort beaches. The wading here is some of the firmest in Cuba, and the spring migration brings tarpon along the outer edges.

## Havana's blue water — Hemingway's marlin alley

The Gulf Stream brushes Cuba's north coast within sight of the Malecón, and from May to September white and blue marlin ride it east. The Hemingway International Billfish Tournament, running since 1950, launches from Marina Hemingway west of the city. Charters troll lures and rigged ballyhoo a short run from the harbour — it is some of the closest-to-shore marlin fishing anywhere. Off Cojímar, the village where Hemingway moored the Pilar and found the model for The Old Man and the Sea, local skiffs still handline dorado and wahoo along the same current edge.

## Isla de la Juventud — tarpon and bonefish off the beaten path

The Isle of Youth, Cuba's second-largest island, sees a fraction of the traffic of the marquee destinations. Its southern flats and the surrounding cays hold bonefish, tarpon and snook, fished from the small operation at the island's south. The diving culture around Punta Francés spills over into the fishery: clear water, healthy reef and fish that behave as if it is 1950. Getting there — ferry or small plane from Havana — is part of the adventure.

## The reservoirs — largemouth bass by the thousand

Inland Cuba is dotted with presas, agricultural reservoirs stocked with Florida-strain largemouth bass in the mid-20th century. Presa Hanabanilla, set in the Escambray mountains near Santa Clara, is the storied one — a deep, scenic lake where double-digit bags are routine and the record fish run far beyond. Presa Zaza near Sancti Spíritus, Cuba's largest reservoir at roughly 113 square kilometres, produces astonishing numbers of 2-to-5-pound fish on spinnerbaits and soft plastics. Local boats are simple, the bass have rarely seen pressure, and a topwater dawn on Zaza will recalibrate what you think a "good day" means.

## Punta Arenas and the western tips

Cuba's extremities hide quieter fisheries. Around the western cape near María la Gorda and the sandy points of the Guanahacabibes peninsula — a national park — rocky drop-offs meet the flats, giving shore-based shots at bonefish, snapper and jacks. Small named marks like Punta Arenas, the sand spits favoured by local boatmen, are typical of how Cuban fishing works: ask in the village, find the man with the skiff, share the day. Bring leaders, hooks and flies to leave behind — tackle is scarce and gifts are gold.

## Practicalities — seasons, licences and logistics

Flats fishing is organized through licensed operators who hold zone concessions; you cannot freelance a skiff inside the marine parks, and the weekly packages include the fishing licence. The dry season, November to April, brings the most stable weather; tarpon peak May-July, permit fish well March-June, bonefish bite year-round. Bring everything — 8, 10 and 12-weight rods, spare lines, sun gloves — because replacing gear on the island is near impossible. US citizens must still navigate specific travel categories; most anglers book through European or Canadian agencies. Offshore, May to September is billfish time.
