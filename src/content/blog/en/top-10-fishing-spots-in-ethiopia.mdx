Ethiopia's fishing heritage is ancient and largely unexplored by outside anglers. The country sits at the headwaters of two of Africa's greatest river systems: the Blue Nile, which rises from Lake Tana at an elevation of 1,788 m above sea level and contributes 85 percent of the Nile's total flow, and the Omo River, which drains independently into Lake Turkana — the world's largest permanent desert lake. Lake Tana itself, Ethiopia's largest lake at 3,600 km², holds a tilapia population of extraordinary abundance, with Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) averaging 2 to 4 kg and occasional fish to 8 kg taken on lures and live bait. The catfish of the Blue Nile proper — particularly the enormous African catfish Clarias gariepinus and the torpedo-shaped vundu (Heterobranchus longifilis) — reach proportions that rival any freshwater species on the continent. Further south, the Omo River's rapid, rock-strewn gorge is the last frontier of East African tigerfish fishing.

## Lake Tana: Nile Tilapia and Nile Perch

Lake Tana near Bahir Dar in the Amhara Region is a high-altitude lake of unusual character: its waters are clear near the outflow but turbid around the inflow of the 37 rivers that feed it. Nile tilapia here are the dominant sport species, averaging 1.5 to 3.5 kg with individuals to 7 kg documented. Fishing from traditional papyrus tankwas — the same style of boat used on the lake for over a millennium — is a unique cultural experience. The northern bays around Gorgora, near the remains of a Portuguese fort, hold the largest fish. Nile perch (Lates niloticus) are also present in the lake in smaller numbers, reaching 15 to 20 kg. The best fishing is during Ethiopia's dry season, November through May, when the lake is at its clearest.

## The Blue Nile Falls Area (Tis Issat)

The Blue Nile exits Lake Tana through a broad, shallow delta before narrowing to pour over the 42-metre-high Tis Issat Falls — the "smoking water" — near Bahir Dar. The plunge pools below the falls and the deep canyon immediately downstream concentrate massive catfish that feed on the stunned fish swept over the falls. African catfish (Clarias gariepinus) of 15 to 25 kg are the primary target here, taken on large cut-bait rigs or whole dead fish on 7/0 to 10/0 hooks. The gorge section of the Blue Nile below the falls is accessible only on foot; the 4-hour trek from Tis Issat village is rewarded with virtually unfished water.

## Blue Nile Gorge, Northern Ethiopia

As the Blue Nile carves south and then west through the Ethiopian Highlands, it creates a gorge sometimes compared to a smaller version of the Grand Canyon — dropping 1,200 m in elevation over 500 km. The river running at the gorge bottom is turbid with red clay for most of the rainy season (June–September) but clears dramatically in October and November. These months produce excellent catfish fishing in the deep pools at river bends: both Clarias gariepinus and vundu (Heterobranchus longifilis) to 40 kg have been caught by Ethiopian anglers using traditional longlines. Sport anglers reaching these remote pools with heavy spinning gear can encounter fish that have never been targeted on rod and reel.

## Rift Valley Lakes: Ziway and Langano

The Ethiopian Rift Valley hosts a chain of lakes at elevations between 1,500 and 1,800 m. Lake Ziway, 160 km south of Addis Ababa, is a shallow freshwater lake covering 434 km² with excellent tilapia fishing from traditional boats. Tilapia here average 1 to 2 kg but are extraordinarily abundant — schooling in dense formations over the weed beds along the eastern shore. Lake Langano, 50 km south of Ziway, is a saline-alkaline lake with lower fish diversity but hosts the endemic Langano barbus (Varicorhinus beso), a large carp-like fish reaching 3 kg that provides excellent light-tackle sport on small spinners.

## Lake Hawassa

Lake Hawassa, at 1,680 m elevation in the southern Rift Valley, is the cleanest and most accessible of the rift lakes for visiting anglers. The lake covers 157 km² and is famous among Ethiopian anglers for its Nile tilapia reaching 4 to 6 kg and its population of African catfish that can be taken from the waterfront restaurants' jetties. The hippo pool area on the northeastern shore provides deep water accessible by boat where catfish concentrate at 8 to 12 m depth. Ethiopia's mild Hawassa climate and regular air connections from Addis Ababa make this the most practical entry point for overseas fishing visitors.

## Omo River: Tigerfish and Crocodile Country

The Omo River drains southwestern Ethiopia through one of the most remote and biologically diverse landscapes on the continent, ultimately emptying into Lake Turkana in Kenya. The middle Omo — particularly the stretch through the Omo National Park and the Mago National Park — holds African tigerfish (Hydrocynus vittatus and H. forskahlii) in its fast rocky sections. Fish of 2 to 6 kg are present, with larger individuals reported by Ethiopian fishers using traditional gear near the park boundary. The Omo is also home to enormous Nile crocodiles: fishing from a boat with a second person watching the water is essential. Access to the river fishing is via Jinka, a small town reached by road from Arba Minch.

## Lake Turkana (Ethiopian Side) and Chew Bahir

The Ethiopian shore of Lake Turkana — accessible via the Omo delta — is wild and utterly remote, one of the few places in the world where one can fish for Nile perch exceeding 100 kg in conditions barely changed since the Pleistocene. The alkaline, jade-green waters of Turkana hold the world's largest population of Nile crocodiles alongside the densest population of large Nile perch known anywhere. Fish of 60 to 80 kg are relatively common when targeting them with large swimbaits or live tilapia fished at 15 to 25 m depth. The nearby Chew Bahir wetland at the confluence of the Weito and Sagan rivers offers additional tilapia and barbus fishing in a spectacular landscape.

## Awash River and Awash National Park

The Awash River flows east from the Highlands near Addis Ababa before disappearing into the Afar Depression. Within Awash National Park, the river runs through a dramatic basalt gorge flanked by doum palms and fig trees. African catfish of 8 to 15 kg patrol the deeper pools at the base of Awash Falls, and large labeo (Labeo forskalii) — the African carp relative — are abundant in the runs above the park. Fishing the Awash is an experience framed by the sight of baboons drinking at the water's edge and squadrons of yellow-billed hornbills overhead.

## Lake Abiata and Shala

Lakes Abiata and Shala, twin lakes in the central Rift Valley connected by a narrow channel, represent contrasting habitats. Shala is deeply alkaline and largely fishless, but Abiata — a soda lake — holds concentrations of flamingos and surprisingly robust tilapia populations along its freshwater inlet channels. Barbus populations are accessible in the river above the lake, particularly the Bulbulo River where it enters the western end of Abiata, and provide good light-tackle sport on small lures and flies.

## Genale River, Southern Ethiopia

The Genale River (known as the Jubba River in Somalia) rises in the Sidama highlands and flows southeast. Its upper reaches near Dilla and Yirgalem run over granite bedrock in clear, well-oxygenated water unusual for Ethiopian lowland rivers. These conditions support large catfish and Nile tilapia populations in the deeper pools, with the catfish averaging 5 to 12 kg and occasionally exceeding 20 kg. The Genale is the least explored major fishing river in Ethiopia and represents a genuine frontier for fly fishing in the country's south.
